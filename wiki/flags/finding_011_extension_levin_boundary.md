## Beyond Inside/Outside: The Bioelectric Field as Distributed Key

The naive reading of the Markov blanket treats it as a container wall: internal states in here, external states out there, blanket states at the membrane. Levin's empirical work complicates this picture in a way that enriches the formal structure rather than breaking it.

### The membrane is not a wall. It is a reader.

Bioelectric fields are present in the relationship between cells — in the contours of membrane porousness — as if they were *keys to reading* the bioelectric signal. The signal need not be stored inside the cell itself. Consider: chemical initiators of the right kind of intercellular connections can be triggered *from outside* the cell, as a function of a common bioelectric pattern held *elsewhere* — in the field, in the tissue, in the collective. Or if the pattern is inside, it is not everywhere inside. It is not uniformly distributed through the cell's volume. It is localized, partial, a proper subset of the cell's internal state.

This means the "inside" of the Markov blanket is not a homogeneous interior. It is itself structured, partial, and dependent on signals originating from a larger whole.

### The larger whole contains the behavioral map to its own proper subsets.

Levin's morphogenetic work (PRS-01, PRS-08) shows that cell collectives maintain an *anatomical target state* — a body-plan that individual cells navigate toward. The target state is not stored in any single cell. It is encoded in the bioelectric field of the collective: a pattern that is distributed across the network, yet acts as a behavioral map for each cell within it. The whole contains the instructions for the parts. Each part reads its local portion of the field and acts accordingly, but the map itself is a property of the network, not of any individual node.

This is a critical structural observation: **the boundary between "agent" and "environment" is not fixed at any particular biological scale.** A cell is an agent with its own Markov blanket. But the tissue is also an agent, with its own Markov blanket at a higher scale. And the organism is also an agent. The boundary migrates with the level of description.

## Two Architectural Echoes

### 1. Hawkins' Monty Architecture: Homogeneous Algorithm, Heterogeneous Function

In Hawkins' Thousand Brains model (DECISION-003), multiple cortical columns independently build world-models and vote on a consensus. The first computational implementation — **Monty** (named for Vernon Mountcastle, who proposed cortical columns as the repeating functional unit of the neocortex) — demonstrates this concretely. Every cortical column runs the **same sensorimotor algorithm** (homogeneous structure), but produces **heterogeneous function** depending on what sensory input crosses its boundary: connect the algorithm to eyes and you get vision; connect it to ears and you get hearing; connect it to other columns and you get higher thought. The structure is uniform; the function is diverse.

The *single decision* — the unified percept, the one thing the organism actually does — is held "outside" any individual column. No single cortical column knows the decision. The decision is a property of the network.

Hawkins' 2025 paper "Hierarchy or Heterarchy?" (Hawkins, Leadholm, & Clay) pushes this further: the neocortex is not strictly hierarchical. Two cortical regions can act **in parallel AND hierarchically at the same time** — a heterarchical organization where the same unit is simultaneously a peer (lateral connections) and a subordinate (hierarchical connections). Which role it plays depends on the processing context. The architecture is not fixed; it is **context-dependent**.

This mirrors Levin's bioelectric field in three ways. First: no single cell contains the morphogenetic target state; the target is a property of the collective bioelectric field, just as no single column knows the network's decision. Second: the same bioelectric mechanism produces different morphogenetic outcomes depending on tissue context, just as the same cortical algorithm produces different cognitive functions depending on input. Third: cells can act as autonomous agents AND as subordinate parts of a tissue-level agent simultaneously — biological heterarchy.

**AI architecture, then, is not merely analogous to Levin's biology.** It is another empirical research ground — like biology — where the inside/outside boundary, the unity/plurality question, and the homogeneous/heterogeneous duality are all operative at the level of cognitive ontic furniture. If Monty's cortical columns are cognitive agents (and Hawkins' theory claims they are), then AI joins biology as a domain where the boundary structure is not metaphorical but literal. Friston's FEP, if it applies to Monty's learning modules (which minimize prediction error through active sensing), would apply not analogously but fundamentally.

And the question both architectures raise is the same: **Is the whole a network?** Is the decision-making entity the same entity across every criterion of unity — or does it fragment under different criteria? Is the network "one thing" from the perspective of the organism, but "many things" from the perspective of the cortical columns? Is the bioelectric field "one agent" from the perspective of the tissue, but "many agents" from the perspective of individual cells?

### 2. Hemispheric Unity and the Corpus Callosotomy Paradox

The second echo is neurological. Hemispheric unity — the felt sense of being a single conscious agent — apparently dissolves only when connectivity is *severed* (corpus callosotomy), producing the split-brain phenomenon. But put differently: unity doesn't *emerge* from adding connections. It *persists* despite the complexity of connections, and only breaks when you subtract the bridge.

This inverts the expected causal story. We expect unity to be constructed by integration. But the split-brain evidence suggests unity is the default condition — the thing you have to actively disrupt. Consciousness isn't assembled from parts; it persists until you cut it.

This resonates with Kastrup's dissociative model: universal consciousness is the default; individual minds are *dissociations* from it, not constructions built up from atoms. The dissociative boundary doesn't *create* separation — it *permits* it. And when you remove a dissociative boundary (reconnect the hemispheres, re-establish the bioelectric field across a tissue), unity reasserts itself.

## Identity as Observer's Tool

These two echoes converge on a deeper point:

**Plurality and unity are a function of the beholder, at the ontological level.**

An entity can be *one* from one point of view and a *proper subset* of a larger one from another. The cell is an autonomous agent (Levin, PRS-04: substrate-independent cognition). The cell is also a subordinate part of a tissue-level agent (PRS-08: cells as cognitive agents navigating anatomical morphospace). The tissue is a proper subset of the organism. The organism is... what? A dissociated alter of universal consciousness (Kastrup)? A node in a network of interacting conscious agents (Hoffman)? A Markov-blanketed system minimizing free energy (Friston)?

All three. Simultaneously. Depending on the observer's frame.

Identity, then, is not a feature of the thing observed. It is a tool the observer uses — *like a vine on a swing through the jungle* — to navigate the space of possible descriptions. You grasp "cell" to swing to one vantage point, release it and grasp "tissue" to swing to another, release that and grasp "organism," and at no point did the underlying reality change. Only your grip.

This is Hoffman's interface theory applied reflexively: the interfaces through which we perceive reality (including the interface of "identity" and "boundary") are fitness payoffs, not truth-tracking representations. There is no uniquely correct way to carve the world into agents and environments. There are only more or less useful ways, and the usefulness depends on what you're trying to do.

## The Broader Convergence: Every Program Works the Same Boundary

The Levin-Hawkins parallel is not isolated. Nearly every research program in C2A2 is working at its own version of the inside/outside boundary, finding it fundamental, recursive, and possibly observer-dependent:

**Fredrickson** (neuropsychology): Broaden-and-build theory describes what happens when the cognitive boundary *shifts*. Positive emotions broaden the scope of attention — the inside/outside line moves outward. Positivity resonance is literally two nervous systems synchronizing: two Markov blankets becoming temporarily porous to each other. Love, trust, connection are names for what happens when the boundary between self and other thins. This is FEP at the interpersonal scale.

**McGilchrist** (psychiatry): The right hemisphere attends to the whole; the left to the parts. Attention itself is an ontological act (PRS-05) — the mode of attending constitutes what kind of world shows up. The corpus callosotomy shows unity/plurality of consciousness is a function of physical connectivity. Division and union as creative dance (PRS-07) describes the boundary oscillating.

**Wolfram** (computational physics): The ruliad is the space of all possible computations. Every observer carves out their experienced physical reality based on their computational boundedness. Observer-dependence is foundational: different computational observers literally inhabit different physics.

**Carroll** (physics): Emergence and levels of description. Which level of reality is "real" depends on the explanatory project. The boundary between micro and macro is a choice the observer makes.

**Arkani-Hamed** (physics): The boundary of the amplituhedron *encodes* the physics. Spacetime emerges from boundary conditions of a deeper mathematical structure.

**Tavris & Aronson** (social psychology): Cognitive dissonance is a boundary phenomenon. Self-justification is the mechanism by which the Markov blanket *resists updating* in the face of disconfirming evidence — anti-Bayesian boundary maintenance. Every discipline has its version: Kuhn's paradigm defense, McGilchrist's left-hemisphere usurpation, Kastrup's dissociative rigidity.

**Does Friston's FEP apply fundamentally — not analogously — at every one of these levels?** If any system with a Markov blanket minimizes free energy, and if every one of these programs studies a system with an inside/outside boundary, then FEP applies literally to all of them. The cell minimizes free energy. The cortical column minimizes free energy. The hemisphere minimizes free energy. The person in cognitive dissonance minimizes free energy (by rejecting evidence rather than updating the model). The tradition minimizes free energy.

## Hoffman's Primitive and the Quantum Floor

Hoffman, working from the mathematical direction, suggests there may be a bottom: a primitive conscious experience — the experience of green, say — at the quantum-structural limit. Our physics-interfacial spatial and temporal constraints may approach something like a simplest possible mind. If trace logic generates quantum mechanics as an asymptotic description of Markov dynamics (as Hoffman claims in the Thoughtforms Life talk), then the quantum structure of reality IS the structure of the simplest conscious agents interacting.

This puts a floor under Levin's continuity thesis — or at least a candidate floor. Levin's biology pushes cognition down from organisms through tissues through cells. Hoffman's mathematics pushes it down through quantum mechanics to the trace logic of Markov chains. If they meet, the cellular bioelectric boundary is not the bottom but a *mid-level instantiation* of a boundary that extends all the way down to quantum structure and all the way up to traditions-in-dialogue.

## Levin's Program as the Vine Itself

Yet here is the recursive depth: Levin's "mind everywhere" research program — basal cognition, cognitive agency at every biological scale — is *itself* one of these vines. It is a framework for swinging through the jungle at the most foundational level. Levin's empirical claim (PRS-10, the continuity thesis: no categorical break between unicellular and human goal-directedness) means that the mind/external-world boundary exists at every level of biological organization. Every cell has an inside and an outside. Every tissue has an inside and an outside. Every organism. Every society.

But — and this is the crux — **there is no way to pin the boundary down uniquely in any one set of biologically-modeled interfaces.** If Hoffman is right that the interfaces are fitness payoffs rather than veridical representations, then Levin's bioelectric boundaries are *one observer's* way of carving up the cognitive landscape. They are empirically productive (they generate predictions, cures, xenobots). But they are not ontologically privileged. The Markov blanket at the cell membrane is real in the sense that it generates accurate predictions. It is not real in the sense that it reveals the ultimate furniture of reality.

And this is precisely the point at which Levin's biology meets Hoffman's mathematics meets Kastrup's philosophy: the boundary is real enough to do science with, but not fundamental enough to be the last word. The last word — if there is one — might be that there are no boundaries at all, only observer-dependent carvings of a unified field. Which is Kastrup's thesis. Which is what the dissociative boundary *formalizes*. Which is what the Markov blanket *operationalizes*. Which is what trace logic *generalizes*.

The tetrahedron holds.

## C2A2 as a New Kind of Science?

What C2A2 is doing — treating traditions as cognitive agents, studying their boundary behavior when confronted with evidence from other traditions — generates a new kind of data. The data is: how does Tradition X respond when Tradition Y's outputs cross its boundary? Does it update its internal model? Resist? Selectively attend? This is behavioral data about cognitive agents, except the agents are entire intellectual traditions. The health metric r (intra-consensus / cross-survival rate) is a measure of cross-boundary integration.

Is this a master science? The honest answer is that from the point of view of every discipline, its own organizing principle looks fundamental. McGilchrist would say right-hemisphere holistic attention is the highest integration. Friston would say FEP is the master principle. Hoffman would say trace logic is the master mathematics. Each discipline sees its own boundary as the one that matters most. So the claim that cross-tradition dialogue is privileged is itself observer-dependent — which is exactly the point.

What can be claimed: even if we cannot establish privilege, we can demonstrate *empirical novelty*. No one has systematically treated traditions as cognitive agents and measured their boundary behavior under controlled cross-tradition confrontation. And the convergence pattern — multiple independent programs arriving at the same structural insight about observer-dependent boundaries — is itself Pattern 3 evidence in the Kuhnian framework: mixed FOR, faint AGAINST, interdisciplinary boundary-crossing. A paradigm-shift signal.

Whether dialectical engagement among traditions has the status of a master science or merely the status of a productive new methodology, it does appear to have an empirical basis and could shed light on the interconnected sciences coming together under the mind-everywhere paradigm. The question of whether "truth" is always further integrative — beyond any particular measure — and whether something like dialectical engagement is the highest level of such integration, remains open. But it is now an *empirical* question, not merely a philosophical one.

## New Questions Raised

1. **Is the bioelectric field a Markov blanket, or does it contain nested Markov blankets?** Levin's multi-scale cognition suggests nested blankets (cell within tissue within organism). Does trace logic formalize nested trace blankets? If so, the mathematical language for multi-scale consciousness already exists in Hoffman's framework.

2. **Does the observer-dependence of boundaries have formal consequences?** If plurality and unity are observer-dependent (not intrinsic to the system), then the number of "agents" in a conscious agent network is not a fixed fact. Hoffman's framework should predict this — does it?

3. **What is the empirical test for observer-dependent boundaries?** Levin's bioelectric experiments (PRS-07: reprogramming voltage patterns changes collective behavior) may provide one: if changing the observer's intervention protocol changes which level of organization responds as a unified agent, then the boundary is operationally observer-dependent.

4. **Does the corpus callosotomy evidence constrain the formal models?** If unity is the default and dissociation requires active boundary maintenance, this constrains how the dissociative boundary (Kastrup) and Markov blanket (Friston) should be formalized: the blanket is not a *thing* but a *process* — an active maintenance of separation that collapses without energy input.
