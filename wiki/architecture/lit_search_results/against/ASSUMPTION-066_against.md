    1. Critiques of "A New Kind of Science": Drysdale (2003); Krantz (2002); various reviews in Mathematical Intelligencer — argue Wolfram's "new kind of science" is repackaging of prior ideas (cellular automata theory of von Neumann, Conway, Langton) rather than method-export of something new.
    2. Philosophy-of-physics scholarship (Maudlin, Norton) — reads parts of Wolfram's program (especially the Wolfram Physics Project) as reinventing primitives already worked out in causal-set theory, computational mechanics, and category-theoretic physics.
    3. Direction-of-influence ambiguity: Wolfram's framework borrows heavily from category theory (Eilenberg-Mac Lane, Lawvere) and from algorithmic information theory (Solomonoff, Kolmogorov, Chaitin); these are imports, not exports, even if the framing is export-style.
    4. Critiques of "ruliad as philosophical primitive" (e.g., physics-blog commentary 2020–2023; Hossenfelder podcast critiques): the ruliad is an extension of a "space of all computations" idea already present in the algorithmic-information-theory and modal-realist literatures.
    5. No-consistent-direction reading: some reception treats Wolfram as oscillating between "physicist exporting to philosophy" and "computational metaphysician importing from logic and mathematics," rather than holding a stable direction.

  Strength of challenge: Moderate

  Summary: The export framing captures Wolfram's self-description but understates the extent to which his program imports primitives from algorithmic information theory, category theory, and prior computational physics. A more accurate reading is "method-circulation" — Wolfram both imports (often without explicit acknowledgment) and exports. The C2A2 reading as pure export risks reading too cleanly.

  Specific risks: (a) CROSS-016/024/026 inherit a mis-characterization if Wolfram is read as pure export when he is mixed; (b) syntheses using Wolfram as a "philosophy will have to adopt this" tradition may misrepresent his actual relation to existing philosophy.
