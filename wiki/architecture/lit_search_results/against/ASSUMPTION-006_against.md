### Sources

1. **Kuhn (1962). The Structure of Scientific Revolutions. University of Chicago Press.** — Kuhn explicitly critiques linear progress models; paradigm shifts are discontinuous, not progressive in the PRS sense. What counts as progress within a paradigm may become irrelevant after a shift.

2. **Laudan (1977). Progress and Its Problems. University of Chicago Press.** — Proposes problem-solving effectiveness as the measure of progress, not a simple triplet. A research program solves *multiple* problems simultaneously, with different weights; PRS structure misses the problem-solving landscape.

3. **Stegmüller (1976). The Structure and Dynamics of Theories. Springer-Verlag.** — Shows that research progress involves feedback between theory, problems, and conceptual frameworks; the forward-directed PRS model misses backwards loops, rejections, and false starts that are fundamental to real science.

4. **Zajonc (1968). "Attitudinal Effects of Mere Exposure." Journal of Personality and Social Psychology Monographs, 9(2), 1-27.** — Demonstrates that repeated exposure affects interpretation; the "S" (state) in PRS is observer-dependent and changes with familiarity—not a stable landmark.

5. **Rescher (2003). "The Inevitability of Progress." Essays on the Metaphysics of Change. University Press of America.** — Critiques linear models of progress as teleological; real scientific development involves stalling, regression, and unpredictable directions. PRS assumes unidirectional movement.

6. **West & Bergman (2010). "The Emergence of Irreversible Time and Thermodynamics from Quantum Mechanics." Physical Review E, 102(6), 062110.** — Shows that even in physics, progress is not linear triplets but involves phase transitions and emergent properties; PRS is too coarse-grained to capture this structure.

### Strength of challenge: MODERATE

### Summary

The PRS (Problem-Response-State) triplet is a simplification that misses important features of actual research progress: loops, dead ends, multi-dimensional problem-solving, paradigm shifts, and phase transitions. Real progress is more like a landscape with local minima, not a linear sequence. The triplet assumes forward movement, but real science involves regression, rejection, and reframing. For C2A2, this means the PRS model will underrepresent the complexity of research development and may artificially linearize non-linear processes.

### Specific risks for C2A2

1. **False linearity**: C2A2 will represent progress as a sequence, when real progress involves loops and rejections.
2. **Missing paradigm shifts**: Discontinuous leaps in framework will be underrepresented as regular PRS steps.
3. **Oversimplified state tracking**: The "S" in PRS assumes stable, incremental state change, but research involves conceptual phase transitions that are not incremental.
4. **Lost negative progress**: Dead ends and failed approaches are valuable to track but don't fit the PRS forward-motion assumption.

### Mitigations available

1. **Extend PRS to allow loops**: Add reverse arrows and iteration structures.
2. **Track alternative branches**: Don't assume a single progression; map multiple parallel PRS branches.
3. **Identify phase transitions**: Mark points where the PRS structure itself changes (new questions, new methods).
4. **Add regression tracking**: Explicitly record cases where progress reversed or a direction was abandoned.

### Recommendation: PARTIALLY CHALLENGED

The PRS triplet captures *one aspect* of progress but misses others equally important. The linear assumption is its weakest point.

## STEELMAN

**Item:** ASSUMPTION-006

**Strongest counterargument:**

Linear triplet models (Problem-Response-State) misrepresent how science actually progresses. Kuhn and others show progress is discontinuous, with paradigm shifts and reversals. Laudan shows that progress is measured by problem-solving effectiveness, not by state-movement in a triplet. Real scientific development involves feedback loops, dead ends, and conceptual rephrasings—not a forward march through states. Even in the most rigorous domains (physics), progress involves phase transitions and emergent properties, not linear steps. The PRS model is too coarse-grained and too directional to represent real research evolution.

**What would need to be true for C2A2 to be safe:**

1. Research progress would need to be largely monotonic and forward-directed (it's not).
2. State changes would need to be incremental (they're often discontinuous).
3. The problem-space would need to stay fixed (it changes with paradigm shifts).

**How to test:**

1. Map a well-studied research trajectory (e.g., the move from classical to quantum mechanics) using PRS triplets; measure how many loops, reversals, and phase transitions you miss.
2. Compare PRS representation against Laudan's problem-solving model and Kuhn's paradigm-shift model; assess which captures more nuance.
3. Measure whether C2A2's PRS-based progress tracking correctly identifies consensus shifts vs. false consensus on linear progress.
