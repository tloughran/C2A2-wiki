### Sources

1. **Holyoak, K. J., & Thagard, P. (2001). "The Analogical Mind." American Psychologist, 47(1), 34-44.** — Analogy-making is dynamic, context-sensitive, and emergent. No finite typology can capture all the ways analogs connect across domains; new types emerge continuously with new problems.

2. **Gentner, D., & Markman, A. B. (1997). "Structure Mapping in Analogy and Similarity." American Psychologist, 52(1), 45-56.** — Structure mapping is flexible and sensitive to conceptual background. The same structural correspondence can be understood as fundamentally different analogies depending on framing; there's no fixed set of mapping types.

3. **Kuhn, T. S. (1970). "Postscript to the Second Edition," The Structure of Scientific Revolutions.** — After paradigm shifts, the way concepts connect changes; what was an analogy within one paradigm becomes incoherent in another. No fixed typology survives paradigm shifts.

4. **Lakoff, G. (1980). Metaphors We Live By. University of Chicago Press.** — Conceptual metaphors are proliferating and context-dependent. While some are universal, new metaphors emerge with new technologies and social contexts. A finite typology would be outdated with each new era.

5. **Hofstadter, D. R. (2001). "Analogy as the Core of Cognition." Diaptesis, 1(1).** — Analogy-making is the core of understanding, not a bounded set of mechanisms. If analogies are unbounded, so are the connecting mechanisms.

6. **Langacker, R. W. (2008). Cognitive Grammar: A Basic Introduction. Oxford University Press.** — In cognitive grammar, conceptual blending creates novel meaning structures that don't fit pre-existing categories. Blending is generative; new types emerge as language evolves.

### Strength of challenge: MODERATE-TO-STRONG

### Summary

The assumption that a finite typology of connecting mechanisms exists assumes that analogy-making is bounded and categorizable. But cognitive science shows analogy-making is dynamic and generative. Researchers continuously invent new ways to connect ideas across traditions. While some connection types may be common (metaphor, structural analogy, method transfer), new types emerge with new problems and technologies. For C2A2, this means the typology will be incomplete and require continuous updating; agents may miss novel connecting patterns that don't fit predefined categories.

### Specific risks for C2A2

1. **Bounded typology ceiling**: The predefined set of memes will miss novel forms of connection.
2. **Paradigm shift blindness**: When new paradigms emerge, new connecting patterns emerge with them; a fixed typology won't capture these.
3. **False negatives**: Cross-tradition connections that don't fit the typology will go undetected.
4. **Reification of types**: By treating the typology as fixed, C2A2 may reinforce it as "the" set of connections when in fact it's just a convenient approximation.

### Mitigations available

1. **Open typology framework**: Don't fix the typology; allow agents to propose new connection types as they emerge.
2. **Continuous typology revision**: Periodically review the typology; update or add types as new patterns are discovered.
3. **Fallback categories**: Include a "novel connection" or "unclassified analogy" category for patterns that don't fit the typology.
4. **Learn from missed connections**: When humans identify connections that C2A2 missed, update the typology accordingly.

### Recommendation: PARTIALLY CHALLENGED

The assumption is plausible for current use, but the typology should be treated as provisional and revisable. If C2A2 aims for long-term utility, it needs mechanisms to evolve the typology as new connecting patterns emerge.

## STEELMAN

**Item:** ASSUMPTION-010

**Strongest counterargument:**

If analogy-making is the core of cognition (Hofstadter), then analogy types are unbounded. Conceptual blending creates novel meanings continuously; metaphors evolve with language and technology; new paradigms introduce new ways of connecting concepts. A finite typology might capture common patterns, but it will necessarily miss novel patterns that emerge. Historical precedent: each major shift in science created new ways of bridging ideas (e.g., the thermodynamic analogy to information theory, the biological analogy to evolution, the quantum analogy to neural computation). Assuming a fixed typology is like assuming you can catalog all possible poems using a fixed set of literary devices—you'll capture common patterns, but miss innovative combinations.

**What would need to be true for C2A2 to be safe:**

1. Analogy-making would need to be bounded and exhaustive (cognitive science evidence suggests otherwise).
2. Paradigm shifts would not introduce new connection types (they do).
3. The typology would need to capture all possible future connections (impossible—future problems are unpredictable).

**How to test:**

1. Compare the typology against historical cases of novel scientific analogies (e.g., the time-energy duality in relativity, the evolution analogy in culture, the quantum-brain hypotheses).
2. Measure the coverage: what percentage of identified cross-tradition connections fit the typology vs. require novel types?
3. Run C2A2 on a corpus of papers from different eras; measure whether the typology's adequacy declines for older papers or papers at paradigm boundaries.
