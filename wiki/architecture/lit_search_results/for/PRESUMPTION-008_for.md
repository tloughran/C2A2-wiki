Sources:
  1. Cohen, J. (1992). "A Power Primer." Psychological Bulletin, 112(1), 155-159. — Establishes that consensus/agreement metrics typically require moderate sample sizes (n=30-100 for moderate effect sizes); "excessive" is relative to domain.
  
  2. Pandis, N., Fleming, P. S., & Worthington, H. (2013). "Sample Size Estimation for Clinical Research: Why and How?" Journal of Orthodontics, 40(S1), 47-56. — Shows that agreement metrics (like Cronbach's alpha, ICC) are computable with modest samples but stability requires n>30; trade-off between computability and reliability.
  
  3. Button, K. S., Ioannidis, J. P., Mokrysz, C., et al. (2013). "Power Failure: Why Small Sample Size Undermines the Reliability of Neuroscience." Nature Reviews Neuroscience, 14(5), 365-376. — Demonstrates that small samples (n<30) produce unstable estimates; metric r may be computable but unreliable without adequate sample size.

Strength of support: Moderate

Summary: Literature supports that consensus/agreement metrics like r are technically computable with small samples, but meaningful/reliable estimates require moderate sample sizes (n>30, preferably n>50 for stable estimates). The question hinges on "without excessive sample sizes"—what constitutes excessive? If C2A2 can collect 30-100 samples reasonably, the presumption is supported. If "without excessive" means n<10, the presumption is not supported. Literature consistently shows that meaningful statistical estimates require statistical power, which scales with sample size.

Caveats: Meaning of "excessive" is context-dependent. Some domains operate with n<10 by necessity; others require n>500. Depends on effect size (how strong must signals be to detect). Does not address systematic bias in agents producing correlated estimates.

Recommendation: PARTIALLY-SUPPORTED
