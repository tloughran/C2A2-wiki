  Sources:
    1. PRISMA 2020 (Page et al., BMJ 2021) and PRISMA null-reporting guidelines: systematic review methodology explicitly endorses reporting "no studies found" / "no eligible records" as legitimate, informative outcomes. Null reports are treated as first-class outputs, not failures.
    2. Negative-results publication literature (Fanelli 2012; Dirnagl & Lauritzen 2010 "Fighting publication bias"): negative and null results carry scientific value by correcting publication bias and informing resource allocation. The norm that null-reporting is preferable to weak-positive padding is widely endorsed in meta-research.
    3. Selection-effect literature (Ioannidis 2005 "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False"; Simmons et al. 2011 "False-Positive Psychology"): systems that surface weak signals at scale produce higher false-positive rates than systems that accept null outcomes. Null-preference reduces the cost of downstream false-positive filtering.
    4. Cochrane editorial practice (2020-2024 methodological guidance): a review that finds no high-quality studies is a valid, publishable product. The output "honest null" has standing independent of any positive findings.
    5. Signal-to-noise engineering (information theory; Shannon 1948 as applied to monitoring/alerting in Beyer et al. 2016 SRE): noisy outputs degrade downstream decision quality. Null-preference protects the signal-to-noise ratio of downstream review.

  Strength of support: Strong

  Summary: The methodological stance that "honest null > thin proposals" is well-grounded in systematic-review methodology, negative-results advocacy, and signal-to-noise reasoning. PRISMA explicitly treats null outputs as legitimate; the negative-results literature treats the absence of findings as informative; selection-effect literature shows that weak-signal inflation has downstream costs that compound. The stance is consistent with Cochrane practice and with SRE monitoring discipline. For a specialist feeding into a filter-based briefing pipeline, null-preference reduces downstream false-positive load.

  Caveats: (a) The stance does not specify the criterion for "honest" — literature distinguishes "honest null" (exhaustive search found nothing) from "convenient null" (cursory search gave up). Without a criterion, the stance is vulnerable to the convenient-null failure mode (see 15b); (b) PRISMA and Cochrane apply in settings with documented search protocols and pre-registered criteria; specialist agents operating without such protocols cannot fully inherit the methodological warrant; (c) the stance's value is conditional on downstream filter-audit checking whether nulls are indeed honest (see PRESUMPTION-067).

  Recommendation: SUPPORTED (strong methodological support from PRISMA, negative-results literature, and SRE signal-to-noise practice; conditional on a criterion for "honest" and on downstream filter-audit)
