  Sources:
    1. LLM-as-judge literature (Zheng et al. 2023 "MT-Bench"): LLMs achieve high alignment with human experts on many evaluation tasks (>80% in some benchmarks); supports moderate confidence in single-agent recognition for some kinds of pattern.
    2. Multi-agent-ensemble validation (Du et al. 2023; Liang et al. 2024): ensembling multiple LLM agents improves cross-domain pattern detection; relevant if C2A2 specialists are read as ensemble rather than single-agent.
    3. Embedding-based cross-domain similarity (Reimers & Gurevych 2019; Lewis et al. 2020): vector-space methods can recognize cross-domain analogies that survive surface-form differences; supports specialist agents' capacity to detect structural convergence.
    4. C2A2 internal precedent: prior cross-program convergences (e.g., Friston ↔ Hawkins) have been independently confirmed at the genus level; some specialist-agent recognitions have held up.
    5. Tradition-specialist-as-domain-expert: a specialist agent fine-tuned on a tradition has more domain knowledge than a generalist agent; supports moderate confidence in their pattern-detection within domain.

  Strength of support: Moderate (for genus-level patterns; weaker for species-level)

  Summary: There is literature support for LLMs / specialist agents reliably detecting cross-domain patterns at the genus level, especially with embedding-based methods or ensembled judgments. The literature is weaker on species-level technical convergences, where the surface pattern (anti-physicalism, emergent-spacetime) may not survive technical scrutiny. The presumption is supported for the kinds of recognition the system has been doing well; it is weaker for the kinds of recognition that depend on technical detail.

  Caveats: (a) "Reliably" is vague — what reliability rate counts? (b) "Without independent expert verification" is strong; even reliable systems benefit from spot-checking; (c) the reliability rate likely varies with the type of convergence claim.

  Recommendation: PARTIALLY-SUPPORTED (genus-level recognition is moderately supported; species-level is weak; "without verification" is too strong)
