  Sources:
    1. Sopact & CoastAdapt frameworks (2023-2024). "Monitoring And Evaluation Framework + Learning (MEL)." — Confirms that incorporate-monitor-revise cycles are standard in M&E systems, specifying when findings will be reviewed, who decides, and how programs adapt. Emphasizes timeliness: evidence that arrives in time to change a decision creates learning.
    2. Stichler, J.F., 2016. "Research, Research-Informed Design, Evidence-Based Design." SAGE Journals. — Establishes research-informed design as continuous learning cycles where monitoring informs revisions. Widespread adoption of three-part cycles in professional practice.
    3. Structured Decision Making (SDM) methodology. structureddecisionmaking.org. — Demonstrates that structured three-step decision architectures are recognized methodology for tactical decisions, though primarily in clinical/emergency contexts.
    4. ScienceDirect, 2021. "A translational triage research development tool: standardizing prehospital triage decision-making systems." — Documents three-category dispositioning framework (transfer/reject/pending) as standard in emergency medicine triage, with pending items cycled back for re-evaluation.
    
  Strength of support: Moderate
  
  Summary: The three-part disposition cycle (incorporate/monitor/revise) is well established in monitoring-and-evaluation systems, organizational learning frameworks, and emergency medicine triage. Industry evidence confirms that timely feedback loops where evidence informs revision create organizational learning. However, the literature does not specifically validate this framework for self-awareness systems or meta-cognitive evaluation pipelines. Support is broad but domain-adjacent rather than directly targeted at AI architectural self-evaluation.
  
  Caveats: Support is strongest in organizational M&E contexts and clinical triage; transferability to meta-cognitive self-evaluation of AI systems is unstated in literature. No competing three-category frameworks were found, but research did not survey alternative dispositioning schemes (four-category, five-category, continuous scoring, etc.).
