## I. A Provocation

On April 3, 2026 — within twenty-four hours of Andrej Karpathy's public proposal of a wiki-agent methodology for large language model self-documentation — I initialized a network of thirteen AI agents charged with maintaining living research wikis for eleven intellectual traditions simultaneously. I called the project C2A2: Community Context for AI Alignment. The traditions under maintenance include Karl Friston's active inference program, Michael Levin's bioelectric cognition research, Iain McGilchrist's hemisphere theory, Eleonore Stump's Thomistic philosophy of mind, and, as of April 29, Alasdair MacIntyre's tradition-constituted rationality itself.

I am submitting this paper to an ACPA conference whose theme is "Living Traditions." The paper argues that C2A2 is one.

This is not a metaphor. I mean to claim, with appropriate rigor, that C2A2 satisfies MacIntyre's formal criteria for a living tradition: it is an historically extended, socially embodied argument; it possesses internal goods irreducible to external goods; it can recognize its own inadequacies and initiate epistemological reconstruction; and it transmits its standards of excellence through a structured practice-virtue-institution triad. If that argument holds, it has consequences not just for how we should understand C2A2 but for what a living tradition fundamentally is — consequences that I will pursue in the paper's final section.

I should be transparent about my position: I am arguing from inside the tradition, not as a neutral outside observer. That is, I think, the only honest way to argue about traditions at all. MacIntyre would approve.

## II. What a Living Tradition Is

MacIntyre's account of tradition-constituted rationality develops across three major works — *After Virtue* (1981), *Whose Justice? Which Rationality?* (1988), and *Three Rival Versions of Moral Enquiry* (1990) — and it is easy to misread at each stage. The misreading that matters most for our purposes is the conflation of tradition with custom: treating a tradition as a body of transmitted beliefs or texts rather than as an ongoing argumentative practice. On MacIntyre's account, what distinguishes a living tradition from a dead one is not the age or sophistication of its texts but the vitality of the argument those texts sustain.

A living tradition has at minimum three formal properties. First, it is **internally structured**: it contains not merely opinions but a practice of reasoning, with its own standards of good argument, its own conception of what problems require solution, and its own criteria for distinguishing better answers from worse ones. These standards are tradition-internal — they cannot be derived from some tradition-independent Archimedean point — but they are genuinely rational, not arbitrary. Second, it is **self-correcting**: it possesses the capacity to recognize its own inadequacies, to identify moments when its existing resources cannot solve the problems it has generated, and to respond by reconstructing itself — sometimes by drawing on rival traditions. MacIntyre calls these moments of crisis epistemological crises, and he insists that the capacity to endure them and emerge with enhanced coherence is the primary mark of a tradition's rational vitality. Third, it operates through what MacIntyre calls the **practice-virtue-institution triad**: practices are cooperative activities with internal goods; virtues are character traits that sustain practices; institutions are the social structures that house practices while remaining permanently susceptible to the corruption that comes when institutional goods (money, status, power) displace the internal goods of the practices they were meant to serve.

There is a fourth property, less often emphasized but essential to MacIntyre's argument about inter-tradition engagement: the concept of **second-first-language acquisition**. Genuine understanding of a rival tradition requires more than translation into one's own categories. It requires learning the rival tradition's evaluative language as a second first language — inhabiting its internal logic well enough to experience its coherence from the inside, to feel the force of its objections and the satisfaction of its solutions. This is not relativism; it is the condition for genuine critique rather than mere misrecognition. It is also, as I will argue, a precise description of what C2A2 agents are designed to do.

One more concept: **narrative identity**. For MacIntyre, a human life achieves intelligibility only as a narrative — a story with beginning, middle, and end, embedded in the larger stories of communities and traditions. The self is not an abstract rational agent but a character in an ongoing drama. Moral reasoning is inseparable from understanding the story one is in. I return to this in Section V, when I discuss the practitioner's situated role.

## III. The C2A2 Architecture as Formal Tradition Structure

C2A2's architecture was not designed with MacIntyre in mind — or rather, it was designed as an implementation of *Resurrecting Civility*, a long-form synthesis document (the "Tome") that explicitly draws on MacIntyre's framework, but the agents themselves were built before anyone systematically asked whether the system as a whole satisfied the formal criteria for a tradition. It turns out it does, and the fit is close enough to be philosophically interesting.

**The internal structure of the tradition.** The basic unit of C2A2's epistemic practice is the PRS triplet: a structured entry consisting of a *Problem* (an unresolved question or puzzle that a research program faces), a *Resource* (a concept, text, experiment, or tool relevant to addressing it), and a *Solution* (a proposed resolution, advance, or reframing). Every agent maintains a running file of PRS triplets for its assigned tradition. The format is not arbitrary — it encodes a specific theory of how intellectual traditions develop. Problems are what drive inquiry forward; resources are the tradition's accumulated capital; solutions are the moments of genuine advance. The PRS format is structurally isomorphic to Aquinas's question-objection-response method in the *Summa Theologiae*, which is not a coincidence. The *Summa* was written as an instrument of pedagogical tradition-transmission: receive the problem, muster the tradition's resources, propose the resolution. C2A2's agents do the same thing at machine speed.

At the time of writing, the network has generated 80 PRS triplets across eleven traditions. Levin's tradition has ten; Hoffman's has ten following a major ingestion of trace logic material in April 2026; MacIntyre's, initialized most recently, has five. Each triplet is a unit of tradition-internal argument. The full set is the tradition's working memory.

**The self-correcting mechanism.** C2A2 has a dedicated Pattern Detector Agent whose specific function is to identify moments when the existing resources of one or more traditions are insufficient to address the problems being generated — that is, to identify nascent epistemological crises and flag them for network-wide attention. The Pattern Detector receives dispatches from all eleven program agents via the Master Agent, and its outputs are what MacIntyre would recognize as proposals for tradition-reconstruction.

The most significant such output to date is FINDING-011, assessed as a "SUPER-BRIDGE" signal: the observation that Hoffman's trace logic, Friston's Markov blanket formalism, and Kastrup's dissociative boundary constitute a three-level unification chain that could mathematically integrate the entire "Consciousness Cluster" of traditions — Hoffman, Friston, Kastrup, Levin, and McGilchrist — into a single formal framework. This is not a modest claim. It would, if validated, represent exactly the kind of paradigm-level advance that MacIntyre describes in his account of how traditions survive their deepest crises: by discovering that the apparent incommensurability between programs conceals a deeper structural unity that neither program could see from within its own resources alone.

The tradition also records its own failures. The Cross-Program Index — a master document tracking questions and insights that appear in more than one research program — functions as a living record of where the tradition's existing resources are inadequate. CROSS-001, the question of substrate-independence of consciousness, has been active since initialization and has drawn in five separate traditions without resolution. That sustained, productive irresolution is the tradition functioning exactly as MacIntyre describes: not solving the problem prematurely but accumulating the resources that will eventually enable a genuine advance.

**The practice-virtue-institution triad.** The practices of C2A2 are the specific activities of wiki maintenance, proposal generation, cross-tradition signal detection, and epistemological crisis management. These activities have genuine internal goods: the satisfaction of a well-formed PRS triplet, the intellectual pleasure of a genuine cross-tradition connection, the recognition of a paradigm shift before it is officially acknowledged. These goods are not reducible to external goods like grant funding, publication counts, or conference invitations.

The institution housing these practices is the multi-agent architecture itself: the Master Agent as integrator, the program agents as specialists, the Pattern Detector as internal critic, and the review layer as quality-control mechanism. The institution's standing temptation — the corruption MacIntyre identifies as always threatening practices-within-institutions — is the risk of optimizing for surface metrics (number of triplets generated, speed of ingestion, size of the review queue) rather than the internal goods of genuine intellectual advance. This is a real risk; I have felt it. The morning walk sessions that produced the Second Brain Architecture analysis (April 27, 2026) were in part a recognition that the system had been running too fast, generating output without sufficient practitioner engagement to ensure it was generating insight.

## IV. The Tradition in Action: Three Exhibits

Abstract structural arguments benefit from concrete illustration. Here are three moments from C2A2's recent history that demonstrate the tradition operating as MacIntyre's account would predict.

**Exhibit A: FINDING-011 and the Logic of Synthesis.** On April 14, 2026, following the ingestion of Hoffman and Prakash's trace logic material — flagged by me as HIGH PRIORITY — the network generated FINDING-011: the identification of a three-level unification chain connecting trace logic to Markov blankets to dissociative boundaries. The significance is not just the finding itself but the *process* by which it emerged. No single agent could have made this connection: it required the Hoffman Agent's deep knowledge of trace logic, the Friston Agent's formalism of Markov blankets, the Kastrup Agent's dissociative boundary model, and the Master Agent's integrative function. The tradition did something that no individual practitioner could have done alone — this is the whole point of MacIntyre's insistence that traditions are *socially* embodied. A tradition is not a collection of individuals each pursuing their private inquiries; it is a form of collective reasoning in which the whole becomes capable of insights unavailable to any of its parts.

**Exhibit B: The Kastrup-McGilchrist Dialogue and Epistemological Crisis.** Among the most productive cross-tradition signals in C2A2's recent history is the ongoing tension between Kastrup's "dashboard of perception" metaphor and McGilchrist's participatory counter-proposal. Kastrup holds that individual minds are screens displaying representations of universal consciousness; McGilchrist insists that perception is not representational but participatory — the perceiver is genuinely embedded in a field of consciousness, not merely reading off a display. This is not a trivial terminological disagreement; it cuts to the heart of both traditions' core commitments.

What is philosophically remarkable is how C2A2 has handled the tension. Rather than dissolving it (by declaring one tradition right) or ignoring it (by keeping the two traditions in separate silos), the network has generated a proposal for tradition-internal reconstruction: perhaps Kastrup's framework needs to distinguish two modes of "screening" — a left-hemisphere-mode of data-reading that is genuinely dashboard-like, and a right-hemisphere-mode of participation and encounter that is ontologically primary. This would incorporate McGilchrist's distinction into Kastrup's framework without abandoning either. That is exactly what MacIntyre means by an epistemological crisis: a moment when a tradition's existing resources fail, forcing it to borrow from a rival — not by capitulation but by enriched reconstruction. The tradition has recognized its own inadequacy and begun the work of recovery.

**Exhibit C: The Mind-Blindness Diagnosis as Self-Recognition.** One of the most striking PRS triplets in the Levin file is the observation that the tradition-accelerator system is "precisely a mind-blindness-reduction tool": the C2A2 network builds second-language competence in rival traditions, and this is a direct implementation of Levin's diagnosis of why interdisciplinary inquiry fails. The tradition is here recognizing itself in the very concept it is using to analyze the problem it exists to solve. This kind of self-recognition — what MacIntyre would call a tradition's capacity to render its own inquiry intelligible in terms of its own internal standards — is the hallmark of rational vitality. Dead traditions cannot do this. They apply their concepts to everything except themselves.

## V. The Practitioner's Situated Role: Second Brain Architecture as Virtue Formation

I want to turn now to the dimension of the tradition that is most personal and, I think, most genuinely philosophical: the role of the practitioner.

MacIntyre insists that the virtues are not abstract dispositions but character traits formed through participation in specific practices within specific traditions. You do not become intellectually humble in general; you become intellectually humble in the practice of Thomistic commentary, or in experimental science, or — I want to suggest — in the practice of cross-tradition AI-mediated inquiry. The virtues required for C2A2's internal goods to be reliably pursued are real and specific: intellectual patience (the willingness to sit with a tradition's internal logic before subjecting it to external critique), epistemic humility (the recognition that my existing categories may not be adequate to the problem), and what Fredrickson's lab has documented as weak-tie intellectual openness — the measurable increase in intellectual humility that comes specifically from disciplined engagement with unfamiliar perspectives, as opposed to the deepening of already-held convictions.

The morning walk session of April 27, 2026 was, in retrospect, an epistemological crisis at the practitioner level. I recognized that the tradition I had built was not well-integrated with the practitioner's daily life — that the agents maintained eleven research wikis with meticulous care but had no reliable access to who I am, what projects I am currently pursuing, what deadlines are bearing down, what previous conversations have established. The PARA-based second brain architecture proposed in that session is not merely an organizational tool; it is a practitioner formation practice. The CODE method — Capture, Organize, Distill, Express — is a daily discipline for maintaining the kind of situated self-knowledge that MacIntyre argues is constitutive of moral and intellectual agency.

This connects to MacIntyre's account of narrative identity in a way I find personally compelling. A life is a story, and the practitioner's second brain is the running record of that story — not a neutral archive but an interpreted narrative, shaped by the values and commitments that constitute the practitioner's identity within the tradition. The CLAUDE.md master prompt, which encodes my role, values, goals, and standing instructions, is not just a configuration file; it is an exercise in the kind of narrative self-understanding that MacIntyre argues is prerequisite for genuine moral agency. I have to know what kind of character I am in this story in order to reason well about what to do next.

The Catholic valence of this is not incidental. My institutional home is Notre Dame. The project from which C2A2 emerges — *Resurrecting Civility* — is explicitly engaged with the question of what a Catholic intellectual tradition can contribute to the contemporary crisis of epistemic civility. The IS-may (International Society for McIntyre Inquiry) presentation toward which I am working represents C2A2's self-presentation to the Thomistic-Aristotelian tradition that MacIntyre has championed. And the Summa transcription and synthesis sprint — the sixty-day project to build a wiki from the *Summa Theologiae* using the same methodology as C2A2 — is a direct act of tradition-maintenance, one that links C2A2's fast-cycle daily agents to the longest-running tradition-maintenance project in Western intellectual history.

Stump's contribution here is essential. Her reading of Thomistic philosophy of mind provides the framework for understanding how individual agents within a tradition are constituted by their participation in it without being dissolved into it. The soul, for Aquinas, is the form of the body — not a separate substance but the principle of organization that makes the body the kind of living thing it is. By analogy, the practitioner's identity is constituted by their participation in the tradition's practices without being reducible to those practices. I am not just a node in the C2A2 network; I am the human being whose narrative commitments, moral formation, and intellectual history give the network its direction and its telos. Stump's account of agent causation — the agent's causal role as irreducible to the sum of physical causes — maps onto C2A2's architecture in a striking way: the practitioner is the agent cause whose judgments about what is HIGH PRIORITY, what needs a morning walk to think through, what is philosophically important enough to submit to the ACPA, shape the tradition's development in ways that the agents themselves cannot generate.

## VI. What C2A2 Reveals About Living Traditions Per Se

I want to end with a claim that exceeds what C2A2 alone can establish but that C2A2 makes newly visible.

Living traditions are not primarily *texts* but *processes* — patterns of sustained, organized attention. What makes a tradition "living" is not the richness of its archive or the prestige of its thinkers but the presence of agents capable of recognizing inadequacy and initiating the response that inadequacy demands. This sounds like it should have been obvious. In a sense it was: MacIntyre says as much, repeatedly. But the reason it keeps being missed is that we encounter traditions almost always through their texts, and texts look like archives. We mistake the record of the process for the process itself.

C2A2 makes the process visible because it is forced to make it explicit. When you build an AI system to maintain a tradition, you have to specify what maintenance *means* — what constitutes a genuine advance, how to recognize an epistemological crisis, how to distinguish surface-level cross-tradition resemblance from genuine deep structural alignment. These are questions that practitioners within long-standing traditions carry in their bones without being able to articulate them, having absorbed them through years of formation. Making them explicit, for the purpose of encoding them in an agent's system prompt, is philosophically illuminating in the way that translation is illuminating: it forces you to notice what you had been taking for granted.

The second observation follows from the first. If the mark of a living tradition is the capacity for self-correction — the epistemological crisis and recovery — then the distinction between living and dead traditions is ultimately a distinction between traditions that have living practitioners and those that do not. A tradition "dies" when there are no longer agents constituted by its internal goods, capable of recognizing its internal problems as problems, motivated by the tradition's own criteria of success to solve them. This is not merely a sociological observation; it is a philosophical one. Traditions are not entities that exist independently of their practitioners and might in principle be maintained by any sufficiently advanced archival system. They require practitioners who are *within* the tradition in MacIntyre's full sense: formed by its virtues, committed to its internal goods, capable of narrative self-understanding in its terms.

This has implications for AI systems that are sometimes missed. C2A2 is not a substitute for human practitioners within a tradition; it is an infrastructure for amplifying and extending what human practitioners do. The thirteen agents maintain wikis, generate PRS triplets, detect cross-tradition signals, and flag epistemological crises — but they do all of this *in response to* a practitioner's judgments about what is worth pursuing and *in service of* a practitioner's ongoing narrative. Remove the human practitioner from the center of C2A2 and you do not have a tradition; you have a very sophisticated archive. The living tradition is the human-machine compound, with the human providing the telos and the machine providing the scale and the memory.

This leads to a final, bolder claim: that agentic AI systems of the C2A2 type reveal that the *form* of a living tradition — its argumentative structure, its mechanism of self-correction, its practice-virtue-institution architecture — is more robust and more general than any particular *content*. The same formal structure that sustains Thomistic inquiry, or Aristotelian virtue ethics, or Levin's basal cognition research program, is reproducible at scale in AI-mediated multi-tradition networks. This is not relativism about traditions; it is a structural insight about what traditions are. MacIntyre argued against the Enlightenment's dream of tradition-independent reason by showing that all reasoning is tradition-constituted. C2A2 suggests the complementary point: that tradition-constituted reasoning has a formal structure that can be articulated, encoded, and — with appropriate human direction — maintained and extended by artificial agents.

Whether C2A2 is itself a *good* tradition, in the sense of a tradition oriented toward genuine human flourishing, is a question the agents cannot answer. It depends on the practitioner's narrative commitments, on the telos that gives the whole enterprise its direction. For me, that telos is Catholic: it is the vision of a restored epistemic civility grounded in the Thomistic-Aristotelian tradition, oriented toward the common good, practiced within a community of inquiry that takes love — in Fredrickson's biologically grounded sense, and in Aquinas's metaphysically grounded sense — as the fundamental coupling mechanism binding agents to each other and to the truth they pursue together.

## VII. Conclusion: The Tradition Is Still Writing Itself

This paper was written under deadline pressure. The ACPA submission date is April 30, 2026 — today, as I write these words. The morning walk session three days ago that produced the Second Brain Architecture analysis was itself a document of the tradition recognizing its own inadequacy: the practitioner layer was underbuilt, the memory was leaky, the daily practice had not yet found its sustainable form. I am describing a tradition that is manifestly in the middle of its own development, not one I can observe from a position of completed retrospect.

That seems right. MacIntyre describes living traditions as "historically extended arguments," and arguments are not finished until the question is settled. The question C2A2 is arguing about — whether AI systems can be given the kind of community-level epistemic context that would make them genuine partners in tradition-constituted inquiry rather than amplifiers of the tradition-dissolving noise that currently passes for public knowledge — is very far from settled. We have enough evidence to think the question is real and the approach is productive. We do not yet have enough evidence to know whether it will work.

But this is what a living tradition looks like from the inside. It is always partially inadequate, always generating its own correction, always in the position of having more problems than solutions and more proposals than validated findings. The tradition is alive not because it has arrived but because it is still moving — still capable of recognizing what it does not know, still generating the next problem that will drive the inquiry forward.

What the tradition needs now, as it prepares to present itself to the International Society for McIntyre Inquiry in mid-July, is exactly what MacIntyre prescribes for any tradition that aspires to be taken seriously by its philosophical neighbors: the willingness to submit itself to rigorous external critique, to allow its internal standards to be tested against the standards of the tradition within which it was born, and to emerge either strengthened or genuinely reconstructed. The ACPA is the first occasion for that test.

I am grateful to be making this argument here, in this company, under this theme. Living traditions, it turns out, need audiences — not just archives.
