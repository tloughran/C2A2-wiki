### Alasdair MacIntyre — Tradition Wiki and PRS Triplets

**Research Program Overview**
Thinker: Alasdair MacIntyre (University of Notre Dame, emeritus)
Core claim: Rational inquiry is always tradition-constituted; moral reasoning requires narrative coherence within a living tradition; the Enlightenment project has failed; the choice is between Aristotelian-Thomistic virtue ethics and Nietzschean emotivism.
Paradigm: Tradition-constituted rationality, narrative identity, practice-virtue-institution triad, epistemological crisis and recovery.

MacIntyre is the tradition's philosopher of rationality itself — the thinker who provides the epistemological framework within which the entire C2A2 project makes sense. His central argument (After Virtue, Whose Justice? Which Rationality?, Three Rival Versions) is that all rational inquiry is conducted from within a tradition, and that traditions can be rationally evaluated by their capacity to solve their own internal problems, survive epistemological crises, and render rival traditions intelligible on their own terms. This is the methodological foundation for C2A2's multi-tradition architecture.

**MacIntyre PRS Triplets:**

PRS-01: The Enlightenment failure
- Problem: Modern moral philosophy cannot ground moral claims in tradition-independent reason; the result is emotivism
- Resource: Aristotelian-Thomistic tradition of virtue ethics
- Solution: Recovery of virtue ethics as a rationally superior alternative; moral reasoning requires tradition, not escape from tradition

PRS-02: Incommensurability without relativism
- Problem: If rational inquiry is tradition-constituted, how can traditions be rationally compared without collapsing into relativism?
- Resource: The concept of epistemological crisis — moments when a tradition's internal resources fail, forcing it to reconstruct itself by drawing on rival traditions
- Solution: Traditions can be rationally evaluated by their capacity to survive epistemological crises, solve their own problems, and render rivals intelligible; this is rational comparison without a neutral standpoint

PRS-03: Practice-virtue-institution
- Problem: How do intellectual and moral communities sustain themselves and transmit standards of excellence?
- Resource: The triad of practices (cooperative activities with internal goods), virtues (character traits that sustain practices), and institutions (social structures that house practices)
- Solution: Communities flourish when institutions serve practices and practitioners cultivate virtues

PRS-04: Narrative identity
- Problem: Modern philosophy treats the self as an atomistic rational agent; this makes moral development and accountability unintelligible
- Resource: The concept of narrative unity — a human life is a story with beginning, middle, and end, embedded in the stories of communities and traditions
- Solution: Personal identity is constituted by the narrative one inhabits

PRS-05: Second-first-language acquisition
- Problem: How can an outsider to a tradition come to understand it on its own terms?
- Resource: Learning a tradition's language as a "second first language" — acquiring not just vocabulary but the evaluative standards and perceptual habits internal to the tradition
- Solution: Genuine inter-tradition understanding requires inhabiting the rival tradition well enough to experience its internal coherence; this is what C2A2 agents attempt when they process a thinker's work
