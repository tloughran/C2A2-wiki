# Day 76 — Timeless Delight

*Aquinas, Summa Theologiae, Prima Secundae, Q.30 (concupiscence/desire) and Q.31 (delight considered in itself).*

[00:00] Hello, my name is Austin Habash, founder of Think Catholic, and you're listening to the Summa Podcast where we study St. Thomas Aquinas's *Summa Theologiae* in a way simple and insightful for anyone to understand. The *Summa in a Year* podcast is brought to you by Think Catholic; taking two questions of the *Summa* a day, we'll seek to summarize St. Thomas's responses, discovering the brilliance of Aquinas and his Catholic faith. This is Day 76 — covering questions 30 and 31 of the Prima Secundae, that is, of concupiscence and of delight considered in itself.

[00:54] **Article 1: Whether concupiscence is in the sensitive appetite only.** *Concupiscentia* is the Latin root for this English word and is more or less synonymous with desire — sensual desire, a passion within the concupiscible passions as opposed to the irascible passions. Aquinas writes: properly speaking desire may be not only in the lower but also in the higher appetite, for it does not imply fellowship and craving as concupiscence does, but simply movement towards the thing desired. So if what we mean by desire is simply movement towards the object of one's love, it can be found in both the sensual and intellectual appetite; but if we take concupiscence as a craving for some pleasant thing specifically pleasant to the senses or to the body, then it is properly a sensitive passion.

[02:18] **Article 2: Whether concupiscence is a specific passion.** A good question, because powers are distinguished by their objects, and the pizza on the table is one object but can be the object of love, desire, and joy. The passions differ because, although the external object is the same, there is according to Aquinas a diversity in its active power — the passion in us differs according as the good is really present or absent. Aquinas writes: the object of sensible pleasure causes love inasmuch as it attunes and conforms the appetite itself; it causes concupiscence inasmuch as when absent it draws the faculty to itself; and it causes pleasure inasmuch as when present it makes the faculty find rest in itself. Accordingly concupiscence is a passion differing in species or kind from both love and pleasure.

[03:53] **Article 3: Whether some concupiscences are natural and some are not natural.** Natural concupiscences are those which coincide with our animal nature — desire for food and drink, animal self-preservation, propagation. Unnatural concupiscences are those desires not directed simply to those corresponding goods of our animal nature, but rather are perceived goods via reason which because of our reason are then apprehended by the senses under the aspect of good — for example, looking at money and desiring it, or looking at trophies and desiring them. The feeling of desire still comes from those objects, but as stemming from a more intellectual understanding. Aquinas writes: man has not only universal reason pertaining to the intellectual faculty but also particular reason pertaining to the sensitive faculty… so that even rational concupiscence may pertain to the sensitive appetite. This *particular reason* is what Aquinas calls in other places the *cogitative sense* — one of man's internal senses.

[05:52] **Article 4: Whether concupiscence is infinite.** Concupiscence considered as a sensitive passion cannot have an actually endless object, since its object following upon sensual apprehension must be a particular sensitive thing. But it can be infinite in succession (desiring one object then another endlessly) or quantitatively (desiring money or pleasure without end). Aquinas explains: the reason is possessed of infinite power in a certain sense, insofar as it can consider a thing infinitely, as appears in the addition of numbers. The universal which reason apprehends is infinite in a sense, inasmuch as it contains potentially an infinite number of singulars. On the other hand, every object of concupiscence is taken as something finite — either because it is finite in reality as being actually desired, or because it is finite as apprehended, for it cannot be apprehended as infinite.

[07:36] **Question 31: Of delight considered in itself.** **Article 1: Whether delight is a passion.** What we mean by delight is essentially the same as joy. Delight *is* a passion because it arises from sensual apprehension along with the rest of the passions, and is caused by the presence of some becoming, suitable good — think of seeing a beautiful sunset, hearing a beautiful song, taking a bite out of a slice of pizza. We delight in the presence of the perceived good.

[08:21] **Article 2: Whether delight is in time.** A curious question. To be in time means to be subject to change — time is the measure of change. God cannot change, which is why we say he is not in time. We are in time since we change; our passions arise and pass. But once delight has begun, since it is a *resting* in the good, insofar as it denotes rest or non-change it can be said to be outside of time. Aquinas: delight of itself is not in time, for it regards good already gained, which is as it were the term or end of the movement; but if this good gained be subject to change, the delight therein will be in time accidentally; whereas if it be the good gained altogether unchangeable, the delight therein will not be in time either by reason of itself or accidentally. **This is the heart of today's title — Timeless Delight: when the good gained is altogether unchangeable, the delight resulting is unchangeable, considered outside of time.**

[09:33] **Article 3: Whether delight differs from joy.** Aquinas: we do not speak of joy in irrational animals, whereas we do speak of delight in them. We reserve the word *joy* for the specifically spiritual goods we happen to be resting in, while *delight* is a broader notion which can apply to both intellectual and sensual rest. In summary: we do not speak of joy except when delight follows reason; and so we do not ascribe joy to irrational animals but only delight.

[10:15] **Article 4: Whether delight is in the intellectual appetite.** Yes — delight can apply to both the sensual and intellectual appetite; in both cases it means a kind of resting in the apprehended good. There is this difference, however: delight of the sensitive appetite is accompanied by a bodily transmutation or change, whereas delight of the intellectual appetite is nothing more than the mere movement of the will. Hence Augustine says that desire and joy are nothing else but a volition of consent to the things we wish. Delight is an act of willing in the intellectual appetite and an act of the passions in the sensitive appetite.

[11:15] **Article 5: Whether bodily and sensible pleasures are greater than spiritual and intellectual pleasures.** What is required for pleasure: (1) the object or perceived good, (2) the subject or power the good is united to, (3) the union itself. In each arena, the intellectual plane is greater than the sensitive — abstractly considered, intellectual pleasures should be greater. A man loves the good of honor (a spiritual thing) more than bodily pleasure: think of those who've given up their lives rather than partake in dishonor. The intellectual power penetrates to the essence of things known in an instant, where sensual apprehension can only apprehend sensitive parts of a thing, gradually and partially. The union with the known good is simpler and more intimate than union with food or song or drink, which must happen sequentially.

[12:43] **But to us broken, fallen human beings**: because sensual goods are readily apparent to us, come with a change in the body, and are often sought to remedy bodily griefs or pains, sensual goods can seem greater. Aquinas: the reason why more seek bodily pleasures is because sensible goods are known better and more generally; and again because men need pleasures as remedies for the many kinds of sorrow and sadness; and since the majority cannot attain spiritual pleasures which are proper to the virtuous, hence they turn aside to seek those of the body.

[13:32] **Article 6: Whether the pleasures of touch are greater than the pleasures afforded by the other senses.** Everything gives pleasure according as it is loved. Our love for our senses is due to the sensual knowledge they give us and their overall usefulness. Touch (and to a lesser degree sight, as the handmaid of the mind) is most naturally loved because of how evidently damaging it is to those who cannot sense heat, cold, puncturing, or bruising of the body — "It is evident that in everything that which is natural is most powerful, and it is to these pleasures of touch that the natural concupiscences such as those of food, sexual union, and the like are ordained. If however we consider the pleasures of sight inasmuch as sight is the handmaid of the mind, then the pleasures of sight are greater, forasmuch as intellectual pleasures are greater than sensible."

[14:51] **Article 7: Whether any pleasure is not natural.** Pleasures natural to man are those owed to the kind of thing he is — eating food, sleep, contemplating truth, doing virtuous acts. But there are unnatural pleasures which can be considered *connatural* (Aquinas's word here) — they appear suitable to us based on things like physical illness, illness of the soul (vice), or evil custom. Aquinas: it happens that something which is not natural to man either in regard to reason or in regard to the preservation of the body becomes connatural to this individual man on account of there being some corruption of nature in him.

[15:49] **Article 8: Whether one pleasure can be contrary to another.** Yes — insofar as pleasure follows upon resting in the perceived good, and there are certain goods that are opposed to resting in other ones at the same time, like a stone cannot be resting on the table and on the floor at the same time. If I'm taking pleasure in eating a slice of pizza, I cannot at the same time be taking pleasure in eating a donut. Aquinas: things of the same genus or category that impede one another are contraries, as the philosopher states; but some pleasures impede one another, therefore some pleasures are contrary to one another.

[16:36] That closes today's episode. We have just finished covering questions 30 and 31 of the Prima Secundae — of concupiscence and of delight considered in itself.

## Notes

- **The pivotal claim for the synthesis**: Q.31 a.2 — *delight in an altogether unchangeable good is outside of time*. This is Aquinas's bridge from finite, time-bound pleasure to eternal beatitude. The contemporary frame must say something about what "unchangeable good" and "outside of time" mean under conscious-realist monism.
- Q.30 a.4: concupiscence's object must be finite (because sensitively apprehended), but reason apprehends the universal/infinite. The intellectual–sensitive boundary maps onto the interface/dissociation distinction in Hoffman/Kastrup.
- Q.31 a.3: joy follows reason; delight does not. The reason/sensation split is doing heavy lifting; under predictive-processing accounts (Friston), affective valence is *prediction-error gain* — there isn't a clean dual.
- Q.31 a.5: spiritual pleasures are abstractly greater but sensual ones seem greater because they remedy sorrow and are immediately apparent. This is the Fredrickson terrain — what makes positivity resonance available, and the *inflammation gating* finding (PRS-11): population-level chronic distress narrows the radius of available higher-order pleasures.
- Q.31 a.6: touch/sight as the natural baseline for pleasure-as-utility, with sight elevated when treated as "handmaid of the mind." This pre-figures Hoffman's interface theory — sense modalities as fitness-tuned channels, not realism windows.
