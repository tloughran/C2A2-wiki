# Day 97 — Grave Sin

Day 97 covers I-II Q.72 (*the distinction of sins*) and Q.73 (*the comparison of one sin with another*) — 19 articles together. Q.72 asks how sins are specifically differentiated: by their *objects* (a.1), spiritual vs. carnal (a.2), by their causes (a.3, no), by their target — God / self / neighbor (a.4), by punishment (a.5, no), by commission vs. omission (a.6, no), by thought / word / deed (a.7), by excess vs. deficiency (a.8), by circumstances (a.9). Q.73 then asks about relative *gravity*: whether all sins are connected (a.1, no), whether all sins are equal (a.2, no), and what makes one sin graver than another (a.3-10) — variation by object, by opposed virtue, by carnal vs. spiritual, by cause, by aggravating circumstance, by harm, by victim's condition, by sinner's condition.

## Transcript

### Q.72 — Distinction of sins

[00:32] Day 97. Today's questions Q.72 and Q.73 of the Prima Secundae — the distinction of sins and the comparison of one sin with another. About 19 articles to cover, so we'll be brief.

[01:05] **Article 1 — whether sins are distinguished specifically by their objects.** Yes. As the proper object of sight is color, so words, deeds, and desires are directed toward various objects. Lust as a disordered desire has a definite object; so does blasphemy. Sins are distinguished by what they are *directed toward* — by content.

[01:43] **Article 2 — whether spiritual sins are fittingly distinguished from carnal sins.** Yes, by their objects. Sins consisting in spiritual pleasure (vain praise) are spiritual; those consisting in carnal pleasure (gluttony at the table, lust in sexual pleasure) are carnal. 2 Corinthians 7:1: "let us cleanse ourselves from all defilement of the flesh and of the spirit."

[02:19] **Article 3 — whether sins differ specifically in reference to their causes.** No. Sirach 10:15: "pride is the beginning of all sin." 1 Timothy 6:10: "the desire of money is the root of all evils." Since the beginning and root are common, sins cannot be distinguished by *these* causes. The deeper principle: "objects in relation to external acts have the character of matter about which, but in relation to the interior act of the will they have the character of end, and it is owing to this that they give the act its species." Example: a man kills another man — that is the *material* about which. Whether it is a sin, and what *kind*, depends on the intended end (defense of family, defense of country, bank robbery, car theft).

[03:53] **Article 4 — whether sin is fittingly divided into sin against God, against oneself, and against one's neighbor.** Yes. Three orders to which our acts can be inordinate: the order within ourselves (passions/actions subordinate to reason), the order to neighbor (justice, rendering due), the order to God (belief, hope, love).

[04:36] **Article 5 — whether the division of sins according to their debt of punishment diversifies their species.** No — the relation runs the other way. "Punishment follows sin as an effect thereof; therefore sins do not differ specifically according to the debt of punishment."

[05:16] **Article 6 — whether sins of commission and omission differ specifically.** No. Like fire that simultaneously heats and drives out cold, every act of commission carries a correlated omission. The greedy man hoards money *and* fails to give alms. The glutton eats too much *and* fasts too little. Commission and omission inhabit the same species of sin.

[06:25] **Article 7 — whether sins are fittingly divided into sins of thought, word, and deed.** Yes — and we know it from experience. "The angry man through desire of vengeance is at first disturbed in thought, then he breaks out into words of abuse, and lastly he goes on to wrongful deeds."

[06:53] **Article 8 — whether excess and deficiency diversify the species of sins.** Yes — by entailing different intended ends. The hoarder and the spendthrift aim at different things. The motive in intemperance is love for bodily pleasures; the motive in insensibility is *hatred* of the same. These sins not only differ specifically; they are *contrary*.

[08:04] **Article 9 — whether sins differ specifically in respect of different circumstances.** Only when the circumstance enters into the intended end. Eating hastily because one is starving vs. eating hastily out of gluttony — same outward circumstance, different ends, different sins.

### Q.73 — Comparison of sins

[08:41] **Article 1 — whether all sins are connected with one another.** No. Virtue *connects* a person by aligning all passions and operations with right reason; vices *fragment* a person into multiple appetites that need not align with each other or with reason. This is why the demoniac in the Gospel is called Legion: sin makes us *many*, the virtuous man is *one* under reason.

[09:32] **Article 2 — whether all sins are equal.** No. Our Lord said to Pilate: "He that hath delivered me to thee hath the greater sin." Sins differ by how much they depart from the rectitude of reason.

[10:00] **Article 3 — whether the gravity of sin varies according to their objects.** Yes. As a disease of heart or brain is graver than a rash on the arm, the more *ultimate* the mark missed, the graver the sin. Sins against God are most grave; then those bearing on human nature itself (murder); then those bearing on external goods (theft).

[11:03] **Article 4 — whether the gravity of sins depends on the excellence of the virtues to which they are opposed.** Yes. "Charity is not any kind of love but the love of God; hence not any kind of hatred is opposed to it directly but the hatred of God, which is the most grievous of all sins."

[11:26] **Article 5 — whether carnal sins are of less guilt than spiritual sins.** Yes. Spiritual sins (blasphemy) are graver: the dignity of their subject (intellect, which alone knows God) is higher; they entail turning *away* from the spiritual good, where carnal sins turn *toward* a sensible good; and they are colder — more diabolical — less swayed by bodily passion.

[12:09] **Article 6 — whether the gravity of a sin depends on its cause.** Yes. The more *willful*, the graver. "Causes which weaken the judgment of reason (ignorance) or the free movement of the will (weakness, violence, fear) diminish the gravity of sin even as they diminish its voluntariness; so much so that if the act be altogether involuntary, it is no longer sinful."

[12:51] **Article 7 — whether a circumstance aggravates a sin.** Yes. A circumstance can change the *kind* of sin (fornication → adultery via the spouse-circumstance), or aggravate within a kind (murder of one's parents within murder), or aggravate by quantity (stealing $100 vs. $1).

[13:38] **Article 8 — whether a sin is aggravated by reason of its causing more harm.** Yes, when the consequences were *foreseen and intended*. Throwing away medicine intending the patient's death is graver than throwing it away without that intention.

[14:31] **Article 9 — whether a sin is aggravated by reason of the condition of the person against whom it is committed.** Yes — proportional to how principal an end the victim is. Order: God; man's own soul; neighbor's physical wellbeing; one's own physical wellbeing. (We are to be willing to lay down life for neighbor.) "It is more grievous for a man to kill himself than another," because self-harm to non-volitional goods (life, spiritual welfare) exceeds harm to another.

[16:11] **Article 10 — whether the excellence of the person sinning aggravates the sin.** Yes — because (a) the better person should have known better, (b) greater scandal, (c) greater ingratitude to God who especially loved them, (d) greater incongruity (a religious in a vow of continence sinning by fornication).

[17:07] That's Q.72 and Q.73 — the distinction of sins and the comparison of one sin with another. Tomorrow continues.

## Notes

- **Sin as species-by-end, not species-by-matter** (Q.72 a.3) is the deepest analytic move in the question — and it lands hard in the contemporary frame: action is individuated by *what it is for*, not by surface motion. Active-inference-native: an act is the policy that drives it, and the policy is fixed by its preferred outcomes.
- **"Vices fragment a man into Legion; virtues unify him under reason"** (Q.73 a.1) is the perspective-limitation theme stated in 13th-century Latin. Multiple competing appetites = multiple perspective-bound goal-states without coordinator; the unified rational order under loving unity = the coordinated agent. This is one of the densest hits to the central theme so far.
- The order God / self / neighbor in Q.72 a.4 — refined in Q.73 a.9 to God / own soul / neighbor's physical wellbeing / own physical wellbeing — encodes a *priority queue* over moral targets. Worth surfacing the cross-program-index entry on "agency at multiple scales" — Levin/Friston/Stump.
- Q.73 a.6 — voluntariness as the *gravity-scaling* parameter — fits cleanly with free-energy / precision-weighting accounts: an action is "more willed" precisely when the higher-level model retains high precision over the lower-level priors that produced it. Ignorance, fear, violence reduce that precision; involuntariness drops it to zero.
- The carnal-vs-spiritual asymmetry (Q.73 a.5) — *spiritual sins are graver because colder and more diabolical* — is the inverse of the usual modern intuition. Worth pausing on: Aquinas thinks impassioned sin is closer to animal-error (reasoning hijacked by appetite), unimpassioned sin is closer to demonic-error (reasoning hijacking itself). McGilchrist's pathology-of-left-hemisphere argument is the natural bridge.
