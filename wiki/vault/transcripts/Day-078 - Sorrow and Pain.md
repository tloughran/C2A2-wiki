# Day 78 — Sorrow and Pain

*Aquinas, Summa Theologiae, Prima Secundae, Q.34 (goodness and malice of pleasures) and Q.35 (pain or sorrow in itself).*

## Transcript

[00:00] Day 78 — questions 34 and 35 of the Prima Secundae, of the goodness and malice of pleasures and of pain or sorrow in itself.

[00:54] **Article 1 (Q.34): Whether every pleasure is evil.** Good and evil in the moral order depend on agreement or disagreement with reason. A pleasure elicited by an object, circumstance, or intention in accord with right reason is morally good (e.g., taking pleasure in healthy food at the right time, in the right amount, for the right reasons). An evil pleasure is one taken in an evil act or in some particular good in an evil way. Aquinas: "The temperate man does not shun all pleasures, but those that are immoderate and contrary to reason."

[01:52] **Article 2: Whether every pleasure is good.** Q.34 a.1 was the Stoic position; a.2 is the Epicurean. Aquinas writes: "While some of the Stoics maintained that all pleasures are evil, the Epicureans held that pleasure is good in itself and that consequently all pleasures are good." His correction: "Pleasant depends on agreement with the appetite, which tends sometimes to that which is discordant from reason; consequently not every object of pleasure is good in the moral order, which depends on the order of reason." Pleasures follow upon the attainment of some perceived good; but being perceived as good doesn't make a thing actually good for oneself or in conformity with right reason.

[02:57] **Article 3: Whether any pleasure is the greatest good.** Aquinas: "The greatest good of everything is its last end; and the end is twofold, namely the thing itself and the use of that thing. Accordingly, Man's last end may be said to be either God, who is the Supreme good simply, or the enjoyment of God, which implies a certain pleasure in the last end; and in this sense a certain pleasure of man may be said to be the greatest among human goods." Pleasure is the greatest good "due not to the mere fact that it is pleasure but due to the fact that it is a perfect repose in the perfect good."

[04:01] **Article 4: Whether pleasure is the measure or rule by which to judge moral good or evil.** Pleasure can reveal a good or evil will, because the will rests in (takes pleasure in) the attained good. If a man's will takes pleasure in evil things, the pleasure indicates a disordered will. Aquinas: "All pleasures are uniform in the point of their being repose of the appetite in something good; and in this respect pleasure can be a ruler or measure, because that man is good whose will rests in the true good, and that man evil whose will rests in evil."

[04:48] **Question 35 — Of pain or sorrow in itself.** **Article 1: Whether pain is a passion of the soul.** Two things are required for pain: conjunction with some evil and *perception* of this conjunction. Since the good is the proper object of the appetite (we love and will goods), so too on the radar is the lack or missingness found in goods, which we call evils. Since pain is something we endure and perceive as suffering, it is a passion of the soul, not merely an undergoing of the body.

[05:35] **Article 2: Whether sorrow is the same as pain.** Aquinas: "Just as that pleasure which is caused by an exterior apprehension is called pleasure but not joy, so too that pain which is caused by an exterior apprehension is called pain indeed but not sorrow. Accordingly, sorrow is a species or kind of pain, as joy is a species of pleasure." Within pain we find specifically interior pain — sorrow. Aquinas: "External sense perceives only what is present, but the interior cognitive power can perceive the present, past, and future; consequently sorrow can regard present, past, and future, whereas bodily pain, which follows apprehension of the external sense, can only regard something present."

[06:45] **Article 3: Whether sorrow or pain is contrary to pleasure.** Aquinas: "Since the objects of pleasure and sorrow or pain are the present good and the present evil, are contrary to one another, it follows that pain and pleasure are contrary to one another."

[07:11] **Article 4: Whether all sorrow is contrary to all pleasure.** Only if the objects are contrary (losing vs. winning the same soccer game). If the objects are only *disparate* (Aquinas's word), "sorrow and pleasure in respect of objects that are not contrary but disparate are not specifically contrary to one another but are also disparate; for instance, sorrow at the death of a friend, and then pleasure in contemplation."

[08:02] **Article 5: Whether there is any sorrow contrary to the pleasure of contemplation.** No: "no sorrow is contrary to pleasure taken in the very act of contemplation, nor is any sorrow connected with it save accidentally" — contemplating something sorrowful like the Passion of Christ can be a cause of sorrow, but the *act* of contemplation has no sorrowful counterpart.

[08:38] **Article 6: Whether sorrow is to be shunned more than pleasure is to be sought.** Aquinas: "Good is stronger than evil, but pleasure is desirable for the sake of the good which is its object, whereas the shunning of sorrow is on account of evil; therefore the desire for pleasure is more eager than the shunning of sorrow."

[08:59] **Article 7: Whether outward pain is greater than interior sorrow.** Aquinas: "The cause of outward pain is a conjoined evil repugnant to the body, while the cause of inward pain is a conjoined evil repugnant to the appetite, while inward pain arises from an interior apprehension of the imagination or the reason." So inward pain is greater than physical pain because it affects higher faculties more directly. "A sign of this is that one is more willing to undergo external pain than internal pain" — many would give their lives rather than yield to dishonor.

[10:30] **Article 8: Whether there are only four species of sorrow.** Yes — (1) **pity**: sorrow at another's evil taken as relating to oneself; (2) **envy**: sorrow at another's good taken as an evil for us — "keeping up with the Joneses"; (3) **anxiety**: a resting in some perceived evil that weighs on the mind, making escape seem impossible; (4) **torpor**: sorrow so debilitating one cannot move or speak under its weight. The sorrow of repentance, whose proper object is one's own evil, does not get its own species because its object is the same as sorrow-in-general.

[11:36] That closes Day 78 — Q.34 and Q.35.

## Notes

- **Pivotal claim for synthesis**: Q.35 a.7 — inward pain is greater than outward; the *appetite* is more directly engaged by interior apprehension. Under Friston this is mathematically natural: prediction-error at higher levels of the generative hierarchy has larger downstream consequences than peripheral error.
- Q.35 a.2: sorrow ranges over past, present, and future; bodily pain only the present. Active inference (Friston) generalizes appetite to span time via the generative model.
- Q.35 a.8: four species — pity, envy, anxiety, torpor. Maps with revealing accuracy onto contemporary affective taxonomies (compassion, envy/social comparison distress, anxiety disorders, depressive/anhedonic withdrawal).
- Q.34 a.3: greatest good = repose in the perfect good; not pleasure as such. Under conscious-realist monism (Kastrup/Hoffman), "repose in the perfect" reads as alter/interface returning to fundamental coherence with mind-at-large.
- Q.34 a.4: pleasure as a *diagnostic* of will-state. Anticipates Fredrickson's vagal tone as a biomarker — bodily affect reveals where attention is *actually* resting (PRS-04, PRS-11).
- Q.35 a.1: pain requires both conjunction with evil and *perception* of the conjunction. Aligns with global-workspace / higher-order theories of consciousness — nociception is not yet pain until centrally apprehended.
- Q.35 a.5: contemplation has no proper sorrowful counterpart. Reads forward to Rohr/contemplative tradition: non-dual contemplation as the practice that breaks the pleasure/pain binary.
