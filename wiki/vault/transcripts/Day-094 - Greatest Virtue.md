# Day 94 — Greatest Virtue

*Summa Theologiae*, Prima Secundae, Q.66 (Of the equality among the virtues) and Q.67 (Of the duration of virtues after this life).

## Transcript

[00:05] Day 94. Today we cover Q.66 and Q.67 of the Prima Secundae: *of the equality among the virtues* and *of the duration of virtues after this life*.

[00:54] **Article 1: Whether one virtue can be greater or less than another.** Some virtues are greater in themselves than others — charity, the supernatural habitual love of God, is of a superior order than the natural virtue of temperance. Also, an individual virtue can be greater or less in one person than another, "because one man is better disposed than another to attain to the mean of virtue on account of either greater habituation, or a better natural disposition, or a more discerning judgment of reason, or again a greater gift of grace." And when it comes to the intellectual virtues, such as one's habitual knowledge of science, that habit can be greater in one than another in its extent to more content within a particular branch of knowledge. The key takeaway: we call someone virtuous as they approach the mean established by right reason, and since there can be more-or-less in that proximity, we can call one more virtuous than another. Aquinas: "Here the Stoics erred, for they held that no man should be deemed virtuous unless he were in the highest degree disposed to virtue. The nature of virtue does not require that man should reach the mean of right reason as though it were an indivisible point, but it is enough that he should approach the mean. Moreover, one same indivisible mark is reached more nearly and more readily by one than another, as may be seen when several archers aim at a fixed target."

[02:42] **Article 2: Whether all the virtues that are together in one man are equal.** Some virtues are better in themselves, others in a given person, in so far as their effects are better or as they are more directly related to reason. The deeper insight is summarized by Augustine: "If you say these men are equal in fortitude, but that one is more prudent than another, it follows that the fortitude of the latter is less prudent. Consequently they are not really equal in fortitude, since the former's fortitude is more prudent. You will find that this applies to the other virtues if you run over them all in the same way." The virtues are connected to one another in such a way that they must increase or decrease together as a kind of cohesive whole. The only exception is if some other factor causes a particular readiness to a particular virtue — by nature, habit, or the grace of God.

[04:13] **Article 3: Whether the moral virtues are better than the intellectual virtues.** Aquinas: "Moral virtue is in that part of the soul which is rational by participation, while intellectual virtue is in the essentially rational part. Now rational by essence is more excellent than rational by participation. Therefore intellectual virtue is better than moral virtue."

[04:43] **Article 4: Whether justice is the chief of the moral virtues.** Yes — because we take something to be a moral virtue strictly as it modifies the will or flows from the will, and the one virtue among the four cardinal virtues which affects the will directly is justice. Prudence is the virtue of the mind, and temperance and fortitude are dispositions in the sensitive appetite (the passions). Justice has for its object rendering to one his or her due, which sets a man aright not only in regard to himself but also in regard to his neighbor.

[05:27] **Article 5: Whether wisdom is the greatest of the intellectual virtues.** "The greatness of a virtue as to its species or kind is taken from its object. Now the object of wisdom surpasses the object of all the intellectual virtues, because wisdom considers the supreme cause, which is God... Since it is by the cause that we judge an effect, and by the higher cause that we judge the lower effects, hence it is that wisdom exercises judgment over all the other intellectual virtues, directs them all, and is the architect of them all."

[06:15] **Article 6: Whether charity is the greatest of the theological virtues.** St. Paul: "Faith, hope, and charity abide these three — but the greatest of these is charity." Charity has for its object God himself (as do faith and hope), but also in this life it is greater to love God than to know him — on account of the limits of our knowledge. As St. Paul says: "For now we see in a mirror dimly, but then face to face." Right now the supernatural habitual love of God which is charity is allowing us to possess him as he is — because "the love of charity is of that which is already possessed, since the Beloved is in a manner in the lover, and again the lover is drawn by desire to union with the Beloved." 1 John 4:16: "He that abideth in charity abideth in God, and God in him." In regard to faith and hope, faith is of what is not seen and hope is of what is not possessed. Therefore St. Paul is correct in saying "the greatest of these is charity."

[07:36] **Question 67: Of the duration of virtues after this life. Article 1: Whether the moral virtues remain after this life.** A moral virtue consists of a material element and a formal element. The material element is a certain inclination of the appetitive faculty toward passion or operation; the formal element is that inclination's agreement with right reason. Temperance is a certain disposition of the appetitive faculty (specifically here the concupiscible passions) to be in accord with right reason. Will temperance remain after death? Not when one is in a disembodied state, unless we consider it rooted in the will — because the passions themselves entail the body, which we lose after death. However, virtues like justice, which dispose the will directly, remain both in the disembodied state and in the general resurrection. Aquinas of justice: "It is specifically said that it is perpetual and immortal, both by reason of its subject, since the will is incorruptible, and because its act will not change."

[09:13] **Article 2: Whether the intellectual virtues remain after this life.** Yes — based on scripture (the wealthy man of Luke 16 remembering after death things of his former life) and because the potential intellect, once it receives the knowledge of a thing, continues to hold it in memory even when one is not actually considering that thing. Since the intellect is an immaterial faculty, not essentially dependent on the body nor housed in a bodily organ, there is no reason to hold that habitual knowledge would not be retained after the body decays.

[10:07] **Article 3: Whether faith remains after this life.** Short answer: no. "Faith of its very nature contains an imperfection on the part of the subject — that the believer sees not what he believes — whereas bliss of its very nature implies perfection on the part of the subject — that the blessed see that which makes them happy. Hence it is manifest that faith and bliss are incompatible in one and the same subject."

[10:38] **Article 4: Whether hope remains after death in the state of glory.** No. Romans 8:24: "Who hopes for what he sees? But the blessed see that which is the object of hope, God. Therefore they do not hope."

[10:55] **Article 5: Whether anything of faith or hope remains in glory.** Just as when you take away the specific difference from a general category the species changes (take away "rational" from "animal" and you no longer have a human being), then since the knowledge of faith is an obscure knowledge of God and the knowledge from the beatific vision is a clear knowledge of God, in the beatific vision the knowledge received by faith will not remain specifically. "It is therefore evident that in heaven nothing remains of faith either identically or specifically the same, but only generically." And hope is not even within the same genus as heavenly bliss, because "it is compared to the enjoyment of bliss as movement is to rest in the term of movement."

[12:00] **Article 6: Whether charity remains after this life in glory.** Yes — it remains not only generically but specifically the same. "The object of charity is not knowledge itself — if it were, the charity of the wayfarer would not be the same as the charity of heaven. Its object is the thing known, which remains the same: God himself."

## Notes

- Q.66.1's archer metaphor is the doctrine's escape from Stoic rigorism: virtue is **graded proximity to the mean**, not an indivisible point. The contemporary continuous-trait model maps directly onto this.
- Q.66.2 (Augustine's chiasmus on equality within one person) is, again, a network claim: the strengths of the virtues are *coupled*. Decouplings are explainable but require a *specific cause* (nature, habit, grace).
- Q.66.3's ranking (intellectual > moral) is a controversial claim by Aquinas's own metric — it cuts against Q.66.4's elevation of justice as "chief moral virtue" if we ask which virtue is *operationally* most important in a community. Aquinas is doing pure essence-ranking here.
- Q.66.6 explicitly says: in this life, **love > knowledge** of God (the mirror-dimly argument). In Q.67, however, knowledge in glory exceeds the obscure knowledge of faith. So the love-vs-knowledge ranking inverts depending on the life-stage. Charity is the only continuous theological virtue across stages.
- Q.67 is an eschatological filter: the virtues partition by whether their formal object survives the transition. Charity alone survives identically. Faith and hope dissolve into their fulfillment. Moral virtues partition by whether they were rooted in the will (justice) or in the passions (temperance, fortitude).
- For contemporary parallel: this is doing the same conceptual work as asking which capacities of a current-life agent are *terminal* vs *instrumental* under a transformed phenomenology. Hoffman's interface argument and Kastrup's analytic-idealism both make analogous moves about which features are absolute vs interface-relative.
