# Day 99 — Lusts of the World

Day 99 covers I-II Q.76 (*the causes of sin in particular* — specifically ignorance) and Q.77 (*the cause of sin on the part of the sensitive appetite*). Q.76 walks ignorance carefully: ignorance *can* cause sin (a.1); ignorance is itself a sin when culpable (a.2 — vincible vs. invincible); ignorance does not always excuse from sin (a.3); ignorance diminishes sin to the extent it reduces voluntariness (a.4). Q.77 then asks how the passions cause sin: whether the will is moved by passion of the sensitive appetite (a.1, indirectly), whether reason can be overcome by passion against its knowledge (a.2, yes), whether such sin is a "sin of weakness" (a.3), whether self-love is the source of every sin (a.4, yes), whether the Johannine triad — concupiscence of the flesh / concupiscence of the eyes / pride of life — fittingly names the causes (a.5), whether passion alleviates a sin (a.6, can both lessen and aggravate), whether passion excuses altogether (a.7, only when it removes voluntariness entirely), whether passion-driven sin can still be mortal (a.8, yes).

## Q.76 — Causes of sin in particular (ignorance)

[01:00] **Article 1 — whether ignorance can be a cause of sin.** Yes. Augustine: "some sin through ignorance." The example: a man is restrained from parricide both by knowing that killing one's father is wrong (universal principle) and by knowing that *this* man is his father (particular circumstance). Ignorance of either could enable the act. Acts 17 — Paul on the Greeks: "the times of ignorance God overlooked but now he commands all men everywhere to repent."

[02:49] **Article 2 — whether ignorance is a sin.** When the ignorance is culpable (vincible) — concerning what one is bound and able to know — yes. Invincible ignorance is not a sin. The general rule: "all are bound in common to know the articles of faith and the universal principles of right, and each individual is bound to know matters regarding his duty or state." Even articles of faith — which come from hearing — are culpably-missing if one fails to *dispose* oneself for the message (Acts 10, Cornelius, the devout pagan given a vision and sent to Peter). "Privation of grace is not a sin in itself; yet by reason of negligence in preparing oneself for grace, it may have the character of sin, even as ignorance."

[04:51] **Article 3 — whether ignorance excuses from sin altogether.** Only when the ignorance is truly involuntary and concerns matter one is not bound to know. Voluntary ignorance — chosen "of set purpose to be ignorant of certain things that he may sin the more freely" or by negligence to acquire knowledge one is bound to acquire — does not excuse.

[06:06] **Article 4 — whether ignorance diminishes a sin.** Yes — to the extent it reduces voluntariness. Paul on his pre-conversion blasphemy: "I received mercy because I had acted ignorantly in unbelief" (1 Tim 1:13). The drunkard's later sins are *somewhat* diminished by his impaired reason, but not excused, because the drunkenness was voluntary in the first place.

## Q.77 — Cause of sin on the part of the sensitive appetite

[07:13] **Article 1 — whether the will is moved by a passion of the sensitive appetite.** Not directly — the will follows reason, not the passions. But passion can move the will *indirectly*: (a) by fixating attention on a particular object (the smell of food drawing thought to the nearby restaurant); (b) by distracting or diluting focus, degrading the quality of deliberation.

[08:21] **Article 2 — whether reason can be overcome by a passion against its knowledge.** Yes — by causing reason to *misapply* a universal it still knows abstractly to a specific case. The man who knows adultery is wrong in general but, under passion, judges *this* act of adultery to be not so bad. Three mechanisms: distraction, opposition, and bodily change (intense emotion blunting the use of reason).

[09:11] **Article 3 — whether a sin committed through passion should be called a sin of weakness.** Yes. "When the concupiscible or irascible power is affected by any passion contrary to the order of reason... it is said to be a sin of weakness."

[09:41] **Article 4 — whether self-love is the source of every sin.** Yes. "The proper and direct cause of sin is to be considered on the part of the adherence to a mutable good, in which respect every sinful act proceeds from inordinate desire for some temporal good. Now the fact that anyone desires a temporal good inordinately is due to the fact that he loves himself inordinately... therefore it is evident that inordinate love of self is the cause of every sin." Even when willing a disordered good *for* a friend — the friend as *alter ego* — the loop runs through inordinate self-love.

[10:44] **Article 5 — whether concupiscence of the flesh, concupiscence of the eyes, and pride of life are fittingly described as causes of sin.** 1 John 2:16. Pride defined here, worth memorizing: *the inordinate appetite for excellence*. Concupiscence of the flesh: inordinate appetite for easily-attained goods, especially those of our animal nature (food, drink, sleep, sex). Concupiscence of the eyes: inordinate appetite for goods spiritually delectable that go beyond animal lust (wealth, prestige). Pride of life: inordinate appetite for *arduous* spiritual goods — not called *concupiscence* because the irascible appetite is the proper home of arduous-good-desire. All sin reduces to one of these three, since all sin arises from disordered self-love.

[12:53] **Article 6 — whether sin is alleviated on account of a passion.** Both ways. Passion *before* the act (diluting focus, fixating attention) *diminishes* gravity by reducing deliberateness. Passion *following* the will — passion as expression of voluntary choice — *increases* gravity: "the greater the pleasure or the concupiscence with which anyone sins, the greater the sin."

[14:06] **Article 7 — whether passion excuses from sin altogether.** Only if it removes voluntariness entirely — and rarely. "Sometimes so strong as to take away the use of reason altogether, as in the case of those who are mad through love or anger." Even this only excuses if the agent did not voluntarily induce the state. Chesterton: "if one does the thing forbidden, one imperils all the things provided."

[15:00] **Article 8 — whether a sin committed through passion can be mortal.** Yes — when the passion does not fully remove the use of reason, the free will remains free to turn away from God. If reason is taken away altogether, the act is *neither* mortal nor venial. "Passion does not always hinder the act of reason altogether; consequently, the reason remains in possession of its free will so as to turn away from God or to turn to him."

[16:08] That's Q.76 and Q.77 — causes of sin in particular and the cause of sin on the part of the sensitive appetite.

## Notes

- **The Johannine triad** (1 John 2:16) folded into the cause-of-sin treatise is the deep architectural move of Q.77 a.5. It maps cleanly onto Levin/Friston/Hawkins-style three-level architectures: lower-level appetitive (flesh), mid-level reward-bearing-cognition (eyes), upper-level self-modeling-and-excellence (pride). The contemporary frame can re-derive the same Triad with different vocabulary: misallocation of precision at each of the three levels.
- **The "passion before / passion after" asymmetry** in Q.77 a.6 — passion before the act diminishes culpability, passion after expresses it — is one of the most underappreciated structural insights in this part of the *Summa*. It distinguishes *capture* (the agent's policy hijacked by precision-spike upstream) from *commitment* (the agent's policy *re-prioritizing* downstream). Same passion, different role in the causal chain, different moral weight.
- **Self-love as the universal cause** (Q.77 a.4) lands directly on the central theme: every limited perspective biases the agent's optimization toward its own apparent-good, and *that bias* — not malice as such — is what individuates failed loving-unity from accomplished loving-unity. Augustine's *amor sui* and the perspective-limitation account converge on this exact point. The synthesis-day for this article can name this convergence outright.
- **Ignorance-as-sin** (Q.76 a.2) — *culpable* failure to dispose oneself for knowledge one is bound to acquire — is the medieval precursor of "epistemic responsibility." Worth flagging the cross-program-index entry on Friston-style active-inference accounts of curiosity and ignorance-as-policy-failure.
- **The mad-through-love/anger case** in Q.77 a.7 — when reason is taken away altogether the act is *neither* mortal nor venial — is a careful limit. It refuses to let mental-illness-style loss of agency be moralized in either direction. Important guardrail for any contemporary synthesis on sin and mental illness.
