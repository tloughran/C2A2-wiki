# Day 80 — Sorrowful Remedies

*Aquinas, Summa Theologiae, Prima Secundae, Q.38 (remedies of sorrow/pain) and Q.39 (goodness and malice of sorrow/pain).*

## Transcript

[00:00] Day 80 — questions 38 and 39 of the Prima Secundae, the remedies of sorrow or pain, and the goodness and malice of sorrow or pain.

[00:56] **Article 1 (Q.38): Whether pain or sorrow is assuaged by every pleasure.** Aristotle: "Sorrow is driven forth by pleasure, both by a contrary pleasure, and by any other, provided it be intense." Sorrow and pleasure are contraries — like heat and cold: to drive cold from a room, add heat; to drive out sorrow, add pleasure. People say of one drowning her feelings in ice cream or pizza that she "went to eat her feelings" — pleasure, even passing and no nobler than food or drink, can take the edge off sorrow. **An aside: if we find ourselves given to pleasures of the flesh it may be because we're trying to medicate a sorrowful spiritual heart.**

[02:06] **Article 2: Whether pain or sorrow is assuaged by tears.** Yes, in two ways. (1) Actions connatural to us please us — just as good deeds please the virtuous man, so acts of sorrow please the man in a state of sorrow. (2) Outward sorrow diffuses inward sorrow. Aquinas: "A hurtful thing hurts yet more if we keep it shut up, because the soul is more intent on it; whereas if it be allowed to escape, the soul's intention is dispersed as it were on outward things, so that the inward sorrow is lessened. This is why men burdened with sorrow make outward show of their sorrow by tears or groans or even by words — their sorrow is assuaged."

[02:58] **Article 3: Whether pain or sorrow are assuaged by sympathy of friends.** Yes — in two ways: (1) showing affection demonstrates love for us, and being loved is a good which elicits pleasure, assuaging sorrow; (2) our friends seem to carry our burdens with us, like a Simon of Cyrene. Aquinas: "Sorrow has a depressing effect, it is like a weight; whereas if we strive to unburden ourselves, so that when a man sees others saddened by his own sorrow, it seems as though others were bearing the burden with him, striving as it were to lessen its weight; wherefore the load of sorrow becomes lighter for him."

[03:59] **Article 4: Whether pain and sorrow are assuaged by the contemplation of truth.** Aquinas: "The greatest of all pleasures consists in the contemplation of truth. Now every pleasure assuages pain; hence the contemplation of truth assuages pain or sorrow, and the more so the more perfectly one is a lover of wisdom; and therefore in the midst of tribulations men rejoice in the contemplation of divine things and of future happiness, according to James 1:2, 'My brethren, count it all joy when you shall fall into divers temptations'; and what is more, even in the midst of bodily tortures this joy is found, as the martyr Titus, when he was walking barefoot on the burning coals, said: 'Methinks I walk on roses in the name of Jesus Christ.'" The narrator notes from his time as a Carthusian: "frostbitten, hungry, tired, alone — and happy."

[05:14] **Article 5: Whether pain and sorrow are assuaged by sleep and baths.** Yes — like eating one's feelings (a.1), the physical comfort of sleep or a warm bath can counteract or assuage the pains of the soul.

[05:30] **Question 39 — Goodness and malice of sorrow or pain.** **Article 1: Whether all sorrow is evil.** All sorrow considered in the abstract is evil — not morally, but as a privation of some good which elicits sorrow. Aquinas: "The mere fact of a man's appetite being uneasy about a present evil is in itself an evil, because it hinders the response of the appetite in good." But not all sorrow is evil if what we are sorrowful for *ought* to be sorrowful for — Jesus weeping over Lazarus; our sins, this present age. Ecclesiastes: "Sorrow is better than laughter; for by sadness of countenance the heart is made glad. The heart of the wise is in the house of mourning, but the heart of fools is in the house of mirth." Aquinas: "Supposing the presence of something saddening or painful, it is a sign of goodness if a man is in sorrow or pain on account of this present evil; for if he were not in sorrow or pain, this could only be either because he feels it not, or because he does not reckon it as something unbecoming — both of which are manifest evils. Consequently it is a condition of goodness that, supposing an evil to be present, sorrow or pain should ensue."

[07:47] **Article 2: Whether sorrow can be a virtuous good.** Our Lord teaches: "Blessed are those who mourn." Aquinas: "Sorrow is a good in as much as it denotes perception and rejection of evil." Virtuous sorrow includes both *intellect* (perception of present evil as such) and *will* (setting oneself against the perceived evil).

[08:34] **Article 3: Whether sorrow can be a useful good.** Useful if the evil can be escaped or avoided in the future. Sorrow over an unchangeable handicap is not useful — even harmful. But sorrow for one's sins, leading to confession, penance, restitution, and resolve, is very useful. Sorrow from voluntary removal of temporal goods that may have been harmful (which we still feel as losses) is also useful — useful insofar as it stems from shrewd voluntary removal of goods which at the end of the day may not have been good to keep.

[09:40] **Article 4: Whether bodily pain is the greatest evil.** The witness of Christ in his humanity is the response — he would rather suffer death than act unjustly or shrink back from his divine will. Aquinas takes a surprising angle: "Pain or sorrow for that which is truly evil cannot be the greatest evil, for there is something worse — namely either not to reckon as evil that which is really evil, or not to reject it." **The greatest evil is not pain or sorrow, but to be *desensitized* to evil, or to sense it and not reject it. The greatest evil is to *be* evil.** A person too evil to sorrow over evil would not sorrow not because she shouldn't but because she is too evil to sorrow. Aquinas: "The very fact of the will being opposed to evil is a good; and for this reason sorrow or pain cannot be the greatest evil, because it has an admixture of good; and guilt is a greater evil than punishment, and this is all that is called evil, i.e., sin and the punishment of sin." So guilt is a greater evil than bodily pain or sorrow.

[11:15] That closes Day 80 — Q.38 and Q.39.

## Notes

- **Pivotal claim for synthesis**: Q.39 a.1 + a.4 — *not feeling sorrow at evil is itself a greater evil*. This is Aquinas's deep convergence with the Wright/Rohr lineage of lament: feeling rightly about the world's brokenness is part of being well. The contemporary parallel: positivity research (Fredrickson) does *not* claim positive affect is always optimal; it claims chronic *deficit* of positivity is destructive. The fully-flourishing agent grieves what should be grieved.
- Q.38 a.3: sympathy of friends as the key remedy — Fredrickson's positivity resonance is the empirical mechanism. PRS-01 (loving-kindness as remedy for disconnection) and PRS-08 (high-quality listening) directly correspond.
- Q.38 a.4: contemplation of truth as supreme remedy. Reads forward to Rohr: non-dual contemplative practice as the stable foundation under suffering; Wright: hope-as-resurrection-orientation.
- Q.38 a.2: tears diffuse inward sorrow. Active inference frame: externalizing prediction error makes it available for downward-flowing prior updates from social model (others' attention).
- Q.38 a.5: sleep and baths. Vagal nerve stimulation, parasympathetic shift — Fredrickson's biology.
- Q.39 a.4: the *greatest* evil is desensitization to evil. The synthesis writes: under perspective-limitation, the agent who *cannot perceive* the privation is the worst-off agent, because the resource for return is unavailable.
