# Day 67 — Intention and Choice

> Episode: <https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1TtZOjIlpIc>

Today's episode covers Prima Secundae Q.12 (intention as an act of the will) and Q.13 (choice as the act of the will with regard to the means). Together they articulate the distinction between *willing the end* (volition, intention, enjoyment) and *willing the means* (choice, consent, use), with intention as the bridge.

## Q.12 — Intention

**a.1 — Whether intention is an act of the intellect or of the will.** There are three acts of the will regarding the end: *volition* (willing something simply, like health), *intention* (willing the end as the term toward which something else is ordered), and *enjoyment* (the will's resting in the end). Aquinas: *intention as the very word denotes signifies to tend to something; consequently intention belongs first and principally to that which moves to the end. Now the will moves all the other powers of the soul to the end; wherefore it is evident that intention properly speaking is an act of the will.*

**a.2 — Whether intention is only of the last end.** No. We intend going to work, praying, reading — all true ends pursued by various means but not the last end. *Intention regards the end as a terminus of the movement of the will. Now a terminus of movement may be taken in two ways: first, the very last terminus, when the movement comes to a stop — this is the terminus of the whole movement; secondly, some point midway, which is the beginning of one part of the movement and the end or terminus of the other. Thus in the movement from A to C through B, C is the last terminus, while B is a terminus but not the last. And intention can be both. Consequently, though intention is always of the end, it need not be always of the last end.*

**a.3 — Whether one can intend two things at the same time.** Not two ultimate ends — the last end must be one (Matt 6:24, no man can serve two masters). But proximate or middle ends ordered to a single ultimate end can be intended together. Aquinas: *where we have many things in reality we may take them as one term of intention in so far as the reason takes them as one, either because two things concur in the integrity of one whole, as a proper measure of both heat and cold conduce to health, or because the two things are included in one which may be intended; for instance, the acquiring of wine and clothing is included in wealth as in something common to both.*

**a.4 — Whether the intention of the end is the same act as the volition of the means.** *In so far as the movement of the will is to the means as ordered to the end, it is called choice; but the movement of the will to the end as acquired by the means is called intention.* Acts are distinguished by their objects. Considered separately, end and means are distinct objects, denoting distinct acts (intention and choice). But considered as one object — *the end as the reason why we will the means* — the single act of intention includes both. Analogy: light and color are distinct objects but in one act of sight I see both; principles and conclusions are distinct objects but in one act of assent to a conclusion I assent on account of the principles.

**a.5 — Whether intention is within the competency of irrational animals.** No, strictly speaking. They cannot understand goals as goals to be intended. But they have built-in tendings — *moved to an end not as though they thought that they can gain the end by this movement (this belongs to one that intends), but through desiring the end by natural instinct.* The stone tends toward union by gravitational motion; the bee toward making a beehive; dogs and rabbits by instinctual tendency.

## Q.13 — Choice

Aquinas's preamble: *we must now consider the acts of the will with regard to the means. There are three of them: to choose, to consent, and to use. And choice is preceded by counsel.*

**a.1 — Whether choice is an act of the will or of reason.** Both intellect and will are involved, but choice is *primarily* an act of the will: choice is *a movement or an act towards that good which is chosen*. Aquinas: *choice is the desire of things in our power; but desire is an act of the will; therefore choice is.*

**a.2 — Whether choice is to be found in irrational animals.** No. *In those things which are altogether determined to one, there is no place for choice.* Animals are determined to one by external/internal sense and natural instinct. The lamb fleeing the wolf is acting on built-in instinct, not choosing. We choose because, although our will is determined to the good in general, it is indifferent to any particular good short of God in the beatific vision.

**a.3 — Whether choice is only of the means, or sometimes also of the end.** Choice stands at the end of a *practical syllogism*. Example: (a) I intend to be at work by 8:00am. (b) The best way is to drive. (c) Therefore I choose to drive. From this perspective choice is never of the end but only of the means. But just as conclusions of one science can serve as principles of another (mathematics → music theory), so a chosen means like *being a successful doctor* (means to wealth) can become an end requiring further means. From that perspective choice can sometimes concern the end.

**a.4 — Whether choice is only of those things that are done by us.** *The means must needs be either an action or a thing with some action intervening whereby man either makes the thing which is the means or puts it to some use; and thus it is that choice is always in regard to human acts.*

**a.5 — Whether choice is only of possible things.** Yes. We do not choose to breathe underwater or physically fly to the moon. *Where the end is impossible the means is impossible as well.* The end and means must be possible in some way.

**a.6 — Whether man chooses of necessity or freely.** The will follows the intellect; if reason apprehends some good as worth willing, the will follows. But since nothing in this life is entirely good, nothing must necessarily be seen as good and willed except that to which the will is directed by nature — the good in general, and complete satisfaction (happiness). Choice concerns means, not the end. *The perfect good alone, which is happiness, cannot be apprehended by the reason as an evil or as lacking in any way; consequently man wills happiness of necessity, nor can he will not to be happy or to be unhappy. Now since choice is not of the end but of the means, it is not of the perfect good which is happiness, but of other particular goods; therefore man chooses not of necessity but freely.*

## Notes

- Six acts of the will across Q.10–17 form the pipeline: volition → intention → enjoyment (of the end); counsel → consent → choice → use (of the means). Today is intention (the bridging act) + choice (the consummating act of practical reason).
- The structure of intention is the *terminus-of-movement* pattern: end-as-stopping-point and end-as-midpoint-of-larger-movement. This maps almost too cleanly onto active inference's hierarchical generative models — every prior is a terminus relative to its sub-policies and a way-station relative to higher priors.
- The single-act-with-distinct-objects move (a.4) is structurally identical to the predictive-coding move where one act of perception integrates many channels (light + color + form). Bridges to Hoffman, Friston, Hawkins.
- Choice as the conclusion of a practical syllogism (Q.13 a.3) is the bounded-rationality + active-inference picture: practical reason proceeds from goal-prior to means-policy via inference. McGilchrist's right-hemispheric integration is the phenomenology side; Friston the formalism.
- The freedom argument (Q.13 a.6): necessity attaches only to the perfect good; partial goods leave the will indifferent and so genuinely free. This is the metaphysical anchor for the central-theme claim that perspective-limitation individuates agents — agents are individuated precisely *because* they perceive partial goods and must choose under that limitation.
- Animals don't choose — but they do tend. The "natural instinct as built-in intention" picture (Q.12 a.5) is exactly what Levin's bioelectric-pattern frame and Friston's active-inference frame describe at the cell-and-organism level: tending without representing.
