# Day 70 — Morality

> Episode: <https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0YW3kxz-7bg>

Today's episode covers Prima Secundae Q.18 (the good and evil of human acts in general — 11 articles) and Q.19 (the goodness and malice of the interior act of the will — 10 articles). This is the moral-evaluation arc proper: object, circumstances, end, and reason as the rule by which voluntary action is judged morally good or evil.

## Q.18 — The good and evil of human acts in general

**a.1 — Whether every human action is good.** No. Evil is privation — a lacking of the goodness or being proper to a thing (cf. ep.25). A human action lacking proper goodness in regard to its object, circumstances, or intended end is an evil action.

**a.2 — Whether the goodness or evil of a man's action is derived from its object.** Yes — primarily. *Just as the primary goodness of a natural thing is derived from its form which gives it its species, so the primary goodness of a moral action is derived from its suitable object.* The object is "the material with which an act deals; it gives the specific nature to an act" — the action's subject matter (Gilby translation). Murder vs. almsgiving differ in their objects: taking innocent life vs. giving to those in need.

**a.3 — Whether man's action is good or evil from a circumstance.** Yes. Circumstance = *that which stands around* the action as outside it. Eating meat for dinner is morally indifferent in itself, but on a Friday in Lent for a Catholic the circumstances of time alter the action's moral character.

**a.4 — Whether a human action is good or evil from its end.** Yes. *It may happen that an action which is good in its species or in its circumstances is ordained to an evil end, or vice versa.* Almsgiving for the sake of being praised (Matt 6) is the paradigm — the end tarnishes a good object.

**a.5 — Whether a human action is good or evil in its species.** *An action is said to be evil in its species not because it has no object at all, but because it has an object in disaccord with reason; for instance, to appropriate another's property.* Right reason is the rule of moral evaluation; theft's object is against right reason and so the species is evil.

**a.6 — Whether an action has its species of good or evil from its end.** Yes. *Now in a voluntary action there is a two-fold action: the interior action of the will and the external action; and each of these actions has its object. The end is properly the object of the interior act of the will, while the object of the external action is that on which the action is brought to bear. Therefore just as the external action takes its species from the object on which it bears, so the interior act of the will takes its species from the end as from its own proper object.* Some acts (adultery, blasphemy) are evil in themselves regardless of intended end.

**a.7 — Whether the species derived from the end is contained under the species derived from the object as under its genus, or conversely.** Aquinas's answer: the man who commits theft for the sake of adultery is *guilty of a two-fold malice in one action* — the two species are considered separately. Where they converge (theft for the sake of being rich), it is the more particular species (theft) that gives the action its primary species.

**a.8 — Whether any action is indifferent in its species.** In its kind, yes — picking up a piece of straw, considered abstractly. But once *intentionally willed*, no action is morally neutral: it must be good or evil.

**a.9 — Whether an individual action can be indifferent.** No. Gregory's *idle word* is one that lacks usefulness, rectitude, motive of just necessity, or pious utility — and is therefore evil. *Every word is either good or bad; for the same reason every other action is either good or bad. Therefore no individual action is indifferent.* Anything proceeding from deliberate choice is either in accord or in discord with right reason.

**a.10 — Whether a circumstance places a moral action in the species of good or evil.** Sometimes. Stealing a chalice from a particular cathedral makes what might have been theft now an act of *sacrilege* — the circumstance specifies the species. *To appropriate another's property is specified by reason of the property being another's, and in this respect it is placed in the species of theft.*

**a.11 — Whether every circumstance that makes an action better or worse places a moral action in a species of good or evil.** No. Some circumstances merely *aggravate* — stealing a larger or smaller amount changes the magnitude of the evil but not its species.

## Q.19 — The goodness and malice of the interior act of the will

**a.1 — Whether the goodness of the will depends on the object.** Yes. *In so far as the good in question is in accord with reason, it enters the moral order and causes moral goodness in the act of the will.*

**a.2 — Whether the goodness of the will depends on the object alone.** The end is included in the object of the will (since we do not act without an end in mind). *The end is the object of the will; hence in regard to the act of the will, the goodness derived from the object does not differ from that which is derived from the end.* Circumstances are *accidents of the act* — they don't alter the species.

**a.3 — Whether the goodness of the will depends on reason.** Yes — absolutely. *Hilary says it is an unruly will that persists in its desires in opposition to reason; but the goodness of the will consists in not being unruly; therefore the goodness of the will depends on its being subject to reason.*

**a.4 — Whether the goodness of the will depends on the eternal law.** Yes. *It is from the eternal law, which is the divine reason, that human reason is the rule of the human will, from which the human will derives its goodness; it is therefore evident that the goodness of the human will depends on the eternal law much more than on human reason; and when human reason fails, we must have recourse to the eternal reason.*

**a.5 — Whether the will is evil when it is at variance with erring reason.** Yes. To inquire whether the will is evil at variance with erring reason is the same as asking whether an erring conscience binds. It does. *Absolutely speaking, every will at variance with reason, whether right or erring, is always evil.*

**a.6 — Whether the will is good when it abides by erring reason.** Depends on the kind of ignorance. If intentional or caused by disordered passion or vice, the erring conscience does not excuse. If through no fault of one's own (the man taking what he sincerely thought was his own billfold), then *that error of reason or conscience excuses the will that abides by that erring reason from being evil*. Paul's pre-conversion persecution of the church (1 Tim 1:13) is the scriptural exemplar.

**a.7 — Whether the goodness of the will as regards the means depends on the intention of the end.** Yes — when the end *causes* the willing of the means. But ends *external to* the action (other goods one hopes to obtain) added afterward do not retroactively determine the goodness of the prior act. *Intention that follows the act of the will is added to a preceding act of the will, and then the goodness of the previous act of the will does not depend on the subsequent intention except in so far as that act is repeated with the subsequent intention.*

**a.8 — Whether the degree of goodness or malice in the will depends on the degree of good or evil in the intention.** Not equivalently. A bad intention can spoil what would have been a good act, but cannot make an intrinsically evil act (killing the innocent, blasphemy, adultery) somehow good.

**a.9 — Whether the goodness of the will depends on its conformity to the divine will.** Yes. *Human knowledge is conformed to the divine knowledge in so far as it knows truth, and human action is conformed to the divine in so far as it is becoming to the agent, and this by way of imitation.*

**a.10 — Whether it is necessary for the human will, in order to be good, to be conformed to the divine will as regards the thing willed.** *In order that a man will some particular good with a right will, he must will that particular good materially and the divine and universal good formally.* The fasting Dominican who is told by his religious superior not to fast must forego the particular good of fasting for the universal good of obedience. *The human will is bound to be conformed to the divine will as to that which is willed formally, for it is bound to will the divine and universal good — but not as to that which is willed materially.*

## Notes

- Aquinas's evaluative architecture: every voluntary act is morally specified by *object* (primary), *circumstances* (sometimes specifying, sometimes only aggravating), and *end* (primary for the interior act of will). No deliberate act is neutral.
- The privation theory of evil (a.1) is the metaphysical anchor: evil is not a substance but a *lack* of due goodness. Bridges to Levin on form-as-bioelectric-pattern (deficient morphogenesis as analog to moral defect), Stump on suffering, and Wright on creation-as-good and Fall as privation.
- The two-fold action (Q.18 a.6) — interior act of the will (object: end) and external act (object: thing on which it bears) — is structurally identical to the dual-process picture in cognitive science: there is a higher-order intentional act and a lower-order executed act, each with its own object.
- The erring-conscience pair (Q.19 a.5–6) is the masterstroke: conscience binds, but excusable ignorance excuses. This is the moral-knowledge-under-resource-limitation picture — agents must act under their best apprehension, which is constitutively limited.
- The conformity-to-divine-will articulation (Q.19 a.10) — *material vs. formal* willing of the universal good — is the central-theme keystone of today's day. To be a good agent is to will the universal good *formally* even when material specifics are uncertain. This is precisely the picture of *salvation in fear and trembling* under perspective-limitation.
- The Dominican-fasting example is striking: obedience to one's superior overrides the particular good of fasting because the superior is the proximate visible articulation of the universal will. Bridges to Wright on covenanted community as the locus of obedience, Rohr on contemplative submission, MacIntyre on tradition-bounded rationality.
- The bridge to bounded-rational decision-making is throughout: agents act under uncertainty, with limited apprehension, and right action is conformity-to-the-rule even when the rule's full content is not in view.
