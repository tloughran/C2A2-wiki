# Day 79 — Sorrowful Effects

*Aquinas, Summa Theologiae, Prima Secundae, Q.36 (causes of sorrow/pain) and Q.37 (effects of pain or sorrow).*

## Transcript

[00:00] Day 79 — questions 36 and 37 of the Prima Secundae, of the causes of sorrow or pain and the effects of pain or sorrow.

[00:55] **Article 1 (Q.36): Whether sorrow is caused by the loss of good or by the presence of evil.** Aquinas: the loss of good itself can be perceived as a present evil, just as the loss of an evil would be seen as a present good. The question: when it comes to the sorrowful object, is it chiefly regarded as a *missing good* or as *positively contrary*? Aquinas: "Since in the movements of the appetite sorrow is a kind of flight or withdrawal… sorrow regards the evil that is present." Sorrow is caused chiefly by flight from something perceived to be presently evil or contrary to us, not merely an absent good.

[01:45] **Article 2: Whether desire is a cause of sorrow.** Proverbs: "Hope deferred makes the heart sick, but a desire fulfilled is a tree of life." Desire is a cause of sorrow if the object of desire is delayed or no longer attainable. But desire is not the *only* or *greatest* cause of sorrow: "We sorrow more for the loss of a present good in which we have already taken pleasure than for the withdrawal of a future good which we desire to have."

[02:23] **Article 3: Whether the craving for unity is a cause of sorrow.** Yes — desire can cause sorrow, and everything desires unity (oneness) as it desires goodness, because the two are *transcendentals* or convertible with one another. For a thing to exist it must have some unity in itself; being and unity are convertible — one in reality, distinct only in our consideration of them. The more united and simple a thing becomes, the more Godlike — God has no composition whatsoever; even his Triune nature does not divide him into parts. Aquinas: "Sorrow is caused by the craving not for any kind of unity, but for that unity in which the perfection of nature consists; separation from things hurtful and corruptive is desired insofar as they destroy the unity which is due."

[03:55] **Article 4: Whether an irresistible power is a cause of sorrow.** Augustine: "Sorrow in the soul is caused by the will resisting a stronger power; while pain in the body is caused by sense resisting a stronger body." Sorrow is an acquiescing in some perceived evil which the soul or body would not do if it could avoid it — hence the sorrowful object must be deemed irresistible for the soul or body to simply rest in it.

[04:39] **Question 37 — Of the effects of pain or sorrow.** **Article 1: Whether pain deprives one of the power to learn.** The soul being one, to the extent its powers are directed toward one action, focus is drawn away from others — a painful stomach ache or wound can pull our attention from everything else. Yet moderate sorrow or pain can actually be an impetus to learn — physical discomfort leads one to study and find a treatment; many people go searching for God intellectually because of pain or sorrow in their lives. Aquinas: "Moderate sorrow that does not cause the mind to wander can conduce to the acquisition of learning, especially in regard to those things by which a man hopes to be freed from sorrow; and thus men are more apt to be taught by God."

[05:43] **Article 2: Whether the effect of sorrow or pain is to burden the soul.** We use *burden* metaphorically: sorrow is not a physical weight but is analogous to one. Aquinas: "Sorrow is caused by a present evil; and this evil, from the very fact that it is repugnant to the movement of the will, depresses the soul inasmuch as it hinders it from enjoying that which it wishes to enjoy." Insofar as sorrow is perceived as a hindrance — the passion elicited by resting in some obstacle to the desired good — it can be said to be burdensome.

[06:36] **Article 3: Whether sorrow or pain weakens all activity.** If sorrow comes from the activity itself (someone working at a job he considers evil and sorrowful), the passion will weaken that activity. But if work is for the sake of freeing himself from some sorrow or pain, sorrow can motivate or strengthen the activity — think of the resolve of one trying to free himself from physical pain or poverty.

[07:15] **Article 4: Whether sorrow is more harmful to the body than the other passions of the soul.** Yes — sorrow is a movement of the soul in flight, under the influence of something perceived as contrary. Since passions are accompanied by physical change, the change affected by sorrow is a shrinking back, a resting repugnance. Fear and anger presuppose sorrow, and chronic anger or anxiety are not beneficial to health. Scripture confirms: Proverbs 17, "A joyful mind maketh age flourishing; a sorrowful spirit drieth up the bones"; Proverbs 25, "As a moth doth by a garment and a worm by the wood, so the sadness of a man consumeth the heart"; Sirach 38, "Of sadness cometh death."

[08:41] That closes the episode — Q.36 and Q.37.
