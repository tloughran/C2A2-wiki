### Q.7 — The infinity of God

**Article 1 — Is God infinite?**

[00:01:00] If we consider a thing infinite as to mean *not finite, not limited*, then yes. But what we don't mean here by infinite is something *infinitely large* or extending infinitely in some direction — that will be discussed in the following articles. Now, things are limited in different ways. An essence or nature of a thing is limited by its particular *material*: the matter of which I consist pins me down to this particular place and time. On the other hand, *matter* is limited by the particular essence or nature it consists of in becoming actual — actually existing instead of just being sheer potency, which is what matter is. (See a previous episode about that discussion: *what is matter and quantum mechanics?* Werner Heisenberg, Nobel Prize winner in physics, believed that matter was what we today call energy; there's good reason for not believing that. See that previous episode.)

[00:02:12] Now with non-material things — say, angels or immaterial spirits — even *they* would be limited insofar as their *nature* limits existence to that particular form of existing, their particular nature. But since God is not material, neither is his existence particular or received in some particular essence or nature. Because he simply is *existence itself*, then he is in no way limited — not by matter, not by a particular nature. He is unlimited as being-being-itself.

**Article 2 — Whether anything but God can be essentially infinite**

[00:02:45] If anything were infinite (again, without limit), it would have no beginning — but everything created has God for its beginning, its first principle, and is therefore not essentially without limit. Now, there are *relative* infinites, as Aquinas will call them: you could turn a ball of dough into an unlimited number of various shapes; you could count numbers without limit; we could continue to learn things without limit. But this doesn't make dough infinite, the mind infinite, or any particular number infinite.

[00:03:18] Here is also posed a well-known objection to the existence of God: well, if God is unlimited (at least in power), he should be able to create something unlimited like himself; if not, then it must mean that he is not actually all-powerful or unlimited himself. The response: only the *uncreated* can be without limit in all respects. To ask why the uncreated cannot create an uncreated thing — why God cannot do that — does not prove that God doesn't exist; it just proves that contradictions do not exist, as we would expect.

**Article 3 — Can an actually infinite magnitude exist?**

[00:03:51] Could there be an infinitely large thing in existence — something of infinite size? Insofar as a thing takes up space, it must have some definite shape — three-sided, four-sided, five-sided, etc. — because it must have some kind of boundary to be one thing, to be a thing at all. Whatever shape that happens to be, the thing will be limited by that shape. But to be limited is to be *finite*, not infinite. Now, in mathematics we do talk about infinities by way of *abstraction*, but not as concrete existing kinds of things. Mathematics is inherently abstract, same with physics. As Aquinas explains: *a geometrician does not need to assume a line actually infinite, but takes some actually finite line from which he removes or subtracts whatever he finds necessary, and that line he then calls infinite*.

**Article 4 — Whether an infinite multitude can exist**

[00:05:24] Can an infinite number of things exist? Aquinas puts it quite plainly: if we're talking about a numbering of things, that number would have to exist within the category of multitude, of numbering — *but no species of number is infinite*. To explain that: for any particular total number of a thing, there could always be another added to it, making whatever that present number is *not* infinite. So the number of a thing would itself be finite. That seems simple enough.

### Q.8 — The existence of God in things

**Article 1 — Is God in all things?**

[00:06:29] *A thing is where it operates* is our thematic foundation for this question. Where is your soul, for example? It is where it is operating — that is the extent of your body. Where is an angel? Aquinas says: again, wherever it happens to be operating. And since the cause and the effect must be related to one another — joined in some way for that effect to take place, by the power of the cause acting in the affected (kind of like your soul being joined to your body by giving it its particular existence at any moment) — then where is God?

[00:06:59] For this article we can say that God is in all things by affecting anything which happens to exist, at any moment that it happens to exist. This was Aquinas's *second way*, or proof of the existence of God, which we covered in our second episode. *He is in all things at their most fundamental depth — more inmost to us than we are to ourselves.* If how we are to ourselves, or know ourselves, is via consciousness — which is at a very superficial level compared to the most fundamental level of us, which is our existence — it is here that we can understand the often-quoted words of St. Augustine, who wrote in his *Confessions*:

> Late have I loved thee, O Beauty so ancient and so new; late have I loved thee. For behold, thou wert within me, and I outside; thou didst touch me, and I have burned for thy peace.

[00:08:01] But often we say that God is in some things in a more particular way than others, and Aquinas will speak to that as well. He says: *things are said to be distant from God by the unlikeness to him in nature and grace*. This is how God is particularly, especially in his saints — by the presence of grace within them and their imitation of him.

**Article 2 — Is God everywhere?**

[00:08:31] If God is in all things, is he *everywhere*? It seems more like he would be *nowhere*, because he's not corporeal — not extended in space, not material, not bodily so as to take up a place. (Again, we are setting the human nature of the Second Person of the Trinity, the Word in Jesus Christ, to the side for later treatment in the *Summa*.) But on the other hand, we have the authority of scripture, where God says through Jeremiah, *"I fill heaven and earth."* So how do we understand Jeremiah in scripture?

[00:09:03] God is not in a place so as to exist corporeally, or in such a way as to exclude other things taking up that place. But, as the cause of all things existing (Aquinas's Second, Third, Fourth Ways), of their movement (Aquinas's First and Fifth Ways), as well as the cause of that *place* that they happen to fill — he is everywhere.

**Article 3 — Is God everywhere by his essence, presence, and power?**

[00:09:33] By **power**: we have a kind of analogy in the way a king is present to every part of his kingdom as ruler and lawgiver — in this case, of the universe.

[00:10:05] By **presence**: he is present like the things in front of you right now are present to you — you can observe them, watch them, have oversight of them. This refers to God's providence.

[00:10:21] By **essence**: which again is existence itself, because he alone is Creator. *Creation* meaning *to be brought out of nothing*, which would require an infinite amount of power, since the distance, the gap, is an infinite gap between nothing and anything *ontologically*. Aquinas mentions this as a response to those who thought otherwise.

[00:10:36] He is also in rational beings — *like knowledge is in the person who knows it* — either *habitually* (that is, by faith for us on earth) or *actually* (like the blessed know God, seeing him in the mind as he is, in heaven). And, in St. Thomas's words: *"No other perfection except grace added to substance renders God present in anything as the object known and loved; therefore only grace constitutes a special mode of God's existence in things."*

**Article 4 — Whether to be everywhere belongs to God alone**

[00:11:09] To this question Aquinas brings up six objections against it — a conspicuously large number of arguments contrary to his opinion, so this must have been a well-debated opinion, or one that Aquinas wanted to be very thorough with. Our compass in this question will be the sentence by Aquinas: *"It belongs to a thing to be everywhere absolutely, when on any supposition* — on any condition or situation — *it must be everywhere."*

[00:11:40] So if the universe were no larger than a grain of sand, then there would be a way in which we could say the grain of sand would be everywhere — but not *absolutely*. Because that grain of sand, being extended, the entirety of it would not be everywhere; only all of its parts taken together as a whole would be everywhere. And as Aquinas is getting at here, *only* in the case or supposition of this bizarre hypothetical scenario which we've conjured up. But *in any scenario possible*, God must be everywhere — as causing that *where* or place by causing the existence of everything that happens to be within it. And *all* of God must be in that place, because when God acts, all of God acts — which follows upon his simplicity, that he is not composed.

[00:12:43] The Catholic Church will state this succinctly in the *Catechism*:

> For as the Trinity has only one and the same nature, so too it has only one and the same operation. The Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit are not three principles of creation but one principle.

[00:13:15] The noteworthy objections and responses within this article include the discussion of *matter* and *universals*. It would seem matter is everywhere, because matter is what all material things are made up of. Aquinas will respond that matter in itself — being the most fundamental bottom of all material things — *doesn't actually exist*; it's just potential, sheer potency for existing things. This would be very different than as we say of God *to be everywhere absolutely*. The same would apply with universals or natures, like *Humanity*: the essence or form of which is pinned down to not being everywhere by the particular matter (in our case) in which it informs. That is a sense of everywhere or not-limited-to-a-place that is *not* what Aquinas will consider absolutely being everywhere — because actually existing outside of the mind of God, this nature is limited and is joined to some material. You're not going to run into *Humanity itself*; you're always going to run into particular humans, particular instances of that overarching universal form, essence, or nature.

[00:14:18] That was kind of a rabbit hole, I know, but something we will explore in greater detail in the approaching questions of this first volume of the *Summa*.

## Notes

- **"A thing is where it operates" (Q.8 a.1)** is the day's deepest formal anchor for the contemporary frame. Habash glosses it with the soul-and-body example, which is straight Aristotelian hylomorphism, but the *principle* — *location is constituted by operation, not the other way around* — is exactly what Hoffman's interface theory says about spacetime: spatial location is what *operations* (perceptions) under the species-specific interface generate, not a container in which things sit. This is the article that lets the Day 5 synthesis operate the keystone at full strength.
- **Augustine's *late have I loved thee* (Q.8 a.1)** is the day's central-theme citation. *"Thou wert within me, and I outside"* is the perspective-limited agent failing to recognize the unconditioned ground in itself. Wright's covenant-inclusion vocabulary and Rohr's "everything belongs" both circle this; Augustine names it in the first person.
- **"More inmost to us than we are to ourselves" (Q.8 a.1)** is the immanent-divinity claim Rohr most needs and that Kastrup's mind-monism most directly underwrites. The bare *being-of-experiencing* is the substrate of every alter; finite consciousness is *less inmost* to the alter than the substrate that holds it.
- **The king/sight/creator triple (Q.8 a.3)** maps cleanly onto contemporary structures: power = top-down active inference / morphogenetic field control; presence = the agent's perceptual frame within which the field operates; essence = the substrate-grounding of the alter in mind-at-large. The synthesis can use the triple as it stands.
- **"All of God acts when God acts" (Q.8 a.4)** combined with the Catechism's "the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit are not three principles of creation but one principle" is a Trinity-deferral move — the day flags it but leaves it for I Q.27–43. Rohr's relational ontology and Kastrup's open question on internal relations within mind-at-large are both queued here.
- **Anti-magnitude move (Q.7 a.1)** — God is infinite *as unlimited-by-essence-or-matter*, not as *infinitely large* — is critical for the synthesis. Mind-monism has the same constraint: mind-at-large is not "a very big mind" (which would be a magnitude error); it is mind *as ontological primitive*, unlimited because not constituted by any particular essence-or-matter limitation. Habash's distinction is the same distinction the synthesis must hold.
- **The geometrician's line (Q.7 a.3)** — math handles infinity by abstraction-from-finite, not by encountering an actual infinite — is the tradition's discipline for *not confusing the formal infinity of the unconditioned with the magnitude infinities of created things*. The Day 5 synthesis will respect this discipline.
