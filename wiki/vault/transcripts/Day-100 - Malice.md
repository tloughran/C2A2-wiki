# Day 100 — Malice

Day 100 covers I-II Q.78 (*the cause of sin which is malice*) and Q.79 (*the external causes of sin*). Q.78 distinguishes *sin through malice* from sin through ignorance and sin through passion: malice is "choosing an evil as evil for the sake of some other good" (a.1), it is implied whenever one acts from a vicious habit (a.2), can occur without habit (a.3), and is graver than sin through passion (a.4). Q.79 then turns to *external* causes: whether God is a cause of sin (a.1, no, neither directly nor by withholding due assistance), whether the *act* of sin is from God (a.2, yes — as God is cause of every act qua act — but not the *defect* in the act, which is from the will), whether God causes spiritual blindness and hardness of heart (a.3, only by *just* withdrawal of grace), and whether these are directed to salvation (a.4, not necessarily for the blinded, but always to some good — the martyrs' good in the case of tyrants' sin, the glory of God's justice in the case of the lost).

## Q.78 — The cause of sin which is malice

[00:34] Day 100. Today's questions Q.78 and Q.79 of the Prima Secundae — the cause of sin which is malice and the external causes of sin.

[01:02] **Article 1 — whether anyone sins through certain malice.** A profound article. Aquinas distinguishes three modes of sin: through *ignorance* (one didn't know the act was evil), through *passion* (one knew but judged it differently in the particular case under appetite), and through *malice* (one chose the evil *knowing it as evil* for the sake of some other good). The murderer who knows killing is evil but acts for the wealth he gains. The adulterer who tells himself "adultery is better than being alone" — choosing a spiritual evil for the sake of a fleeting temporal good. "Evil is merely the privation of some good, and so a man wishes knowingly a spiritual evil... in order to possess a temporal good; wherefore he is said to sin through certain malice or on purpose, because he chooses evil knowingly."

[03:25] **Article 2 — whether everyone that sins through habit sins through certain malice.** Yes. Vicious habits make vicious acts *connatural* — proportionate to us, even lovable in our own eyes — and acting from the habit necessarily means acting *against* the spiritual good opposed to that vice. So habit-driven sin entails the malice-structure: spiritual evil knowingly chosen for the sake of what is connatural.

[04:53] **Article 3 — whether one who sins through certain malice sins through habit.** No — malice can occur without habit. Possible routes: ignorance from a malformed conscience (culpable); passion creating a particular ignorance; willing the spiritual evil for some pleasure without yet having the disposition, perhaps because one forgot the fear of God or hope of eternal life that would otherwise have protected. Malice can come *from* habit but does not *require* habit.

[06:21] **Article 4 — whether it is more grievous to sin through certain malice than through passion.** Yes. "He that sins through passion is induced through the passion to choose what he would not choose were it not for the passion. On the other hand, he that sins through certain malice chooses evil of his own accord... so that his choosing, of which he has full control, is the principle of his sin." The man who kills for theft (malice) is graver than the man who kills in rage (passion) — more diabolical, more deliberate, more revealing of habitual disorder.

## Q.79 — External causes of sin

[07:39] **Article 1 — whether God is a cause of sin.** No — neither directly nor indirectly. God inclines all things to their proper good and to himself. He cannot indirectly cause sin by withholding grace except *justly*, in response to a creature's having forfeited its claim by its own prior evil. (Treated more in subsequent articles.)

[08:49] **Article 2 — whether the act of sin is from God.** Nuanced — best to quote: "Sin denotes a being and an action with a defect; and this defect is from the created cause, the free will, as falling away from the order of the first agent, which is God. Consequently this defect is not reduced to God as its cause, but to the free will: even as the defect of limping is reduced to a crooked leg as its cause, but not to the moving power, which nevertheless causes whatever there is of movement in the limping. Accordingly God is the cause of the act of sin: and yet he is not the cause of sin, because he does not cause the act to have a defect." The structure: *act* (positive being, traced to God as first cause); *defect in the act* (privation, traced to the will).

[10:54] **Article 3 — whether God is the cause of spiritual blindness and hardness of heart.** Two senses. (a) As the *movement of the mind* cleaving to evil and turning from divine light — God is *not* the cause. (b) As the *withdrawal of grace* such that the mind is not enlightened and the heart not softened — God *is* the cause. The withdrawal must be just. Then in that just withdrawal, God is rightly said to cause blindness and hardness.

[11:48] **Article 4 — whether blindness and hardness of heart are directed to the salvation of those who are blinded and hardened.** Not necessarily for those blinded. Hell is real and is not empty; some were blinded and never returned. But for some, yes — those who acknowledge the sin and turn humbly. "Every evil that God does or permits to be done is directed to some good; yet not always to the good of those in whom the evil is, but sometimes to the good of others or of the whole universe: thus he directs the sin of tyrants to the good of the martyrs, and the punishment of the lost to the glory of his justice."

[12:42] That's Q.78 and Q.79 — malice and the external causes of sin.

## Notes

- **The trichotomy ignorance / passion / malice** (Q.78 a.1) is one of the cleanest threefold typologies in the *Prima Secundae*. It maps onto a contemporary control-theoretic distinction: (a) *model error* — the agent's generative model lacks the relevant prior (ignorance); (b) *precision error* — the agent's model is correct in general but the local precision weights are corrupted by appetitive signal (passion); (c) *policy malice* — the agent's model is intact, the precision weights are intact, but the policy *selects* the disordered outcome because it dominates on some other dimension (malice). The third is the hardest to model — and the most morally weighty.
- **Habit ⇒ malice but not vice versa** (Q.78 a.2-3) is structurally identical to the active-inference claim that high-precision priors entrain the policy distribution but do not require it on every step. A bad disposition guarantees the malice-structure when it fires; the malice-structure can also be assembled de novo without the disposition.
- **Sin as defect-in-an-act-that-is-still-an-act** (Q.79 a.2) — the limping/crooked-leg analogy — is one of the most underrated metaphysical structures in Aquinas. The act has full positive being; the *deficiency* in the act is privation; the privation has a cause distinct from the cause of the positive being. Levin-style "form is real" lands well on this: the positive act has form (causally efficacious bioelectric/policy pattern); the disorder of the act is the local breakdown of that form, which has its own causal story (regulator failure).
- **Hardness of heart as *just* withdrawal of grace** (Q.79 a.3) is the hardest article to translate into mind-monism without losing Aquinas's content. The contemporary frame would say: the agent has detached itself from the relational fields that supplied its coherence-maintenance signal, and the *relational source* has not chosen to override that detachment. This preserves the asymmetry (the agent caused the rupture; the divine source's role is non-coercive withholding) without making God an *active* hardener.
- **Q.79 a.4 — every evil ordered to *some* good, not always the good of the one in whom the evil is** — is the cosmic-scope move. The martyrs benefit from the tyrants' sin; the divine justice is glorified in the punishment of the lost. The synthesis on this article will need to engage Wright on the new-creation reading of evil's role in the cosmic redemption and Rohr on the Universal-Christ frame for the *whole* of suffering being held in the divine pattern. Important: this is one of the few places in I-II where the central theme — perspective-limitation + loving-unity — can be stated explicitly *as a Thomistic move*, since Aquinas himself reaches for the *whole universe* perspective to make sense of the *part's* evil.
- The episode marks Day 100. We've crossed about a third of the year — and we're crossing the hinge from sin's *internal causes* into sin's external context. The transition into the *effects of sin* (Q.85-89), then into *original sin* and *law*, sits just ahead.
