# Day 95 — The Beatitudes

*Summa Theologiae*, Prima Secundae, Q.68 (Of the gifts of the Holy Spirit) and Q.69 (Of the Beatitudes).

## Transcript

[00:05] Day 95. Today we cover Q.68 and Q.69 of the Prima Secundae: *of the gifts of the Holy Spirit* and *of the Beatitudes*.

[00:55] **Article 1: Whether the gifts differ from the virtues.** What we mean by "gifts" are the gifts of the Holy Spirit as seen in Isaiah 11 (Vulgate): "The spirit of the Lord shall rest upon him: the spirit of wisdom and of understanding, the spirit of counsel and of fortitude, the spirit of knowledge and of piety, and he shall be filled with the spirit of the fear of the Lord." Recall Augustine's definition of infused virtue: "A good quality of the mind, by which we live righteously, of which no one can make bad use, which God works in us without us." The difference: virtues denote a supernatural quality coupled with motion from within (they perfect us in our own actions), whereas the gifts of the Holy Spirit perfect us in order that we may be moved by divine inspiration — that we may receive motion from the outside. Aquinas: "These perfections are called gifts not only because they are infused by God, but also by them man is disposed to become amenable to the divine inspiration... For those who are moved by divine instinct there is no need to take counsel according to human reason, but only to follow their inner promptings, since they are moved by a principle higher than human reason."

[02:43] **Article 2: Whether the gifts are necessary to man for salvation.** Yes — we have a supernatural end for which we are not of ourselves sufficient, nor are we even with the theological and infused virtues alone able to direct ourselves toward that end in a way requisite for salvation. We require, in addition, the aid of the Holy Spirit — to be moved by the Holy Spirit, similar to how the moon requires the sun to shine, or an apprentice requires a master to work well. Aquinas: "Whether we consider human reason as perfected in its natural perfection, or as perfected by the theological virtues, it does not know all things, nor all possible things. Consequently it is unable to avoid folly or other like things — dullness, rashness, fears, ignorance, hardness of heart, etc. God, however, to whose knowledge and power all things are subject, by his motion safeguards us from all folly, ignorance, dullness of mind, and hardness of heart and the rest. Consequently the gifts of the Holy Ghost which make us amenable to his promptings are said to be given as remedies to these defects."

[04:13] **Article 3: Whether the gifts of the Holy Spirit are habits.** Yes — just as the moral virtues perfect the appetitive part of man by giving the will and passions a disposition to be moved in accord with right reason, so the gifts dispose man in relation to both his intellect and appetite to the movement of the Holy Spirit. It is fitting that this motion be via a disposition (not coercion), because as rational animals we move ourselves by free choice. As St. Thomas says many times: grace builds on nature; it does not destroy it.

[05:26] **Article 4: Whether the seven gifts of the Holy Spirit are suitably enumerated.** Taking Isaiah 11 as authority: in regard to the intellect of man as it is directed to the reception of truth, God bestows *understanding*; for judgment on that truth, *wisdom*. In regard to the intellect as directed toward action, *counsel*; and for correct judgment on that counsel, *knowledge*. In regard to the appetitive faculty: as it pertains to the will toward others, *piety*; against fears of danger, *fortitude*; and against inordinate pleasures, *fear of the Lord*. A particularly beautiful quote on piety: "The name of piety denotes the reverence which we give to our father and to our country. And since God is the father of all, the worship of God is also called piety. Therefore the gift whereby a man through reverence for God works good to all is fittingly called piety."

[06:58] **Article 5: Whether the gifts of the Holy Ghost are connected.** Yes. Just as moral virtue perfects the appetite of man so as to dispose it to the rule of reason, so the gifts of the Holy Spirit perfect the powers of man — both intellect and will — so as to dispose them to the impulse of the Holy Spirit. Since the Holy Spirit dwells in us by charity (Romans 5: "God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit who has been given to us"), "just as the moral virtues are united together in prudence, so the gifts of the Holy Spirit are connected together in charity — so that whoever has charity has all the gifts of the Holy Spirit, none of which can one possess without charity."

[07:48] **Article 6: Whether the gifts of the Holy Ghost remain in heaven.** In regard to their essence — a disposition to the promptings of the Holy Spirit — they will exist most perfectly in heaven where God will be "all in all" (1 Cor 15). But in regard to particular objects like strengthening man against danger or withholding man from lewd pleasures (fortitude, fear of the Lord), because neither danger nor lewd pleasures exist in heaven, the gifts will not remain *as they are now* in the same way.

[08:24] **Article 7: Whether the gifts are set down by Isaiah in their order of dignity.** Aquinas places, following Isaiah, wisdom first (it judges); understanding second; then counsel and fortitude (because of the arduous character of their object); then knowledge and piety (which regard ordinary everyday things); and lastly fear (it modifies a passion, and specifically the concupiscible passions whose object is not arduous). As an aside, fear of the Lord — though last in dignity — is "the beginning of wisdom" (Prov 9): "Fear is chiefly required as being the foundation, so to speak, of the perfection of the other gifts. For 'the fear of the Lord is the beginning of wisdom,' not as though it were more excellent than the others, but because in the order of generation man departs from evil on account of fear (Prov 16:6) before doing good works."

[10:09] **Article 8: Whether the virtues are more excellent than the gifts.** "There are three kinds of virtues: theological, intellectual, moral... On the other hand, the gifts of the Holy Ghost dispose all the powers of the soul to be amenable to the divine motion." Are the virtues more excellent than the gifts? The theological virtues — based on their object, God himself — are on par with the gifts. One could argue the gifts are greater because "to be moved by God supernaturally is a more exalted notion than to be the sole mover of oneself." St. Thomas concludes: "The gifts are bestowed to assist the virtues and to remedy certain defects... therefore the gifts are more excellent than the [acquired and moral] virtues. Yet at the same time, charity is a theological virtue, and such we grant to be more perfect than the gifts."

[11:46] **Question 69: Of the Beatitudes. Article 1: Whether the Beatitudes differ from the virtues and gifts.** Aquinas: "The Beatitudes differ from the virtues and gifts not as habit but as act." Worth reflecting on what the Beatitudes are, and whether they exist in our own lives as acts. Our Lord said: "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the Kingdom of Heaven. Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted. Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth. Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they shall be satisfied. Blessed are the merciful, for they shall obtain mercy. Blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God. Blessed are the peacemakers, for they shall be called sons of God. Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness' sake, for theirs is the Kingdom of Heaven."

[12:44] **Article 2: Whether the rewards assigned to the Beatitudes refer to this life.** Holy saints and doctors of the church have differed on this question. Aquinas is of the opinion it can mean both — by indicating the beginning of a blessedness or reward in this life, to be fully possessed in the life to come.

[13:13] **Article 3: Whether the Beatitudes are suitably enumerated.** "Beatitude has been held to consist in one of three things: some have ascribed it to a sensual life, some to an active life, and some to a contemplative life. Now these three kinds of happiness stand in different relations to future beatitude... Sensual happiness, being false and contrary to reason, is an obstacle to future beatitude; happiness of the active life is a disposition of future beatitude; and contemplative happiness, if perfect, is the very essence of future beatitude, and if imperfect, is a beginning thereof." Aquinas now shows how the Beatitudes indicate (i) an absence of obstacles, and (ii) the presence of active and contemplative perfection.

When it comes to the absence of obstacles (sensual goods): *Blessed are the poor* — contempt for riches, whether honor or wealth. *Blessed are the meek* — absence of indulgence in wrath or inordinate irascible passion. *Blessed are those who mourn* — descriptive of those who have chosen to forgo certain sensitive goods.

When it comes to perfection in the active life: *Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness* — intensity for acting justly, rendering to others what is due. *Blessed are the merciful* — beyond justice, a prompt eagerness to give gratuitously, to give more than required by justice.

When it comes to perfection in the contemplative life: *Blessed are the pure in heart* — the point of the active life as it pertains to oneself is to become perfect, to integrate ourselves so as to give of ourselves wholeheartedly to God. *Blessed are the peacemakers* — when a man is ordered in himself, ordered to his neighbor, ordered to God, there is peace. As Augustine says: "peace is the tranquility of order."

The eighth and final beatitude — *Blessed are the persecuted* — Aquinas writes: "The eighth beatitude is a confirmation and declaration of all those that precede, because from the very fact that a man is confirmed in poverty of spirit, meekness, and the rest, it follows that no persecution will induce him to renounce them."

[16:09] **Article 4: Whether the rewards of the Beatitudes are suitably enumerated.** The first three Beatitudes promise to those who have forgone sensual goods a corresponding eternal good: to the poor in this life, the excellence of the heavenly kingdom; to the meek, inheritance in the life to come; to those who mourn, a spiritual and eternal comfort. The next two promise an eternal compensation for what was given away: to those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, that they in eternity will have their fill; to the merciful, that their own miseries will be wiped away. The last two belong to contemplative happiness: cleanness of eye disposes one to see — hence the clean of heart are promised they shall see God; to make peace (in oneself or among others) shows a man to be a follower of God, the God of unity and peace — hence the glory of divine sonship. And the eighth beatitude: "Just as the eighth beatitude is a confirmation of all the Beatitudes, so it deserves all the rewards of the Beatitudes... Or else, according to Ambrose, the Kingdom of Heaven is promised to the poor in spirit as regards the glory of the soul, but to those who suffer persecution in their bodies it is promised as regards the glory of the body."

## Notes

- Q.68 introduces an **agency layered above virtue**: gifts are dispositions to be *moved by another* (the Holy Spirit), not dispositions to move oneself well. This is the explicit introduction of *passivity-as-perfection* into the virtue framework — non-trivial.
- Q.68.2's argument from epistemic insufficiency ("human reason does not know all things, nor all possible things") is Aquinas's most candid acknowledgment that natural and even infused-virtue rationality has hard limits, and that closing those limits requires external supervision. Maps directly onto contemporary humility-under-uncertainty arguments.
- Q.68.5 on charity as the unifying root of the gifts re-asserts what Q.65 said about the moral virtues: there is a **single hub** at the top of the network through which everything else is integrated. Same network-topology argument, different layer.
- Q.69 reframes the Sermon on the Mount as **act** (not habit), telling us how to read the Beatitudes in the context of the virtue/gift architecture: the Beatitudes are the *empirical signatures* you'd see in someone whose virtues and gifts are working.
- Q.69.3's three-tier reading (obstacle-removal, active perfection, contemplative perfection) maps directly onto a Rohrian non-dual developmental arc — and the eighth-beatitude-as-confirmation aligns with the contemplative claim that suffering well-borne is the integrity test for the whole edifice.
- This is the cluster where Wright's New-Testament work and Rohr's contemplative work become unavoidable. The Sermon on the Mount is not a private mystical doctrine; it is Jesus's covenant-renewal program. And it is also the contemplative tradition's roadmap.
