# Day 77 — Delightful Sorrow

*Aquinas, Summa Theologiae, Prima Secundae, Q.32 (cause of pleasure) and Q.33 (effects of pleasure).*

[00:00] Day 77 — covering questions 32 and 33 of the Prima Secundae, of the cause of pleasure and of the effects of pleasure.

[00:55] **Article 1 (Q.32): Whether operation is the proper cause of pleasure.** Yes — both the operation of *knowing* one has attained some good, and the act of *union* of that good with the relevant appetitive faculty (whether the passions or the will), are required for pleasure. Even if one's faculty of smelling some pleasant aroma is exercising while asleep, it gives no pleasure, precisely because one is unaware of that union with the good. So both the operation of knowing one has the good and the actual union with it are required.

[01:39] **Article 2: Whether movement is a cause of pleasure.** Change can cause pleasure if the passing of time increases the effect of the pleasing agent on us — warming oneself by the fire — or if time lets us grasp more completely the pleasing object — learning more about God. However, Aquinas admits a perfect pleasure would be a *changeless possession* of some perfect unchangeable good, such as the pleasure God has in knowing himself. Aquinas: "If therefore there be anything whose nature is unchangeable, the natural mode of whose being cannot be exceeded by the continuation of any pleasing object, and which can behold the whole object of its delight at once, to such a one change will afford no delight."

[02:33] **Article 3: Whether hope and memory cause pleasure.** Yes — by bringing into the present the beloved good. When I hope for heaven, there is a sense in which it is in that moment already united to me, at least in hope. Memory of a lost loved one can elicit a kind of pleasure by bringing the beloved into the present in remembering them. But memory produces a less perfect pleasure than hope, because hope carries the *possibility of attaining* the good. Aquinas: "Hope and memory are indeed of things which absolutely speaking are absent, yet those are after a fashion present, i.e., according to apprehension only or according to apprehension and possibility of attainment."

[03:28] **Article 4: Whether sadness causes pleasure.** We'd think not — sadness is the passion elicited by resting in the perceived evil. But St. Thomas reminds us nothing is entirely evil, since evil is merely a privation (a non-being). Therefore in union with this perceived evil there are accompanying goods adjacent to it, surrounding it, which can elicit pleasure. **"Sometimes accidentally a thing is the cause of its contrary; and like manner sorrow is the accidental cause of pleasure insofar as it gives rise to the apprehension of something pleasant."** Even in thinking about the loss of a loved one — perceived as an evil, an absence — the mere remembrance of that person who was a beloved good can elicit pleasure. So too can considering perceived evils that have passed as no longer afflicting us. **This is the heart of today's title — delightful sorrow.**

[04:39] **Article 5: Whether the actions of others are a cause of pleasure.** Yes — most apparently if their actions directly benefit us. Also, since we take friends as *alter ego* (another self), love binds us such that we take what happens to them as happening to us; when friends do good or succeed, we rejoice as if their actions were our own. Most interestingly: the praise and admiration of others is a cause of pleasure precisely because *knowledge* of the attained good is required for pleasure. When someone you trust tells you you are beautiful or strong, you come to know more deeply some good you possess. Aquinas: "From the fact that another's actions makes us to know or appreciate our own good… men take pleasure in being praised or honored by others, because thereby they thus become aware of some good existing in themselves; and since this appreciation receives greater weight from the testimony of good and wise men, hence men take greater pleasure in being praised and honored by them."

[06:32] **Article 6: Whether doing good to another is a cause of pleasure.** Yes — for several reasons: (1) if the person is a friend, the good that happens to them we take as having happened to us; (2) Christ says whatever one does to the least, he did to Our Lord — no good deed will go unrewarded; (3) in doing good in the world we prove to ourselves that we have some aspect of goodness; (4) if a man is virtuous, doing good deeds has a connaturality, a likeness to him, in which he rejoices as in food, drink, sleep, or contemplation; (5) love is the primary cause of pleasure, so the greater our love for the friend, the greater the pleasure in doing good. Aquinas also gives an insight into why some take pleasure in doing evil (correcting, punishing): "To contradict and to punish give pleasure not as tending to another's ill, but as pertaining to one's own good… first, as making man imagine himself to be wise and excellent, since it belongs to wise men and elders to reprove and to scold; secondly, insofar as by scolding and reproving one does good to another. It is also pleasant to an angry man to punish, insofar as he thinks himself to be removing an apparent slight which seems to be due to a previous hurt."

[09:18] **Article 7: Whether likeness is a cause of pleasure.** Yes — every creature loves its like, every person his neighbor. Aquinas: "Likeness is a kind of unity; hence that which is like us, as being one with us, causes pleasure." The human being next to me is taken as united to myself in his humanity; if I limit consideration to that respect, I perceive him as a good (as I perceive my own human nature as a good), so he can be a cause of pleasure to me.

[10:08] **Article 8: Whether wonder is a cause of pleasure.** Aquinas: "Wonder is a kind of desire for knowledge, a desire which comes to man when he sees an effect whose cause either is unknown to him or surpasses his knowledge of faculty of understanding." Wonder is a cause of pleasure insofar as it includes a hope of getting the knowledge one desires. Just as hope unites us in thought with some future perceived good, so wonder is a cause of pleasure by promising the possibility of discovering the cause of the effect before us.

[10:58] **Question 33 — Of the effects of pleasure.** **Article 1: Whether expansion is an effect of pleasure.** Yes; pleasure is called *laetitia* derived from *latitudo* (expansion). We use the term metaphorically to denote the union with the good in the apprehensive faculty (mind/senses) as well as reception into the appetitive faculty (passions/will) — both faculties "expand" in receiving this perceived good.

[11:51] **Article 2: Whether pleasure can cause thirst or desire for itself.** Pleasure considered in itself is an emotion arising from the good possessed — a kind of rest, not denoting further thirst. But it can lead to desire if the thing possessed leads us on to desire other things or this one thing more perfectly. Pleasure in the first verse of a song might lead to desire for the second; imperfect possession of the knowledge of God might lead to desire for perfect possession.

[12:38] **Article 3: Whether pleasure hinders the use of reason.** The use of reason itself can elicit pleasure and so be motivated by it; but pleasure can hinder reason by (1) swaying it from the true to the apparent good, (2) altering the body (alcoholic drink impairs use of the body), or (3) distracting attention. Aquinas: "When the attention is firmly fixed on one thing it is either weakened in respect of other things or it is entirely withdrawn from them; and thus if the bodily pleasure be great, either it hinders the use of reason by concentrating the mind's attention on itself or it hinders it considerably."

[13:28] **Article 4: Whether pleasure perfects operation.** Yes — Aquinas: "Pleasure perfects operation as some end added to it, that is to say, in as much as to this good which is operation there is added another good which is pleasure, denoting the repose of the appetite in a good that is presupposed; and inasmuch as the agent through taking pleasure in his action is more eagerly intent on it and carries it out with greater care." So as crowning end *and* as impetus to operate more effectively, pleasure perfects operation.

[14:11] That closes the episode — questions 32 and 33 of the Prima Secundae.

## Notes

- **Pivotal claim for the synthesis**: Q.32 a.4 — sadness/sorrow can be the *accidental* cause of pleasure. This is the bridge to grief, lament, and what contemporary affect theory (Fredrickson) calls *positivity within negativity*. The synthesis can articulate the perspective-limitation point: a limited agent cannot apprehend a privation as pure non-being; the good adjacent to the absence is always available to apprehension.
- Q.32 a.2: perfect pleasure = changeless possession of unchangeable good. Reads forward to Day 76's "timeless delight." The contemporary monist frame: under analytic idealism the "changeless" is the universal consciousness; under Hoffman, the interface is where time appears at all.
- Q.32 a.3: hope and memory bring the absent into the present. This is *active inference*: agents construct present states from generative models of past and projected futures.
- Q.32 a.5: pleasure from praise/honor depends on *trust* in the praising agent. Mirrors Fredrickson PRS-08 (high-quality listening → safety + sensory connection) and PRS-09 (weak-tie intellectual humility).
- Q.32 a.6: pleasure in doing good to another, especially to a friend; ego-extension via love. Fredrickson's positivity resonance grounds this biologically (vagal tone, neural synchrony).
- Q.32 a.7: likeness = unity = pleasure. Friston's free-energy principle: shared generative models reduce surprise, which is intrinsically rewarding.
- Q.32 a.8: wonder is desire-for-knowledge that *promises* the cause; pleasure here is anticipatory. Friston again: epistemic value as a component of expected free energy.
- Q.33 a.3: pleasure hinders reason — narrowing of attention. McGilchrist territory: hemispheric attention as the substrate.
