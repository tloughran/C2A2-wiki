### Q.28 — Of the effects of love

[00:00:05] Hello, my name is Austin Habash, the founder of Think Catholic, and you're listening to the Summa podcast where we study St. Thomas Aquinas's *Summa Theologiae* in a way simple and insightful for anyone to understand. This is Day 75. We're covering today Questions 28 and 29 of the Prima Secundae — that is, *of the effects of love* and *of hatred*. (Translation: Ave Maria Press five-volume set, code Summa10.)

**Article 1 — Whether union is an effect of love**

[00:00:53] Dionysius says that every love is a unitive love. As we mentioned in our previous episode, since we must first know a thing before we can love it, the apprehension of the perceived good denotes itself a kind of union with it — which is why Aquinas says love itself is this union or bond. Just in knowing the slice of pizza on the table exists, it is already united to my sensitive appetite; in this union of *affection*, love itself consists. From that kind of union — what Aquinas calls a *union of affection* — the passion of desire can follow, which draws me toward another kind of union with the pizza: *real union*, actually eating it. Real union then elicits joy. So love is both itself a kind of union with the lovable object and the cause of my later actual union with that object by spurring on desire and then joy.

[00:01:54] In Aquinas's words, beginning with a quote from Augustine: *"Love is a vital principle uniting, or seeking to unite, two together; for in describing it as 'uniting' he refers to the union of affection, without which there is no love; and in saying that 'it seeks to unite' he refers to real union."*

**Article 2 — Whether mutual indwelling is an effect of love**

[00:02:26] St. Thomas responds: every love makes the beloved to be in the lover, and vice versa. Because the appetite must follow apprehension, for me to love the slice of pizza on the table I must first know about it or sense it in some way, making it to be in me — in my senses — from which I can then desire it. So in this first way love denotes indwelling: the loved object must be in me, in my sensual apprehension, before I can desire it.

[00:02:59] Yet still according to apprehension there is a manner in which one could say also that I, the lover, am in the loved object. As St. Thomas teaches: *"The lover is said to be in the beloved inasmuch as the lover is not satisfied with a superficial apprehension of the beloved, but strives to gain an intimate knowledge of everything pertaining to the beloved, so as to penetrate his or its very soul."*

[00:03:31] Next, according to the appetitive powers, the loved object is in the lover by that complacency it elicits in the lover. Also, if this love is a love of friendship, there is a way in which my friend is in me, in so far as I am currently willing some good to him, and in so far as I take what happens to him to also be happening to me (since a friend is, as we described, *alter ipse* — another self). Lastly, in regard to friendship, St. Thomas writes: *"Mutual indwelling in the love of friendship can be understood in regard to reciprocal love, inasmuch as friends return love for love, and both desire and do good things for one another."*

**Article 3 — Whether ecstasy is an effect of love**

[00:04:06] First, what is ecstasy? Aquinas writes that *"to suffer ecstasy means to be placed outside oneself."* We can be said to be placed outside ourselves both in regard to the apprehensive and the appetitive faculties. When I think about God, or think about the pizza on the table, there is a way in which I am drawn out of myself into those objects outside of me — at least in thought. Moreover, as it pertains to the appetitive powers, my desire for the pizza does draw me out of myself toward it, toward the object of my love. This would also be the case with love for a friend in wishing some good for him.

[00:05:09] As an aside, in our understanding of friendship and the willing of goods for another, we should keep in mind — because it's easy to forget — what we mentioned in the Prima Secundae about happiness: truly everything we do, we do for the sake of being happy, including what we do for our friends. That doesn't make our good will toward our friends somehow mercenary, because it is within our pursuit of happiness that simply follows from our nature. The will's object is good, and as an end, its complete satisfaction is what happiness means; because that is our end, all other things must find themselves as means to that end, including friendship. As St. Thomas puts it: *"He who loves goes out from himself, in so far as he wills the good of his friend and works for it; yet he does not will the good of his friend more than his own good."*

**Article 4 — Whether zeal is an effect of love**

[00:05:45] What is zeal? *"Zeal, whatever way we take it, arises from the intensity of love."* So the question is: is intensity of love — a.k.a. zeal — the effect of love? It seems it would be better to say that zeal is primarily an effect of the particular *good* in question — that the greater this particular good is in one's eyes, the greater will their love be for it, and the more zeal they will have in procuring it, either for themselves or for a friend. We can see that with our Lord, where it is said *"Zeal for his house will consume me"* — Christ's intense love in his human nature for the glory and honor of his divine nature is the passion behind his act of driving out the money changers from the temple, and is evidenced in his voluntary crucifixion for the salvation of mankind. In summary, we can, as Aquinas does, define zeal as always denoting an intense love within the lover for the object of his or her love.

**Article 5 — Whether love is a passion that wounds the lover**

[00:06:54] If we take a wound to be a kind of corruption or illness of the lover, we must say that love in itself does not wound the lover — because love is, on the contrary, a kind of *adaptation* (Aquinas's word here) within the lover to the object of his love. As we've said previously, it is an affective union of the thing loved within the lover. However, love does cause a handful of other effects. The first is a kind of *melting* — an expression to denote the entrance into the heart of the beloved object in intention, opening the possibility for subsequent actual union with that object. Another term mentioned is *fervor*, which denotes the ensuing desire that follows upon the love of an object. Then lastly are *enjoyment* or *languor* — enjoyment ensues when actually united to the object of our love, and languor is the pain or desire of absence of the lovable object.

[00:07:58] St. Thomas then reminds us that since the passions are bound up with the body and thereby come with bodily effects, in this way they can be said to wound us — if the emotion's effect on the body is detrimental in some way to the function or use of our body.

**Article 6 — Whether love is the cause of all that the lover does**

[00:08:15] Dionysius says that all things, whatever they do, they do for the love of good. Aquinas responds in this article with one of the shortest responses we have probably encountered thus far in the *Summa*: *"Every agent acts for an end, or some goal, as was already stated. Now the end is the good desired and loved by each one; wherefore it is evident that every agent, whatever it be, does every action from love of some kind."*

### Q.29 — Of hatred

**Article 1 — Whether evil is the cause and object of hatred**

[00:08:59] The major takeaway from this article is that there is manifestly subjectivity in our passions. Although evil in things is something very objective — like blindness in sight, or vice in the soul — when it comes to our passions, things are taken to be good or evil not so much as they are in themselves, but as we perceive them to be. If we consider a thing to be good for us, to be in harmony with us or benefit us — to have some kind of positive relation to us in our eyes — like the adulterer might see adultery as enjoyable or good in some other aspect, then we love and enjoy it, yet that does not make adultery good. In the same way, as another example, when I knock on someone's door to proclaim Christ Jesus and his mystical body the Catholic Church, many times I am clearly hated — yet that doesn't mean that I am evil (God willing), but it does mean that I am being perceived as an obstacle or hindrance or threat to those who are apprehending me.

[00:10:02] Therefore, just as love is a kind of complacency in the apprehended perceived good, its opposite — hatred — is a kind of dissonance perceived in some object (to use St. Thomas's words here, as something opposed to us and therefore perceived as an evil). So in response to this article: evil is the cause and object of hatred.

**Article 2 — Whether love is a cause of hatred**

[00:10:35] It is — and in the manner alluded to in the previous article. If love is based on the perceived good, then whatever is opposed to the attainment of that good — or opposed to someone we will it for, either ourselves or a friend — will be considered an evil, and therefore an object of hatred. A word from St. Thomas: *"Love consists in a certain agreement of the lover with the object loved, while hatred consists in a certain disagreement or dissonance. Consequently love must needs precede hatred, and nothing is hated save through being contrary to a suitable thing which is loved. Hence it is that every hatred is caused by love."*

**Article 3 — Whether hatred is stronger than love**

[00:11:22] The key to this article: *"A thing is moved to the end more strongly than to the means."* Everything is ordered to some good as its end. Every agent acts for an end, and that end must necessarily be good — whether true or just apparent — or there would be no reason for pursuing it. Thus hatred can never be directed toward the end, because the end as such must be a perceived good. Hatred can only come in at the means — in the removal of some means opposed to our intended end — and since the means can never be greater than the end in our eyes, then hatred can never be greater or stronger than love.

[00:11:54] Nevertheless, hatred can sometimes *seem* stronger than love, for two reasons. First, because hatred can be more keenly felt than love — and this because hatred is based on a kind of glance toward those things opposed or contrary to us. As an analogy, think of the difference between the feeling of physical pain and the physical tranquility of health: the former is just more noticeable. Secondly, hatred can seem to be greater than love when a greater hatred is compared to a lesser love — such as the hatred for one's enemy versus the love of some song or some item on the menu. Yet if we compare the hatred of one's enemy to our love for the good of happiness, we will quickly see that the latter is much stronger — strong enough to motivate all of our actions, of which the former is definitely not able to do.

**Article 4 — Whether a man can hate himself**

[00:13:12] Only in the sense that he can take something to be good for him which is not actually good for him — for we must, and can only, will goods. Like we can only see color: look around the room and see if you can see anything not colored, and you cannot. In the same way, we cannot will anything unless somehow, in some respect, we take it to be good for us. The object of the faculty of sight is color; the object of the faculty we call the will is the good. Even in the case of suicide, as St. Thomas says: *"No man wills and works evil to himself except he apprehend it under the aspect of good — for even they who kill themselves apprehend death itself as a good, considered as putting an end to some unhappiness or pain."*

[00:14:13] So even the man who takes his own life does so for his own good — although it is obviously a distorted apparent good and not truly good for him to do so. In response: based on the nature of the will, man cannot hate himself in the sense that he must always will goods as a means to his ultimate good (happiness); yet in another way a man can hate himself by willing goods which are only apparent and not truly good for him.

**Article 5 — Whether a man can hate the truth**

[00:14:40] St. Augustine famously wrote in his *Confessions*: *"They hate the truth for the sake of that thing which they love instead of the truth — they love truth when she shines on them, and hate her when she rebukes them."* Which is what Aquinas will explain in this article: we cannot hate truth *in general* because it is a transcendental, in reality the same as goodness and being, only different in our consideration of them according to reason; and since no one can hate the good in general, they also cannot hate the truth about things in general. But one can hate a *particular* truth in so far as it is perceived repugnant or at variance with one's desires. We have many examples from Scripture, including that of King Herod, who married his brother's wife (which is unlawful) — which then caused him to hate John the Baptist for bringing attention to it, and he presumably hated the truth itself that adultery is evil.

[00:15:43] St. Thomas will say on this point: *"Nothing hinders some particular being or some particular truth being an object of hatred, in so far as it is considered as hurtful and repugnant."* Aquinas then gives a couple of examples of how a truth can be considered repugnant: first, if we simply wish some truth to not be true (likely as Herod did toward the truth that adultery is wrong); second, if one wishes he did not know some particular truth (like one may hate knowing the Ten Commandments if his desires are set against them); thirdly, one may hate a truth for it being known by others (the person who hates the truth about one of his sins being discovered). In all of these ways, and surely others, man can be said to hate the truth.

**Article 6 — Whether anything can be an object of universal hatred**

[00:16:27] Can anything be hateful to all — universally hated? In the sense of the actual act of hatred considered as a sensitive passion: since the passions follow upon sensual apprehension, which is always of some particular thing, we cannot be said to actually hate every particular instance of something at the same time — our passions are always directed to the particular, not the abstract and universal. But we can be said to always hate certain kinds of things when we do encounter them, like the sheep can be said to hate the wolf, or have a repugnance to it, whenever it happens to apprehend it. In the same way Aristotle says the thief and the backbiter are hated by everyone — these are examples of objects universally hateful or repugnant to all. No one likes being spoken of poorly behind their back, and no one likes being stolen from. So according to hatred considered as a passion, some objects can be hateful to all.

[00:17:29] And that is all for Questions 28 and 29 of the Prima Secundae — *of the effects of love* and *of hatred*. My name is Austin Habash with Think Catholic, and I cannot wait to see you tomorrow.

## Notes

- Counterpoint to Day 74: Aquinas's privation-frame of evil applies again — hatred is structurally derivative of love. *"Every hatred is caused by love"* is the load-bearing claim; under a mind-first reframe it reads as: in a network of conscious agents, repugnance presupposes orientation toward goods, and the orientation is the primitive.
