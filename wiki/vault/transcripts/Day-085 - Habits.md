# Day 85 — Habits

*Aquinas, Summa Theologiae, Prima Secundae, Q.48 (effects of anger — closing the passions tract) and Q.49 (habits in general — opening the habits tract).*

[00:00] Day 85 — questions 48 and 49 of the Prima Secundae, of the effects of anger and of habits in general as to their substance.

[00:50] **Article 1 (Q.48): Whether anger causes pleasure.** Yes. Aquinas: "As soon as vengeance is present, pleasure ensues — and so much the greater according as the sorrow was greater." Two reasons. (1) Just as dehydration makes water more pleasurable, the greater the sorrow the more enjoyable will be hope of vengeance. Anger is a combination of hope and sorrow; hope brings the desired good into the present, eliciting pleasure. (2) Aquinas: "Before vengeance is really present, it becomes present to the angry man in two ways: in one way by hope, and another way by thinking of it continually. For to everyone that desires a thing, it is pleasant to dwell on the thought of what he desires; wherefore the imaginings of dreams are pleasant. Accordingly an angry man takes pleasure in thinking much about vengeance." This pleasure is not perfect — it does not banish sorrow — and consequently anger persists.

[02:23] **Article 2: Whether anger above all causes fervor in the heart.** St. Gregory the Great: "The heart that is inflamed with the stings of its own anger beats quick, the body trembles, the tongue stammers, the countenance takes fire, the eyes grow fierce; they that are well known are not recognized; with the mouth indeed he shapes a sound, but the understanding knows not what it says." Anger above all affects heart rate and physical preparedness to strive against the perceived evil that has wounded.

[03:00] **Article 3: Whether anger above all hinders the use of reason.** Yes. Acts of the mind are not acts of a bodily organ, but in this life the mind utilizes both external and internal senses; when the body is disturbed or impaired, so too is reason. Aquinas: "Anger above all causes a bodily disturbance in the region of the heart, so much as to affect even the outward members; consequently, of all the passions anger is the most manifest obstacle to the judgment of reason." Psalm 30: "My eye is troubled with wrath."

[03:40] **Article 4: Whether anger above all causes taciturnity.** Yes — a kind of silence; muteness. Aquinas: "The disturbance of anger reaches to the outward members, and chiefly to those members which reflect more distinctly the emotions of the heart, such as the eyes, face, and tongue; consequently anger may cause such a disturbance that the tongue is altogether deprived of speech, and taciturnity is the result."

[04:16] **Transition.** With Q.48 the treatment of the passions completes. Aquinas turns to *habits* — beginning with their substance and proceeding to virtues and vices. His own bridge: "After treating of human acts and passions, we now pass on the consideration of the principles of human acts, and firstly of intrinsic principles. The intrinsic principle is power and habit. It remains for us to consider them in general; in the second place we shall consider virtues and vices and other like habits which are the principles of human acts."

[04:54] **Article 1 (Q.49): Whether habit is a quality.** Existing things fall within categories: substance (being human), quantity (being five foot tall), quality (being wise, being temperate), action and passion (running, being sunburned), and various relations. A *habit* is a *quality* — specifically a quality of disposition. The host suggests: substitute the word *disposition* for *habit*, or think of *habit* like a religious habit. As a Carthusian he wore an ivory-white robe — a habit on him. Habits or dispositions are *on* us, affecting how we are and how we act: knowledge of English makes you an English-speaker; the virtue of temperance makes you temperate. Aquinas's definition: "A disposition whereby that which is disposed is disposed well or ill, and this either in regard to itself or in regard to another; thus health is a habit." Health, temperance, knowledge — qualities of you. Habits don't constitute substance, aren't quantitative, aren't action or passion or relation — they must fall in the category of *quality*.

[07:10] **Article 2: Whether habit is a distinct species of quality.** Yes — distinct from the *powers* (faculties) of the soul. The powers (intellect, will) are themselves a kind of quality, but they are *proper qualities* — flowing naturally from our substance. Habits flow not from substance but from repeated act, and are *changeable* (easy or difficult). Aquinas: "In habit and disposition we consider both evil and good and also changeableness, whether easy or difficult. And so the philosopher defines habit: 'a disposition whereby someone is disposed well or ill,' and he says that 'by habits we are directed well or ill in reference to the passions.'" Distinguishing habit from disposition simpliciter: "It is clear that the word habit implies a certain lastingness, while the word disposition does not."

[09:27] (The Gilby Summa, as a synopsis: "If one has a habit to X, then it is easier to X than if one has not — examples are being generous and speaking French. If one has a habit of Xing, then it is harder not to X than if one has not — examples are smoking and saying *I say* before each sentence.")

[10:04] **Article 3: Whether habit implies order to an act.** Yes. Things exist not just to exist but for the sake of some particular action; everything tends toward its own perfection, which (for everything short of God) entails obtaining that perfection through its own action. A habit is a stable disposition either to good or ill, in reference to some particular action. Aquinas: "It is essential to habit to imply some relation to a thing's nature in so far as it is suitable or unsuitable thereto. But a thing's nature is further ordained to another end, which is either an operation or the product of an operation. Wherefore every habit is subjected or within a power, [and] implies principally relation to an act."

[12:18] **Article 4: Whether habits are necessary.** Yes — habits are necessary to give a sufficient account of human action. Why did this man eat too many donuts? We cannot appeal to external circumstances alone, because his intellect and will are not determined by external circumstances. We cannot appeal to his powers alone — they were not determined to this action. To give an adequate account, we have to look at the *disposition* of those powers, the habits of intellect, will, and appetite. Aquinas: "If the form be such that it can operate in diverse ways, as the soul, it needs to be disposed to its operations by means of habits."

[14:33] That closes Q.48 and Q.49 — and the first of many episodes on habits.

## Notes

- **Q.48 a.1 (anger causes pleasure via vengeance-imagination)** is a striking article. The angry man takes pleasure *in thinking about* vengeance — the imagination brings the future good into the present and elicits hope-pleasure. This is the *reinforcement-learning* signature: imagining vengeance is reward-predictive, the prediction generates pleasure, the pleasure reinforces the rumination, the rumination perpetuates the anger. Aquinas anticipates the modern phenomenon of *angry rumination* (Sukhodolsky, Golub, Cromwell; Bushman) as the maintenance mechanism of trait anger.
- **Q.48 a.3 (anger hinders reason via bodily disturbance)**: classic embodied-cognition / autonomic-arousal-degrades-cognition finding. Behavioral and neuroimaging literatures are clear on the executive-function decrement under sympathetic activation. Anger is structurally the worst because it produces high arousal *with* engagement of vengeance-policy — the policy is active while the deliberative resources to evaluate it are degraded.
- **Q.49 is the structural pivot** of the entire Prima Secundae. Aquinas leaves transient affective states (passions) and enters stable dispositions (habits). The synthesis must mark this transition as a major architectural move in the treatise.
- **Q.49 a.1 — habit as quality, not substance**: the medieval categorial machinery places habits as an *accidental* feature of the agent that nevertheless shapes how the agent operates. The contemporary translation: habits are the *learned parameters* of the agent's generative model — not the agent itself, but a feature *of* the agent that determines how the agent computes. Hawkins's cortical reference frames, Friston's deep generative models, Levin's bioelectric memory of pattern — all are contemporary articulations of "quality-on-substance."
- **Q.49 a.2 (habit distinguished from disposition by lastingness)** is the critical distinction. A disposition becomes a *habit* by stabilization through repeated act. Active inference: a transient affective state becomes a habit when the prior is shifted by accumulated evidence. Levin's bioelectric memory: a passing pattern becomes morphogenetic when stabilized in the bioelectric field. McGilchrist's hemispheric specializations: a hemispheric tendency becomes characterological by long use.
- **Q.49 a.4 (habits are *necessary* to explain action)**: this is the keystone medieval claim. Without appeal to stable internal dispositions, human action is *underdetermined* by external circumstances and faculties. The argument is structurally an early dispositional-realism argument — habits are the explanation-needed entities. Modern parallels: dispositional accounts of personality (Funder, Roberts); habit-based models in cognitive science (Wood & Rünger).
- **Aquinas's etymology of habit**: from *habere* (to have). A habit is something one *has* — but as a quality, not a possession. The contemporary frame can articulate: a habit is a learned-and-stabilized parameter, not an object.
