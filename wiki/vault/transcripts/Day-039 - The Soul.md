### Q.75 — The Essence of the Soul

**Article 1 — Whether the soul is a body**

Is the soul material — a material thing like hydrogen?

Aquinas responds: to seek the nature of the soul, we must premise that the soul is defined as *the first principle of life of those things which live*. For we call living things *animate* (i.e. having a soul) and those things which have no life *inanimate*. Now [00:04:28] life is shown principally by two actions, knowledge and movement.

And then he goes on to say that because material things are not necessarily alive, we must look for a cause for why some are — which is what we mean by *the soul*: an *act* or *actuality* to be found in certain material bodies, and therefore cannot be a material thing existing in itself or by itself.

We [00:04:53] should also take note of this definition of the soul being used here: *the first principle of life of those things which live*. Now this is a much broader notion of the word *soul* which Aquinas uses, and Aristotle used as well. If this is the definition of the soul most generally speaking, then one could say *plants have a soul* or *animals have a soul* — again, the soul meaning the form of [00:05:20] a thing having self-motion, moving itself potentially. Now this very broad definition of the soul is one which we don't really use on a day-to-day basis; what we normally mean by *the soul* in everyday conversations is that *incorruptible intellectual form of the human body*. But Aquinas uses this broader definition of the soul, which is why we encounter it in this article.

**Article 2 — Whether the human soul is something subsistent**

*Subsistent* meaning: does it exist in itself, like an angel is subsistent? Now it's worth quoting Aquinas's response at length because of just how famous this article is, before we explain it. St. Thomas says:

> It must necessarily be allowed that the principle of intellectual operation, which we call the soul, is a principle [00:06:13] both incorporeal and subsistent. For it is clear that, by means of the intellect, man can have knowledge of all corporeal things. Therefore it is impossible for the intellectual principle to be a body. It is likewise impossible for it to understand by means of a bodily organ, since the determinate nature of that organ would impede knowledge of all bodies. Therefore the intellectual [00:06:40] principle, which we call the mind or the intellect, has an operation in itself apart from the body. Now only that which subsists can have an operation in itself.

In summary, the human soul must exist in itself because it has an activity in itself — one that is not found in the body — that is, intellection, understanding, a [00:07:07] thoroughly immaterial activity.

Now how does Aquinas prove that? He says man knows not just particulars but the general essence of things. As an example, think about *justice*. What shape is justice? What color is it? What is its length, its width? Because if it is a material thing it must have some [00:07:35] particular size and shape — but it doesn't. Justice, the general concept which you and I know, which is real enough for the state to sentence men to death by fair trial on account of, real enough to make us feel angry or guilty instantly, is manifestly not a particular material [00:08:00] thing.

And for us to know it, to think about it, to act based upon it, it must be in some way *in* us, but cannot merely be in the brain — because the brain is material, and again, this concept of justice is not a particular material thing but an immaterial, general, abstract concept. Therefore the immaterial aspect [00:08:28] of us that can hold and entertain immaterial concepts such as justice is what we mean by *the intellect*, a capacity of the intellectual soul.

Now that can be a lot, and it may take time to sink in. The problem is, these truths — like the existence of God, the immateriality of the soul — are so close and obvious in one [00:08:53] way, then in another way they are almost imperceptible. Kind of like trying to see your own nose: it's so close and apparent that it's almost invisible or difficult to be seen.

**Article 3 — Whether the souls of brute animals are subsistent**

What about the soul of the dog? Does it exist in itself, or does it exist merely and entirely in informing the material of its own body and nothing [00:09:20] more?

St. Thomas writes:

> The sensitive soul has no *per se* operation of its own, and every operation of the sensitive soul belongs to the composite or the body. Wherefore we conclude that, as the souls of brute animals have no *per se* operations, they are not subsistent.

Everything the dog does involves and is reliant upon the body, [00:09:48] and since its soul has no activity apart from the body, there is no reason to think that its soul has any existence in itself without the body.

**Article 4 — Whether the soul is man**

Are you a ghost in the machine? Are you *using* your body, but not the body — just a soul? And this is the evil of our day, imagining that you [00:10:13] and I are not our body and therefore we can define our body or use our body in any number of arbitrary, contrary-to-common-sense ways.

To this, St. Thomas responds:

> Sensation is not the operation of the soul only. Since, then, sensation is an operation of man, but not proper to him, it is clear that man is not a soul only, but something composed of soul and body.

What that means is: just as you take the *you that thinks* to be you, so too we take our imaginings, our feelings, our sensations to be ours as well — just as our thoughts are. In other words, we consider ourselves not to be angels (which we're not) but rational animals (which we are), a body-soul composite — which will become increasingly clear as we go [00:11:04] through the remainder of these articles.

**Article 5 — Whether the soul is composed of matter and form**

I will save the deeper discussion on matter and form for question 76 within this episode (but again, one could return to episodes 23, 24, and 34 for a discussion on these terms as well). But to answer this article, it is sufficient to say that if the soul itself were material, it could not grasp [00:11:31] the general universal immaterial essence of things — because you have never run into the *general idea of dog* walking down the sidewalk; all dogs must be *particular* dogs, and the same goes for all other material things.

Aquinas then takes that thought and adds to it one more to bring us to his conclusion. And that thought is: *everything that is [00:11:58] received is received according to the manner or the mode of the receiver*. Which sounds complicated, but hopefully a couple of examples will make it easier to grasp.

As an example: if yourself and a blind man are staring at a blue wall, you receive or see it as blue, but the blind man does not — not because the wall is different, but because the sense of sight [00:12:24] in you and the blind man is different. Another example: a priest gives a homily on transubstantiation, and you understand it, but your Hindu friend does not understand it — not because the homily was different for each of you, but because the listener of that homily is different. You look at the *Summa* along with your dog, and you are able to grasp the concepts [00:12:49] behind the ink scribbled on the page because you have the capacity for conceptual abstract thought — precisely because your mind is not material; but the dog cannot grasp those concepts because it is entirely material.

Whatever is received is received according to the manner of the receiver. *You* receive im[00:13:15]material concepts, which means the intellect cannot be composed of matter and form.

**Article 6 — Whether the human soul is incorruptible**

Does the soul go on after the body dies? As blessed Job says:

> If a man dies, shall he live again? All the days of my service I would wait, till my renewal should come. You would call, and I would answer you; you would [00:13:41] long for the work of your hands.

St. Thomas says:

> But it is impossible for a form to be separated from itself, and therefore it is impossible for a subsistent form to cease to exist.

Water is made up of the raw matter — pure potency, or *energy* as Heisenberg calls it — as informed by some particular form, and [00:14:09] so to destroy it we need only affect the material until it loses that form and becomes something else, namely hydrogen and oxygen distinctly. But since the soul is immaterial — existing in itself and having an immaterial operation which is not the work of the body — then even when a man's body is destroyed, this form of the body, [00:14:34] the soul, will just go on existing and operating. However, for sure, in an estranged, not ideal way, since we do receive knowledge through the senses; but it will continue. Similar to how angels, which are immaterial things, cannot be taken apart or destroyed — they exist in themselves like the soul does, and both must necessarily continue.

**Article 7 — Whether the soul is of the same species as an angel**

[00:15:02] And we can see why this article would naturally follow the last one, because the last one demonstrated the human soul is incorruptible kind of like angels are — yet *we are not angels*, which is what Aquinas wants to make sure we understand in this article. He writes:

> Things which have different natural operations are of different species. But the natural operations of the soul and of an angel are different. Therefore the [00:15:29] soul and an angel are not of the same species.

Angels receive knowledge naturally by the infusion of God — that's how they are made to think. We receive knowledge by abstracting the essence of things from the natural world — that's how *we* are made to think. Therefore humans and angels are different kinds of things, different species.

### Q.76 — The Union of Body and Soul

And with that, trying to keep this episode within our usual [00:15:54] time, we turn to question 76, *the union of the body and soul*.

**Article 1 — Whether the intellectual principle is united to the body as its form**

Now as we've said in previous episodes: what do we mean by *matter*? We mean that absolute bottom of all material things — that doesn't even exist actually but only potentially — whether it be called *energy* (like Werner Heisenberg, or [00:16:20] Nobel-Prize-winner-in-physics whom we've been quoting along the way, called it) or *matter* as we call it (because that's what Aristotle and Aquinas called it). Whatever it happens to be called, this bottom of all material things, this potential existence, is going to need some cause for its coming into actual existence and for why it exists in some particular way. This cause is *the form*. It [00:16:46] is the innermost structure of a material thing that causes that raw potential existence (whether it be called energy or matter) to actually exist, and to exist either as a stone, plant, animal, or us. Our form — the informing, structuring principle making our material body to exist actually, and not [00:17:12] just as raw energy or raw matter — is what we mean by *the human soul*.

St. Thomas writes:

> Nothing acts except so far as it is in act, wherefore a thing acts by that whereby it is in act. Now it is clear that the first thing by which the body lives is the soul.

And then we receive an incredible amount of text from Aquinas defending that position against what I [00:17:39] or we have been calling already the *ghost in the machine* notion of the soul — that the soul is not the form of the body, but just a part of it. Aquinas says: if your soul is not the form of the body, then *why is it so reliant upon it*? If someone gets hit on the head long enough, their understanding just doesn't work as well as it did before. How is it that thought is hampered without the senses, both external (like sight) or internal [00:18:07] (like the imagination)? If the soul was not the form of the body, why is it that you call that person in the mirror by your own name? If the body is not you, as your soul is you. Lastly, how could you be called *human, a rational animal*, if both the body and the soul were not co-principal of you, the matter and the form which together make you what you are?

**Article 2 — Whether the intellectual principle is multiplied according to the number of bodies**

Is there a distinct soul informing every distinct human person's body? Aquinas says yes, and shows that the contrary cannot be true. So for example, my own act of understanding and my own willing of various things is clearly not the same as every other human being on this planet's thoughts and choices. [00:19:02] Your thoughts and choices are different than mine — that's pretty clear. And that's because your soul — *the* soul, the form of your body — is your own. You have your own soul, your own particular body, with your own distinct thoughts and choices. Therefore yes, you must have your very own soul, as well as everybody else corresponding to their very own body.

**Article 3 — Whether besides the intellectual soul there are in man other souls essentially different from one another**

And this article follows from the definition of the soul given by Aristotle which we've already seen — *the act of a body having life potentially*. Are there multiple souls in man, since in man there are multiple and varying capacities — some of which we share with the brute animals (like sense), some of [00:19:53] which we share with plants (like the capacity to take in nutrition and grow)?

To respond to our article, Aquinas responds in the negative, because for a thing to exist it must be *one*. One thing, an existing thing, must be undivided in itself, divided from other things, for it to even be called a thing at all. Think about your phone: if it didn't have [00:20:19] boundaries and a unity within those boundaries, it would not be its own thing separate from the stuff around it. Think about our country: it has parts, for sure — it is divided into states, those states into counties, those counties into cities, etc. But for it to be a country at all, to be *one* country, it must have some unifying principle. Let's call that the federal government. So too, the thing that [00:20:45] makes you one person out of all the material/immaterial components of you — like the federal government makes the states around us to be one country — is the soul. It must be one soul for you to be one thing, just like we must have one federal governing body to make all of these states one thing for the US to be considered one country.

**Article 4 — Whether in man there is another form besides the intellectual soul**

For time's sake: there is in you that most fundamental structuring principle, making you to exist and to exist in a particular way as a human; but there are also other things in you, making you to know English, to be courageous, to be of a certain height and width, etc. The first is what we call the intellectual soul — that's your [00:21:40] *substantial form*. These other forms that modify that form, we call your various qualities, quantities, relations, etc. — these other things we call *forms*, or real aspects of you, but *within* the one overarching substantial form of you, that is the soul informing the material of [00:22:07] your body.

That's also why, when you die and the soul ceases to inform the body, these lesser, varied forms which were *in* the soul already then come to the forefront, taking its place in a lot of ways, making your body no longer substantially human but now all those other lesser things that your body will decompose into.

**Article 5 — Whether the intellectual soul is properly united to such a body**

Is the human body the right kind of thing for the human soul? St. Thomas says that it is, because humanity is at the bottom rung of intelligent things, and so it's fitting that we would have to receive knowledge through the senses (which requires a body with the external and internal senses) and that we would have to reason our way down from a fundamental premise to its logical conclusions as well — both require a body [00:23:01] susceptible to sense and physical change. And that is indeed, in fact, the kind of body that we do have.

**Article 6 — Whether the intellectual soul is united to the body through the medium of accidental dispositions**

There's a lot of philosophical jargon here, but the gist of it is: do qualities — the various quantities, your various relations of a thing — come before its actual existence? [00:23:26] And the answer is no. Raw matter must first come out of potential existence into actual existence before any features, properties, qualities can follow upon that existence; and the cause of what would otherwise just be raw potential existence now actually existing as your body — that is the soul. So to answer the question we must say no: [00:23:52] the soul is united to your body *immediately*, as the first thing in-forming your body, and not through a medium of accidental dispositions.

**Article 7 — Whether the soul is united to the animal body by means of a body**

St. Thomas responds:

> If, however, the soul is united to the body as its form, as we've already said, it is impossible for it to be united by means of another body. [00:24:17] Wherefore the unity of a thing composed of matter and form is by virtue of the form itself, which by reason of its very nature is united to matter as its actualizer. Nor is there any other cause of union except the agent which causes matter to be in actual existence.

And for time's sake we will keep moving.

**Article 8 — Whether the soul is in each part of the body**

Augustine says that *in each body the whole soul is in the whole body, and in each part is entire*. Then St. Thomas explains: since the soul is united to the body as its form, it must necessarily be in the whole body and in each part thereof — a proof of which is that on the withdrawal of the soul, no part of the [00:25:07] body retains its proper action.

So when you die, it's not like a part of you changes only — the ghost within the machine. When you die, the *entire body* begins a process of decomposition. Now it's true, of course, that we can pull out a man's heart, transplant that material into another man in a way that it continues to work; but it's also true that when you die — when the soul ceases [00:25:33] to inform the body — the whole thing begins to crumble. As Dr. Sam Parnia from NYU Health, the published expert on what happens when you die, wrote: *during the last decade we've realized it's only after a person has died that the cells inside their body, including the brain, begin their own death process*. And he will go on to say much more about that. But the point is that everything about [00:25:58] your body begins to die once you have died, or the soul ceases to inform that body — which indicates that your soul is influencing each part of your body at every moment.

Which concludes the first of many, many episodes on the soul and the body of man.

And for a very impressive recent resource proving the existence of the soul, the immateriality of the mind, I would refer [00:26:24] the listener to Dr. Ed Feser's new book titled *Immortal Souls*. ## Notes

- Habash opens with the *empirical* preface (NYU Health 2022 / Parnia AWARE-II; the 2022 study collated work from Harvard, Baylor, UC Riverside, Virginia, VCU, Wisconsin, Southampton, King's College London) and closes with the *magisterial* anchor (Council of Vienne 1311–1312 anathema). The frame is deliberate: the substantive theological-philosophical claim (soul-as-form-of-body) is bracketed by contemporary scientific narrowing on one end and irrevocable doctrinal definition on the other.
- Habash flags the *narrow* vs. *broad* sense of *soul* explicitly in a.1: Aquinas (following Aristotle) uses the broad sense (*first principle of life*) — which would allow saying *plants have a soul* — but everyday usage means the narrow sense (*incorruptible intellectual form of the human body*). The synthesis needs to thread both.
- The *justice example* (Q.75 a.2) is the textual hook into universal-essence-grasping. Habash extends it pedagogically: justice has no shape, no color, no length, no width — yet we know it; yet it has real causal force (the state sentences on the strength of it). This is the empirical handle on intellection-as-not-bodily-organ-operation. The synthesis maps this to Kastrup's substrate-mode-of-participation and to Hoffman's interface-recognition-as-such.
- The *blue-wall-and-blind-man* and *priest-homily-and-Hindu-friend* examples (Q.75 a.5) are receiver-mode illustrations. The synthesis reads these as Hoffman's interface theory in classical vocabulary: the same substrate-event registers differently in differently-tuned receivers.

The medieval doctrine and the contemporary developmental biology say structurally the same thing: form withdrawing initiates the death-cascade at the cellular level.
