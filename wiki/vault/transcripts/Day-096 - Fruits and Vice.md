# Day 96 — Fruits and Vice

Austin Habash, Think Catholic, walks through I-II Q.70 (the *fruits of the Holy Ghost*) and Q.71 (*vice and sin considered in themselves*) in one episode — the hinge between the long treatise on habits/virtues (Q.49-70) and the treatise on vices/sin (Q.71-89). Q.70 closes the virtues by asking how the *fruits* listed in Galatians 5:22-23 (Charity, joy, peace, patience, benignity, goodness, longanimity, mildness, faith, modesty, continency, Chastity) relate to acts, to the Beatitudes, and to the works of the flesh. Q.71 opens the vices treatise by asking whether vice is contrary to virtue, whether vice is contrary to nature, whether vice is worse than the vicious act, whether sin is compatible with virtue, whether every sin includes an action, and whether sin is fittingly defined as a "word, deed, or desire contrary to the Eternal law."

## Transcript

### Q.70 — Fruits of the Holy Ghost

[00:29] Day 96. Today's questions: Q.70 and Q.71 of the Prima Secundae — the fruits of the Holy Ghost and of vice and sin considered in themselves.

[00:54] **Article 1 — whether the fruits of the Holy Ghost which the Apostle enumerates in Galatians 5 are acts.** Aquinas grants two senses of "fruit." The fruit *of* a tree is what the tree produces; man's fruit is also what man *gathers* (delights in as end). Applied to the Spirit: the fruits are acts of man brought about through the workings of the Holy Spirit, and they are crowned with sweetness — "man must delight in God for his own sake as being his last end, and in virtuous Deeds not as being his end but for the sake of their inherent goodness."

[02:33] **Article 2 — whether the fruits differ from the Beatitudes.** Yes, in genus: fruits are the broader category, Beatitudes a narrower one. "It is sufficient for a fruit to be something ultimate and delightful; whereas for a beatitude it must be something perfect and excellent. Hence all the Beatitudes may be called fruits but not vice versa."

[03:42] **Article 3 — whether the fruits are suitably enumerated by the Apostle.** Aquinas reads the twelve-fold list as ordered by how the Holy Spirit orders the human mind — toward itself, toward what is near it (neighbor), toward what is beneath it (the body's desires). Toward self: Charity, joy, peace (good disposition toward good), and patience, longanimity (good disposition toward evils/delays). Toward neighbor: goodness (willing the neighbor's good), benignity/kindness (excellence in doing the neighbor good), meekness (receiving evils without undue anger), faith (not dealing unfaithfully). Toward what is beneath: modesty (right manner in word and deed), continence and Chastity (Chastity withdraws from unlawful desires; continence withholds from lawful ones not currently ordered to it).

[06:30] **Article 4 — whether the fruits of the Holy Ghost are contrary to the works of the flesh.** Galatians 5 names the works of the flesh — fornication, uncleanliness, immodesty, luxury, idolatry, witchcrafts, enmities, contentions, emulations, wraths, quarrels, dissension, sects. Paul does not write the list as a direct point-for-point negation of the fruits, but the two lists are *generically contrary*: the Spirit inclines us above natural reason; the flesh inclines us beneath the rule of reason.

### Q.71 — Vice and sin considered in themselves

[07:18] **Article 1 — whether vice is contrary to virtue.** Aristotle's definition is in play: virtue is "a disposition of a perfect thing to that which is best," where *perfect* means *disposed according to its nature.* Vice is then a disposition to what is *against* one's nature — for the rational animal, that means *contrary to reason*. Sin is contrary to virtue *as act*, malice is contrary to virtue *as goodness*, vice is contrary to virtue *properly as such*.

[08:20] **Article 2 — whether vice is contrary to nature.** It is contrary to *man's* nature *as rational animal* — vices dispose a man to act poorly, not in agreement with reason. "Man's good is to be in accord with reason and his evil is to be against reason." Vices and sins enter when a man follows the inclination of his sensitive nature against the order of his reason.

[09:00] **Article 3 — whether vice is worse than a vicious act.** Briefly: no. A man is justly punished for a *vicious act*, not for the *vicious habit* alone; a vicious action is worse than a vicious habit, because it is more blameworthy to *do* evil than to be *able to do* evil — just as it is better to *do* well than to be able to do well.

[09:28] **Article 4 — whether sin is compatible with virtue.** Yes — a man may have the disposition of temperance and still fail in a single act of intemperance without losing the virtue entirely (though the virtue may be diminished). The exception: charity and the infused virtues that depend on charity *can* be lost through a single mortal sin, because their hold on the soul does not work the way acquired dispositions work.

[10:28] **Article 5 — whether every sin includes an action.** No. Sins of omission are still sins. The omission must be voluntary — choosing soccer over Mass, choosing to stay up late so as to miss Mass, simply choosing in one's heart not to go. The last is a sin of omission paired with a positive interior act; the first two omit by way of a contrary action; some sins are simple absences of due good.

[11:42] **Article 6 — whether sin is fittingly defined as a word, deed, or desire contrary to the Eternal law.** Yes — Augustine's definition. It is fitting because (a) it captures *voluntariness* (sin must be willed, whether by indulgence in passion or by interior word, by deed) and (b) "contrary to the Eternal law" includes both revealed law and natural law, since the Eternal law is *God's reason*. So far as a man departs culpably from God's will in things natural or supernatural, he sins.

[12:39] That's Q.70 and Q.71. Tomorrow continues into the vices proper.

## Notes

- The hinge structure matters: Q.70 closes the virtues with their *experiential register* (sweetness, delight, fruit) — virtue isn't only correct disposition, it is *fruit* one can taste. Q.71 then opens vices by inverting that frame: vice is disposition *against* one's rational nature.
- Aquinas's threefold ordering of the fruits (toward self / toward neighbor / toward what is beneath) maps unexpectedly cleanly onto the perspective-limitation frame: the Spirit's work orients the limited rational creature toward (a) its own goal-state (charity/joy/peace), (b) the goal-states of co-agents in its environment (goodness/benignity/meekness/faith), and (c) the lower-order sensitive systems it must integrate (modesty/continence/Chastity). Each fruit names a *coordination* the limited agent makes with itself, neighbor, or body.
- Q.71 a.4 — "sin compatible with virtue" — is the under-discussed nuance: a habit is a *trajectory*, not a deterministic output. The free-energy / active-inference frame natively handles this: a generative-model prior can be strong without being sufficient against high-precision contrary evidence. One bad act doesn't refute the prior, but enough bad acts will retrain it.
- The treatment of mortal sin as habit-destroying *via a single act* — for the infused virtues only, not the acquired ones — flags a categorical difference Aquinas wants between gift-grounded states and habit-grounded states. Salvage candidate for the synthesis: gift-grounded states are *relational* (held by another agent / by the cosmic order), not *substrate-encoded*, so they are revocable by a single relational rupture, whereas habit-grounded states are substrate-encoded and decay only with repetition.
- Augustine's "word, deed, or desire contrary to the Eternal law" — given the Eternal law = God's reason — already points toward an account where sin is *out-of-tune-with-the-whole* rather than *rule-violation*. Holds up under mind-monism: sin is local-perspective optimization against the global goal of loving unity.
