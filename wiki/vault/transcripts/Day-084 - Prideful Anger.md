## Transcript

[00:00] Day 84 — questions 46 and 47 of the Prima Secundae, of anger in itself and of the cause that provokes anger.

[00:51] **Article 1 (Q.46): Whether anger is a special passion.** Yes, anger is its own distinct passion, not a general one. Aquinas: "The movement of anger does not arise save on account of some pain inflicted, and unless there be desire and hope of revenge." This is the differentia from sorrow — both register a perceived evil, but anger adds desire and hope of vengeance.

[01:20] **Article 2: Whether the object of anger is good or evil.** A mix of both, under different aspects. The good of justice or revenge is the *good* aspect: "Desire for revenge is a desire for something good, since revenge belongs to justice." But the vengeance is to be inflicted on a perceived noxious person, which is the *evil* aspect. Aquinas: "Anger regards one object under the aspect of evil — the noxious person on whom it seeks to be avenged. Consequently it is a passion somewhat made up of contrary passions": sorrow at the evil received, hope of being avenged.

[02:31] **Article 3: Whether anger is in the concupiscible faculty.** No — anger is in the *irascible* faculty. The concupiscible passions take perceived goods or evils *simply*, not under the aspect of difficulty. We do not struggle against the donut — we just desire and enjoy it. But we struggle for a promotion and so feel hope, courage, fear — irascible passions. Anger's two objects (the vengeance sought, the person on whom it is sought) both have the character of arduousness. Aquinas: "Anger regards two objects: the vengeance that it seeks and the person on whom it seeks vengeance; and in respect of both, anger requires a certain arduousness. Therefore it is evident that anger is not in the concupiscible but in the irascible faculty."

[03:42] **Article 4: Whether anger requires an act of reason.** Yes — and the article is rich. On the one hand passions belong to the sensitive appetite, which is found in irrational animals; so one might think anger needs no reason. But anger requires not only apprehension of received evil but also the *comparison* of that evil with a proportionate punishment to be inflicted — and comparing-and-inferring is an act of reason. Aquinas: "Vengeance implies a comparison between the punishment to be inflicted and the hurt done. Now to compare and to draw an inference is an act of reason. Therefore anger in a fashion requires an act of reason." The dog growling at a threat reads, by natural instinct (the *estimative sense*), not only the threat but the vengeance to be inflicted if it continues to approach. In human beings, reason informs the *cogitative sense* (particular reason) — the internal sense informed by reason. Anger thus distinguishes the dog's instinct from our reasoned cogitative apprehension.

[05:33] **Article 5: Whether anger is more natural than desire.** *Natural* here is what results from nature — but human beings have a rational nature *and* an individual temperament (a kind of second nature). According to the rational nature, anger is more rational-leaning than desire (per a.4). And as to second nature: it is natural to resist obstacles, and if a particular man has a short fuse, anger springs forth more naturally than desire. So in two ways anger can be more natural than desire.

[06:41] **Article 6: Whether anger is more grievous than hatred.** Hatred is more grievous. The angry man wishes evil to the one with whom he is angry *under the aspect of justice* — not as evil simply. The hater wishes evil to his enemy *as evil*. Aquinas: "For the one who hates wishes evil to his enemy as evil; whereas the angry man wishes evil to him with whom he is angry not as evil, but in so far as it has an aspect of good — that is, in so far as he reckons it as just, since it is a means of vengeance. Hatred implies application of evil to evil, whereas anger denotes application of good to evil. Now it is evident that to seek evil under the aspect of justice is a lesser evil than simply to seek evil to someone."

[07:51] **Article 7: Whether anger is only towards those to whom one has an obligation of justice.** Yes — anger requires the structure of just/unjust relation. Parents become incensed at children because children owe parents obedience, gratitude, respect. We get angry at irrational animals (especially our pets) because they too in a sense ought to be subordinate to us. We can be angry with ourselves when we commit injustice to ourselves by sinning. Aquinas: "Anger desires evil as being a means of just vengeance. Consequently anger is towards those to whom we are just or unjust, since vengeance is an act of justice and wrongdoing is an act of injustice. Therefore both on the part of the cause (the harm done by another) and on the part of the vengeance sought, it is evident that anger concerns those to whom one is just or unjust."

[09:29] **Article 8: Whether the species of anger are suitably assigned.** Citing St. John Damascene, Aquinas gives three. (1) *Wrath* — anger increased by the facility/ease with which the passion is elicited. (2) *Ill will* — anger increased by the endurance of grief over the sorrow received and sought to be repaid. (3) *Rancor* — anger increased by the agent's fixation on the goal of vengeance, refusing to rest until satisfied.

[10:03] **Question 47 — The cause that provokes anger. Article 1: Whether the motive of anger is always something done against the one who is angry.** Mostly yes, but with extension. Anger requires offense against the one angered. *However*, we take our friends as *alter suus* (another self); their sorrows and slights become ours. We can be angry with those who despise things we love — love itself is a kind of union. Aquinas: "When we take a very great interest in a thing, we look upon it as our own good; so that if anyone despise it, it seems as though we ourselves were despised and injured."

[11:18] **Article 2: Whether the sole motive of anger is slight or contempt.** Yes — all motives of anger reduce to slight. Aquinas: "Whatever injury is inflicted on us, in so far as it is derogatory to our excellence, seems to savor of a slight; since nothing elicits anger in a man save that which is seen as an injustice to us — as not giving us what is our due." **Practical takeaway**: the greater one is in one's own eyes, the more readily one perceives others' treatment as derogatory and gets angry. This is why the Lord's burden is light: it is the burden of the humble. We are all sinners deserving hell, and so any treatment short of hell should be seen as a lesser evil than we deserve. (Even irrational animals: "Although a dumb animal does not seek honor as such, yet it naturally seeks a certain superiority and is angry with anything derogatory thereto.")

[12:55] **Article 3: Whether a man's excellence is the cause of his being angry.** Yes — and Aquinas separates it from a.2 for emphasis. "The more excellent a man is, the more unjust is a slight offered to him in the matter in which he excels; consequently those who excel in any matter are most of all angry if they be slighted in that matter — for instance a wealthy man in his riches, or an orator in his eloquence, and so forth." Not only is the great-in-own-eyes man more susceptible to anger, he is *angriest in the very matters in which he sees himself as excellent*.

[13:56] **Article 4: Whether a person's defect is a reason for being more easily angry with him.** Yes. Contempt — more than anything else — provokes anger; and deficiency or littleness in the person with whom we are angry tends to increase our anger because it adds to the unmerited aspect of being despised. The king berated by the peasant is more provoked than the king berated by his mother.

[14:58] That closes Q.46 and Q.47.

## Notes

- **Q.46 a.4 (anger requires an act of reason)** is the keystone for the synthesis. Anger involves the *comparison* of received evil and proportionate punishment — an inferential act. This is exactly what Friston's predictive-processing account requires: anger is a model-based passion, not a model-free one. Distinct from rage as undifferentiated arousal, anger is structurally cognitive.
- **Q.46 a.6 (anger vs. hatred)** is crucial. The angry man wishes evil *as means of justice*; the hater wishes evil *as evil*. This is the structural distinction between corrective and destructive aggression. The synthesis must hold this: anger has a moral structure (justice-oriented) that hatred does not.
- **Q.47 a.2-3 (anger reduces to slight; excellence amplifies it)** is the day's psychological dynamite. Aquinas's claim — that the greatness of one's self-estimation is the proportionate driver of anger's intensity — is *precisely* the modern social psychology of narcissistic rage. Twenge's narcissism epidemic literature, Baumeister on threatened-ego aggression, Bushman's narcissism-aggression correlations — all in the empirical literature. "Prideful anger" (the episode title) is exactly the contemporary clinical pattern.
- **Q.47 a.4 (defect in the offender amplifies anger)**: the king-and-peasant case. Asymmetric status amplifies the offense. Modern parallels in workplace and family literatures on perceived insubordination.
- **Q.47 a.1 (alter suus — friend as another self)**: the empathic-anger structure. Anger on behalf of those we love is well-documented (e.g., parental anger on behalf of injured children; communal anger over injustice). The synthesis can map this onto positivity-resonance / co-regulation findings — emotional contagion runs in both directions.
- **Q.46 a.8 (wrath, ill will, rancor)**: three species, three temporal/processural profiles. *Wrath* = high-precision fast trigger (low irritability threshold). *Ill will* = enduring grief seeking outlet. *Rancor* = goal-fixation refusing satisfaction. The synthesis can map these onto specific clinical phenotypes (irritable mood, depressive-aggressive comorbidity, obsessive-grudge formation).
- **Q.46 a.4 (estimative vs. cogitative sense)** is the medieval anticipation of the predictive/deliberative distinction. The estimative sense (dog's instinct) is fast-affective; the cogitative sense (particular reason) is reason-informed appraisal. This is structurally tomorrow's Q.49 (habits) — both are about *how* the appetitive system is *informed* by accumulated experience and rational endorsement.
