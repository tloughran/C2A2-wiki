### Q.77 — The Powers of the Soul in General

**Article 1 — Whether the essence of the soul is its power**

In response to this article,

[00:04:50] consider the following hypothetical scenario. Let's say you're in China and you're staring at a Chinese billboard on the street. In order for you to be able to understand it — the writing on it — you're going to need a couple of things. First, you're going to need to exist; that's what your soul is doing — informing your body, causing you (the body-soul composite) to exist. Secondly, you're going to have to be the kind of thing that has [00:05:17] the ability to understand just generally speaking — that ability is what we call the *power* or *faculty* of the intellect. Thirdly, you're going to have to know Chinese — or have on you or in you the *habitual knowledge* of what those scribbled figures mean, what those figures are meant to signify. And lastly, you're going to have to not just be staring at that [00:05:44] billboard but actually trying to read or understand the scribbled figures on it.

And we could say much more, but at the very least in this process of understanding what the Chinese sign means we can distinguish a couple of things: first, the soul causing you to exist; second, the power of the intellect in the soul allowing you to understand in general; third, the habitual knowledge of Chinese in the mind [00:06:11] allowing you to understand Chinese specifically; and then fourth, the actual *act* of understanding and trying to read the Chinese.

Now the present article is asking whether the first and the second of that list are identical, and the answer is no. And Aquinas gives us two reasons why. The first is that if your essence was identical to your powers (your capabilities), you would be God — because only in God, in which there is no division or potential, is his power [00:06:38] identical to his essence, identical to his existence. Secondly, if your faculties were identical to your essence, then at every moment you were actually existing you would also have to be actually thinking, willing, imagining, seeing, touching, etc. — all of the things you have the power of doing — which is clearly not our experience of ourselves. Therefore there must be some distinction in you between what you can do (your faculties [00:07:05] or powers) and your act of existing at any moment as a human being.

**Article 2 — Whether there are several powers of the soul**

If what you were able to do (your powers or faculties) is distinct from your mere act of existing (the essence of your soul), then the next question is: what are those faculties? Are there multiple faculties in you, or just one?

Aquinas writes: *Of necessity we must place several powers in the soul*. He then [00:07:32] explains: first, that since everything attains its perfection through its operation — the plant through growth, the animal through sense, and us through the perfect contemplation of God in the beatific vision — and since *the better the thing, the fewer the actions necessary for it to reach its perfection* (he probably has the angels in mind here), but since we are at the bottom of intellectual things with a supernatural [00:07:58] end, we will need multiple powers and acts to reach our goal.

He also says: another reason why the human soul abounds in a variety of powers is because it is on the confines — on the edge — of spiritual and corporeal creatures, and therefore the powers of both meet together in the soul.

**Article 3 — Whether the powers are distinguished by their acts and objects**

Incredibly

[00:08:25] important article here, because the faculties of the soul are not distinguished by being in a different place or being of a different kind of stuff, since their principle is all in the one soul which is informing your body — unless one means that some of these powers are *in a place* meaning they are in a corporeal organ, meaning they are operating through that organ, like the power of sight through the eye. But then [00:08:51] what about the faculties of intellect and will? How are they distinguished, because not only do they not take up space (being thoroughly immaterial) but also do not operate through a bodily organ? And if these faculties are distinct — which we know just from our own experience of ourselves they are — then what makes them distinct?

Aquinas says: *powers are distinguished by their acts and objects*. Acts like seeing; objects [00:09:19] like color. For example, in the act of seeing that object which is color, we realize that we have the power of sight. Now that's an easy example; others can be more difficult. Another example: in the act of getting the essence of things, we realize that we have the power of understanding as well. And then based upon these differing acts and objects of [00:09:46] these faculties, we come to understand the difference between them — between the power of understanding and the power of sight. And that's pretty straightforward, God willing.

**Article 4 — Whether among the powers of the soul there is order**

Of the kinds of orders St. Thomas gives in this article, the one to zoom in on — which is just as relevant today as it was in Aquinas's day — is what he calls the *order of superiority*: namely that the powers of sense, or the faculties to take [00:10:14] in nutrition or to move ourselves, these are all for the sake of our higher faculties — that is, the intellect and will. St. Thomas writes:

> Perfect things are by their nature prior to imperfect things. Thus according to the first kind of order, among the powers, the intellectual powers are prior to the sensitive powers; wherefore they direct them and command them. Likewise the sensitive powers are prior in this order [00:10:40] to the powers of nutrition, generation, and growth.

**Article 5 — Whether all the powers of the soul are in the soul as their subject**

Where are these powers or faculties housed, is our question. Are they just in the soul? St. Thomas cites Aristotle, who says *sense belongs neither to the soul nor to the body but to the composite*. Then Aquinas takes that quote, [00:11:06] explains it, and responds to this article very clearly, so we will simply quote him. He says:

> The subject of power is of necessity the subject of operation. Now it is clear from what we have said above that some operations of the soul are performed without a corporeal organ, as is understanding and will. Hence the powers of these operations are [00:11:32] in the soul as their subject. But some operations of the soul are performed by means of corporeal organs, as sight by the eye, as hearing by the ear, and so it is with all the other operations of the nutritive and sensitive parts. Therefore the powers which are the principles of these operations have their subject in the composite and not in the soul only.

**Article 6 — Whether the powers of the soul flow from its essence**

St. Thomas says:

> It is clear that all the powers of the soul, whether their subject be the soul alone or the composite, flow from the essence of the soul as from their principle.

Aquinas will then explain that the soul makes the body to exist as the human body, and so the faculties of the body as well as inequalities or powers [00:12:27] to be found solely in the soul itself must flow from or find their origin in the soul's being — the soul giving existence to the body and existing subsistent. Therefore yes, the powers of man flow from the essence of the soul.

**Article 7 — Whether one power of the soul arises from another**

St. Thomas says:

> Powers are known by their acts. But the [00:12:52] act of one power is caused by the act of another power, as the act of the imagination by the act of the senses. Therefore one power of the soul is caused by another.

As an example: we can't imagine the brown dog unless we have first seen that brown dog; and we cannot know what *a dog is* unless we have seen multiple of them and from there [00:13:19] abstracted within the mind the underlying essence among them. So yes, in this way one power of the soul (let's say sense) gives rise to another (imagination), by allowing the other power to carry out its proper action.

**Article 8 — Whether all the powers remain in the soul when separated from the body**

St. Thomas gives us a tidy brief response. He says:

> As we [00:13:45] have said already, all the powers of the soul belong to the soul alone as their principle. But some powers belong to the soul alone as their subject — namely the intelligence and the will. These powers must remain in the soul after the destruction of the body. But other powers are subjected in the composite, such as the powers of the sensitive and nutritive parts. So it is false that, as [00:14:11] some say, these powers remain in the soul even after the corruption of the body; it is much more false that, as they say also, the acts of these powers remain in the separated soul, because these powers have no act apart from the corporeal organ.

### Q.78 — The Specific Powers of the Soul

And with that we move on to the next question — question 78, *the specific powers of the soul*.

**Article 1 — Whether there are five categories of powers within the soul**

[00:14:37] Now keep in mind as we go through this article that these powers are distinct by object and act, but they are united in or within that one thing which is making you one thing — which is the soul. The faculties of the plant and the dog: we also have those faculties, but without dividing us into multiple kinds of things, but as within [00:15:03] the one thing that we are — the one rational soul, informing the body. And so in this question we'll look at each of these categories of faculties.

St. Thomas begins this first article by quoting Aristotle, who wrote: *the powers are the vegetative, the sensitive, the appetitive, the locomotive, and the intellectual*. And I would definitely recommend reading the text of this article because of how rich it is. But in [00:15:28] brief: there are three kinds of living material things out there, very broadly speaking. The first is the plant — its powers do not extend far beyond its material; it can grow, take in nutrition, reproduce, and move itself. The second kind of thing is the animal, with the power of sense; its power reaches out to sense anything sensible — it can see, smell, [00:15:55] hear, etc. It has interior senses as well, which we're going to see in a later article as applied to us and in many ways shared with animals. The third is the rational animal — we have all the powers below us just named, but our faculties reach out not just to particular sensed things but even to the notion of *being*, of *goodness*. Our intellect reaches out to the nature of things, the world behind the world, and [00:16:22] we are looking for not just particular goods but we are looking for *goodness* — which is God himself.

We call the powers of the plant *the vegetative powers*, the powers of the animal *the sensitive powers*, and the powers unique to us *the intellectual powers*. Then the faculties common to each of these categories are those other terms from Aristotle: *locomotion* (the ability to move oneself) and *appetite* (the inclination which [00:16:49] anything has for something), which in sensual creatures and intellectual creatures follows upon our apprehension of those things either as sensed (or with us, as known).

**Article 2 — Whether the parts of the vegetative soul are fittingly described as the nutritive, augmentative, and generative**

And please don't panic on the use of the word *soul* here — again, what we mean by *soul* in the broadest sense is [00:17:14] just *the form of a thing that is living*. So: are the powers of the plant fittingly described as nutritive, augmentative, and generative? Yes — because that's what plants do: they take in nourishment, they grow, they create others like themselves. As Aristotle says, *the operations of this soul are generation, the use of food, and growth*.

**Article 3 — Whether the five exterior senses are properly distinguished**

Sight, sound, smell, taste, [00:17:41] touch — this is our shorthand list of the senses. Aquinas will then describe them according to the physics of his day (which I'm not necessarily saying invalidates his conclusions on how the senses work, most especially when it comes to the sense of sight, which has of late seen a renewal in academic writings and interest by neo-Thomistic philosophers). But for the scope of our podcast and for time's sake, we will continue on.

**Article 4 — Whether the interior senses are suitably distinguished**

What are the interior senses? This will probably strike the everyday listener as a novel term. Well, first of all: the exterior senses (sight, sound, taste, touch), they don't sense one another, and yet we do get the sense that the donut we see, taste, and touch is [00:18:34] the same donut. So then what is this sense pulling all these senses into one common sensual experience that we are having? And we can't say *the intellect*, because the mind knows the essence of things, general things — but this is a particular sensual experience.

Therefore this first interior sense — we can distinguish, and Aquinas names it — is [00:19:01] called *the common sense*, which is a sensible name based on what it senses. Then we have the power of *imagination* — we can think about a horse and a rhino and combine those images to think about a unicorn. We also have the power to remember particular images and events in the past, reminisce upon them — the power of *memory*. And we also have a kind of natural instinct not entirely dissimilar [00:19:26] to how the lamb just knows when it sees the wolf that it's bad for it, as well as the bird, in seeing straw, knowing that it would be advantageous for building a nest.

And so Aquinas concludes:

> There is no need to assign more than four interior powers of the sensitive part — namely the common sense, the imagination, and the estimative (that would be like our instinct-sense), and the [00:19:54] memorative powers.

And tomorrow we'll go over the intellectual powers. But that brings us to the end of this episode. We have just covered questions 77 and 78 of the Prima Pars — that is, the powers of the soul in general and the specific powers of the soul.
