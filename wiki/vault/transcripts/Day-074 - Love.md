### Q.26 — Of love itself

[00:00:04] Hello, my name is Austin Habash, the founder of Think Catholic, and you're listening to the Summa podcast where we study St. Thomas Aquinas's *Summa Theologiae* in a way simple and insightful for anyone to understand. The Summa podcast is brought to you by Think Catholic. Taking two questions of the *Summa* a day, we'll seek to summarize St. Thomas's responses, discovering the brilliance of Aquinas and his Catholic faith. This is Day 74, so let's get started. We're covering Questions 26 and 27 of the Prima Secundae — that is, *of the passions of the soul in particular, and first of love*, and *of the cause of love*. The translation that I always use is the one generously provided to me by Ave Maria Press, the five-volume set translated by the Fathers of the English Dominican Province (code Summa10 for a discount).

**Article 1 — Whether love is in the concupiscible power**

[00:00:55] The answer is yes, as we described in the last two episodes — but we can say much more. First of all, in so far as everything tends toward some good, we can attribute the term *appetite* to that thing. To the stone we find a *natural appetite*, or tending toward the good of unity, oneness with other massive objects. In the dog we find a *sensitive appetite*, or an aptitude to the sensed good. And in us we have the *rational appetite* — the will — an inclination to the known good. In each of these appetites — whether natural, sensitive, or intellectual — we can attribute love, provided what we mean by love in each one of these appetites is, according to St. Thomas, the principal movement toward the end. As applied to the various kinds of appetites, St. Thomas continues: *"In the natural appetite, the principle of this movement is the appetitive subject's connaturalness with the thing to which it tends, and may be called natural love. The complacency of the sensitive appetite, or of the will, to some good — that is, its complacency in good — is called sensitive love, or intellectual or rational love."*

**Article 2 — Whether love is a passion**

[00:02:09] St. Thomas writes: *"Love consists in a change wrought in the appetite by the appetible object — it is evident that love is a passion."* Love is a passion in so far as it is an undergoing in the patient (that's us) caused by some agent, which in this case is the lovable object. When a person looks at a doughnut, and the doughnut, becoming present to one's sensitive appetite and being united to us "in intention" (the word Aquinas uses), elicits in us the passion of love — from which desire can follow, and finally joy if one is later actually united to the object of love (which, in this case, would be by eating the doughnut). In St. Thomas's words: *"Because the appetible object moves the appetite, introducing itself, as it were, into its intention, accordingly the first change wrought in the appetite by the appetible object is called love, and is nothing else than complacency in that object."*

**Article 3 — Whether love is the same as dilection**

[00:03:17] It seems often that these Latin words *amor* and *dilectio* — love and dilection — are used synonymously. But St. Thomas, taking the term *dilectio* as akin to *electio* (choice), takes *dilectio* to denote specifically a *chosen* love. Therefore he concludes that love is a broader notion than dilection, and that dilection denotes specifically a rationally willed kind of love within the larger notion of love.

**Article 4 — Whether love is properly divided into love of friendship and love of concupiscence**

[00:03:46] The division of these loves stems from the definition we get from Aristotle on love, which is *"to wish good to someone."* The wished good is loved with a love of concupiscence — for it is not loved for its own sake, but for the sake of the someone included in our definition. For example, one loves the doughnut not for the doughnut's sake, but that one might eat it; so they love the doughnut for their own sake, or for the sake of a friend enjoying it. The doughnut is loved with a love of concupiscence; but the someone for whom it is loved is loved with a love of friendship — whether that someone be oneself or another. In Aquinas's words: *"Man has love of concupiscence toward the good that he wishes to another, and love of friendship toward him to whom he wishes that good."*

### Q.27 — Of the cause of love

**Article 1 — Whether good is the only cause of love**

[00:04:50] Our passions, being a passive power of the soul, must be directed to some object — like the power of sight is directed to receiving color — and therefore must be directed to some good. Since good and being are convertible with one another (to use St. Thomas's words; we covered that in Episode 4), therefore we must admit that the sensitive appetite — which we term the passions — must be essentially directed and caused by some sensed good. The direction to the good simply considered, a kind of complacency in the good, is our definition of love. Therefore good is the cause of love.

[00:05:22] But is it the *only* cause of love? Do evils also draw our love — can they be the object of our love? Here St. Thomas reminds us that evil, in whatever arena it happens to be found — whether in things or in actions — is simply a privation of a good that ought to be there, a missingness in good things (that was the topic of Episode 25). Therefore it cannot be the cause of anything directly, for it is in itself non-being. But goods with certain privations in them — evil things — can be the cause of our love, but only *under the aspect of good*. As St. Thomas says: *"Evil is never loved except under the aspect of good — that is to say, in so far as it is good in some respect and is considered as being good simply. And thus a certain love is evil, in so far as it tends to that which is not simply a true good. It is in this way that man loves iniquity, inasmuch as by means of iniquity some good is gained — pleasure, for instance, or money, or such like."*

**Article 2 — Whether knowledge is the cause of love**

[00:06:44] We cannot love that which we do not first know. If I asked you whether you loved my cousin Matthew, the answer would be no — because probably until this moment you didn't know I had a cousin Matthew. The appetite — whether it be the intellectual appetite, which is the will, or the sensitive appetite, which are the passions — both must necessarily follow apprehension: in the former, intellectual understanding; in the latter, sensual apprehension. But in either case, apprehension comes before appetite. Even natural love — like the rock's tendency toward the ground and the most efficient means to that end — presupposes a kind of pseudo-knowledge. This is why things like the principle of least action in physics spook scientists (which we've spoken about in previous episodes) — how do unknowing things seemingly move knowingly? St. Thomas says: *"Natural love, which is in all things, is caused by a kind of knowledge — not indeed existing in natural things themselves, but in Him who created their nature."* So whether the love be natural, sensual, or intellectual, knowledge in things is a cause of their love.

**Article 3 — Whether likeness is a cause of love**

[00:07:47] *"Every creature loves its like, and every person his neighbor"* — as it is written in the book of Sirach 13:19. Because we tend naturally to the three Ps — perfection, preservation, and propagation — for those things which do propagate, we naturally wish ourselves goods (which was in part the definition of love from Aristotle, "to wish good to someone"). Therefore we naturally love ourselves; and so too we naturally love those things we take to be part of us — our particular nature as human, our particular size and shape, our particular ethnicity, our particular religion, etc. Since we naturally love those things about us, we can easily love those same things when they are found outside of us — which is what the verse from Sirach is indicating: we naturally love things that are similar to us.

[00:08:48] This is the first way in which likeness can be a cause of love. As St. Thomas says: *"For the very fact that two men are alike, having as it were one form, makes them to be in a manner one in that form. Thus two men are one thing in the species of humanity, and two white men are one thing in whiteness. Hence the affections of one tend to the other as being one with him, and he wishes good to him as to himself."*

[00:09:20] The second way in which likeness can be a cause of love is by inclination or tendency — that by tending toward particular objects, our faculties can be said to be proportioned or likened to that object, like sight to color. This relation or inclination can itself be a cause of love. That's why we naturally love to learn, we naturally love to see and to hear, and we even naturally love the virtuous man — because virtue is the perfection of our nature; and even if one may despise the virtuous man in other respects, because we ourselves are naturally inclined to perfection, we will love that perfection which the virtuous man embodies. As St. Thomas says: *"Not all men have these virtues in complete habit, yet they have them according to certain seminal principles — the reason whereof is that the man who is not virtuous loves the virtuous man as being in conformity with his own natural reason."*

**Article 4 — Whether any other passion of the soul is a cause of love**

[00:10:38] All the other emotions of the soul are caused by love, St. Thomas writes. What we love determines what we hate, what we desire, what we have an aversion to, what brings us joy, what makes us sad; it is the reason for our hopes, our fears, our courage, our despairs, our anger. As summarized very clearly and briefly by Aquinas: *"Every other passion of the soul implies either movement toward something or rest in something. Now every movement toward something or rest in something arises from some kinship or aptness to that thing — and in this does love consist. Therefore it is not possible for any other passion of the soul to be universally the cause of every love."*

[00:11:10] Which brings us to the close of another episode. We have just finished Questions 26 and 27 of the Prima Secundae — *of the passions of the soul in particular, and first of love*, and *of the cause of love*. My name is Austin Habash with Think Catholic, and I cannot wait to see you tomorrow.

## Notes
- Q.26 a.1: love is the principal movement toward the end at every scale of appetite — natural (stone-to-mass), sensitive (dog-to-sensed-good), intellectual (will-to-known-good). This is the explicit Aquinas form of agency-at-scale; reads almost as if dictated for Levin.
- Q.26 a.4 — *amor amicitiae* (love of friendship: willing the good of the other) vs. *amor concupiscentiae* (love of desire: willing the good for the sake of the lover). The central-theme "loving unity is the computational telos" maps directly to *amor amicitiae* at scale.
- Q.27 a.2 ("knowledge is the cause of love") is Friston territory: appetite presupposes apprehension; predictive coding and active inference give the formal shape. Habash himself flags the principle of least action — that's the explicit science cross-over he names.
- Q.27 a.3 (likeness causes love) is the moral-psychology twin of formal similarity/resonance — Fredrickson's positivity resonance is one contemporary articulation; the medieval "two men are one in the form of humanity" prefigures the modern claim that shared model-structure grounds interpersonal recognition.
- **Central-theme load-bearing day**: Q.26 a.4's *amor amicitiae* and Q.27 a.4's "every passion arises from love" together state — in Aquinas's idiom — exactly the claim that loving unity is the computational telos toward which limited agents move. Q.28 (effects: union, indwelling, ecstasy) is reserved for the next day and will sharpen this.
- Bridge candidates: Levin (PRS chain on agency-at-scale; the natural-appetite/sensitive-appetite/rational-appetite triad), Friston (knowledge-causes-love as active-inference precursor), Fredrickson (positivity resonance and likeness), Rohr (charity as cosmic belonging — *Universal Christ*).
