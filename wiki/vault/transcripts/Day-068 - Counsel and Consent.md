# Day 68 — Counsel and Consent

> Episode: <https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sGdzNVURWDM>

Today's episode covers Prima Secundae Q.14 (counsel, which precedes choice — 6 articles) and Q.15 (consent, an act of the will with regard to the means — 4 articles). The "ballroom dance" between intellect and will continues: counsel is the intellect's *investigation* of means; consent is the will's *approval* of those means.

## Q.14 — Counsel

**a.1 — Whether counsel is an inquiry.** Yes — counsel is a kind of investigation into the most suitable means to some end. *There is much uncertainty in things that have to be done because actions are concerned with contingent singulars; now in things doubtful and uncertain the reason does not pronounce judgment without previous inquiry; wherefore the reason must of necessity institute an inquiry before deciding on the objects of choice, and this inquiry is called counsel.* The dance between intellect and will: choice is strictly an act of the will following intellect's consideration; counsel is strictly an act of intellect motivated by what is desired by the will.

**a.2 — Whether counsel is of the end or only of the means.** Of the means. *That which is looked upon as an end is already fixed; consequently as long as there is any doubt about it, it is not looked upon as an end; wherefore if counsel is taken about it, it will be counsel not about the end but about the means.*

**a.3 — Whether counsel is only of things that we do.** Yes — primarily of action and inquiry into what ought to be done, mind bringing universal truths and specific circumstances into consideration. We seek counsel about others' actions in so far as they are one with us — *either by union of affection (as a man is solicitous about what concerns his friend as though it concerned himself) or after the manner of an instrument for the principal agent (the master takes counsel about what he would do through his servant).*

**a.4 — Whether counsel is about all the things that we do.** No — not about fixed or insignificant things. *A writer does not take counsel how to form his letters... for this is determined.* Reason looks upon small things as mere nothings.

**a.5 — Whether the process of counsel is one of analysis.** Yes. *To proceed from cause to effect is to proceed synthetically; but if that which proceeds in knowledge is later in the order of being, the process is one of analysis, as when our judgment deals with effects which by analysis we trace to their simple causes.* Counsel takes the intended end as principle and works backward from it. *It begins, that is to say, from that which is intended in the future, and continues until it arrives at that which is to be done at once.*

**a.6 — Whether the process of counsel is indefinite.** No. From either end of the process — the end-goal or the reasoner — counsel is bounded. The chandelier needs a definite ceiling; the chain of reasoning needs a definite terminus. *Just as a chandelier cannot be held off the ground by an endless series of chain lengths above it without some definite singular ultimate ceiling, so too counsel cannot even begin without a very definite singular end-goal.*

## Q.15 — Consent

**a.1 — Whether consent is an act of the appetitive or of the apprehensive power.** Of the appetitive — the will. *Consentire — to consent — is to feel with, literally; and this implies a certain union to the object of consent. Hence the will, to which it belongs to tend to the thing itself, is more properly said to consent.* Important distinction from choice: *choice includes something that consent has not, namely a certain relation to something to which something else is preferred. And therefore after consent there still remains a choice.* By counsel several means may be found conducive to the end and through approval consent given to each — but after approving many we give preference to one by *choosing* it.

**a.2 — Whether consent is to be found in irrational animals.** No. They lack freedom of the will and possession of themselves. *The irrational animal is not properly said to consent; this is proper to the rational nature, which has the command of the appetitive movement, and is able to apply or not to apply it to this or to that thing.*

**a.3 — Whether consent is directed to the end or to the means.** To the means. *First there is the apprehension of the end, then the desire of the end, then the counsel about the means, then the desire of the means.* Since counsel is only about the means, consent — coming after counsel — is properly speaking of nothing else but the means.

**a.4 — Whether the act of consent belongs only to the higher part of the soul.** Yes. Moral blameworthiness or praise has its root in the will; consent must take its root in that part of the soul which allows for culpability — the intellectual faculties. Augustine: *it is impossible for man to make up his mind to commit a sin unless that mental faculty which has the sovereign power of urging his members to act or restraining them from act yields to the evil deed and becomes its slave.*

## Notes

- The pipeline now reads end-side: apprehension of end → volition → intention → enjoyment; means-side: counsel → consent → choice → use. Counsel (Q.14) and consent (Q.15) are the *deliberation pair*: intellect investigates, will approves.
- Counsel as *analytic* reasoning (Q.14 a.5) — working backward from the intended end through the means — is precisely the pattern of model-based planning in active inference and reinforcement learning. Friston's expected-free-energy framework formalizes this.
- The "definite ceiling" argument against indefinite counsel (Q.14 a.6) is the bounded-rationality argument: real agents reason within finite resource budgets. This is the central-theme link — *limited resources* are constitutive of perspectival agency.
- The consent–choice distinction (Q.15 a.1) is subtle but important: consent approves *each viable option*; choice picks *one* by preference. This maps onto the multi-arm-bandit structure of decision-making — entertaining multiple feasible policies before committing.
- Friendship-and-instrumentality account of counsel-about-others' actions (Q.14 a.3) is a beautiful early articulation of *extended cognition*: deliberation properly extends across the affective and instrumental boundaries of self.
- Both counsel and consent require freedom — and freedom requires the partial-good situation. Animals don't deliberate because their goods are determinate.
