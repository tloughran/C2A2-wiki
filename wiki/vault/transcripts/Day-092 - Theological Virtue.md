# Day 92 — Theological Virtue

*Summa Theologiae*, Prima Secundae, Q.62 (Of the theological virtues) and Q.63 (Of the cause of virtues).

## Transcript

[00:05] Day 92. Today we cover Q.62 and Q.63 of the Prima Secundae: *of the theological virtues* and *of the cause of virtues*.

[00:54] **Article 1: Whether there are any theological virtues.** Because God has destined us for a supernatural end — himself, via the beatific vision — in order to direct ourselves toward an end which surpasses both our natural apprehensive and appetitive faculties, we need supernatural dispositions in the mind and will. As St. Thomas says, "Such-like principles are called theological virtues: first, because their object is God, in as much as they direct us aright to God; secondly, because they are infused in us by God alone; thirdly, because these virtues are not made known to us save by divine revelation contained in Holy Writ." The answer is in the affirmative: there are theological virtues.

[01:50] **Article 2: Whether the theological virtues are distinct from the intellectual and moral virtues.** Habits are distinct from one another by their formal object. As sight is directed toward someone in front of us specifically as formally colored, and hearing toward that same person as making a sound, in a similar way our natural habitual knowledge and love of God must be formally distinct from that supernatural knowledge and love of God — because those habits are directed to formally different objects. The knowledge of God as specifically triune, or the love of him as specifically our beatific end, requires habits formally distinct from natural virtues.

[03:00] **Article 3: Whether faith, hope, and charity are fittingly reckoned as theological virtues.** Beginning with scripture: 1 Corinthians 13 — "So faith, hope, love abide, these three." These three are what we term the three theological virtues, and they dispose our intellect and will toward a supernatural end — the triune God via the beatific vision — analogous to how our intellect and will are already disposed to a natural end. Just as naturally we are able to come to the knowledge of first principles in speculative matters (the principle of non-contradiction, the principle of sufficient reason) and practical matters (the good ought to be done and evil avoided), with our will's natural inclination to that proposed to it by reason in pursuit of natural happiness — so too, in order to pursue supernatural happiness we need certain principles in the mind and a certain inclination of the will. The principles in the mind are the articles of faith, given to us by some messenger ("since faith comes through what is heard") and held by a divine light. For the will to be disposed to a supernatural end proposed to it by the mind, it requires a supernatural disposition called charity. Lastly we have hope, of which Aquinas writes: "The will is directed to this end both as to that end as something attainable — and this pertains to hope — and as to a spiritual union, whereby the will is, so to speak, transformed into that end — and this belongs to charity."

[05:09] **Article 4: Whether faith precedes hope, and hope charity.** We cannot love that which we do not first know, therefore faith must come before charity and hope. The more intricate question is whether love precedes hope or hope precedes love. Aquinas: "Hope regards two things. One, as its principal object, the good hoped for; with regard to this, love always precedes hope, for good is never hoped for unless it be desired and loved. Hope also regards the person from whom a man hopes to be able to obtain some good; with regard to this, hope precedes love." In the order of perfection, however, charity ranks first before faith and hope, because both faith and hope are quickened by charity and receive from charity their full complement as virtues.

[06:16] **Question 63: Of the cause of virtues. Article 1: Whether virtue is in us by nature.** Since virtue is manifestly not in all men and women, it cannot be said to simply flow from our nature. However, all of us do have a certain aptitude to virtue: "In so far as in man's reason are to be found instilled by nature certain naturally known principles of both knowledge and action, which are the nurseries of intellectual and moral virtues, and in so far as there is in the will a natural appetite for good in accordance with reason." Moreover, to the extent one person is more disposed by their particular body, their particular temperament, to a certain intellectual or moral virtue, they can be said to be inclined to some virtue by nature.

[07:09] **Article 2: Whether any virtue is caused in us by habituation.** Because vices come about through bad action repeated, we would expect that virtues also could be caused by habitual good action — and that is why we see a kind of natural temperance and fortitude in those who practice such acts (and their opposite vices in those given to actions in accord with those vices). However, when it comes to the infused virtues, only God can give them and only God can increase them. Though we can dispose ourselves better or worse to our reception of these increases in faith, hope, and charity, it must come from God.

[07:54] **Article 3: Whether any moral virtues are in us by infusion.** The question is whether along with the theological virtues, God also infuses into us a supernatural kind of prudence, justice, temperance, and fortitude. This must be the case, because just as we require certain moral and intellectual virtues to dispose us to our natural end, it is necessary that we acquire certain moral and intellectual virtues to dispose us to our supernatural end. The theological virtues by themselves are not sufficient — just because we have a new supernatural habitual love, knowledge, and hope of God does not necessitate that we will rightly judge and will the means to that end. To correctly dispose our intellect and will in regard to the more proximate ends and means as directed to our final end, we require certain infused virtues as well.

[08:55] **Article 4: Whether virtue by habituation belongs to the same species as infused virtue.** Are the acquired virtues the same in kind as the infused virtues? They cannot be — not only because they have a distinct origin (one stemming from our nature, the other infused by God), but also because they dispose us to a different mean or rule of reason, as aiming at distinctively different ends. To be a virtuous philosopher is not the same as being a Christian saint. The end and the means between these two are different. From St. Thomas: "In the consumption of food, the mean fixed by human reason is that food should not harm the health of the body nor hinder the use of reason; whereas, according to the divine rule, it behooves man to chastise his body and bring it into subjection — 1 Corinthians 9:27 — by abstinence in food, drink, and the like. It is therefore evident that infused and acquired temperance differ in species, and the same applies to the other virtues."

[10:20] St. John Vianney, the patron saint of parish priests, would eat — for at least the span of ten years according to Trochu in his biography *The Curé of Ars* — two potatoes a day, remarking that to eat a third potato would be solely for the pleasure of eating. Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle would have looked at that as contrary to reason and therefore not virtuous; but as looked at by Jesus Christ, who came into this world to die for it, St. John Vianney is a saint. Let us pray that God may make us saints as well.

## Notes

- Q.62 carves out a **categorically distinct** species of virtue keyed to a supernatural object. The formal-object individuation move parrots Q.60 but raises the stakes: faith/hope/charity are NOT continuous with natural virtue, even in degree.
- Q.62.4 on the priority order — knowledge precedes love (faith → hope/charity by intellect) but charity is **first in perfection** — is the classic Thomist solution to a perennial mystical-tradition tension.
- Q.63's two-source theory (habituation + infusion) sets up the entire moral-theology architecture: acquired virtues form by practice, infused virtues are gratuitous gifts. This is the seam where contemporary skill-acquisition / predictive-processing accounts meet a "grace" claim that wants a different metaphysical engine.
- Q.63.4's "two potatoes a day" anecdote — and the Aristotle-vs-Christ contrast — is doing serious work: it asserts that the *teloi* differ enough that what is reasonable under one is unreasonable under the other. A real fork in the moral-realist road.
- For contemporary parallel: the infused/acquired distinction maps onto questions about whether some moral capacities are scaffolded from outside the agent's own optimization loop (cultural/sacramental transmission, social learning under high-prior priors, possibly grace-as-prior).
