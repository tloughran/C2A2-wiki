# Day 98 — Sinful Causes

Day 98 covers I-II Q.74 (*the subject of sin* — which faculty/power is the seat of sin) and Q.75 (*the causes of sin in general*). Q.74 walks down the powers: the will (a.1, primary subject because sin must be voluntary), other powers under the will (a.2), sensuality (a.3, can host sin; a.4, cannot host *mortal* sin), reason itself (a.5-10, including the technical questions of *morose delectation*, *consent to delectation*, and the distinction between higher and lower reason in venial vs. mortal sin). Q.75 then asks about causes: whether sin has a cause at all (given evil is privation — a.1), an internal cause (a.2, reason/will proximate, imagination/sensitive-appetite remote), an external cause (a.3, can influence but cannot compel), and whether one sin causes another (a.4, in all four causal modes).

## Q.74 — Subject of sin

[00:30] Day 98. Today: Q.74 and Q.75 of the Prima Secundae — the subject of sin and the causes of sin in general.

[00:54] **Article 1 — whether the will can be the subject of sin.** Yes. For sin to be sin, it must be voluntary. "The proper subject of sin must needs be the power which is the principle of the act. Now since it is proper to moral acts that they are voluntary, it follows that the will... is the principle of sins."

[01:32] **Article 2 — whether the will alone is the subject of sin.** No — the other powers can be subjects of sin in so far as they are commanded by or flow from the will. "We do not sin except by the will as first mover, but we sin by the other powers as moved by the will."

[02:04] **Article 3 — whether there can be sin in the sensuality.** Yes. The sensitive appetite — passions shared with non-rational animals — *can* be moved by the will, so it can be inordinate, so sin can reach it. "Sin may be found in any power whose act can be voluntary and inordinate."

[02:54] **Article 4 — whether mortal sin can be in the sensuality.** No. *Recap of the mortal/venial distinction*: mortal sin is one in which the *intended last end* is disordered (idolatry, apostasy — perversions of the true end, God); venial sin is one in which the ultimate end is right but the proximate ends are disordered (correcting a brother out of right motive but in unjust anger). Sensuality cannot order anything to the last end — only reason can. Hence mortal sin cannot reside in sensuality, only in reason. Sensuality can *incline* the agent toward what is mortally sinful, but the formal locus of the mortality is in reason.

[04:30] **Article 5 — whether sin can be in the reason.** Yes — in two ways. First, when reason errs in the knowledge of truth, where the error is culpable (an ignorance about what it ought to have known). Second, when reason commands the inordinate movements of the lower powers or deliberately fails to check them.

[05:05] **Article 6 — whether the sin of morose delectation is in the reason.** *Morose delectation* — Augustine's phrase — is "deliberately holding and turning over what should have been cast aside as soon as it touched the mind." The spark of a feeling (lust, wrath) need not be sinful; but holding the eliciting thought voluntarily to take pleasure in it is. Yes, this is a sin of the reason.

[05:58] **Article 7 — whether the sin of consent to the act is in the higher reason.** Consent is an act of the will following deliberation by the intellect. "Consent may be ascribed both to the will and to the reason."

[06:37] **Article 8 — whether consent to delectation is a mortal sin.** Yes when the act delighted in is itself mortal. Matthew 5: "everyone who looks at a woman lustfully has already committed adultery with her in his heart." Dismissing the thought is no sin; entertaining it for didactic/protective reasons is no sin; deliberately consenting to the delight is tantamount to consenting to the act.

[10:00] **Article 9 — whether there can be venial sin in the higher reason as directing the lower powers.** Yes, when higher reason consents to a disordered means without turning away from God as last end (eating a little too much, speaking a little too much).

[08:42] **Article 10 — whether venial sin can be in the higher reason as such.** Yes — a sudden movement of unbelief in an article of faith that arises before the eternal law is consulted, and is then immediately rejected, is venial. If the movement *remains after deliberation*, it becomes mortal.

## Q.75 — Causes of sin in general

[10:00] **Article 1 — whether sin has a cause.** Yes — even though evil itself is privation (Q.18 / Day 25), there must be a *reason* for the absence of right order. "The will lacking the direction of the rule of reason and of the Divine Law, and intent on some mutable good, causes the act of sin directly and the inordinate of the act indirectly and beside the intention; for the lack of order in the act results from the lack of direction in the will." The act has a positive cause; the disorder is a privation traceable to the will's failure to direct.

[11:31] **Article 2 — whether sin has an internal cause.** Yes — *two* internal causes: *proximate* on the part of reason and will, *remote* on the part of imagination and sensitive appetite. Knowledge from senses (external) and from imagination (interior), plus passions arising from sensitive apprehension, feed in. Reason and will are the ultimate seat of culpability.

[12:14] **Article 3 — whether sin has an external cause.** Only God moves the will directly as first cause. External things — teachers, role models, environmental cues (a donut in the room) — *influence* but do not coerce. The will remains the sufficient and direct cause of *culpable* sin.

[13:02] **Article 4 — whether one sin is a cause of another.** Yes, in all four causal modes. *Efficient*: directly (acts cause dispositions, dispositions incline to like acts) and indirectly (one sin removes a protective state — grace, charity, shame — and that loss exposes the agent to the next sin). *Material*: covetousness prepares the matter of strife. *Formal* and *final*: one sin can be committed *for the sake of* another (simony for ambition; fornication for theft), in which case the latter gives the form, the former is the matter. Aquinas wants us to dwell especially on the *indirect efficient* mode — when one mortal sin removes the supernatural habit of charity, the protection against further sin collapses with it.

[15:31] That's Q.74 and Q.75 — the subject and causes of sin.

## Notes

- **The reason-as-seat-of-mortality move** (Q.74 a.4) is the keystone of the question's anti-eliminative-materialist content. Sensuality can host plenty of disordered movement — but *mortality*, the thing that severs the agent from its last end, can only happen in the faculty that *represents* the last end. Active-inference frame: only the highest-precision generative model (the one that defines the agent's preferred long-run state) can mis-orient the system in a way that re-targets the whole policy stack.
- **Morose delectation** (a.6) is the explicit Thomistic flagging of *attention-as-action*. Whether a fleeting thought becomes sin depends on whether the agent *holds* it — i.e., on the precision-weighting policy applied to involuntary content. Hawkins-style HTM and Friston-style precision-control both give a clean operationalization: holding = continuing to allocate the inferential resources that turn the trace into a deepening prior.
- **The privation-with-positive-cause structure** (Q.75 a.1) — the act has a positive cause, the *disorder of the act* is a privation that traces back to the will's *lack* of direction — is one of the most underappreciated medieval moves. It anticipates a structure that shows up everywhere in current control-theoretic work: a system can have a complete causal account of *what it did*, while the *misalignment with its goal* must be explained by the *absence* of a regulating signal. Sin is the unregulated-policy case.
- **One sin causes another via four modes** (Q.75 a.4), especially the *indirect efficient* mode (losing grace/charity removes the very state that would have prevented the next sin), maps cleanly to feedback-coupled systems: one perturbation can destabilize the regulator that normally suppresses the next perturbation. The Stump-tradition account of charity-as-relational-state makes this even sharper: lose the relation, lose the *organizing field*.
- The note on external influence (Q.75 a.3) — environmental cues *influence* but do not *compel* — is a precise place to mark the difference between predictive-coding-as-fate and predictive-coding-as-policy. The donut in the room shifts priors; the will sets the action.
