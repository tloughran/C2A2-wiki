### Q.1 — The nature and extent of sacred doctrine

[00:00:47] Question one, the nature and extent of sacred doctrine. Since Aquinas's work here is titled the *Summa Theologiae* — the summary of theology — it's fitting that he begins with its definition. What is theology? Is it necessary? What is it about? Is the information from it reliable? And how is it to be understood specifically when we turn to interpreting Scripture? These are our divisions within the chapter.

[00:01:17] Beginning with the first: Aquinas asks, *is sacred doctrine — knowledge inspired and given by God — necessary for man?* He says yes: "It was necessary for man's salvation." Revelation is necessary for man because the goal of human life is just not intuitive. Some people think life is about pleasure. Others, that it's about being successful or being comfortable. And even if someone of their own investigation could come to the realization that their perfection consisted in the most perfect use of our most perfect faculty — namely the mind, via contemplation of God — even then we would not know in which way we ought to carry it out: indirectly (contemplating God through the natural world) or directly (by God's gift, seeing him as he is in himself), or any number of possibilities between those two points. Because it could be any of them, it's up to the divine choice — which means he will have to reveal it to us. So yes, it is necessary.

[00:02:34] *Is it a science?* — in the sense of a body of knowledge that proceeds from legitimate principles to knowledge reasoned and certain. Mathematics is a science: we discover its principles through the natural world and use it in other sciences. Music theory, for example. The musician doesn't try and prove the multiplication table or the table of sums; he assumes them and utilizes them when he's writing music. In the same way, by the knowledge revealed by God — revelation — a science can be established concerning God. This is the science of theology. *Revelation is to theology what math is to musical theory.* Theology takes the truth of revelation for granted, like music theory takes the truth of mathematics for granted, and both move from their principles to the contents of their particular science.

[00:03:39] *Is theology one science or many?* It's one because it has one essential object: God. The Greek for theology is *theos logos*, literally the thought of God. Which is why the very first questions of this volume are concerned directly with God, and the rest of the *Summa* will deal with God's creatures, as Aquinas says, "only so far as they are referable to God as their beginning and end." Like everything we see with our eyes we see under the aspect of color: look around your room and try to see anything colorless — you can't, you see many things but all of them under the one aspect of being colored. So in the *Summa*, Thomas covers a lot of particular things but only as they are either God himself or related to God — under the same aspect.

[00:04:42] *Is it a practical science?* Aquinas says: not primarily, because its primary object and concern is on knowing God, not on action — which is where we get the word *practical* from in the Greek *praxis*, doing. So theology is less about doing and more about knowing God in so far as we can know him in this life. But because God has destined us for himself, which requires our own involvement, theology does pertain to action. We have moral theology, for example — a field in itself under theology dedicated to right action.

[00:05:24] *Is sacred doctrine nobler than the other sciences?* Yes, both speculatively and practically. Speculatively, because it is more certain. Yesterday Pluto was a planet; today it's not. Yesterday Newton was right about gravity; today Einstein is, until he's not. But revelation and the articles of faith are more certain because they have come from God, who can neither deceive nor be deceived, and that revelation has been interpreted by God through his infallible magisterium, as Aquinas would understand in the Catholic Church. It is also superior to all the other speculative sciences (e.g. physics) because its object is the greatest object of study possible — God himself. It is even better than the practical sciences (e.g. politics) because it is concerned with our ultimate end, not these various means to that end. It's concerned with heaven and beatitude, to which all the other practical sciences must find themselves subordinate, assisting, or in conformity with.

[00:06:47] *Is sacred doctrine the same as wisdom?* Absolutely. We consider a man wise who has a solid grasp of the big picture or the most fundamental, loftiest concern that bears on whatever our particular concern happens to be. The bricklayer goes to the architect for advice on where to lay his bricks. But the architect goes to the physicist or the mathematician for guidance on how to shape his building. And both the physicist and the mathematician could go to the theologian on how to best orchestrate their whole lives so that they end successfully in beatitude. The theologian has the view from the highest mountain.

[00:07:39] *Is God the object of this sacred doctrine?* An interesting objection comes from St. John Damascene: "It is impossible to define the essence of God; therefore God is not the object of this science." Aquinas agrees with the objection — he affirms: "We cannot know in what consists the essence of God; nevertheless in this science we make use of his effects" so as to understand him. We cannot know what God is in himself — that's literally to know what God is in himself, which is what Catholics hold heaven to be. Knowing God as God knows God. If we knew that, we would be perfectly and eternally happy. Down here, we know God like we know a painter by staring at his paintings. We know much more about what the painter is *not* like than what he is like.

[00:09:39] *Is theology a matter of argument?* It is and it isn't. Not in regard to its principles — what God has revealed (Scripture as interpreted by the Church) — those are foundational. But you can argue from common ground with people who hold parts of revelation; for those who do not hold that God exists or that he has revealed himself, there is no common ground for theology — only philosophy. Aquinas notes that in the natural sciences, an argument from authority is the weakest form of argument, but in theology it is the strongest: if God said it, and God also interprets what he said, then "this is true because God said so" is the strongest possible argument.

[00:11:15] *Should Scripture use metaphors?* Yes — first because by things we can see and touch and imagine, we are easily led to things more abstract and spiritual; that's how human beings work. Second, sacred things are kept hidden in this way from those who would disparage them.

[00:12:39] *Does Scripture have multiple senses?* Aquinas: yes, four. The **literal sense**, which doesn't necessarily mean *literally what the words mean* but rather what was intended by the author. (When Job says "God trampled on the waves of the sea," the literal sense is the intended meaning — perhaps that God is sovereign over creation, conveyed via metaphor. Discovering that meaning is the role of biblical exegesis.) The **allegorical sense** — pointing to things done in Christ. (Jesus actually one day walks on the waters to the boat where the apostles are.) The **moral sense** — a type or figure of what we ought to do. (Trample worldly desires under our feet.) ### Q.2 — The existence of God

[00:14:53] We turn to the infamous question two of the first part of the *Summa* — the existence of God. Three articles, the last being Aquinas's famous Five Ways.

[00:15:00] *Is the proposition "God exists" self-evident?* If it were, there would be neither atheists nor agnostics. So God's existence is not self-evident — but in another sense it kind of is. As St. John Damascene says, "the knowledge of God is naturally implanted in all." Aquinas: Damascene is right insofar as we are all pursuing happiness, which can only be found in God. So in pursuing our own happiness we are pursuing God. But to pursue something we must have *some* knowledge of it — though confused, easily mistaken, easily misplaced (riches, honor, pleasure, etc.). Self-evident things are things in which the predicate is included in the essence of its subject — *man is an animal*, *the part is less than the whole*. "God exists" is not self-evident *to us*, although it is self-evident *in itself* — because God is existence; "existence exists" is self-evident. But we don't know the essence of God; that would be heaven. Therefore the proposition "God exists" is not self-evident to us — or else there would not be atheists.

[00:17:21] *Is the existence of God demonstrable?* Yes. Demonstration is either: from cause to effect (God can do this — he prophesies what will certainly happen) or from effect to cause (the painter from the painting; the investigator from the crime). The second form — effect to cause — is the form of demonstration that lets us prove God's existence.

[00:18:24] *Whether God exists.* In Exodus 3:14 God says, "I am who am" — God says he exists. What do we say? Aquinas's famous Five Ways. Why these five? Not because they were the only five proofs in his day. But, as some commentators say, perhaps to map onto Aristotle's four causes. And Habash's view: Aquinas is proving Paul's words in Acts 17 — "in him we live and move and have our being." Aquinas picks these five proofs to show that God *encompasses* us. The whole Five-Ways structure is the architecture of "in extending your arm toward a Monday-morning coffee, you prove God's existence in no fewer than five ways."

[00:19:42] *Mnemonic.* Your hand could not move unless God existed (First Way: motion). You wouldn't be able to *cause* your hand to move unless God existed (Second Way: efficient causality). You wouldn't *exist* unless God existed (Third Way: contingency). You wouldn't *judge* the coffee to be good unless God existed (Fourth Way: gradations of perfection). And you wouldn't *want* the coffee unless God existed (Fifth Way: directedness toward an end).

**First Way (motion).** Boxcars — if you ask "how do we get these moving?" and someone says "add more boxcars to the front," that doesn't work; boxcars receive and pass on motion, they don't initiate it. You need a motion-non-receiver at the beginning of the series — the engine car. In the same way, when my hand goes from "can move" to "actually moving," it can only do so because muscles that can contract are actually contracting only because motor neurons that can fire are actually firing. Another series of boxcars, which to get started must begin with a non-receiver — but here, of *actuality*, not of motion. Change is the transition from potential to actual. To be a non-receiver of actuality means being completely devoid of potential. Whatever lacks potential completely (including the potential to be perfected, in power, knowledge, or goodness), while being the cause of all increases in power/knowledge/goodness existing or even possible, must itself be all good, all powerful, all knowing — which is what we mean by the term *God*.

**Second Way (efficient causality).** The lights in the room have electricity because there's something out there which we wouldn't say *has* electricity but simply *is generating* electricity. In the same way, your hand has its existence materially from the cells that make it up; those cells from the atoms; those atoms from the subatomic particles; and so on. But for any of these to *have* existence, there must be something that doesn't *have* existence but simply *is* existence and is generating it for everything else that has it. Whatever just *is* existence necessarily exists, must always have existed, and is responsible for anything else that exists. This is what God claims to be when he reveals himself to Moses: "I am who am" — *I am am*, I am existence itself. (Habash adds the Kalam: whatever begins to exist has a cause; the universe began; therefore an eternal, immaterial, omnipotent cause.)

**Third Way (contingency).** A chandelier in a ballroom, hanging in mid-air. You ask "how is that hanging?" and someone says "no reason." You can't accept that — if things can go without explanation, your very thoughts might have no explanation, ending science and any certainty about your own faculties. Nothing about chandeliers in themselves explains why this one is hanging. The chain has to terminate in something that doesn't merely *have* existence (and thus could have not had it) but *must* exist and *could not be different*. This necessary, immutable explanation for all contingent things is what we mean by the term *God*.

**Fourth Way (gradations of perfection).** Grading a math test. Students answer 2+2 with 7, 1, 6 — you grade them confidently because there exists a *table of sums* you compare against, even when you're not consciously thinking about it. In the same way, we judge the world more or less perfect, more or less good, and we can only do so because perfection itself, in all respects, actually exists somewhere — like the table of sums. Perfection in all respects is also all good, all powerful, all benevolent. Or, from another angle: would 2+2=4 still be true before the universe existed? Yes — it's eternal and necessary. But truth is a quality of mind-with-thing; in *what* mind does this eternal, necessary truth reside? In an eternal and necessary mind. That's what we mean by *God*.

**Fifth Way (directedness toward an end).** Standing on a beach, someone drowning diagonally in the water — the optimal path isn't straight-then-90°, or all-diagonal, but the path that minimizes total time given that you're faster on land than in water. You can do this because you're intelligent. But Pierre de Fermat showed that's also what light does — and light does not think. As Feynman emphasized, things take not the most direct path but the most efficient path, which presupposes knowing where to enter each medium, which presupposes knowing where you want to end up before you begin moving — which presupposes intelligence. When universally unintelligent things move intelligently, it can only be because there exists a universally affecting intelligence from which they ultimately derive their nature. That's what we mean by *God*. (And similarly in us: we desire happiness *necessarily*, an end written into our nature, for the sake of which we desire all these other things voluntarily — placed within us.)

[00:31:00] **Mnemonic, restated.** Your hand could not move unless God existed (First Way). You wouldn't be able to cause your hand to move unless God existed (Second Way). You wouldn't exist unless God existed (Third Way). You wouldn't judge the coffee to be more or less good unless God existed (Fourth Way). And you wouldn't want the coffee — or see it as a means to your divinely-appointed end — unless God existed (Fifth Way). That is questions one and two of the first part of the *Summa*: the nature and extent of sacred doctrine, and the existence of God.

## Notes

- **First metaphysical-guardrail day.** The Five Ways and Q.1 article 7 ("we cannot know what God is in himself; we know him by his effects") are the loci where the synthesis must lead with Levin / Hoffman / Kastrup, not with Stump.
- **Q.1 Article 7's epistemology** — "we move from effect to cause, from what God is *not* to what we can say he *is*" — is structurally Hoffman's interface theory: the appearance is not the underlying reality; what is fundamental can only be approached negatively, by what it is not.
- **The Five Ways are an *agent-action* anchor.** Habash centers them on "extending your arm toward a Monday-morning coffee" — every Way is grounded in a single intentional action. That's a Levin-shaped framing: agents-with-goals, action under teleological structure. Worth noting in the synthesis.
- **Fourth Way's "table of sums" image** is a clean analytic-idealism crossover: Kastrup's mind-as-primitive lands here directly. The eternal and necessary mathematical truth must reside in *some* mind — Aquinas concludes "an eternal and necessary mind." Kastrup would call this mind-at-large.
- **Fifth Way's Fermat / Feynman move** is a contemporary rewrite of teleology — the principle of least action as embedded directedness toward an end. Carroll territory, but also Levin (morphogenetic teleology) and Friston (variational principles as agency).
