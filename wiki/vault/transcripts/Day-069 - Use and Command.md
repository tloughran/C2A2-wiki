# Day 69 — Use and Command

> Episode: <https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=x_-9Slwv_3s>

This episode closes the Summa's intricate breakdown of human action by treating *use* (Q.16, the will applying chosen means to execution — 4 articles) and *command* (Q.17, the act of reason that directs the executing power — 9 articles). Habash follows the Gilby translation in rendering the Latin *usus* as **application** when the meaning is "applying the will to execute the means."

## Q.16 — Use (Application)

**a.1 — Whether use is an act of the will.** *Application is to refer that which is the object of application to the obtaining of something else.* It is the applying of the will to the execution of some means for the sake of the end. *It is evident that first and principally application belongs to the will as first mover, to the reason as directing, and to the other powers as executing the operation, which powers are compared to the will which applies them to act as the instruments are compared to the principal agent.* The full sequence: counsel's decision (reason's judgment) → choice (will) → reason commands the executing power → the will applies that power to fulfill the command.

**a.2 — Whether application is to be found in irrational animals.** No. *Animals by means of their members do something from natural instinct, not through knowing the relation of their members to these operations; wherefore properly speaking they do not apply their members to action, nor do they use them.*

**a.3 — Whether application regards also the last end.** No, when "use" means application to the means. *No one rightly uses God but one enjoys him; but God alone is the last end; therefore we cannot use the last end.* Enjoyment, not use, is the will's relation to the end.

**a.4 — Whether application precedes choice.** No — application *follows* choice. *The motion of the will to the execution of the work precedes execution but follows choice.* So use stands between choice and execution.

## Q.17 — The Acts Commanded by the Will

**a.1 — Whether command is an act of reason or of the will.** *Command is an act of the reason, presupposing an act of the will, in virtue of which the reason by its command moves the power to the execution of the act.* Command — coming after choice but before application — moves the will *by intimating and declaring*. This is an act of reason.

**a.2 — Whether command belongs to irrational animals.** No. Command presupposes self-direction beyond instinct. *The impulse of the irrational animal arises from natural instinct, because as soon as they apprehend the fitting or the unfitting their appetite is moved naturally to pursue or to avoid; wherefore they are directed by another to act, and they themselves do not direct themselves to act.*

**a.3 — Whether application precedes command.** No — command precedes application. *Application in the user is united to the act of the thing used; for one does not apply a stick before doing something with the stick. But command is not simultaneous with the act of the thing to which the command is given, for it naturally precedes its fulfillment. Consequently it is evident that command precedes use or application.*

**a.4 — Whether command and the commanded act are one act or distinct.** *Command is nothing else than the act of the reason directing, with a certain motion, something to act.* Analogy: the human person's one act of existing is a unity of soul (formal) and body (material) yet considerable as two parts. Similarly, *in human acts the act of a lower power is in the position of matter in regard to the act of a higher power... hence it is evident that command and the commanded act are one human act, just as a whole is one yet in its parts many.*

**a.5 — Whether the act of the will is commanded.** Yes. *The reason can direct the act of the will, for just as it can judge it to be good to will something, so it can direct by commanding man to will.*

**a.6 — Whether the act of reason is commanded.** Partly. We can command reason when we assent to a truth not obviously certain. We cannot command reason on certainties known by the light of natural reason (non-contradiction, sufficient reason). *Some things which are apprehended do not convince the intellect to such an extent as not to leave it free to assent or dissent, or at least suspend its assent or dissent on account of some cause or other; and in such things assent or dissent is in our power and is subject to our command.*

**a.7 — Whether the act of the sensitive appetite is commanded.** Indirectly. We cannot command our passions to arise or cease, but we can manage what enters apprehension — keeping triggering thoughts and imaginings at a distance — since the sensitive appetite follows apprehension. *Strictly speaking, we cannot command our passions, because they follow immediately upon the apprehension of some sensible good.*

**a.8 — Whether the act of the vegetal soul is commanded.** No. *The nutritive and generative power is one over which the reason has no control* (Gregory of Nyssa). We have only indirect influence — passions and actions affect heart rate, the nutritive-generative faculties — but no command.

**a.9 — Whether the acts of the external members are commanded.** Yes. Augustine: *the mind commands a movement of the hand, and so ready is the hand to obey that scarcely can one discern obedience from command.*

## Notes

- The complete pipeline: **end-side** (apprehension → volition → intention → enjoyment) and **means-side** (counsel → consent → choice → command → use → execution). Today closes the means-side by separating the *commanding* (reason) from the *applying* (will) from the *executing* (the powers themselves).
- The substrate-of-command map: will commands itself; reason can be commanded only on uncertain matters; sensitive appetite is influenced only via apprehension; vegetative powers are not commanded; external members obey directly. This is a beautiful early *layered control architecture* — direct control over voluntary motor, gated control over imagination, indirect control over passion, no control over autonomic.
- Bridges: this is McGilchrist's hemispheric account of attention and willed action; Friston's hierarchical active-inference architecture; Levin's multi-scale agency layered hierarchy. The Aquinas account anticipates the contemporary computational picture of nested control loops.
- Q.17 a.4 (command and the commanded act are one act) is the *form/matter* analysis applied to action: the higher-power act formally informs the lower-power act, the two together one human act. Cf. Levin on form-as-bioelectric-pattern providing top-down control over cellular substrate.
- The "stick" example (Q.17 a.3) is wonderful — command precedes use because command is *about-to* while use is *now*. Bridges to Friston's expected-free-energy framework: the prior is established before the policy is enacted.
- Q.17 a.7 (passions follow apprehension) is the cognitive-appraisal theory of emotion: emotions are not commanded directly but are shaped by what the mind attends to. Fredrickson's broaden-and-build research is the contemporary primary.
- This sequence prepares the next arc — Q.18–21 on the goodness and evil of human acts, then the long treatise on the passions (Q.22–48).
