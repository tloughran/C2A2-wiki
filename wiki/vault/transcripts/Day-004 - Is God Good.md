### Q.5 — Goodness in general

[00:01:00] On goodness in general: is goodness and being actually the same? Do they differ only in idea? Is every being good? Is goodness divided into the virtuous, the useful, and the pleasant? These are some of our questions today as we examine the reality of goodness.

**Article 1 — Is goodness the same as being?**

[00:01:06] What is goodness? What do we mean when we call a thing good? We call everything from people to a pair of scissors to a car *good*. What do they share in common to share the adjective? Aquinas responds: *the essence of goodness consists in this, that it is in some way desirable*. This is our operative word — *desirable*. A pair of scissors is good if it is desirable in respect to cutting; the car is good insofar as it is desirable in getting from one place to another, or being enjoyable.

[00:01:37] We will get into the divisions of goodness at the end of this question. But common experience and common language vindicate this essential definition of goodness: *desirability*. Then is it distinct from being-existing? Aquinas says only in thought — because the thing is desirable insofar as it exists, and insofar as existing in it are the things which would cause us to consider it to be good. A man is good who has existing in him knowledge and virtue, and everything naturally desires those things perfective to it — say food, drink, sleep — to become more, to become greater in being, to acquire being. So *desirability, goodness, and being are synonymous in some ways*.

[00:02:40] Which is interesting to think about evil with — evil being the privation of good. We'll talk about that much later as simply being *less*, as becoming less — not just less good but, since being and goodness are the same, *being evil is being less*. So what is goodness? It is *being considered under the aspect of desirability*.

**Article 2 — Is goodness prior in idea to being?**

[00:03:00] Which is first according to our thought? Being comes first as the proper object of all our knowledge, because things are known *because they first exist*; and from here additional notions could be added to being, such as desirableness or goodness.

**Article 3 — Is every being good?**

[00:03:11] Is everything good? Insofar as it exists, yes. Things are called evil insofar as they lack some being proper to them — like a bad eye is missing health, a bad man is missing virtue or the correct intention. Evil men are good insofar as they exist and possess the perfections that they do, and they are evil insofar as they lack those things which they ought to have, that are proper to them. (Here we would further distinguish between moral evils and physical evils, but we'll see that later in the *Summa*.)

**Article 4 — Does goodness have the aspect of a final cause?**

[00:03:43] Aquinas asks whether goodness has the aspect of a final cause — *final cause* here meaning, does goodness have the aspect of an end or goal for which something exists or is done? Is goodness a cause of things precisely by being the goal of things? Yes — because goodness means a kind of desirability, and in its definition is the notion of *tending toward it, toward some goal, toward something we take to be desirable*. Ask yourself: what is the cause of you listening to this podcast? Was it not some good — something you found desirable? Maybe knowledge, or something to pass the time. That end was something desirable, a *good* that positively caused you to listen. This is why it's also said that *the good is diffusive of itself* — like the sun is diffusive of itself; it pulls its surrounding environment toward it. That's what goodness does.

**Aside — beauty and goodness**

[00:04:48] Aquinas gives us an aside, but a very profound one. He asks whether beauty and goodness are the same — perhaps because beauty is definitely a cause of pulling rational beings toward it. He says they are the same fundamentally, both being based on a thing's existence-essence combo (those co-principles), but they differ logically. *Goodness relates to our will* — we will, we desire goodness. *Beauty relates to our mind and senses* — for the beautiful, according to Aquinas, is "those things which please when seen." *Seeing* here means the senses for sure, but most especially the mind — *seen in the mind*, because truth is beautiful.

[00:05:20] The beautiful is *that which is duly proportioned*, Aquinas will mention in another place. Aquinas will expound that beauty includes three conditions: **integrity** or perfection (since those things which are impaired are by the very fact ugly); **due proportion** or harmony; and **brightness** or **clarity** — clarity here being the clarity of *that innermost structure of the thing* coming through it, its form, its nature shining through it. So if our nature is the rational animal, the most beautiful human being should be the one in whom we can see rationality — its nature — as much as that can be perceived visibly in a person, and then also the best of animality clearly. But we're getting a little sidetracked.

**Article 5 — Whether the essence of goodness consists in mode, species, and order**

[00:06:23] Where are these three divisions coming from? At least from scripture: it says in Wisdom 11, *"Thou hast ordered all things in measure and number and weight."* These terms are not likely to be very interesting or relevant to a listener in 2024, but: insofar as a created thing exists, it came from something — its efficient cause, and for material things a material cause; it is a particular kind of thing — its species, it has a kind of nature, form; and it is ordered to some goal or end, which is order. So a created thing would have **mode** (efficient cause), **species** (formal nature), and **order** (final end) — those are the aspects of its being. Because of the relationship between being and goodness, Aquinas then says: *hence the essence of goodness, so far as it consists in perfections, consists also in mode, species, and order*. This applies to created things.

**Article 6 — Whether goodness is rightly divided into the virtuous, the useful, and the pleasant**

[00:07:26] Here are our divisions of goodness. This division comes to us from St. Ambrose and so it's going to be taken as a fact from authority in the *Summa* by St. Thomas Aquinas, who'll explain them. Remember: goodness is being as considered under the aspect of desirability, and a thing can be desirable or good in a couple of different ways:

- **Useful good.** A thing can be desirable not so much for its own sake but for the sake of attaining something else — so medicine might be desirable for the sake of health.
- **Pleasant good.** A thing may be desirable as an end, as something to simply rest in (as Aquinas puts it), but not necessarily *objectively* good for the one resting in it.
- **Virtuous (honest) good.** A thing can be desirable for its own sake, as something objectively good that just ought to be desired and pursued — an end in itself. Loving God and neighbor would fall under this kind of good.

[00:09:00] That's a look at question five.

### Q.6 — The goodness of God

**Article 1 — Is God good?**

[00:09:00] Now that seems like an easy answer. It's going to get more complicated as we continue, but keep in mind our definition of goodness. Aquinas said the essence of goodness consists in this, that it is in some way desirable; that desirability is based on being. So *the greater the being, the more desirable is the thing in itself*. In this way God is definitely good, since whatever being we happen to find out here must pre-exist (as we mentioned in the last episode) in its cause in a more preeminent way, which is God. In this way it's also true that *insofar as a thing is pursuing some good, it is pursuing God* — because all of these goods, beings, are but shadows (or what St. Thomas called *similitudes*) of that font from which they have come. So when loving or looking, desiring anything, we are *desiring God*, in which these created things however remotely and analogously do resemble.

**Article 2 — Is God the Supreme good?**

[00:10:01] Here St. Thomas quotes Jesus, who says in Mark 10, *"No one is good but God alone."* Now that should shock us. It's a good reminder for us — if it could be put in a lesson, these words of Pope Benedict XVI in his Regensburg address: *"The faith of the Church has always insisted that between God and us, between his eternal Creator-Spirit and our created reason, there exists a real analogy in which (as the Fourth Lateran Council in 1215 stated) unlikeness remains infinitely greater than likeness, yet not to the point of abolishing analogy."* So between our notion of goodness down here and the actual goodness of God in himself, as Lateran IV said, *unlikeness remains infinitely greater than likeness*.

[00:11:02] That's really what Jesus is pointing out — God's being and his goodness in himself again transcends category, as Aquinas said at the beginning of the *Summa*: we cannot know what God is, only what he is not. Yet there is an analogy — similarity and dissimilarity — between our notion of goodness down here and God's goodness. So can we say God is supremely good? *We can't* if what we have in mind is a category of good in which God is simply the highest in that category — because again, God is outside of all category. But we *can* say he is the Supreme good as the *cause* of all particular goods, actual or possible. And again, to be in the effects, this goodness must pre-exist in a more preeminent way in its cause, which is God.

**Article 3 — Whether to be essentially good belongs to God alone**

[00:11:34] That bleeds into our next article: whether to be essentially good belongs to God alone — which we can simply agree to, having already spoken about it. Aquinas quotes Boethius, who says: *"All things but God are good by participation; therefore they are not good essentially."* *Participation* here meaning *having* existence, *having* goodness — but *not being existence, not being goodness* — which (as we saw in the questions of God's existence and God's simplicity) *that* is the definition of God alone.

[00:12:06] What's good to take away from this section is a kind of *map to perfection*. Things are perfect, Aquinas will say here, **insofar as they exist** (that's the first step); insofar as **certain qualities and accidental characteristics** (non-essential characteristics) are added to their existence (that's the second step); and insofar as **a thing attains its end and is able to rest in that end** for which it is created (that's the final step). That is a broadest path for someone who wants to increase in goodness.

**Article 4 — Are all things good by the Divine goodness?**

[00:12:38] Lastly: are all things good by the Divine goodness? Well, they're actually good by their own goodness, of course — their goodness as a cause and a cause for its ongoing existence. *But* our goodness, our being, is truly ours. Contrary to pantheism, *we are not merely parts of God*. We are created by God, but as something that exists itself, as something distinct.

[00:13:08] St. Thomas says we are good by the Divine goodness as the **first exemplary**, **effective**, and **final** principle of that goodness — exemplary in that our goodness pre-exists in a better way in God; effective in that God is the positive cause of our goodness; and final principle in that God's goodness is the reason why any other goods happen to exist (that he created any other goods). Yet everything is called *good* by its own goodness, which is a similitude or participation of the Divine goodness. In a word, Aquinas concludes: *"And so of all things there is one goodness, and yet many goods."*

## Notes

- **The being=goodness identity (Q.5 a.1)** is the deepest connective tissue between today's pair and the central theme. *Greater being = greater good = more desirable*. Under mind-monism, *being-of-experiencing* is the primitive; *good* is what mind-at-large pulls toward as its computational telos. This is exactly Friston's expected-free-energy minimization with the prior preference baked in (the agent is constituted by what it pulls toward) and Levin's morphogenetic field (the goal-state is the cause of the parts arranging to it). Synthesis bridge.
- **The good is diffusive of itself (Q.5 a.4)** — the sun-pulling-its-environment image is structurally Levin's bioelectric field (the whole-body pattern recruits the cells), Friston's expected-free-energy gradient (the prior preference *is* the attractor), and Rohr's Universal Christ (divine pattern radiating into matter from creation). Three different vocabularies for one shape.
- **Beauty's three conditions (Q.5 a.4 aside)** — integrity, due proportion, brightness — are not just aesthetics. *Brightness as form-shining-through* is Hoffman's interface theory in reverse: instead of icons hiding the underlying structure, the underlying structure shows through the icon when integrity and proportion align. McGilchrist's right-hemisphere mode of attention is what perceives this; left-hemisphere category-fixation is what blocks it.
- **The Lateran IV / Regensburg passage (Q.6 a.2)** — *unlikeness remains infinitely greater than likeness, yet not to the point of abolishing analogy* — is the most precise statement of the central theme available in classical theology. Conscious agents, individuated by perspective-limitations, are *unlike* the unconditioned ground in every way that perspective-limitation creates; they are *like* the unconditioned in that *being* is one. The synthesis can lean on this directly.
- **The map to perfection (Q.6 a.3)** — exist → acquire accidental perfections → attain end and rest there — is the staircase of *salvation in fear and trembling*. Existence is given; perfections are computed under limited resources; the end is loving union. Wright's covenant-inclusion as the named end; Rohr's "everything belongs" as the contemplative recognition.
- **Anti-pantheism move (Q.6 a.4)** is critical for the synthesis. *We are created by God but as something distinct.* Mind-monism is sometimes accused of pantheism; Kastrup's analytic idealism is *not* pantheism — finite alters of mind-at-large are genuinely individuated, dissociatively, even though their substrate is the one mind. The "exemplary, effective, final principle" structure maps cleanly: exemplary = mind-at-large holds the pattern; effective = mind-at-large generates the alters; final = mind-at-large is what alters integrate back into. The synthesis must mark this carefully — structural isomorphism of the *form* of dependence, not pantheistic identity of substance.
