### Q.73 — The Things That Belong to the Seventh Day

**Article 1 — Whether the completion of the divine works ought to be ascribed to the seventh day**

St. Thomas's response is based on Scripture and then explained within the article. Beginning with Genesis chapter 2, it is written thus:

> The heavens and the earth were finished, and all the host of them. [00:01:24] And on the seventh day God finished his work which he had done; and he rested on the seventh day from all his work which he had done. So God blessed the seventh day and hallowed it, because on it God rested from all his work which he had done in creation.

Aquinas explains what *finished* or *completed* means within this context, telling us that it means *the completeness of the universe at its first founding*, and this is what is ascribed to the seventh day.

[00:01:49] But then he reminds us that there is another kind of completion or perfection to be found in things, which is *the perfection wrought in them through their own operation*. This is the completion that will come about at the end of the world, when all the blessed (to which the rest of the material universe is ordered) engage in that ceaseless beatific worship and contemplation of God.

**Article 2 — Whether God rested on the seventh day from all his work**

[00:02:14] St. Thomas begins by explaining what *rest* means in this context as applied to God, because God does not rest physically (see episode three for that), nor does he cease working entirely in things — or they would simply cease to exist. But he can be said to rest like one may rest in the object of his or her desire — like one feeling at rest eating ice cream, or in prayer.

[00:02:41] To this kind of rest, Aquinas says that *God always has that changeless beatitude which results from his own possession of himself*, and so could be said to always be resting in that possession. God can also be said to be resting not in a complete cessation of action and ceasing to act in creatures, but in the sense that on the seventh day he had completed and therefore could cease to create the [00:03:07] fundamental material of the universe from which all other material creatures would then come — excepting the intellectual soul of human beings.

Of course, St. Thomas writes:

> Nothing entirely new was afterwards made by God, but all things subsequently made had in a sense been made before in the work of the days. Some things indeed had a previous existence materially, as the rib from the side of Adam out of which God formed Eve, [00:03:33] whilst others existed not only in matter but also in their causes, as those individual creatures that are now generated existed in the first of their kind.

Now this analysis by St. Thomas merits a brief digression, because if Aquinas is saying that every kind of species of material things was somehow [00:04:01] present in the very beginning of the universe and then formed or developed over time, then it would seem like he's proposing what we would call today a kind of theory of evolution.

Now when it comes to evolution, for Catholics the first thing we must always keep in mind is that there is not just one theory of evolution — there are many theories. Some of which include the work of God; other theories that exclude him completely [00:04:28] even as a possibility; others that mediate some kind of middle ground between the two. And to clarify: when most people talk about evolution, in my opinion what they don't mean is white moths becoming black moths (a kind of natural selection), but rather *if atoms can become plants can become animals can become us*.

Now as a Catholic we must reject — on both religious and [00:04:54] philosophical grounds — that the rational soul can come from the material world. Which is just impossible, as Aquinas definitely understood, and we will likely see in the upcoming episodes on anthropology and epistemology. But when it comes to *atoms becoming plants becoming animals*, the jury is not out, and there is freedom for the Catholic to make up his own mind, both from the vantage point of the faith as well as [00:05:20] reason.

As it pertains to the faith, the magisterium declared in the 1950 encyclical *Humani Generis*:

> The teaching authority of the Church does not forbid that, in conformity with the present state of human sciences and sacred theology, research and discussions on the part of men experienced in both fields take place with regard to the doctrine of evolution, in as far as it inquires [00:05:45] into the origin of the human body as coming from pre-existent and living matter; for the Catholic faith obliges us to hold that souls are immediately created by God.

Then when it comes to reason: evolution is possible if those higher-level species are somehow present *virtually* in the lower ones, as a kind of [00:06:11] potential which only needs the right concert of causes to then be actualized. In this way this step-process from atom to plant to animal could be theoretically possible. But again, what cannot be said is that the soul of man — which has entirely immaterial faculties, intellect and will — can come from the material world, precisely because they are not material and therefore cannot be [00:06:38] a potential within material things. They must necessarily come from one who is immaterial like those faculties, and also powerful enough to bring them into being from nothing — which is only God the Creator.

**Article 3 — Whether blessing and sanctifying are due to the seventh day**

*Blessing* here meaning the kind of blessing offered to offspring by their parents. St. Thomas says it is fittingly given on the [00:07:03] seventh day, since creation in some way completed, now the work of propagation (reproduction) can begin. Then *sanctifying* is fittingly attributed to this last day, because man, now having been created, can lead the entirety of that creation in giving glory to God as well as order himself to the thought and service of God — which is worship, a holy and sanctifying work.

St. Thomas responds to [00:07:29] this article writing:

> A blessing befits the seventh day, since the blessing referred to the increase by multiplication, for which reason God said to the creatures which he blessed, *Increase and multiply*. And it is right that the seventh day should have been sanctified, since the special sanctification of every creature consists in resting in God; for this reason things dedicated to God are said [00:07:55] to be sanctified.

### Q.74 — All the Seven Days in Common

**Article 1 — Whether these days are sufficiently enumerated**

Are the days sufficiently numbered and distinguished — was the seven-day creation account done in an orderly manner? St. Thomas's response will be a recapitulation of everything we've covered thus far on the order and distinction found within the creation account. St. Thomas says:

> The reason of the [00:08:21] distinction of these days is made clear by what has been said already, namely that the parts of the world had first to be distinguished and then each part adorned and filled, as it were, by the beings that inhabit it. The first part then is distinguished on the first day and adorned on the fourth; the middle part distinguished on the middle day and adorned on the fifth; and the third part distinguished on the third day and [00:08:47] adorned on the sixth.

And that is really all there is to this article.

**Article 2 — Whether all these days are one day**

— which Aquinas probably feels he has to bring up because according to the text this was the opinion of St. Augustine, who said all the days that are called seven are one day represented in a sevenfold aspect. St. Thomas then, out of reverence for Augustine, explains what Augustine [00:09:12] had in mind and what others opposed to his position had in mind, all of which I will refer the listener to if they are interested. St. Thomas himself will answer this objection from Scripture, saying:

> It is written in Genesis chapter 1, *the evening and the morning was the second day*, and then *the third day*, and so on. But where there is a second and a third there are more than one. There was not therefore only one day.

**Article 3 — Whether Scripture uses suitable words to express the work of the six days**

Are phrases like *the spirit of God moved over the waters* [00:09:40] appropriate, as well as many other terms and phrases that are brought up in the objections of this article? I would like to zoom in on that one specifically that I've already quoted, because it gets a lot of attention from St. Thomas, who argues that *in either work of creation and of formation, the Trinity of persons is implied*.

He explains: [00:10:05] in creation, the person of the Father is indicated by *God the Creator*; the person of the Son by *the beginning in which he created*; the person of the Holy Ghost by *the spirit that moved over the waters*. Then in the formation, the person of the Father is indicated by *God that speaks*; the person of the Son by *the word in which he speaks*; and the person of the Holy [00:10:32] Spirit by *the satisfaction with which God saw that what was made was good*.

And then to tie it back to our discussion on evolution — or the raw material of the universe taking on various forms and developing over time — St. Thomas writes:

> But according to the holy writers, the spirit of the Lord signifies the Holy Ghost who is said to move over the water — that is to say, over what Augustine holds [00:10:58] to mean formless matter. It is fittingly implied that the spirit moved over that which was incomplete and unfinished, since that movement is not one of place but a preeminent power.

So we could say maybe the spirit hovering over the water (this formless matter) represented the divine providence and power which would then bring into being over time all the [00:11:24] various and varied things we see all around us.

And that does it for the Thomistic explanation of the seven-day creation account. I hope these last few episodes have been a nice breather, a kind of vacation for the mind, because tomorrow that vacation comes to a sudden end as we dive headlong into our treatment of man: *what is the soul, what are its faculties, what does it mean for [00:11:50] man to be a body-soul composite?* Tomorrow we crash-land into anthropology and epistemology, and I cannot wait.

## Notes

- The episode's title (*Evolution*) is the synthesizer's marketing handle, but the substantive material spans three load-bearing themes: divine rest (Q.73 a.2), evolution proper (Q.73 a.2 digression on *virtual presence as potential*), and the Trinitarian reading of Genesis 1:1–2 (Q.74 a.3). The synthesis lifts all three.
- Habash makes the magisterial *Humani Generis* (1950) framing explicit: body open to evolutionary explanation; rational soul immediately created by God. This is the official Catholic position — not a private theological opinion — and the synthesis treats it as load-bearing rather than bracketed.
- Habash's *the soul of man... cannot come from the material world* sets up the metaphysical keystone of Day 39 (Q.75–76 — the soul; hylomorphism). The synthesis previews how mind-monism preserves the *immediate creation* claim structurally without medieval substantialism: the rational soul is the alter's mode-of-participation in mind-at-large at the human cognitive scale.
- Aquinas's *rest as one feeling at rest eating ice cream or in prayer* (Habash's gloss) is theologically deeper than it sounds: rest as fulfilled-presence-in-the-object-of-desire. The synthesis reads this as the substrate's loving self-possession, not cessation of activity — and matches it to Kastrup PRS-01 (consciousness as the only thing that exists; substrate as its own object of rest).
- The Trinitarian reading of Genesis 1:1–2 — Father / *beginning in which he created* (= Logos/Son) / *spirit moved over the waters* (= Holy Ghost) — is the textual hook into Wright's second-temple-Jewish Trinitarian-language scholarship and Rohr's *Universal Christ* on substrate-relationality. Both bridges land cleanly.
- Aquinas's respectful disagreement with Augustine (*the seven days are one day represented in a sevenfold aspect* vs. *the evening and the morning was the second day, and then the third day*) — the synthesizer reads both readings as contemplating the same substrate-act under different temporal lenses (atemporal divine act vs. temporal-narrative manifestation).
- Bridges developed in the synthesis: Wright (sabbath theology, Trinitarian reading); Levin (PRS-01/03/08, morphogenetic potential as Aquinas's *virtual presence*); Carroll (PRS-04/07, evolutionary mechanism under the arrow of time); Kastrup (PRS-01/02, rational soul as alter-mode); Rohr (Universal Christ on substrate-relationality and always-already-holy matter); CROSS-006/007 (Friston × Levin, morphogenesis as free-energy minimization); Hahn (cosmic-temple consecration); *Humani Generis* magisterial.
