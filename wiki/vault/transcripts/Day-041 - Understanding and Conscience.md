[00:00:04] Hello, my name is Austin Habash, the founder of Think Catholic, and you're listening to the *Summa in a Year* podcast, where we study St. Thomas Aquinas's *Summa Theologiae* in a way simple and insightful for anyone to understand. ### Q.79 — The Intellectual Powers

**Article 1 — Whether the intellect is a power of the soul**

This question is asking whether the intellect is identical to the essence of the soul. Aquinas reminds us that only in God is the intellect identical to his

[00:00:55] essence, identical to his existence. But in us, all of these things are distinct: our soul must be given existence by God, and then from its existence can flow its particular potentials or capacities — such as the power to understand, which is the power of the intellect.

**Article 2 — Whether the intellect is a passive power**

Aquinas answers this article as well as explains what he means by the question, writing:

> Accordingly, whatever [00:01:21] passes from potentiality to act may be said to be passive, even when it is perfected. And thus with us, to understand is passive.

So we have the capability to think but we are not always thinking, and for us to understand what something is we have to be exposed to it — probably multiple instances of this particular thing — before we can abstract the underlying essence of it, which in turn [00:01:49] informs our mind, our understanding. In this way the power of the intellect is a *passive* power — what Aquinas calls (quoting Aristotle) a *blank slate*. And what Aristotle means by that, by a blank slate, is that we come into this world without understanding it; we have to sense it and then abstract the essence of things within it, piece by piece, one thing after [00:02:14] another, to be able to understand those things, to understand the world.

**Article 3 — Whether there is an active intellect**

It's worth thanking God not only for Aquinas and for the *Summa* in general here, as an aside I suppose, but also in its logical sequence — it preempts our questions and provides the answers, which we see in this article especially. Because if it is the case that the

[00:02:40] essence of things is not sensed (because you and I have never seen *humanity* walking down the street, we've never seen the *idea of justice* with our eyes, but only sensed particular colored, sounding, smelly things), however, since we *do* actually know what justice is, what a human being is, which allows us to [00:03:06] recognize particular instances of them when we see them, and lastly since the intellect is a blank slate which comes into the world without knowing these things — then how exactly did we receive the knowledge of these things?

And Aquinas tells us: *in the soul there is something by which it becomes all things, and something by which it makes all [00:03:31] things; therefore we must admit an active intellect*. When you and I imagine the various people we have met, these are still particular sensed images. So how do we get from there to knowing the general essence (or idea) of things like *humanity*? Our mind has to make those imagined [00:03:57] images actually understandable. The mind has to abstract the essence from that image — the essence or form of humanity — which form can then inform the mind in the act of understanding. That is how you and I are able to know what a human being is.

And if it's helpful in trying to grasp [00:04:24] what's going on here, think of the process like this: like a light shining on the picture you have in your imagination, and then the now-illumined picture shining into the mind, illuminating the mind. The power or light making that sensed image actually intelligible — actually able to inform [00:04:52] your mind with the underlying essence within it — *what makes all things*, to use Aquinas's words, is what we mean by *the active intellect*. Then the power to understand, or the power in you to be informed by the essence of things which have been made intelligible by the active intellect — *what becomes all things*, [00:05:20] to use Aquinas's words — is what we mean by *the passive intellect*.

The active intellect is to the potential intellect what visible light is to the power of sight: visible light allows colored things to impress themselves upon the eye so that you can see them; the intellectual light, or the active [00:05:47] intellect, allows potentially understandable things to be actually understandable, allowing you to know them. Here Aquinas says:

> For some say that light is required for sight in order to make colors actually visible; and according to this, the active intellect is required for understanding in like manner, and for the same reason [00:06:15] as light is required for seeing.

**Article 4 — Whether the active intellect is something in the soul**

Is it something *you* can do? Is it a power in the soul? The answer is yes — but it's not a power of a corporeal organ, and therefore it must be a power residing solely within the soul. Aquinas writes:

> We perceive that we abstract universal forms from their particular conditions, which is to make [00:06:41] them actually intelligible. Now no action belongs to anything except through some principle formally inherent therein, as we have said above of the passive intellect. Therefore the power which is the principle of this action must be something in the soul.

What Aquinas is saying here is that the potential to make things actually intelligible — the active intellect — must be a potential of [00:07:09] some already-existing thing (you), because complete raw matter doesn't actually exist; it exists only potentially, as we've said in previous episodes. So this potential or power, the active intellect, must exist in some already-existing thing, which is your soul (the form of the body); therefore the active intellect is something in the soul.

**Article 5 — Whether the active intellect is one in all**

No. Because what we mean by the active intellect is a power of the soul, and since the soul is the form of the human body, and since as many distinct human bodies we have, we must have distinct human souls — then there must be as many active intellects as there are human souls. In St. Thomas's words:

> If the active intellect is something belonging to the soul as one of its powers, we are bound to say that there are as many [00:08:01] active intellects as there are souls, which are multiplied according to the number of men; for it is impossible that one same power belong to various substances.

**Article 6 — Whether memory is in the intellectual part of the soul**

Here we have to distinguish two kinds of memory: the first is a *sensitive* kind of memory, and the other is an *intellectual* kind. If memory means the storage of

[00:08:26] things from the past — then, since that pins down what we are remembering to a particular time, a particular instance (insofar as it is knowledge of a particular, not a general, thing) — just like we see particular things, we hear particular things, we taste particular things — this must be the kind of memory included within the *sensitive* powers, which pertain to the particulars.

But if [00:08:52] what we mean by *memory* is the ability to retain the *essence* of things — just think about all the abstract concepts you have tucked away in your mind, things like what *peace* is, what *justice* is, or memorized mathematical equations, or what things like the Trinity or Incarnation mean — all of these things, which once you have learned and so don't need to relearn to think about them, there must [00:09:17] be a sense in which they are in you *differently* now than they were before you were taught them. You have now this *habitual knowledge* because you have the intellectual capacity to retain the essence of things — which is what we mean by *memory in the intellectual sense*.

St. Thomas says regarding these two kinds of memory:

> If we take memory only for the power of retaining species, we must say [00:09:43] that it is in the intellectual part. But if in the notion of memory we include its object as something past, then the memory is not in the intellectual but only in the sensitive part, which apprehends individual things.

Then he goes on to explain how this capacity to retain the essence of things makes for habitual knowledge. Aquinas writes:

> The intelligible species (or essence or form [00:10:09] of things) is sometimes in the intellect only in potentiality, and then we have to learn; sometimes the intelligible species is in the intellect as regards the ultimate completion of the act, and then it understands in act; and sometimes the intelligible species is in a middle state between potentiality and act, and then we have habitual knowledge. In this way the intellect retains the species even when [00:10:36] it does not understand in act.

**Article 7 — Whether the intellectual memory is a power distinct from the intellect**

Which is a good distinction to make. And how do we answer this question? Aquinas has already given us the tools: powers are distinct by acts and objects. The act of seeing has color as its object; the act of knowing has the essence or form of things as its object; [00:11:03] therefore these two are distinct powers or faculties. The ability to see and the ability to know.

Is the ability to retain the essence of things distinct from the faculties we have already called the passive and active intellect? Aquinas says no. His explanation is the following:

> There can be no other difference of powers in the intellect but that of passive and active. Wherefore it is clear [00:11:28] that memory is not a distinct power from the intellect: for it belongs to the nature of a passive power to retain as well as to receive.

So the object of the passive intellect is the essence of things, and so is the intellectual memory's; therefore, since their objects are not distinct, they cannot be distinct powers. And so it looks like, according to this quote from St. Thomas within the article [00:11:54] which states *intelligence arises from memory as an act from a habit*, that what Aquinas takes intellectual memory to be is a *quality* of the intellect. As a footnote in the Gilby translation of the *Summa* says: *thus the whole range of conceptual knowledge in its habitual state, simply as concepts, constitutes an intellectual memory in a loose sense of the word*.

**Article 8 — Whether the reason is distinct from the intellect**

Aquinas gives us a concise distinction of these terms. He writes:

> For to understand is simply to apprehend intelligible truth, and to reason is to advance from one thing understood to another, so as to know an intelligible truth.

And so, to use a famous example, we apprehend what *man* is simply with the [00:12:49] intellect; then we make a judgment based on that apprehension — we can think *man is mortal* — then we reason down to the conclusion that since *man is mortal*, and *Socrates is a man*, then *Socrates must be mortal*. We can also reason from fundamental premises like *the good ought to be done and the evil avoided* to [00:13:14] *worshiping God in the manner he is appointed is good*, therefore *I ought to worship God in that manner he is appointed*.

In thought we first apprehend the essence of things; then we make a judgment, saying a thing is or is not; and then we reason from those two points to a certain conclusion. So *reason* is not a distinct power from *understanding*; it [00:13:40] is distinct only, as Aquinas says, *as movement is to rest, or acquisition to possession, of which one belongs to the perfect and the other to the imperfect. Reasoning, therefore, is compared to understanding.*

**Article 9 — Whether the higher and lower reason are distinct powers**

This looks like a distinction that we pick up from St. Augustine, whom St. Thomas quotes to the effect saying:

> The higher reason is that which [00:14:07] is intent on the contemplation and consultation of things eternal; the lower reason is that which is intent on the disposal of temporal things.

St. Thomas then responds:

> The higher and lower reasons are one and the same power, but, according to Augustine, they are distinguished by the functions of their actions and according to their various habits; for wisdom is attributed to the higher reason and science to the lower.

So wisdom is the knowledge of divine [00:14:33] things, and science (which Aquinas will later say is the knowledge of conclusions) — these things are on you or in you, qualities of the mind augmenting the intellect in some way. And since St. Thomas uses the word *habit* here — *the habit of wisdom*, *the habit of science* — it's good to give a definition of what we mean here by *habit*, which is [00:14:59] articulated in the Gilby *Summa* as *a stable condition, whether acquired or given, which enables a faculty the better to go about its proper business, or even a bad habit to go about it badly*. And that is an important definition to hold in one's heart, because the Catholic faith holds faith, hope, and charity to be habits, which we will see in a later [00:15:24] volume of the *Summa*.

**Article 10 — Whether intelligence is a power distinct from intellect**

What do we mean by *intelligence* as opposed to *intellect*? Aquinas writes: *this word intelligence properly signifies the intellect's very act, which is to understand*. And we can add that, since to understand is the act of the intellect, there is no distinction here in powers. As St. Thomas concludes:

> Thus intelligence is not distinct from intellect as power is from power, but as act is from power.

**Article 11 — Whether the speculative and the practical intellects are distinct powers**

These are terms which Aquinas explains briefly, as well as in response to this article, by saying:

> It is the speculative intellect which directs what it apprehends not to operation but to the consideration of [00:16:16] truth, while the practical intellect is that which directs what it apprehends to operation. And this is what Aristotle says — that the speculative differs from the practical in its end (or goal); whence each is named from its end: the one speculative, the other practical (i.e. operative).

So the intellect is one, but if it is contemplating truth for truth's [00:16:42] sake, it is called an act of the speculative intellect; and if one is considering something for the sake of action, to end in an action, we call that an act of the practical intellect. So these terms are not distinct powers.

**Article 12 — Whether synderesis is a special power of the soul distinct from the others**

Now, based on the article before this: when the mind applies itself to reach a true conclusion (the [00:17:10] speculative intellect), or applies itself in thinking for the sake of carrying out some action (the practical intellect), in either case the mind must start from some starting point, some certain firm foundation, to proceed either in the discovery of truth or towards the right course of action. This firm foundation, or starting point from which we intelligently choose what to do [00:17:36] or what not to do, is what we mean by this term *synderesis*: the habitual knowledge of the precepts of the natural law — those are the first principles of human action. And the first principle within those principles is this one: *that the good is to be done and pursued, and the evil avoided*.

Now remember Aquinas holds that we come into this [00:18:03] world a blank slate regarding the mind specifically; so this law is something we still have to discover through the senses and abstract with the intellect. But Aquinas calls it a *natural habit* because we are naturally inclined to apprehend this first principle of practical reason as well as many other precepts of the natural law. Just think about the Ten Commandments — even though they were revealed by God, many are [00:18:29] essentially common sense.

Then when it comes to the first principles of the speculative intellect, we have the principle of non-contradiction (that a thing cannot be and not be in the same way and at the same time) and the principle of sufficient reason (that whatever exists must have a reason for its existence and for having the attributes that it does have). Our entire lives are built on these principles, whether the practical or the speculative, [00:18:57] which cannot be the result of some combination of atoms because they are neither sensed nor are they limited to some particular application. These are the rules behind the world, which have to have come from an intelligent Creator of this world.

**Article 13 — Whether conscience is a power**

St. Thomas says:

> Properly speaking, conscience is not a power, but [00:19:22] an act. Knowledge applied to an individual case — but the application of knowledge to something is done by some act; wherefore, from this explanation of the name, it is clear that conscience is an act.

As an aside, sort of, conscience is probably the most obvious proof for the existence of God for all men everywhere and always. But what *is* it — that is our [00:19:50] article here? It is not the voice of God or our guardian angel; it comes from us. It is our own judgment on the moral character of this thing either we are about to do or have done; and because it comes from us, it can be wrong — way wrong. But what cannot be wrong is what lies underneath of that first law of practical reason: that *the good ought to be done and pursued, and the evil avoided*. [00:20:16] It is this law etched into the nature of things that cannot be erased; it cannot be explained, as we have said, short of an intelligent Creator who has placed it there. So conscience is the act of the intellect through the habitual knowledge of the moral law (as we know it) giving the moral character to this thing either we have done or are considering doing.

And we [00:20:41] experience it in the following ways, according to St. Thomas:

> Insofar as through conscience we judge that something should be done or not done, and in this sense conscience is said to *incite* or to *bind*. And the third way: insofar as by conscience we judge that something done is well done or ill done, and in this sense conscience is said to *excuse* or to *accuse* or to *torment*. Now it is clear that [00:21:07] all these things follow the actual application of knowledge to what we do; wherefore, properly speaking, conscience is the name of an *act*.

### Q.80 — The Appetitive Powers in General

And we move to question 80, *the appetitive powers in general*.

**Article 1 — Whether the appetitive is a special power of the soul**

As we mentioned in the previous episode, everything that exists tends towards its own perfection, preservation, and propagation (if it's a kind of thing that [00:21:33] propagates). Everything has a natural — or engraved-into-its-nature — inclination to its particular good. With the inanimate, like stones, that is for example an inclination towards other massive things, an inclination towards the good of unity, what we call *gravity* for short — this is a merely natural inclination. But with creatures endowed with sense (brute animals), as well as those endowed with [00:21:59] intelligence as well (that's us), we call their proper inclinations *appetite*, since it is elicited through the apprehension of things either sensed or known.

For example, you and I — we cannot desire the donut unless we see it (either with the eye or in the imagination), and we cannot desire God unless we have some knowledge of him. And since we have the capability [00:22:26] to desire both the object of sense and the object of the mind, we must say that we have this power of appetite in the soul. And since their objects are distinct, there must be a distinction within these appetites — which is our next article.

**Article 2 — Whether the sensitive and intellectual appetites are distinct powers**

St. Thomas, saying what we have just said but in his own words, writes:

> We must needs say that the intellectual appetite is a distinct power from the sensitive appetite... Therefore, since what is apprehended by the intellect and what is apprehended by sense are generically different, consequently the intellectual appetite is distinct from the sensitive.

And we have much more specific names and distinctions to make within these appetites, which will be tomorrow's episode. But for this episode it is only necessary to understand that the [00:23:17] sensitive and intellectual appetites are in us and they must be distinct because their objects are distinct. In the mind, that is, appetite for things which cannot be sensed — knowledge, virtue, God; and the sensual faculties — it is an appetite for things we can see, smell, hear, touch, taste.

And that is all for today, and for questions 79 and 80 of the Prima Pars, which is *the intellectual powers* and *the appetitive powers in general*. My name is [00:23:44] Austin Habash with Think Catholic, and I cannot wait to see you tomorrow.
