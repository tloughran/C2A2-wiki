# Day 82 — Fear

*Aquinas, Summa Theologiae, Prima Secundae, Q.42 (the object of fear) and Q.43 (the cause of fear).*

[00:00] Day 82 — questions 42 and 43 of the Prima Secundae, of the object of fear and of the cause of fear.

[00:50] **Article 1 (Q.42): Whether the object of fear is good or evil.** The object of fear is *perceived evil in the possible future*. Since the appetite pursues good and avoids evil, and fear is a passion of the sensitive appetite, its object must be cast in terms of good or evil. The thing we pursue we pursue under the aspect of good; the thing we avoid we avoid under the aspect of evil. Fear is in the avoidance register, oriented to the future. Personal experience is sufficient evidence: we are afraid of the possibility of harm, failure, or the unknown.

[01:58] **Article 2: Whether the evil of nature is an object of fear.** *Corruption* of nature — not just the pains accompanying it — is itself an object of fear. We fear death which is a corruption of our nature even where the dying might not be painful, because our nature naturally tends toward its own preservation and shrinks from corruption. But there are two ways the evil-of-nature would *not* be feared. Aristotle: the object of fear is "imagination of a future evil which is either corruptive or painful." (a) If the future evil is *out of mind* (not imagined, not sensed) fear is not present. (b) If we do not take the evil to be *future* we will not fear it: "those who are already on the scaffold are not afraid" — those who take themselves to be already dead while living do not experience fear but sorrow, since sorrow is the passion elicited by an evil that is present rather than future.

[03:05] **Article 3: Whether the evil of sin is an object of fear.** Surprisingly, no — at least not in the strict sense. We do not fear what is entirely in our own power, and the will cannot be coerced by any external cause; therefore we do not fear *sin itself* under the aspect of being forced to commit a sin. But we do fear *occasions* of sin, *influences* toward sin, and *the punishment* of sin from God. Aquinas: "Whatever is entirely subject to our power and will is not an object of fear; and nothing gives rise to fear save what is due to an external cause. Now human will is the proper cause of the evil of sin; and consequently evil of sin properly speaking is not an object of fear."

[04:15] **Article 4: Whether fear itself can be feared.** Yes. Aquinas: "It is possible for fear to be the object of fear, i.e. a man may fear lest he should be threatened by the necessity of fearing through being assailed by some great evil." We can fear that we might encounter something worthy of fearing. The Book of Proverbs 26 is apt here: "The sluggard says, There is a lion in the road, there is a lion in the streets." The man sitting in his home fears that when he steps outside he may meet what is fearful.

[04:55] **Article 5: Whether sudden things are especially feared.** Yes. Fear regards an evil under the aspect of *non-resistible*, and surprise increases the appearance of non-resistibility — both by catching us off guard and by leaving the evil unknown. As we gather ourselves and learn more about the surprising thing it becomes less frightful. Aquinas: "All material things, whether good or evil, the more we consider them, the smaller they seem; consequently, just as sorrow for a present evil is mitigated in course of time, so too fear of a future evil is diminished by thinking about it beforehand."

[06:04] **Article 6: Whether those things are more feared for which there is no remedy.** Yes. The most frightful thing is the thing without remedy. The Lord names it: "I tell you, my friends, do not fear those who kill the body, and after that have no more that they can do; but I will warn you whom to fear: fear Him who, after he is killed, has power to cast into hell." We fear most that evil which is everlasting; everlasting pain or corruption is what we mean by hell — frightful precisely because it cannot be remedied once it has begun. Dante: "Abandon all hope, ye who enter."

[06:35] **Question 43 — The cause of fear. Article 1: Whether love is the cause of fear.** Yes. Augustine, most profoundly: "There can be no doubt that there is no cause for fear save the loss of what we love when we possess it, or the failure to obtain what we hope for." *What we are afraid of tells us what we love* — and what we love the most. It might be worth taking inventory of one's greatest and most consistent fears; they will tell one where the heart is. Are we afraid of disappointing God, of losing him? If so, it may be because we love him. Aquinas: "Love causes fear, since it is through his loving a certain good that whatever deprives a man of that good is an evil to him, and that consequently he fears it as an evil."

[07:40] **Article 2: Whether defect is the cause of fear.** Yes. *Defect* here means weakness. Fear is caused by what we apprehend as non-resistible; our own defects make more things appear non-resistible; therefore defect causes fear. Aquinas: "Defect is of itself the cause of fear, for it is owing to some lack of power that one is unable easily to repulse a threatening evil." From the contrary side, Aristotle: "Wealth, strength, and a multitude of friends impel and drive fear away."

[08:43] That closes Q.42 and Q.43.

## Notes

- **Q.42 a.2 (the man on the scaffold)** is the keystone for the synthesis. Fear collapses if the evil is *not* held in imagination as *future*. This is a clean precision-on-time-prediction frame: anxiety is sustained when imagination keeps the future-evil active in working memory; equanimity in the face of the same evil is the cognitive-shift that ceases to project the model into the future. The clinical mapping is acceptance-and-commitment therapy and contemplative practice — both reduce the time-projection without denying the evidence.
- **Q.42 a.5 (sudden things are especially feared)** = the predictive-processing definition of surprise = high KL-divergence between prior and observation. Familiarity reduces fear by lowering surprise. This is *exactly* exposure therapy in modern clinical practice; the Aquinas-Aristotle account anticipates the mechanism.
- **Q.42 a.6 (the irremediable is most feared)** plus the Lord's word — fear what is everlasting. The synthesis must handle this carefully. Under the Wright-Rohr theological frame, "hell" is not a metaphysical certainty about quantitative duration but the theological name for the state of confirmed self-closure against loving unity. Even so, Aquinas's *formal* point holds: the irremediable evil is structurally the worst because no policy can be selected against it.
- **Q.43 a.1 (love causes fear)** is the central synthesis bridge. Augustine's claim — what you fear reveals what you love — is the affective inverse of the central theme. The agent who fears nothing is either an agent who loves nothing (apatheia in the pejorative sense) or an agent who has so opened the perspective that there is nothing to lose (theosis / cosmic-Christ identification). The inventory of one's fears as inventory of one's loves is a contemplative exercise of real diagnostic value.
- **Q.43 a.2 (defect causes fear)** and Aristotle's "wealth, strength, and friends drive fear away." The synthesis can read "friends" here as the strongest of the three — Fredrickson PRS-08 on high-quality connection as the empirical substrate of fear-reduction; co-regulation literature on vagal tone in attachment.
- The 6-articles-on-fear structure of Q.42 + 2-articles-on-its-causes of Q.43 give us: (Q.42) the *shape* of fear's object; (Q.43) the *forces* that generate fear in the agent. The synthesis can mirror this in the structure of its sections.
