# Day 83 — Daring

*Aquinas, Summa Theologiae, Prima Secundae, Q.44 (effects of fear) and Q.45 (daring).*

[00:00] Day 83 — questions 44 and 45 of the Prima Secundae, of the effects of fear and of daring.

[00:50] **Article 1 (Q.44): Whether fear causes contraction.** Yes. Every passion is accompanied by a bodily change, and that change has a likeness to the essence of the passion eliciting it. Fear is itself a recoiling — a shrinking back of the appetite from the perceived impending evil — and the bodily change corresponding to it is a contraction. Aquinas: "From the very imagination that causes fear, there ensues a certain contraction in the appetite; and in resemblance to this contraction, which pertains to the appetite of the soul, there takes place in fear a similar contraction of heat and vital spirits towards the inner parts in regard to the body."

[01:17] **Article 2: Whether fear makes one suitable for counsel.** Two senses. (1) Does fear make one a *better* counselor? No: passions skew judgment. Augustine: "When a man is affected by a passion, things seem to him greater or smaller than they really are." Fear in particular makes the feared seem more dreadful than it is. Considered in itself every passion hinders right judgment. (2) Does fear *motivate one to seek* counsel? Yes — and decisively. We are more likely to seek help when the obstacle ahead appears beyond our capacity, and this is precisely the object that elicits fear.

[02:27] **Article 3: Whether fear makes one tremble.** Yes. Aquinas: "In fear there takes place a certain contraction from the outward to the inner parts of the body, the result being that the outer parts become cold; and for this reason trembling is occasioned in these parts."

[02:58] **Article 4: Whether fear hinders action.** Not always. St. Paul writes (Philippians 2:12), "Therefore my beloved, as you have always obeyed, so now not only as in my presence but much more in my absence, work out your own salvation with fear and trembling." Paul would not recommend fear if it were always detrimental. *Moderate* fear can produce focused awareness, precaution, and discernment, and motivates the seeking of counsel — these aid action. *Excessive* fear hinders it.

[03:35] **Question 45 — Daring. Article 1: Whether daring is contrary to fear.** Yes. Aquinas: "Fear turns away from the future hurt on account of its victory over him that fears it; whereas daring turns on threatened danger because of its own victory over that same danger." Same object (the impending evil), contrary aspects (non-resistible vs. resistible). When the perceived impending evil is taken as resistible, the passion elicited is daring; when non-resistible, fear. (*Daring* here is the *passion* — the affective movement — not yet the *virtue* of courage.)

[04:06] **Article 2: Whether daring ensues from hope.** Yes. Aristotle: "Those who are hopeful are full of daring." Aquinas: daring's object is "an evil to which, in the estimation of the daring man, the good of victory is conjoined." Hope of the arduous good as attainable spills over into the daring stance toward the evils that ring it. We pursue evils for the sake of the good we hope to attain; hope is therefore cause of daring.

[04:38] **Article 3: Whether some defect is the cause of daring.** Defect is a cause of *fear* (per Q.43 a.2). But *defect* of two specific kinds can be a cause of *daring*: (a) drunkenness, which impairs reason and thus brings unawareness of obstacles, giving inflated hope; (b) being wounded by injustice, which gives the wounded one the feeling that justice is on his side. Aristotle: "Those who have been wronged are courageous because they think that God comes to the assistance of those who suffer unjustly." Not a direct causation but an inadvertent one — defect can in these specific modes cause daring.

[06:21] **Article 4: Whether the brave are more eager at first than in the midst of danger.** Aquinas introduces the standard distinction between *antecedent* and *consequent* passions. **Antecedent passions** follow sensual apprehension simply — they are not strictly voluntary. **Consequent passions** follow apprehension of the *intellect* and *will*. Example: a man may feel fear at first glance of a burglar climbing into his house (antecedent), but on reflection realize he can confront the threat and choose to do so — a consequent passion of daring. The brave may be more eager at first when the antecedent passion runs ahead of the reality, then waver as they learn the danger is greater than supposed. Or contrarily: they may feel little at first but, on heroic reflection, generate consequent daring. *At times* the brave are more eager at first; *sometimes* the opposite is the case.

[08:00] That closes Q.44 and Q.45.

## Notes

- **Q.44 a.1 (fear causes contraction)**: Aquinas explicitly defends the principle that the bodily change resembles the essence of the passion eliciting it. This is a remarkably strong somatic-emotion theory; James-Lange, Damasio's somatic-marker hypothesis, and contemporary affective neuroscience all preserve the structural claim that the body and the affective state are bound. The synthesis can read this as Aquinas's anticipation of embodied cognition.
- **Q.44 a.2 (fear motivates seeking counsel)** is the structurally interesting article. Fear hinders solitary judgment but motivates *interpersonal* policy-search. This is the synthesis bridge: passions are not just signals but action-affordances toward community. The agent under perspective-limitation seeks help precisely under the conditions that make it most needed — fear is the affective force that pushes the limited agent out of isolation.
- **Q.44 a.4 (fear and Phil 2:12)**: this is the article where Aquinas himself cites the keystone of the central theme. Moderate fear aids salvation; excessive fear hinders it. The Aristotelian "moderate" is the synthesis's "calibrated precision" — neither over- nor under-attuned to the actual evidence.
- **Q.45 a.1 (daring is contrary to fear)**: same-object-contrary-aspect structure, exactly parallel to Q.40 a.4 (hope contrary to despair). The irascible-passion economy is now legible as the systematic pairing of affective registers keyed to the same future-state by contrary feasibility/resistibility weightings.
- **Q.45 a.2 (daring follows hope)**: the chain hope → daring is consequent. This is where the central theme connects to action: hope-as-precision-on-future-good gives rise to daring-as-engagement-with-present-obstacle. The agent who is hopeful *acts*.
- **Q.45 a.3 (defect-of-drunkenness, defect-of-wounding)** is fascinating. Two pathological causes of daring: (a) inebriation that removes proper precision on obstacles; (b) injustice that adds the moral conviction of divine support. Both should be read carefully — the wounded-man case has been used to justify violence; Aquinas is descriptively naming the affective dynamics, not prescriptively endorsing them.
- **Q.45 a.4 (antecedent vs. consequent passions)** is *the* important article of this episode. The distinction between (i) passions that follow sense-apprehension simply and (ii) passions that follow apprehension of intellect + will is the distinction between *predictive-processing fast affect* and *deliberative-engagement slow affect*. This anticipates dual-process theory (Kahneman) and the deliberation-versus-habit distinction throughout cognitive science.
