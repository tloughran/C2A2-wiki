# Day 91 — Cardinal Virtue

*Summa Theologiae*, Prima Secundae, Q.60 (How the moral virtues differ from one another) and Q.61 (Of the cardinal virtues).

[00:05] Hello, my name is Austin Habash, the founder of Think Catholic, and you're listening to the Summa Year Podcast, where we study St. Thomas Aquinas's *Summa Theologiae* in a way simple and insightful for anyone to understand. Taking two questions of the Summa a day, we'll seek to summarize St. Thomas's responses, discovering the brilliance of Aquinas and his Catholic faith. This is Day 91. We're covering today Question 60 and 61 of the Prima Secundae — that is, how the moral virtues differ from one another and of the cardinal virtues.

[00:54] **Article 1: Whether there is only one moral virtue.** The moral virtues are habits of the appetitive faculty, and since the appetitive faculty has diverse or varied objects — God, neighbor, one's parents, for example — the habits are going to be diverse as well, such as the virtue of religion, or justice, or piety. We also have virtues which relate specifically to the passions, such as temperance and fortitude. And therefore, on account of the diverse objects of the appetitive faculty there follow diverse good dispositions to those objects, diverse virtues.

[01:30] **Article 2: Whether moral virtues about operations are different from those that are about passions.** The example St. Thomas gives is helpful: when through anger one man strikes another, justice is destroyed in the undue blow, while gentleness is destroyed by the immoderate anger. Moral virtues pertaining specifically to operations are in fact distinct from those virtues pertaining to the passions. Even if justice was not destroyed by an undue blow — if the blow was just — it could still be made morally evil if done out of a contrary-to-reason passion of anger. And on the flip side, even if one's anger was moderate or in accord with reason, if the blow itself, the operation, wasn't in accord with right reason, then the act would still be evil. As St. Thomas says: in some cases virtue is chiefly about operations, in others about passions.

[02:46] **Article 3: Whether there is only one moral virtue about operations.** We might think at first glance that the one virtue is justice — rendering to another what is his or her due. But since there are specific formal differences among those to whom something is due and what exactly is due, our good dispositions or virtues toward those objects must also differ specifically. In St. Thomas's words: "The thing due is not of the same kind in all these virtues, for something is due to an equal in one way, to a superior in another way, to an inferior in yet another. And the nature of a debt differs according as it arises from a contract, a promise, or a favor already conferred. Corresponding to these various kinds of debt there are various virtues: religion, whereby we pay our debt to God; piety, whereby we pay our debt to our parents or to our country; gratitude, whereby we pay our debt to our benefactors; and so forth." There is not only one moral virtue about operations, but many.

[04:00] **Article 4: Whether there are different moral virtues about different passions.** Of the passions within the concupiscible order — love, desire, joy, hate, aversion, sorrow — since these six are more or less various stages of attainment of either the perceived good or the perceived evil, they only require one virtue to moderate them: temperance. However, when it comes to the irascible order, in which we find three distinct kinds of objects, we must have three distinct kinds of virtues: in regard to the future good, magnanimity, which moderates hope and despair; in regard to the future evil, fortitude, which moderates courage and fear; and as regards the present evil, meekness, which moderates anger.

[05:10] **Article 5: Whether the moral virtues differ in point of the various objects of the passions.** Because we are rational, one object of desire can be desirable for different reasons — either as relating to the body or sensitive appetites or intellectual faculties, or toward oneself or one's neighbor. So to moderate the passions as their objects are related differently to us, we have multiple moral virtues. Aquinas enumerates: fortitude, temperance, liberality and magnificence in regard to money, magnanimity and *philotimia* in regard to honor, gentleness, friendship, truthfulness, and *eutrapelia* in regard to others — all of which differ in respect of their diverse matter, passions, or objects. Add justice (which is about operations), and there will be eleven in all.

[06:41] Now to **Question 61: Of the cardinal virtues. Article 1: Whether the moral virtues should be called cardinal or principal virtues.** Because virtues are strictly speaking those dispositions which, as Augustine said, "by which we live righteously, of which no one can make bad use" — and since our good intellectual dispositions short of prudence can be used for good or for ill — then short of prudence, the principal or cardinal virtues must be those which pertain to the will or flow from it in the case of the passions. The cardinal virtue we apply principally to the will is called justice; in regard to the sensitive appetite, temperance and fortitude; and prudence in regard to the mind. Thus we arrive at the four cardinal virtues: prudence, justice, temperance, and fortitude.

[07:50] **Article 2: Whether there are four cardinal virtues.** For an action to be good it must stem from a good will and from a correct command of reason as to the particular action to be done. The virtue disposing the reason is called prudence; with a good will we have the virtue of justice; and as keeping our passions in check from throwing off reason we have temperance (to keep us from that which our passions incite us toward contrary to reason) and fortitude (which keeps us in pursuit of that which our passions incite us away from contrary to reason).

[08:30] **Article 3: Whether any other virtue should be called principal rather than these.** We reserve the term "cardinal" or "principal" to prudence, justice, temperance, and fortitude on account of how general they are and on account of the importance of their subject matter. Justice is the most general virtue of rendering to another their due; prudence is cardinal among the intellectual virtues because by disposing right reason to action it is more important than those intellectual virtues which do not deal with action. As Aquinas says: "Prudence is the virtue which commands; justice the virtue which is about due actions between equals; temperance the virtue which suppresses desires for the pleasures of touch; and fortitude the virtue which strengthens against dangers of death. The other virtues may be principal in some other way, but these are called principal by reason of their matter."

[09:50] **Article 4: Whether the four cardinal virtues differ from one another.** The insightful objection comes from Gregory the Great: "There is no true prudence unless it be just, temperate, and brave; no perfect temperance that is not brave, just, and prudent; no sound fortitude that is not prudent, temperate, and just; no real justice without prudence, fortitude, and temperance." Unless you already have temperance and fortitude, your take on the right course of action is going to be skewed — you're not going to act prudently. Yet if you do judge prudently, you will not act justly without a will set on justice. All the cardinal virtues imply and presuppose the others. Yet that doesn't mean they are not distinct — they are distinct precisely because of their subject and object, the specific power they dispose and the specific object to which they are directed.

[10:55] **Article 5: Whether the cardinal virtues are fittingly divided into social, perfecting, perfect, and exemplar virtues.** Social virtues, according to St. Thomas, are those "by reason of them man behaves himself well in the conduct of human affairs." But since we must act appropriately not only among men but also toward God, who is the Exemplar of all virtue and goodness, there must be a distinction within the progression toward that total and perfect virtue. For those still on the way (perfecting virtue): "Prudence by contemplating the things of God counts as nothing all things of the world and directs all the thoughts of the soul to God alone. Temperance, so far as nature allows, neglects the needs of the body. Fortitude prevents the soul from being afraid of neglecting the body and rising to heavenly things. And justice consists in the soul giving a wholehearted consent to follow the way thus proposed." For those who have attained perfect virtue: "Prudence sees naught else but the things of God; temperance knows no earthly desires; fortitude has no knowledge of passion; and justice, by imitating the divine mind, is united thereto by an everlasting covenant."

[13:00] As a final note to take to prayer: just as our Lord said, "Whoever follows me cannot walk in darkness," let us take our Lord's prudence as our exemplar and not be afraid to live and walk similarly to the way that he lived and walked.

## Notes

- The whole structure of Q.60-61 turns on a *kind*-individuation argument: virtues differ because their **objects** differ. The unity question and the cardinality question are answered by ontology of the appetitive/intellectual faculties.
- Q.60.5 gives the eleven-virtue enumeration that Aquinas inherits from Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* — a useful taxonomy to flag against contemporary moral psychology lists (e.g., VIA strengths classification).
- Q.61.4 (Gregory's chiasmus on the unity of the virtues) is the philosophically heaviest moment: it asserts a *connection* among the cardinal virtues that doesn't collapse them into one. The reframe in modern character-systems language is the "trait-vs-process" debate.
- Q.61.5's perfecting/perfect/exemplar tiering implicitly models virtue formation as a graded trajectory — *journey* metaphysics, not state metaphysics. Aligns with Friston-style "expected free energy minimization over developmental time."
- The cardinal-vs-non-cardinal distinction is made on **generality of object** and **importance of subject matter**: a procedural priority claim, not a substantive ranking. Worth holding lightly when reframing under network-of-capacities models.
