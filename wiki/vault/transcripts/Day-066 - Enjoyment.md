# Day 66 — Enjoyment

> Episode: <https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bEf6DId1Z4E>

## Transcript

Today's episode covers Prima Secundae Q.10 (the manner in which the will is moved) and Q.11 (enjoyment as an act of the will). Habash takes us through eight articles total — four on what moves the will (its own nature, its object, the lower appetite, the exterior mover God) and four on enjoyment (which power has it, which creatures have it, whether it is only of the last end, whether it is only of the end possessed).

[00:30] **Q.10 a.1 — Whether the will is moved to anything naturally.** Yes — we will goods (the will's proper object), our own happiness (proved in episodes 61–63), and individual goods which belong to the perfection of our other faculties. Aquinas: *man wills naturally not only the object of the will but also other things that are appropriate to the other powers, such as the knowledge of truth which befits the intellect, and to be and to live and other like things which regard the natural well-being.*

[01:54] **Q.10 a.2 — Whether the will is moved of necessity by its object.** Aquinas's analogy: just as in a dark or eyes-closed room I see nothing, but in a well-lit room with eyes open I necessarily see all colored things, so when the intellect presents an object that is good in all respects (i.e. happiness, or God via the beatific vision) the will necessarily wills it. But if the good is partial — like a room not totally well-lit — the will is free to will or not will it. *That good alone which is perfect and lacking in nothing is such a good that the will cannot not will it.*

[03:23] **Q.10 a.3 — Whether the will is moved of necessity by the lower appetite.** Normally no, unless a man is made momentarily insane or enraged to the point of blunting his use of reason. Passions — anger, desire, sorrow — influence the will by making the particular good in question seem more fitting or attractive in that brief passionate state.

[04:05] **Q.10 a.4 — Whether the will is moved of necessity by the exterior mover, God.** Aquinas: *since therefore the will is an active principle not determinate to one thing but having an indifferent relation to many things, God so moves it that he does not determine it of necessity to one thing but its movement remains contingent and not necessary except in those things to which it is moved naturally.* God moves us as the unmoved Mover, but according to the kind of thing we are — voluntary agents.

[05:06] **Q.11 a.1 — Whether to enjoy is an act of the appetitive power.** Augustine: *to enjoy is to adhere lovingly to something for its own sake.* Since the appetitive powers are directed toward the end-goal as something to be attained, enjoyment occurs in the appetitive powers' resting in the good loved and sought. But joy must follow upon knowledge of things first loved and then known to be attained, so the apprehensive powers (especially intellect) play a part too. Aquinas: *in delight there are two things — perception of what is becoming, which belongs to the apprehensive power, and complacency in that which is offered as becoming, which belongs to the appetitive power, in which power delight is formally completed.*

[06:21] **Q.11 a.2 — Whether to enjoy belongs to the rational creature alone, or also to irrational animals.** Enjoyment belongs to the rational nature in perfect degree, to irrational animals imperfectly, to other creatures not at all. To enjoy a good attained there must be knowledge of that thing and of its attainment. Animals know things by sense only and so enjoy imperfectly; rational creatures, knowing not only the desired particular but understanding what goodness *is* and what attainment *is*, possess the fullness of enjoyment.

[07:20] **Q.11 a.3 — Whether enjoyment is only of the last end.** Augustine: *a man does not enjoy that which he desires for the sake of something else.* John of the Cross adds: *there can be no joy which is passion where there is no habitual attachment in the heart.* Aquinas: ultimately, only the beatific vision is the end in which we can find enjoyment in its fullest proper sense, but relative ends within or on the way to that end can elicit true delight, even if imperfectly. *We enjoy what we know when the delighted will is at rest therein, but its rest is not absolute save in the possession of the last end; for as long as something is looked for, the movement of the will remains in suspense, although it has reached some.*

[09:01] **Q.11 a.4 — Whether enjoyment is only of the end possessed.** The operative word is "possessed." Can we enjoy some goal even before possessing it? Yes — true knowledge of God in this life, though falling short of perfect possession in the beatific vision, is not without its own enjoyment. Augustine's definition of happiness was *joy in truth*. Aquinas: *perfect enjoyment therefore is of the end already possessed, but imperfect enjoyment is also of the end possessed not really but only in intention, that is in the mind.* In this life we can take hold of God by habitual grace, by prayer, by the sacraments, by the actual thought of God — we can say with the Song of Songs: *I found him whom my soul loves, I held him and would not let him go.*

## Notes

- The will is naturally moved toward goods in general, toward its own happiness, and toward the goods proper to the other faculties (knowledge of truth for the intellect, being and life for nature). This is a natural directedness — telic structure built into the agent.
- The will is **necessarily** moved only by an object that is good in all respects (the beatific vision); for partial goods, the will is genuinely indifferent. This is the metaphysical anchor of free will: the structure of the good is what creates the room for choice.
- The room-and-light analogy is doing real work: presence of the perfect good is *non-optional sight*; presence of partial goods is genuinely under-determined.
- God moves the will *as the kind of thing the will is* — voluntary, indifferent, contingent — not as mechanical necessity. Bridges to the Friston / active-inference frame: the agent's policy-selection is shaped by priors but remains under-determined at the local choice scale.
- Enjoyment requires both apprehension (perception of what is becoming) and complacency (resting in it). This is the perception-action loop with the action pole ending in *rest*. Bridges to Fredrickson's positivity-resonance frame and to predictive-coding accounts of reward.
- Imperfect enjoyment of God in this life — *joy in truth*, prayer, sacraments — is the alter's resource for the work of becoming. The "salvation in fear and trembling" thread runs through this.
