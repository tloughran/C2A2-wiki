### Q.3 — The simplicity of God

[00:01:10] Aquinas says we cannot know what God *is*, but only what he is *not* — so to study him we study what he has *not*, such as composition. As we've said previously: we can't know what God is — meaning his essence, what he is in himself — because that would be heaven by definition: the beatific vision. But we can know what he is *not*. This is the beginning of that journey, discovering true things about God through those things that he cannot be.

[00:01:42] The first thing is that God cannot be made up of parts — whether physical (parts of a body) or metaphysical (being part of a category). **Article 1 — Is God a body?**

[00:02:13] Can God be bodily? Absolutely not. Bodies limit things to a particular place and time; they are susceptible to change via locomotion; insofar as they take up space, they are susceptible to being divided and destroyed. None of these things can be true of God if God is to be perfect. So if God exists — which we proved in five ways in the previous episode — he cannot have any of these limitations which come necessarily from being bodily. God cannot be bodily.

[00:02:46] Now, when it comes to the Incarnation — which we'll get into way down the road — this does not affect the divine nature as such, which of course cannot change. But again, much later. In a word, summarizing this from scripture: in the Gospel of John 4:24 it is written, "God is spirit."

[00:03:50] What's going on here is, in part, Heisenberg (like many physicists) trying to find the absolute bottom of all material things — what is the most fundamental thing everything material is made up of. **Article 4 — Whether essence and existence are the same in God**

[00:06:32] What do these terms mean? Take ourselves as an example. Things out here clearly exist — you and I exist. We exist in a particular way. Looking at your surroundings, all these things share something in common: they exist. They also have a very important *difference* — what they are, the particular way they exist. You are not the same as the tree, or the hydrogen atom, or the moon.

[00:07:04] All these things are the same insofar as they exist, but we need another reason or principle for why they're different. In the broadest possible sense, we call that co-principle (the real metaphysical part of things that makes them exist in a particular way) their *essence*. Existence, which in itself implies no limitation, is *limited* by another principle we call essence. All things which are not just existence itself must have this limiter — making them exist in a particular way and limiting them to it.

[00:07:36] For anything that is not existence itself, it must be composed of existence-as-limited-by-essence; it must have a cause for that composition and for why that composition exists at any moment. [00:08:07] To be existence itself is, actually, the name of God — the name God gives of himself as revealed to Moses in Exodus 3:14: "I am who am." In other words: *I am am. I am existence itself.* God just *is* existence, without any limiting essence or particular manner of existing.

**Article 5 — Is God contained in a genus?**

[00:08:37] Is God part of a larger category? Does he have parts — being in a category with a specific difference making him a species within it? Would he be in the category of *beings*, or *substances*? Because God exists, surely he'd be a substance within the larger category of substances. But the answer is no. He transcends category entirely.

[00:09:07] The easiest way to understand this is to think about what category God *would* fit in if he could. Based on God's revelation of himself, that category would be *being*. But every category is made up of a generic essence with things in it that differ by something that is *not* that generic essence. Take humankind: we have in common with many other things that we are bodily, we have senses, we are animal — that's the generic essence of our category. Our specific difference, *not* included in the notion of animal essentially, is that we are rational, with an immortal and rational soul. That distinguishes us within the larger category of animals.

[00:10:11] So in the category of being: things within it would have to be distinguished by something that is *not* being — that is not existence — but that is *nothing*, literally. So being itself cannot be a category — because what would be outside that category? Nothing. And since God is being, then he too must transcend category entirely.

[00:10:42] In this way, it's actually less precise to call God *a* being, as if he existed within a larger category of being. **Article 8 — Whether God enters into the composition of other things**

[00:12:17] If God is entirely simple, what do we mean when we say God exists *in* all things? ### Q.4 — The perfection of God

**Article 1 — Is God perfect?**

[00:14:25] Now this seems like a silly question, but as Aquinas will note, some thought that the most perfect thing was *matter* — that most fundamental potency at the bottom of all material things, since it was what everything was made out of and came from. But, Aquinas will say, it is true the plant came from a seed — but where did that seed come from? It must come from the plant. Pure matter — pure potency, without actual existence, just potential — is not going to be more perfect than existence itself.

[00:14:54] Also, the Latin root here, *perficere*, can mean "to complete" or "to be completed" — that's where we get the word *perfect*. So to say God is perfect may sound like saying he *has been* perfected, implying change and improvement, all of which we have to deny of God. So if we take "perfect" here to mean *that which is not lacking any perfection of existence*, then yes, absolutely, God is perfect — something completely devoid of any potential.

**Article 2 — Whether the perfections of all things are in God**

[00:15:30] The answer is found in what we call the *principle of proportionate causality* — the thesis that whatever exists in the effect must pre-exist in some way in its cause. For most of us that should feel like common sense: if the effect is me teaching you how to pray the *Our Father*, I must first know it myself. It must be in me before I can give it to you. If not, it would have come out of nothing or nowhere — but from nothing, nothing comes.

[00:16:45] So how do these effects (all created things) pre-exist in their cause (God)? Dr. Ed Feser has a brilliant treatment of this. He gives a couple of ways:

- **Formally** — like if you receive a $20 bill from me because I first had a $20 bill in my hands and gave it to you. I had it first, in just the same way as I give it to you.
- **Virtually** — maybe I don't actually have the $20 bill in my hand right now, but I have it in my bank account. So at this moment the effect of giving you $20 pre-exists in me — not formally or actually, but *virtually* — because I can go get that money and come back and give it to you.
- **Eminently** — if I had a printing machine. What was in the effect was first in the cause, but eminently — in the cause by virtue of the cause having something *even greater* than a $20 bill, namely the power to generate $20 bills.

[00:18:30] How the perfections of all things are in God is like that last way: they are in him, but in a manner greater and simpler than we see them out here as effects. Think of white light hitting a prism. All the colors of the rainbow, in this analogy, are contained in that white light — but not in the same way as actually being purple or red or green.

**Article 3 — Whether any creature can be like God**

[00:19:30] As Catholics, our minds probably turn immediately to Genesis 1:26: "Let us make man in our image and after our likeness" — that we were to be like God. But how can things be alike or compared to one another if they're not in the same category? How is bright like sweet? How can we, rational animals, be like him who transcends category entirely?

[00:20:15] Even Dionysius will say "Holy Scripture declares none are like God," and he might be thinking of Psalm 86: "There is none like thee among the gods, O Lord." Now, although nothing is like God in the way an apple tree is like other trees it creates — *since* God is entirely unique, simple, transcending all category — insofar as all categories exist, everything in a category exists, everything created exists in its own limited way, *insofar as it exists* it bears a kind of similarity to existence itself, which is God, from which all has ultimately derived its origin. In this way creatures can be like God.

[00:20:45] As Aquinas says: "The likeness of creatures to God is not affirmed on account of agreement in form according to the formality of the same genus or species, but solely according to analogy, inasmuch as God is essential being whereas other things are beings by participation."
