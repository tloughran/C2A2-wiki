# Day 93 — The Virtuous Mean

*Summa Theologiae*, Prima Secundae, Q.64 (Of the mean of virtue) and Q.65 (Of the connection of virtues).

## Transcript

[00:05] Day 93. Today we cover Q.64 and Q.65 of the Prima Secundae: *of the mean of virtue* and *of the connection of virtues*.

[00:54] **Article 1: Whether moral virtues observe the mean.** What we mean by "the mean" in this context is the rule of reason, or the middle between excess and deficiency in operations and passions. Since the virtues are good dispositions in the appetite disposing it to the rule of reason, then the moral virtues do observe the mean. Keep in mind: the mean, because it is simply conformity with right reason, may entail total abstinence in some cases — which might not seem like the mean, but is in fact aligned with reason in certain circumstances, such as in the case of those who take vows of virginity or poverty in a religious order. As St. Thomas says: "The good of moral virtue consists in conformity with the rule of reason. Now it is clear that between excess and deficiency the mean is equality or conformity. Therefore it is evident that moral virtue observes the mean. And virginity abstains from all sexual matters, and poverty from all wealth, for a right end and in a right manner — i.e., according to God's word and for the sake of eternal life."

[02:14] **Article 2: Whether the mean of moral virtue is the real mean or the rational mean.** In matters of justice, the "real mean" would be the objective debt due, determined irrespective of the individual. If the bottle of water costs a dollar at the store, the real mean in a just operation of purchasing that water is to give a dollar in exchange. However, other virtues like fortitude or temperance have a mean based on our own prudence, which can be variable as the individual is. Each man's temperament — unique fears, desires — has a middle or mean which takes into account individual differences. As St. Thomas says: "The other moral virtues, unlike justice, deal with interior passions wherein the right cannot be established in the same way, since men are variously situated in relation to their passions. Hence the rectitude of reason has to be established in the passions with due regard to us who are moved in respect of the passions."

[03:27] **Article 3: Whether the intellectual virtues observe the mean.** Yes — but not a conformity to right reason (since reason is precisely what we are dealing with here), but a conformity to reality. When a man as a rational animal makes a judgment, that judgment observes the mean by matching reality — by being a true judgment. As Aquinas puts it: "Both in practical and speculative intellectual virtues the mean consists in conformity with things. But if we consider it in relation to the appetite, it has the character of a rule and measure."

[04:27] **Article 4: Whether the theological virtues observe the mean.** Can we love, hope in, or believe in God too much or too little? Definitely too little — but if we take God's goodness, his truth, his providence and loving-kindness as the measure, we could never measure up to or respond in an appropriately immense corresponding way. Faith, hope, and love therefore ought to spur us on to an ever-increasing faith, hope, or love. The theological virtues can only be called into excess or deficiency in the sense that, for example, presumption or despair would be considered perversions or deficiencies of the virtue of hope.

[05:28] **Question 65: Of the connection of virtues. Article 1: Whether the moral virtues are connected with one another.** This restates and deepens Day 91's chiasmus: in order to have one of the moral virtues, you really have to have all of them, because one cannot have right reason in action (prudence) unless one is already just, temperate, and courageous — the unjust, cowardly, or intemperate end can never be carried out prudently. And even if one is just, temperate, and courageous, without prudence the intended end will be carried out poorly. In St. Thomas's words: "Right choice requires not only the inclination to a due end, which inclination is the direct outcome of moral virtue, but also correct choice of things conducive to the end, which choice is made by prudence that counsels, judges, and commands... In like manner, one cannot have prudence unless one has the moral virtues, since prudence is right reason about things to be done, and the starting point of reason is the end of the thing to be done, to which end man is rightly disposed by moral virtue."

[07:00] **Article 2: Whether moral virtues can be without charity.** Can one be prudent, just, temperate, and courageous without the supernatural habitual love of God? Aquinas: "Only the infused virtues are perfect and deserve to be called virtues simply, since they direct man well to the ultimate end. But the other virtues — those namely that are acquired — are virtues in a restricted sense but not simply, for they direct man well in respect of the last end in some particular genus of action but not in respect of the last end simply." An acquired prudence/justice/temperance/courage can direct a man rightly in some particular category of actions — like acquiring a $1.24 item for $1.24 — but since the acquired virtues are not sufficient to direct our actions well toward our ultimate end (the beatific vision: "no eye has seen, nor ear heard, nor the heart of man conceived, what God has prepared for those who love him"), they seem to be missing a critical piece within the very definition of virtue.

[09:00] **Article 3: Whether charity can be without moral virtue.** In the same way that God would not create a rational animal without also giving us the means to carry out that rationality (a body with various organs and sensitive powers), so too God does not pour charity into the heart of man without the accompanying infused moral virtues required for us to properly pursue that goal. He would not give us the end without also providing the means. "It is therefore clear that the infused moral virtues are connected, not only through prudence, but also on account of charity — and that whoever loses charity through mortal sin forfeits all the infused moral virtues."

[09:51] **Article 4: Whether faith and hope can be without charity.** One can have a supernatural faith and hope in God without charity, but these virtues will be imperfect — because without charity our will is in a way set against God, leading to an imperfect faith; and without the merits which stem from charity, one will have an imperfect hope. As St. Thomas says: "Perfect virtue is that which gives the faculty of doing a perfectly good work, and this consists in not only doing what is good but also in doing it well. Consequently, faith and hope can be without charity, yet without charity they are not virtues properly so-called."

[11:00] **Article 5: Whether charity can be without faith and hope.** We cannot love that which we do not first know; we cannot love God as the object of our supernatural beatitude unless we first know that that end exists and is concretely available to us. Charity is "not any kind of love of God, but that love of God by which he is loved as the object of bliss, to which object we are directed by faith and hope." Charity itself denotes a kind of divinization in us, a communion or friendship with the divine: "Just as friendship with a person would be impossible if one disbelieved in or despaired of the possibility of their fellowship, so too friendship with God, which is charity, is impossible without faith so as to believe in this fellowship, and to hope to attain to this fellowship."

## Notes

- Q.64.1's note that the "mean" can entail **total abstinence** is a quiet logical bombshell: the Aristotelian doctrine of the mean is not a mathematical midpoint but a *fit-with-reason* relation. This rescues the doctrine from caricature but also opens it to redescription as something more like an "optimal-policy-under-constraints" claim.
- Q.64.2's bifurcation (real mean in justice, rational mean elsewhere) cleanly separates *objective social facts* (price, debt, contract) from *individual-state-sensitive optima* (fear thresholds, appetite thresholds). The Friston / active-inference frame would call these different precision-weightings on different generative models.
- Q.64.3 reframes intellectual virtue's "mean" as **truth-conformity**, not balance — a different kind of mean. Aquinas is honest about the equivocation.
- Q.65 is the heart of the cluster: the doctrine of the **connection of the virtues** is structurally a *network*-claim. The cardinal virtues co-exist or co-fail. The infused virtues additionally co-attach to charity. Cut charity, you cut the whole infused virtue network at once.
- Q.65.5's argument that charity presupposes faith ("you cannot love what you do not know") needs care in a Levin/Hoffman-style framing where agentic knowledge is much more graded than the binary medieval picture.
