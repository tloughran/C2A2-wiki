# Day 78 — Sorrow and Pain (Contemporary)

## Frame

Aquinas in Q.34–35 establishes the moral character of pleasure (between Stoic rejection and Epicurean over-affirmation) and then turns to pain and sorrow as such. The pivotal contemporary-bridging claim is Q.35 a.7: *inward pain (sorrow) exceeds outward pain (bodily)* because it engages a higher faculty (intellect/imagination) and more directly. The synthesis writes from a vantage in which this claim is *formally* predictable from contemporary hierarchical-inference models of mind, rather than merely intuitive.

## Problem

The shared problem is *the hierarchy and irreducibility of suffering*. Aquinas needs to distinguish pain from sorrow (Q.35 a.2) — bodily pain regards only the present and arises from external apprehension; sorrow regards past, present, and future and arises from interior apprehension. He needs to say *why* inward suffering is more severe (a.7) without making bodily suffering trivial. And he needs to taxonomize sorrow itself (a.8) — pity, envy, anxiety, torpor — without losing the unity of the passion.

Under predictive-processing models the problem becomes mathematically tractable. The mind is hierarchical: low-level prediction errors (exteroceptive — touch, sight, nociception) flow up; high-level prediction errors (interoceptive, affective, narrative-level) flow up and down. Errors at higher levels have larger downstream consequences because they revise priors that propagate across many lower domains. Aquinas's intuition about the priority of interior pain is exactly this insight.

## Resource

**Friston's active inference** (Friston wiki) is the formal home for Q.35's distinctions. Bodily pain is *exteroceptive* prediction error registering a present mismatch (tissue damage, thermal noxiousness). Sorrow is *interoceptive and self-modeling* prediction error: revision-pressure on the model of one's own state, one's relationships, one's narrative future. Q.35 a.2's claim that sorrow regards past, present, and future falls straight out: the generative model that yields sorrow is precisely the temporally-extended self-model. Q.35 a.7's priority of inward pain is the mathematical fact that errors at the apex of the predictive hierarchy have higher precision-weighting and larger consequences for action policy.

**Fredrickson's positivity-resonance program** (Fredrickson PRS-04, PRS-11) provides the biological grounding for Q.34 a.4 (pleasure as diagnostic of will-state). Vagal tone is a real biomarker of where the body is *actually* resting; the inflammation-gating finding (PRS-11; West et al. 2025) shows that bodily state constrains which goods are even available as objects of resonance. Aquinas's claim that pleasure reveals the will gets a mechanistic substrate.

**McGilchrist's hemispheric framework** sits underneath Q.34 a.4. The right hemisphere is the receptive, contextualizing mode; the left is the categorizing, manipulating mode. A will resting in *real* goods (right-hemisphere openness to the world as gift) versus apparent goods (left-hemisphere construction of objects to consume) is the difference Aquinas tracks affectively — McGilchrist gives it neurological coordinates.

**Kastrup on dissociation** lets the synthesis read Q.35 a.8's four species — pity, envy, anxiety, torpor — as types of constrained alter-state. Pity is sorrow open to another's perspective (alter-loosening toward outward). Envy is sorrow closed against another's perspective (alter-rigidification with respect to the comparison). Anxiety is sorrow that cannot find a model in which to dwell (high free-energy with no available action). Torpor is sorrow that has *collapsed* into a degenerate model — the depressive-anhedonic limit case.

**Stump on suffering** (Wandering in Darkness) and **Rohr on contemplation** contribute the practical-theological layer. Stump's contention that suffering is integrable into love-of-persons coheres with Friston's epistemic-value layer: even unwanted prediction errors carry epistemic content. Rohr's contemplative tradition gives the practice by which the right-hemispheric, receptive mode is cultivated and torpor is interrupted.

## Solution (proposed, speculative)

**(a) Shared with Aquinas.** The distinction between bodily pain and sorrow, the priority of inward suffering, and the four-fold taxonomy are all preserved. The phenomenology Aquinas describes is exactly the phenomenology contemporary predictive-processing models predict.

**(b) Where it reframes.** Aquinas's Q.34 a.3 — pleasure as the *greatest good* only inasmuch as it is "perfect repose in the perfect good" — gets a monist reframe. The "perfect good" is not an external object but the substrate of conscious experience itself (Kastrup) or the mind-at-large from which the alter is dissociated. The repose-in-the-perfect is then *substrate-coherence* — the alter no longer holding a generative model that creates avoidable prediction error against its ground. This is not Aquinas's exact metaphysics, but the structural role of "rest in the perfect" is preserved.

**(c) Where evidence is firm vs. speculative.** *Firm*: hierarchical predictive processing as the mechanism for Q.35's distinctions; vagal-tone and inflammation findings for Q.34 a.4. *Suggestive*: McGilchrist's hemispheric account is a research program with real but contested grounding. *Speculative*: the Kastrup/idealist reading of the four species of sorrow, and the substrate-recovery account of "perfect repose."

## The central-theme thread

Q.35 a.7's "inward pain exceeds outward" is the moment where perspective-limitation becomes morally consequential. The agent who cannot integrate higher-order prediction errors — anxiety that finds no action, torpor that has collapsed the model — is most acutely perspective-limited and *least computationally able* to recover. The remedies arrive on Day 80 (friends, tears, contemplation, sleep, baths); under the central theme, these are the social and somatic resources by which a limited agent re-opens its model to receive evidence from outside its present perspective.

## Bridges

- **Friston, hierarchical active inference** — bodily pain = exteroceptive PE; sorrow = interoceptive/self-model PE; Q.35 a.7 follows from precision-weighted hierarchical inference; *empirical, mathematically robust*.
- **Fredrickson PRS-04, PRS-11** — Q.34 a.4 (pleasure as diagnostic of will) gets vagal-tone substrate; inflammation gates resonance radius; *empirical, high confidence*.
- **McGilchrist hemispheric framework** — right-hemispheric receptivity vs. left-hemispheric construction; underlies the real-good / apparent-good distinction; *neurologically suggestive, contested*.
- **Kastrup analytic idealism + dissociative alters** — Q.35 a.8 four species as constrained alter-states (pity, envy, anxiety, torpor); *speculative metaphysical, internally coherent*.
- **Stump, *Wandering in Darkness*** — suffering as integrable into second-person love; *theological/philosophical, well-developed within Thomism but not yet at mind-monism*.
- **Rohr, contemplative tradition** — practice by which receptive mode is cultivated; *theological, traditional*.

## Where this leaves us

Day 79 (Q.36–37) follows the same arc into the *causes and effects* of sorrow. Q.37 a.4's claim that sorrow is the *most physically harmful* of the passions is where Aquinas's intuition meets the modern psychoneuroimmunology literature. The open thread we carry: if sorrow's harm is mediated by HPA-axis and inflammatory load (Fredrickson PRS-11), then the *remedies* on Day 80 are not metaphors but mechanisms — sympathy, tears, contemplation, sleep, baths each have an identifiable physiological pathway. The synthesis can name this without losing the theological register Aquinas requires.
