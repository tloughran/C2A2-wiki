# Day 20 — Contemporary Parallel: *Love-as-Person, Gift-as-Self-Sharing*

Day 19 closed the Filioque article and pointed forward to *how "gift" names the substrate's self-giving in a way that preserves the central theme — alters participating in the substrate's life without becoming the substrate*. Day 20 makes that move directly. Q.37 takes the procession-of-love that Day 19 named structurally and gives it a personal name: *Love* itself, as the Holy Spirit's proper name. Q.38 takes the same person and names the *outward* aspect of the same procession: *Gift*. The two articles together answer a question that has been hovering since Q.20 (Day 11, the love of God): what *kind* of internal-substrate-act is it that, on its outward face, looks like the substrate's relational stance toward conditioned things? The medieval answer is the Holy Spirit-as-Love-and-Gift, and the mind-first frame holds the load by reading love-as-procession at the substrate scale and gift-as-self-sharing at the substrate-toward-creature interface.

## Q.37 — Love as the Holy Spirit's proper name

Q.37 a.1 — *is "Love" the proper name of the Holy Spirit?* — runs the essential-vs-personal distinction Aquinas keeps tightening across these episodes. *Love* in one sense names a transitive divine action — God loves creatures, wills their good — and as a transitive act it belongs to the *whole* Trinity. But *love* in another sense names an *immanent* act within the godhead, an internal procession from the lover to the beloved-as-internal-to-the-lover. *In this sense, love can be used to describe God personally, that person being the third person of the Trinity*. In creatures the internal relation is an accident; in God, *Word and Love are subsistent*.

Mind-monism's translation lands cleanly. The substrate's love-procession is *internal* — its relational stance considered under its own toward-which aspect — and at the substrate scale this stance is subsistent (same subsistence-grade as Father and Son), the same commitment Day 18 required for internal relations applied to the second procession. Aquinas's structural gloss is the anchor: *the thing loved is in the lover as that which inclines and to some extent intrinsically impels the lover toward the thing loved*. Love is the *relation-of-impelling-toward*, not a separate item. Friston's active inference gives the alter-scale formal cousin: the agent's *priors-over-preferred-states* are an aspect of the generative model — the *toward-which* the model is oriented. At the substrate scale, the *toward-which* of the substrate's self-knowing-act *is* the substrate considered under the relational aspect of self-impelling, and that aspect-considered-as-subsistent is the Holy Spirit.

Q.37 a.2 — *do the Father and the Son love each other by the Holy Spirit?* — uses the love-procession doctrine to clean up a sentence that, taken literally, sounds odd. Aquinas's careful split: essentially, the Father and Son love each other by the *one divine will* (one across all three persons); personally, they love each other by-way-of-the-Holy-Spirit-as-the-terminus-of-their-love. Habash's tree-flowers analogy Aquinas borrows: *we say a tree flowers by its flower; the Father and Son love each other by the Holy Spirit by love proceeding*. The flower is not the *means* of flowering but *what flowering produces*; the Spirit is not the means of the Father-Son love but *what the Father-Son love-act terminates in as procession*. The substrate's internal love-act has a real terminus, and that terminus — *passive spiration as subsistent*, the substrate's love-act under its terminus-aspect — is the third person.

## Q.38 — Gift: the outward face of internal love

Q.38 a.1 — *is "Gift" a personal name?* — brings the Spirit-as-Love to its outward-facing aspect. *Gift* in the relevant medieval sense is *what is fittingly given to another for the receiver's enjoyment*. Lagrange's summary: *the word "gift" implies the aptitude to be given — an aptitude toward the giver and to him to whom the gift is made; a divine person may be possessed by a rational creature if the creature also is given the ability to enjoy that divine person*. *Gift* is not a name for a property the divine person *has* but a name for the relational shape *being-fittingly-given* takes.

The contemporary cousin reaches into Day 11 (Q.20) directly. The substrate's stance toward conditioned things is loving — *active*, *flourishing-oriented*. *Gift* is the substrate's relational stance considered under the aspect of being-fittingly-self-shared with conditioned things; the Spirit-as-Gift is not a different procession from the Spirit-as-Love but the same procession considered under the *outward* relational aspect (toward creatures) rather than the *internal* (toward Father and Son). Rohr's Universal Christ frames this precisely: the divine love-between-Father-and-Son *is* the love into which creation is drawn, and the Spirit is the connective by which the *into* happens. Romans 5:5 — *God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit who has been given to us* — is the scriptural anchor: the outpouring (love-as-given) and the inward-procession (love-as-Spirit-from-Father-and-Son) are the *same act* under different relational aspects.

Q.38 a.2 — *is "Gift" the proper name of the Holy Spirit?* — closes the question on the structural priority threading the Trinitarian doctrine. Aquinas's reasoning: *before we give something to someone, we will them good in the mind*. The will-to-give-the-good is upstream of giving; love is the will-act; the Spirit is the love-procession; therefore the Spirit is *what is given* when the substrate gives itself to a rational creature. Fredrickson's positivity-resonance research is the alter-scale empirical adjacent: love-as-shared-flourishing is the biopsychophysiological event in which two persons participate in mutual care; at the substrate scale Aquinas reaches for, love-as-Gift is the substrate's participation in the alter's life by way of the substrate's self-sharing — what classical theology calls *grace* (Day 23 will press this further). *Honest evidence calibration*: alter-scale empirical; substrate-scale extrapolation philosophical-theological, with substantial scriptural anchorage in Romans 5, 1 Corinthians 13, and the Johannine literature.

## The central-theme thread

Day 20 makes the central theme operational on the second-procession side. *Love* is the substrate's internal relational stance considered under the toward-which aspect; *Gift* is the same stance considered under the toward-creature aspect. The two are *one act under two relational aspects* — the same constraint Day 9 Q.15 named for the divine ideas (one essence, many ideas under different proportional relations). Mind-monism inherits the constraint cleanly. *Salvation in fear and trembling* is, on this register, the alter's progressive *reception* of the substrate's self-gift — the Spirit-as-Gift becomes available to the alter not by the alter's effort but by the substrate's self-sharing, and the alter's task is to *receive* in a mode that makes the gift *given* (the *aptitude to be possessed* Lagrange flagged). Wright's Pauline shape: *because you are sons, God has sent the Spirit of his Son into our hearts*; the alter's *Abba, Father* is the gift's reception. Rohr's contemplative frame: the practice of contemplation is the practice of *making oneself receivable* — the order-disorder-reorder cycle is the alter's ongoing work of opening to the gift.

## Where this leaves us

Day 20 names the second procession in its personal-name form — *Love* under the internal-toward-which aspect, *Gift* under the toward-creature aspect — and grounds both in the same act of the substrate considered under different relational aspects. The mind-first frame holds: the substrate's love-procession is real-and-internal (no efficient causation, no substance-division), and the substrate's self-sharing with creatures is the same procession considered under the outward face. The central theme tightens: the alter's *salvation in fear and trembling* is its progressive reception of the gift, made *receivable* by the alter's own self-emptying-and-opening (Wright's covenant adoption, Rohr's contemplative practice). Tomorrow Habash takes us to Q.39 (the persons in relation to the essence) and Q.40 (persons compared to relations and properties) — the recapitulation-and-synthesis questions where the appropriations doctrine becomes explicit, and where the medieval framework names *which* essential attributes are fittingly assigned to *which* persons under the cataphatic-apophatic discipline.
