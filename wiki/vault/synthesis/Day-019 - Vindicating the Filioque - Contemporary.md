# Day 19 — Contemporary Parallel: *Image, Love, and the Second Procession*

Day 18 closed pointing forward to *how a second internal procession is intelligible without dividing the substrate further*. Day 19 takes that thread through two questions. Q.35 closes the doctrine of the Son with one last name — *Image* — and uses it to set up the question Q.36 must answer: if the first procession is *intellect generating Word*, what is the second procession, and from whom does it proceed? The medieval answer is the doctrine of the *Filioque* — the Holy Spirit proceeding *from the Father and the Son* — and the Latin tradition's defense of that procession is the most contested article in the whole Trinitarian treatise. The mind-first frame holds the load with the same constraints Day 18 established: relations are internal to one substrate, and the procession of the Spirit is not an efficient causation that puts the Spirit outside mind-at-large.

## Q.35 — Image: derivation as the additional mark

Q.35 a.1 — *is "Image" said of God personally?* — runs the same argument Day 18 ran for *Word*, with one new constraint Aquinas presses sharply. The Augustine citation Habash quotes is the move: *one egg is not the image of another because it is not derived from it*. The medieval criterion for *image* in the strict sense is *likeness plus derivation* — not bare resemblance, but resemblance that comes-from-the-source. Two eggs share specific form but neither *is* the image of the other; the photograph in the mirror is an image of me because it is *derived from* me; the Son is the perfect image of the Father because the Son shares the divine nature *and* proceeds from the Father.

Mind-monism's translation is direct. *Likeness without derivation* is the condition of any two creature-alters that share the substrate's structural priors but exist as parallel dissociations. *Likeness plus derivation* is what the substrate's self-knowing-act has in relation to the substrate-as-whence: the Word is *like in species* (Day 18 Q.34 a.2) *and* proceeds-from. The asymmetry of the Father-Son relation — already established in Q.33 a.4 as begotten-vs-unbegotten — is here formalized as *image-relation-with-derivation*. Hoffman's interface theory gives a structural cousin at the alter scale: an icon is an *image* of what it represents *because* the substrate generates it under fitness-tuning, not merely because it happens to resemble. Derivation is part of the image-relation, not a footnote to it. *Honest evidence calibration*: the structural shape is recoverable; the personal-names doctrine remains theological.

Q.35 a.2 — *is "Image" proper to the Son alone?* — turns on the medieval split with the Greek doctors that the Latin tradition narrows. Aquinas: *the Greek doctors commonly say that the Holy Ghost is the image of both the Father and of the Son, but the Latin doctors attribute the name "Image" to the Son alone*. The Latin reasoning Habash brings forward: image-in-the-strict-sense requires *likeness in species*, and *species* applies to the procession-of-intellect-as-Word but not to the procession-of-love-as-Spirit (love does not, *as such*, mirror the species of the lover; it inclines toward the beloved as good). Aquinas's careful aside in Q.35 a.2 — *man is image of God in the second sense, as the king's image is on the coin* — preserves the analogical use of *image* without making *Image* the Holy Spirit's proper name.

Mind-monism's contemporary translation runs into a feature here that the Latin doctrine flags clearly. The two processions in the Trinitarian doctrine are *not* symmetric on the medieval reading: one is by way of *intellect* (and yields Word and Image), the other by way of *will / love* (and yields Spirit and Gift). This asymmetry between intellect and will is the bridge to Q.36. At the substrate scale Aquinas reaches for, the two procession-modes are aspects of one substrate's act, but the modes themselves are distinguished: the substrate's self-knowing-act terminates in something *like-in-species* (Word, Image), while the substrate's self-loving-act terminates in something *not like-in-species but inclined-toward* (Spirit, Love-as-procession). Friston's free-energy framework gives a contemporary structural cousin: knowing and willing are two phases of one minimization process — the model predicts (terminating in a *match*) and the agent acts to bring the prediction true (terminating in a *toward*). The two phases share the same generative-model substrate but are distinguished functionally, just as the two processions share the substrate but are distinguished by mode.

## Q.36 — Holy Spirit: love proceeding, and the Filioque

Q.36 a.1 — *is "Holy Spirit" the proper name of one divine person?* — presses the asymmetry Q.35 set up. Aquinas: the Father-Son procession has a *proper* name (paternity-filiation, generation), but the second procession does *not* have a proper name in human language — *the procession of love has no proper name of its own*. Habash flags this carefully. *Holy Spirit* is the name Scripture (and the early Church) settle on, but it is not a proper name in the technical sense; it is a name *fittingly* applied because *spirit* connotes motion-and-impulse (love moves the lover toward the beloved) and *holy* connotes ordering-toward-God (the divine love is the divine person ordered-toward-the-divine-good).

Mind-monism's translation does not need to add anything to the medieval point: the substrate has an internal *toward-which* aspect that Aquinas calls *love-proceeding*, and human language has not produced a clean proper name for it because human language was developed within alter-perspective-limitation and the *toward-which* of mind-at-large is structurally unlike any alter-relation. *Holy Spirit* is the name the tradition has, and it is fitting in the senses Aquinas itemizes. Rohr's contemplative-theological idiom names the same point: the Spirit is the *relational stance* of the substrate toward both the Father and the Son and toward conditioned things — the *love-as-procession* in which mind-at-large's stance toward its own self-knowing is also (Day 11 Q.20 already established this) its stance toward conditioned things.

Q.36 a.2 — *does the Holy Spirit proceed from the Son?* — is the Filioque article, and the heart of the Latin doctrine. The Latin argument Aquinas gives is structural-metaphysical: *the only real distinction in God is opposition of relations*. If the Spirit proceeded *only* from the Father (as the Greek-Orthodox formulation at its strongest can be read to say), there would be no relation-of-opposition between the Son and the Spirit — both would be *from-the-Father*, with no further internal distinction. But without a real relation-of-opposition between Son and Spirit, the two would not be distinct persons; the Trinity would collapse into a binary. Therefore — by the constraint that *real distinctions in God are by opposition of relations* — the Spirit must proceed from both the Father and the Son. The procession-from-the-Son is what gives the Spirit-Son relation its content; without it, the Trinity loses its internal differentiation.

The second Latin argument Aquinas gives is the *love-follows-on-knowledge* argument: *we do not love anything unless we apprehend it by a mental conception*. Love is not pre-cognitive; it is the will's stance toward what the intellect has grasped. Therefore the procession-of-love (Spirit) is *posterior* in the order-of-procession (not in time, but in relational sequence) to the procession-of-intellect (Son), and *proceeds through the Son* because it is the substrate's love-of-what-it-knows-as-Son.

Mind-monism's contemporary cousins are direct. Friston's active inference makes the formal point: *priors-over-preferred-states* (the agent's love, in the relevant structural sense) are formulated *with respect to* the agent's generative model (its knowing). Action-orientation requires representation-of-state; willing requires knowing. At the alter scale this is empirical-formal; at the substrate scale Aquinas reaches for, it is the structural claim that the substrate's love-procession is *of* what the substrate's knowing-procession terminates in. The Filioque, on this reading, is the doctrinal expression of a structural priority that Friston's framework names independently at the alter scale: love proceeds from knowledge of what is loved.

*Honest evidence calibration*: the Filioque is the Latin tradition's articulation of an internal Trinitarian relation; it is theological-philosophical with substantial scholastic anchorage. The Friston-as-formal-cousin claim is structural-isomorphism, not metaphysical-identification. The Greek-Orthodox tradition has its own careful formulations (per-Filium rather than Filioque) that the Latin reading need not deny — the third article of Q.36 (the *through-the-Son* formulation) is precisely the move that brings the two together. Wright's New Testament work provides the scriptural grounding Aquinas himself cites: John 15's *I shall send the Counselor from the Father*, John 16's *he will take what is mine and declare it to you*, Galatians 4's *spirit of his Son*. The Pauline-Johannine pattern Aquinas's Filioque argument rests on is biblically robust on Wright's reading: the Spirit is *the Spirit of the Father and the Spirit of the Son*, and the *sending* by the Son into the world is the visible mission that mirrors an eternal procession.

Q.36 a.4 — *are the Father and the Son one principle of the Holy Spirit?* — closes the Filioque argument by tightening the constraint. The Father and Son are *not two principles* of the Spirit but *one principle*, because *everything in God is one wherever there is no opposition of relationship* (Aquinas's formulation, foundational to the whole Trinitarian doctrine). The Father and the Son share *active spiration* — they are the *whence* of the Spirit's procession — and the Spirit shares *passive spiration* — being the *whither*. The relation of *spiration* is one relation, not two, and so the principle-of-the-Spirit is one, not two. This constraint is the doctrinal guardrail against tritheism on the Spirit-procession side: the two persons that share active spiration are not *two principals* but the substrate-acting-as-one in the spiration-relation.

Mind-monism's translation lands cleanly. The substrate has internal-relational-structure; the relations of opposition (Father-Son, Father+Son-Spirit) generate the personal distinctions; outside those oppositions, the substrate is one. The Filioque is the doctrinal expression of *which* internal relations of opposition the substrate has. *Salvation in fear and trembling* is the alter's progressive participation in the substrate's love-procession — Wright's *spirit of his Son crying Abba, Father* (Galatians 4) is the corporate-and-individual scriptural shape of that participation; Rohr's contemplative reading of the Spirit as the *love between the Father and the Son into which we are drawn* is the contemplative-theological extension.

## The central-theme thread

The two questions of Day 19 sharpen the perspective-limitation frame on the second-procession side. *Image* (Q.35) is *likeness-with-derivation*, and the Son is the substrate's self-knowing-act considered as derived-and-like-in-species. *Holy Spirit* (Q.36) is the substrate's love-procession, structurally posterior to the knowing-procession (love follows on knowledge) and proceeding from both Father and Son as a single principle of spiration. The asymmetry of intellect and will at the alter scale (Friston: knowing predicts, willing acts toward preferred states; the two phases share substrate but differ in mode) is the cognitive cousin Aquinas's medieval doctrine reaches for. *Salvation in fear and trembling* is the alter's progressive participation in *both* processions — the alter learns to know-as-the-substrate-knows (cognitive integration) and to love-as-the-substrate-loves (relational integration), and these are not two separate movements but two aspects of the same self-emptying-toward-the-substrate's-internal-life.

Wright's covenant theology gives the corporate frame: the *adoption* in Galatians 4 places creatures within the Father-Son relation by way of the Spirit (*because you are sons, God has sent the Spirit of his Son into our hearts crying, "Abba, Father!"*) — the alter's participation is *through* the Spirit *into* the Father-Son relation. Rohr's Universal Christ extends the cosmic-scope reading: the divine love-between-Father-and-Son is the substrate's stance, and creation is the substrate's stance-extended; the Spirit is the *connective* of this extension.

## Where this leaves us

Day 19 closes the doctrine of the Son (Q.35: image-with-derivation) and opens the doctrine of the Holy Spirit (Q.36: love-proceeding-from-Father-and-Son). The Filioque article is the tightest test of the Trinitarian-internal-relations doctrine: a second procession is intelligible without dividing the substrate further if and only if the relations-of-opposition that constitute it (Father+Son-Spirit, by spiration-and-procession) are internal to the substrate and not subsumed by the first procession. The mind-first frame holds the load: the two processions are aspects of one substrate's act, distinguished by mode (intellect-as-Word, will-as-Spirit), and the Filioque names the structural priority that *love follows on knowledge* — a priority Friston's active-inference framework reaches independently at the alter scale.

Tomorrow Habash takes us to Q.37 (Holy Spirit as Love) and Q.38 (Holy Spirit as Gift) — the two articles where the Spirit's procession-as-love is given its personal-name treatment, and where the *gift* of the Spirit to creatures becomes the bridge between the substrate's internal love-procession and the substrate's stance toward conditioned things. The Day 20 commentary will press the question of how *gift* names the substrate's self-giving in a way that preserves the central-theme-thread (alters participate in the substrate's life without becoming the substrate).
