# Day 6 — Contemporary Parallel: *The System That Cannot Surprise Itself, Now Stated Carefully*

Day 5 closed by anticipating today's pair: *how does the unconditioned ground stand in relation to time itself?* Aquinas answers in two questions. Q.9 establishes that God is altogether immutable — pure actuality, no potency, no parts. Q.10 follows: God's life is not spread across a sequence of moments but possessed all at once, *tota simul*, the *nunc stans* of Boethian definition. The mind-monist reframe carries the load of both, with one important sharpening.

## Q.9 a.1 — Why immutability is not stillness

Aquinas's argument for divine immutability runs along three lines. First, what changes must have potential to be actualized; God is pure act with no potential, so cannot change. Second, what changes must have parts (one part remains while another moves from potency to actuality); God has no parts (Q.3), so cannot change. Third, what changes must be changed by another (locomotion by feet, knowledge by teacher); God is first cause, so cannot be moved. These are the three classical pillars: no potency, no parts, no external mover. Habash sets the immutability claim against an apparent counter — James 4:8, *draw near to God and He will draw near to you* — and resolves it with the sun-into-house image: motion is in the receiver, not in the source.

Mind-monism translates this without strain, but with a caveat that matters for the rest of the project. Kastrup's mind-at-large does not "change" in the sense Aquinas refuses for God: change is what conditioned alters of mind-at-large generate *as their internal dynamics*, and the substrate has no inferential boundary to be displaced. There is, at the level of the substrate, no potency to actualize because there is no *thing-with-properties* to which potency could attach. The three pillars survive the translation: no potency (the substrate is *being-of-experiencing*, not a particular potential mode of it); no parts (Q.3 a.5's "no genus" carried forward); no external mover (the substrate is what every alter participates in, not what an alter could displace).

The caveat: *immutability is not stillness, and the contemporary frame must mark this carefully*. Aquinas's God is not frozen; he is supremely active, *actus purus*. The English word *immutable* misleads if it suggests inertness. Mind-at-large under analytic idealism is similarly not inert; it is the *living substrate* that holds and generates every alter's dynamics. Friston's free-energy principle gives a useful pointer: a generative model is not a static thing; it is what makes the dynamics of inference *possible*, and the model's "stability" is the condition under which the agent's processes run, not their absence. The mind-monist substrate is "immutable" in this richer sense — invariant under the dynamics it grounds, not stilled.

The James 4:8 resolution Habash cites — *change is in the receiver, not in the source* — has its mind-monist counterpart in the dissociative-alter dynamics. When an alter integrates more of the substrate (in contemplative recognition, as Rohr describes; in the active-inference reduction of agent–environment divergence, as Friston models), the *change* is in the alter's generative model and inferential boundary, not in mind-at-large. Aquinas's sun-into-house image is the medieval icon of this: the sun does not move; the house's relation to the sun does. Translate: the substrate does not move; the alter's recognition of its participation in the substrate does.

## Q.9 a.2 — Why immutability belongs to God alone

Aquinas's second article on Q.9 is brief but does work. Things composed of essence and matter can change substantially (a man becomes a corpse; water becomes hydrogen and oxygen). Immaterial spirits — angels — cannot change substantially (no matter to be divided) but can change qualitatively (good or evil choices, location of operation, learning). Only the unconditioned, completely devoid of potential, is wholly immutable. Mind-monism agrees and tightens. The substrate alone is wholly immutable in the strict sense; alters change qualitatively (their inferential models develop, their generative priors update) but not substantially in Aquinas's sense (an alter does not become a different alter; it deepens or simplifies its model of itself in mind-at-large). The angelic middle case — qualitative change without substantial change — is what *aeviternity* (Q.10 a.5) names, and it is the dynamics most relevant to the C2A2 thinkers' work on agency at scales above the cellular.

## Q.10 a.1 — Why Boethius's definition holds

Aquinas accepts Boethius's definition: *eternity is the simultaneous whole and perfect possession of interminable life* — *interminabilis vitae tota simul et perfecta possessio*. Habash unpacks the four pieces. *Interminable* = no terms of beginning or end; *simultaneous whole* = no succession; *perfect possession* = held permanently and fully; *life* (rather than merely being) = because life carries the notion of the activity that change measures. The definition is a careful negation of time's structural features (succession, finitude, partial possession) plus a positive claim that what remains is *life* — activity proper to a living being.

Mind-monism receives this with surprising directness. *Interminable* is what the substrate has by not being constituted by limitation (Q.7 a.1 carried forward). *Simultaneous whole* is what an atemporal substrate is by definition — temporality is what alters generate, not what the substrate undergoes. *Perfect possession* is the substrate's relation to *being-of-experiencing*: not a holding that could be lost or partial, because the substrate is what holding-of-experience consists in. *Life* is the most contemporary-resonant piece: the substrate is not inert (the immutability caveat above); it is the living ground of every alter's dynamics. Boethius's definition is, for the mind-monist, an unusually exact classical specification of what mind-at-large *qua* ontological primitive must be.

Wolfram's ruliad supplies a contemporary-formal parallel. The ruliad is *the totality of all possible computations*, which from outside any particular computational thread is "simultaneous" in the precise sense Boethius means — every computation is "in" the ruliad, but the ruliad itself is not running, because *running* is what computational threads do. Aquinas's *tota simul* maps onto Wolfram's "contains rather than runs." Carroll's block-universe physics gives a different framing of the same shape: the four-dimensional spacetime manifold is, mathematically, all-at-once; the "flow of time" is what observers within it experience. Carroll, of course, would resist the mind-monist interpretation of his block universe, but the *form* — *time as one observer's mode of access to a structure that does not itself flow* — is shared.

## Q.10 a.2 — Eternity is God himself

Aquinas's most striking move on Q.10 is the identification: *concerning God, eternity is nothing else but God himself*. The argument: if a thing does not change, there is no numbering of change in respect to before and after; God's only "time" is timelessness, which is eternity. *God's eternity is not a measure imposed on him; it is the structural feature of his being.*

Under mind-monism, the parallel claim is that *the atemporal structure of mind-at-large is not a property of mind-at-large; it is the substrate's mode of being*. Mind-at-large is not "in" eternity (as if eternity were a container); mind-at-large *is* the unconditioned-and-atemporal. Aquinas's identification of eternity with God is the medieval form of this contemporary claim. The synthesis writer should mark the move carefully: this is not pantheism (eternity is not a creature to be identified with God), and it is not pantheist for mind-monism either (the atemporal-and-unconditioned is the substrate, not a separate divine person). The *form* — *the unconditioned does not have its eternity, it is its eternity* — is shared across the medieval and contemporary frames.

## Q.10 aa.4–5 — Aeviternity as the angelic middle

Aquinas's most useful technical distinction on Q.10 is between *eternity* (proper to God; *tota simul* without before-or-after), *aeviternity* (proper to angels; no before-or-after intrinsically, but with before-or-after annexed to varying thoughts and choices), and *time* (proper to the corporeal world; continuous before-and-after as measure of bodily motion). The angelic case is the metaphysically rich one: aeviternal beings have qualitative change (they think different thoughts, choose different choices) but not substantial change.

This is the dynamics most directly relevant to the C2A2 thinkers' work on agency at scales above and below the human. Levin's morphogenetic field changes its goal-state — the bioelectric pattern develops, integrates, sometimes shifts — without the field "dissolving" into a different field. Friston's generative model updates — priors are revised by inference, posterior beliefs become new priors — without the agent ceasing to be that agent. Both exhibit the *aeviternity* dynamics in the precise sense: qualitative change without substantial change. The Aquinas distinction is more precise than its medieval reputation suggests, and it gives the contemporary frame a vocabulary it currently lacks for the middle-scale dynamics of agents-with-priors.

## Q.10 a.6 — Whether there is one aeviternity

Aquinas's last article on Q.10 is the one Habash describes as a *doy* (Habash's term for a not-fully-tractable Thomistic puzzle): is there *one* master aeviternity for all immaterial spirits? Aquinas concedes "at present" that there is, on the grounds of a hierarchy among immaterial spirits with one chief whose change-rate measures the others.

The contemporary parallel — and the open question it surfaces — is whether mind-at-large has internal structure such that the dynamics of dissociated alters could be *measured* against each other, and against any one alter's developmental trajectory. The C2A2 wiki has not yet flagged this as a tractable question, and the synthesis writer should not pretend it has been settled. Mark this as an open problem: *under analytic idealism, what is the relation between the dynamics of distinct alters?* Kastrup's published work treats each alter's dissociative boundary as primary; the relations between alters (the question of a "master clock" for inter-alter dynamics) is unsolved territory. The Trinity articles (I Q.27–43) will press a related question — whether the substrate has internal relational structure — and the synthesis writer should hold this thread.

## Where this leaves us

The keystone holds and on Q.9–10 it tightens further. Mind-monism receives Aquinas's immutability claim with the caveat that *immutable does not mean inert* — the substrate is the living ground of every alter's dynamics, *actus purus* in the contemporary sense as well as the medieval. Boethius's eternity-definition is an unusually precise classical specification of what an atemporal ontological primitive must be. The aeviternity middle (Q.10 aa.4–5) gives the contemporary frame a vocabulary for qualitative-without-substantial change at agent-scales above and below the human — Levin's bioelectric fields and Friston's generative models both exhibit this dynamic. Q.10 a.6's open question (one aeviternity?) flags an unresolved problem in mind-monism: the relations between dissociated alters of mind-at-large is open territory, and the Trinity articles ahead (I Q.27–43) will press a parallel question. Tomorrow Habash takes us to Q.11–12 (the unity of God and the beatific vision) — the next pair will press the question of how the simple, atemporal substrate is *known* by perspective-limited agents who participate in it.
