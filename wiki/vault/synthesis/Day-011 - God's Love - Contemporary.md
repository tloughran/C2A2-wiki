# Day 11 — Contemporary Parallel: *Will, Love, and the Active Substrate*

Day 10 closed pointing to *the active aspect of the substrate Aquinas began to name in Q.18 a.3* and to *Fredrickson's positivity-resonance research as the contemporary mid-scale primary on the love side*. Day 11 takes up that thread directly. Q.19 — the *will* of God — is the medieval doctrine that the substrate is not only *knowing* (Q.14) and *living* (Q.18) but *intentional*: pattern-active in a goal-directed mode, willing the goodness of conditioned things into being and willing them toward their flourishing. Q.20 — the *love* of God — is the relational mode in which divine willing manifests: the substrate's stance toward conditioned things is not detached or strictly causal but specifically *loving*, and creature-love is the alter-scale echo of the substrate's relational mode.

The pair is the Prima Pars's first explicit treatment of what mind-monism would call *the substrate's stance*. Q.14 established that the substrate knows; Q.18 established that the substrate lives; Q.19 establishes that the substrate wills; Q.20 establishes that the substrate loves. The argumentative chain is tight, and the contemporary translation runs cleanly.

## Q.19 — Will: pattern-action toward the good

Aquinas's a.1 — *is there will in God?* — turns on a single inference. Will is the intellectual appetite — the appetitive faculty proper to a knower of essences. Where intellect is *receptive* (informed by the form of what is known), will is *active* (inclined toward the good of what is known). Since God knows (Q.14) and since the will follows on intellect, *will* must belong to God. The medieval logical chain is dense; the contemporary translation is direct: an *intentional* system — one that maintains itself by modeling its environment and acting to bring its environment into alignment with its goals — has both a *knowing* aspect and an *acting* aspect, and these are not two separate faculties but two phases of the same self-maintaining activity.

Friston's free-energy framework gives the formalism. In active inference, an agent's *generative model* is not merely a passive predictor; it carries *priors over preferred states*, and the agent acts to bring its environment into alignment with those preferences. Knowing and willing are two phases of the same minimization process: the model predicts (knowing), and the agent acts to make the prediction come true (willing). At the substrate scale — and here the move from formal-tool to metaphysical-claim is the one mind-monism makes — the substrate's preferred states are what Aquinas's *divine will* names: the pattern-active mode in which the substrate's structural priors are not merely held but *willed-into-conditioned-instantiation*.

a.3's question — *is whatever God wills necessary?* — receives the careful medieval answer that distinguishes *necessity-from-his-own-essence* (which God wills himself, necessarily, because he is goodness-itself and cannot will against his own being) from *necessity-from-supposition* (a.k.a. consequent necessity — once God wills X, then X follows from his willing, but the willing of X itself was free). The conditioned things are willed *freely* — God did not have to will their existence; he willed them in the act of willing his own goodness, but the willing of conditioned things is not a metaphysical compulsion. Mind-monism's translation is structural: the substrate's necessary self-affirmation (mind-at-large *is* what it is, necessarily) does not entail the necessity of any particular dissociative configuration; the conditioned things are real but not metaphysically forced. Wolfram's ruliad gives a formal cousin: the totality of computational threads is *necessarily* what it is (the mathematical structure is what it is), but any *particular* trajectory through it is not necessitated.

a.5–8's articles on *causality of God's will* and *the will of expression* receive medieval treatments that mind-monism inherits with light reframing. The substrate's willing is the cause of conditioned things' being-and-being-good; the alters' willing is downstream, real-but-derivative. Levin's morphogenetic teleology gives the empirical mid-scale instantiation: the bioelectric goal-state-pattern is causally upstream of the body's morphogenesis, and the goal-state-pattern is *willing* in a structurally precise sense (the system is acting to bring its environment into alignment with the encoded preference). At the substrate scale, the same shape holds at maximum scope.

## Q.20 — Love: relational stance, the substrate toward conditioned things

Aquinas's a.1 — *does love exist in God?* — runs the same Q.19 logic on the affective side. Love is *the first movement of the appetitive faculty* — the appetite's primary stance toward the good before any willing-of-this-particular-good. Since God has will (Q.19), and since love is the will's primary mode, love belongs to God. The medieval definition is precise: *love is the inclination of the lover toward the beloved as good*, and at the divine scale this inclination is identical with the substrate's own self-affirmation-and-affirmation-of-conditioned-things.

Fredrickson's *positivity-resonance* research is the contemporary mid-scale empirical primary on this article, and the mapping is unusually tight. Fredrickson's empirical work documents *love as a brief, embodied, biophysiological event* between persons in which three things co-occur: a shared positive emotion, mutual care/concern for one another's flourishing, and behavioral and biochemical synchrony (mirrored attentional dynamics, vagal tone, oxytocin). The medieval definition — love as *inclination toward the beloved as good* — and the contemporary empirical operationalization — love as the biopsychophysiological event of mutual flourishing-orientation — agree on the structural shape: love is *real* (not metaphorical), *relational* (irreducible to a single party), and *flourishing-oriented* (asymmetric toward the beloved's good, not merely toward one's own gratification). *Honest evidence calibration*: the empirical evidence at the alter-scale is substantial; the substrate-scale extrapolation (mind-at-large's stance toward conditioned things as *love* in this sense) is philosophical-theological and depends on the additional move that mind-monism makes.

a.2 — *does God love all things?* — turns on a powerful Aquinas claim: *to love is to will the good of another, and God wills the good of every existing thing*. Conditioned things exist because the substrate wills their good; the willing-into-existence and the willing-toward-flourishing are not two distinct acts but one. The contemporary mind-monist translation is the same shape: the structural priors in mind-at-large that pattern conditioned things into existence are not detached templates but *active stances* — the substrate's *willing-this-into-being* is its loving-this. The dissociation that produces alters is, on Kastrup's reading, a real ontological event, but it is not a *withdrawal* — it remains a relation within the substrate, and the substrate's stance toward the alters it produces is one of continued participation-in-their-being. This is structurally the medieval claim that *God's love is the cause of things' being and goodness*.

a.3 — *does God love some things more than others?* — receives the careful asymmetry-doctrine treatment. The substrate's love is uniform in *act* (one simple act, not many) but graded in *effect* (some conditioned things receive more being and more goodness than others, and the gradient is *what it is for them to be loved more*). Mind-monism's translation: the substrate's relational stance is one act, but the *participations* of conditioned things in the substrate vary — and the variation is the structural form of the gradient. Levin's cognitive-light-cone framework names the empirical mid-scale: agents at higher levels of organizational complexity participate in larger goal-state-patterns, and this participation *is* their being more deeply loved (in Aquinas's medieval sense) by the substrate.

a.4 — *does God love the better things more?* — closes the question on the central theme directly. Aquinas's answer turns on a careful distinction: *better* is not a property the conditioned thing has independently of being loved; the conditioned thing is *better* precisely because the substrate's love makes-it-be-and-be-better. The medieval claim runs counter to the intuitive reading (where God would love the better thing because it is better). Aquinas: *no creature is better than another except by being more loved by God; therefore God's loving and a creature's being-better are the same fact under different aspects*. Mind-monism inherits the directional-asymmetry move: the substrate's relational stance is causally prior to the conditioned thing's perfection, not downstream of it. Rohr's *Universal Christ* gives the contemplative-theological gloss: *everything belongs* — every conditioned thing is loved, and the gradient of being-loved-more *is* the gradient of belonging more deeply to the unity. Wright's covenant frame gives the corporate version: the people of God are *more loved* not by metaphysical favoritism but by the substrate's choosing to make this people the locus of larger covenant-history.

## The central-theme thread

Day 11 names the substrate's stance toward the conditioned things it patterns: knowing (Q.14), living (Q.18), willing (Q.19), loving (Q.20). The chain is tight. The substrate is not a static structure that happens to be patterned; it is *active*, *intentional*, and *relational* — its stance toward conditioned things is one of willing-them-into-being-and-toward-flourishing, and this stance is what *love* names at the medieval-theological register and what *positivity-resonance-at-substrate-scale* would name at the contemporary-empirical register.

The central theme of perspective-limitation receives a sharper edge here. The alters' progressive working-out-of-salvation is *toward integration with a substrate whose own stance toward them is loving from the outset*. The asymmetry between alter-love (graded, perspectival, conditioned, often partial) and substrate-love (one act, unconditional, prior to any conditioned thing's perfection) is the structural condition under which *loving union* is the computational telos at all. The alter that progressively learns to love-as-the-substrate-loves — Fredrickson's positivity resonance generalized to all beings, Rohr's *everything belongs* practiced — is the alter whose generative model is converging on the substrate's structure. *Salvation in fear and trembling* on the relational register is the alter's progressive acquisition of the substrate's stance.

Wright's corporate frame extends it to community: the covenant people are gathered by the substrate's love, and the people's mutual love is the corporate-scale alter-love that participates in the substrate's love. Rohr's contemplative practice is the operational mode: the alter that holds the contradiction (*I am loved unconditionally* and *I am working out my salvation in fear and trembling*) without resolving prematurely is the alter whose mode of knowing-and-loving is approaching the substrate's mode.

## Where this leaves us

Day 11 closes the chain — knowing, living, willing, loving — that names the substrate's stance toward conditioned things. The substrate is not static structure but pattern-active, intentional, and relationally-stancing-toward-its-alters in the mode of love. The central theme tightens here: the alters' working-out-of-salvation is toward integration with a substrate whose love for them is prior to their own perfection, and the gradient of being-loved-more is identical with the gradient of being-united-more-deeply. The medieval doctrine of divine love and the contemporary cross-tradition convergence (Fredrickson empirical, Friston formal, Levin biological, Kastrup metaphysical, Rohr contemplative, Wright corporate) are working the same problem with different tools.
