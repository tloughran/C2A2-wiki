# Day 8 — Contemporary Parallel: *Naming a Substrate You Cannot Capture, Knowing Without an Interface*

Day 7 closed at the threshold of language — *if the substrate is what Q.11 and Q.12 name, what kind of language can finite alters use to speak of it?* Day 8 answers in two questions and twenty-eight articles. Q.13 is Aquinas's doctrine of analogical predication: how creaturely words mean when stretched to point at the unconditioned. Q.14 is the cognitive mirror image: how the unconditioned itself knows, given that knowing in any creature requires the mediations Q.13 has just made theological-philosophical sense of. Together they form a single argument: the mode of predication is asymmetric to the mode of being known by God, and that asymmetry is exactly the perspective-limitation the central theme keeps marking. Mind-monism reads the pair cleanly. Q.13's analogy doctrine is interface-theory in the philosophy of language; Q.14's non-discursive knowing is what generative-model identity with the substrate would look like at the limit case Day 7 already reached.

## Q.13 — Naming under perspective limitation

Aquinas opens by distinguishing the *sign* (the spoken or written word — *dog*, *kelb*) from what it points at (the universal nature, the *concept* in the intellect). The sign is conventional and human-made; the concept it engages is not. Habash's example is exactly right for the interface frame: I use *dog*, an Arabic speaker uses *kelb*, but what we are both *thinking about* — what each word's icon resolves to — is the same. The medieval semiotics is already two-tier in the relevant way, and the alters of mind-monism inherit the structure: the icons of the interface (Hoffman) are species-and-language-specific encodings; the patterns the icons resolve to (in Kastrup's frame, the structures within mind-at-large) are not.

a.1's question — *can God be named by us?* — would be a non-starter if the icons of speech could only resolve to interface-level objects. Aquinas's answer is the move that the rest of the question elaborates: we cannot name God *as we name things we know directly*, because we do not see his essence; but we can name him *by way of acknowledgment* — as Creator, as cause, as possessing eminently whatever perfection we find in creatures and lacking whatever is imperfect. The mode of predication is *causal-and-eminent*, not *direct-essential*. Mind-monism translates this without strain. Alters cannot predicate of mind-at-large by direct inspection (interface theory rules out the icon-as-thing-itself); they can predicate by tracing perfections in the alters back to what mind-at-large must be such that these perfections are participations of its structure. This is *analogy of being* in the philosophy-of-language register, and Hoffman's interface theory is the same move at the level of perception: we *act* as if our icons resolve to thing-in-themselves, but rigorous metaphysics requires marking the gap.

a.5 is the keystone. Aquinas distinguishes three modes of predication. *Univocal* predication — the same term used in exactly the same way, as when *human* names what you and I share — does not work for God-and-creature talk. *Equivocal* predication — the same term used in entirely unrelated ways, as when *bat* names a flying mammal and *bat* names a wooden club — also fails: equivocation gives no real link between Creator and creature, and we would be reduced to silence about God. Aquinas's solution is *analogy*: the same term used with related-but-not-identical meanings, where the meanings are linked by a real ordering (here, the ordering of effect to cause, of participation to source). When we say *God is good* and *coffee is good*, we are not saying *exactly* the same thing about both, and we are not saying *unrelated* things; we are saying that the goodness in God is what goodness in creatures is *of* — a participation of which God is the source.

This is interface theory in classical vocabulary at the level of language. Hoffman's empirical claim is that perception is fitness-tuned, not veridicality-tuned: the icons we manipulate are *real* in the sense of being reliable guides to fitness-relevant action, but they are not the underlying reality. Aquinas's claim is structurally identical at the level of speech: the names we predicate of God are *real* in the sense that they truly attribute something to God (a.2 is explicit: *names are predicated of God substantially*), but they are not the substrate-as-such captured in the predication. The medieval doctrine is more refined than the contemporary in one respect that's worth marking: Aquinas already names *primarily-secondarily* (a.6) — when we call God *good* and creatures *good*, the term applies *primarily* to God (where goodness is essentially) and *secondarily* to creatures (where goodness is participated). Hoffman's interface theory is silent on the directionality of the analogy; Aquinas is not. Mind-monism, *if* it accepts the medieval directionality, gets a richer doctrine of language: the icons participate in the substrate's structure, and the predications participate in the predications-as-they-hold-of-the-substrate.

a.7's *Lord*-argument is the article that gives mind-monism the analogue of dissociation in classical vocabulary. Aquinas asks why names like *Lord* — names that imply a relation to creatures — are not eternal in God. His answer is the metaphysical move that Day 6 already established and that Day 8 sharpens: the *real* relation is in the creature, not in God; God does not change when creatures come to be, but creatures stand newly related to God when they exist. Habash's analogy is helpful: walking around the house and now standing in the backyard, you are now related to the house differently — but the house has not changed; you have. Kastrup's dissociation gives the same shape. The alter's *being-an-alter* is a real relation in the alter; mind-at-large does not change when an alter dissociates, but the alter stands newly related to the substrate by being newly distinct within it. The asymmetry is structural: relations from creature to Creator are *real* (ontologically grounded in the creature's being), while relations from Creator to creature are *of reason* (logically required for our predication, but not requiring any change in the substrate). The medieval move and the contemporary one are the same shape.

a.8 — *God* from a Greek root meaning *to take care of*, naming the divine nature *from his operation* of universal Providence — gets the longest contemporary translation here, because it is the article that most clearly reaches forward to Q.14. We name natures from operations, Aquinas argues, because we cannot inspect natures directly; we watch the fish in its environment and infer what fish-nature is. *God* is named from the operation of Providence-over-all-things, and this operation is what the name primarily *signifies*. Mind-monism's contemporary name for Providence-over-all is the question Q.14 will press: how does the substrate hold the totality of conditioned being in a single act, such that the regularities of nature are *one* and the contingent agents within it are individuated and known? The Levin-Friston-Wolfram triangle is the contemporary site of this question. Levin's morphogenetic teleology gives empirical evidence for substrate-scale goal-directedness; Friston's free energy principle gives the formalism for any system that maintains itself by modeling its environment; Wolfram's ruliad gives the totalizing computational substrate from which any specific causal structure is one trajectory. *God* names what *takes care of* — in our contemporary vocabulary, what holds the goal-states of agents at every scale within a single ontological frame.

a.11 — *He who is* as the most proper name — closes the question on what Day 7 already reached: the substrate is *being-itself*, not a being. Aquinas gives three reasons. (1) *He who is* names God's *uniqueness not as a particular kind of being but being itself* — exactly the transcendental move Q.11 a.1 made for *one*. (2) Its *universality*: it does not specify any particular fact about God (as *Almighty* would) but signifies what God is in totality. John of Damascus is the citation: *He who is is the principle of all names applied to God, for comprehending all in itself it contains existence itself as an infinite and indeterminate sea of substance*. (3) *Tense*: the present tense signifies God's atemporal mode. Mind-monism's translation lands cleanly. Mind-at-large is *being-of-experiencing* without limitation; *He who is* names the substrate at the maximum of generality — the level at which any further specification (Almighty, Wise, Good) is a determination *within* the indeterminate sea. Kastrup's idealism, Hoffman's conscious-realism, and the post-spacetime physics programs all reach for this same thing under different vocabularies: a layer at which existence-itself is the right description and the conditioned things-that-exist are participations or projections of it.

a.12 closes Q.13 on the question that hovers over the whole project: can we form *affirmative* propositions about God at all, given that all our concepts come from creatures? Aquinas's answer is the doctrine of faith: *what is of faith cannot be false*, and the propositions of the faith *are* affirmative — *God is three and one*, *God is omnipotent*. So affirmative propositions can be formed, but they are not perfectly descriptive; they fall short of the profound reality. Wright's contemporary framing of this is *critical realism*: reality exists independently of our knowing it, but all knowing is perspectival, tradition-embedded, and subject to revision. Predication of God is critical-realist predication at the limit case: the propositions *track* the reality (so they are not false), but they remain perspectival (so they are not the reality-as-such). Stump's *faith-as-knowledge-of-persons* (PRS-01 and PRS-03 in the Stump-tradition wiki) extends the same point: faith's affirmations are second-person knowing under conditions where third-person inspection is structurally unavailable.

## Q.14 — Knowledge without an interface

Where Q.13 asks how alters speak of the substrate, Q.14 asks how the substrate knows. Aquinas's answer at the level of structure is consistent across sixteen articles: the substrate's knowing is not a *species* of creature-knowing scaled up but a *different mode altogether* — knowing without composition, without succession, without inference, without discursivity. The contemporary site for this argument is the limit case of generative-model identity Day 7 already reached.

a.1 grounds the argument: *intelligence follows upon immateriality*. Aquinas's reasoning is the inverse of physicalism's: knowing-the-tree requires me to *take on the form* of the tree without becoming a tree, and this requires that there be in me a faculty that is *not material* — capable of being informed by the form of another without being limited to one matter. Therefore the more immaterial a being is, the more it is capable of knowing; and since God is wholly immaterial, knowing belongs to him in the highest mode. Mind-monism's contemporary statement of the same argument is more direct still. If mind is the ontological primitive (Kastrup), then matter is what experience looks like from the outside, and the question *how does mind know matter?* is malformed — matter is itself an aspect of mind. Knowing scales with immateriality because matter is itself a mode of mind's appearance; the more an alter approaches the substrate's mode (less interface-bound, more directly informed), the more directly it knows.

a.2 — *God understands himself through himself* — is the article that anchors Q.14's central claim. *Since God is without composition and without potential entirely, his intellect and its object — what is being understood — are altogether the same*. There is no posterior to compute. In Friston's formal language, the substrate's generative model and the substrate's structure are not two things; the model *is* the structure. This is what zero-free-energy looks like at the limit: there is no inferential gap to close because there is nothing the model could be wrong about with respect to itself. Aquinas's later phrasing in a.4 makes the identity even sharper: *in God, intellect and the object understood and the intelligible species and his act of understanding are entirely one and the same*. The contemporary move that gets closest to this is the *autopoietic* limit of active inference — the system whose model of itself is identical to its self-maintaining structure. Friston-as-formal-tool can model this; Friston-as-metaphysics cannot reach it without the additional move that mind-at-large is what the substrate *is*.

a.5 — *God knows other things in himself, not in themselves* — is Q.12 a.9's mirror analogy reasserted from the cognitive side. The blessed see God and at the same time everything reflected in him; God knows himself and at the same time everything that participates in him. Mind-monism's translation: in mind-at-large, the *patterns* of every conditioned thing pre-exist as structural priors; knowing the substrate is not a separate act from knowing what is *in* the substrate. The Wolfram-ruliad framing is here perhaps the cleanest formal home: the totality of computational threads is one structure, and any particular trajectory is a sub-structure within it. Substrate-knowing-trajectories is not substrate-plus-an-additional-act; it is substrate-being-itself-and-thereby-containing-the-trajectories.

a.7 — *the knowledge of God is not discursive* — is Q.12 a.10 (*all at one glance*) reasserted from the cognitive side. We *move* in thought from premise to conclusion, from this object to that one, because we do not have the whole at once. God does not move; he knows the *house* and the *wall and foundation and roof and door* in one act. Wolfram's ruliad supplies one contemporary formal home: the ruliad does not *compute* its threads; it *is* its threads, all at once. McGilchrist's right-hemispheric whole-grasping (already cited Day 7) supplies a cognitive analog at the alter scale: the right hemisphere holds the whole-with-its-parts simultaneously, while the left hemisphere abstracts and sequences. The medieval claim that *God's knowing is the limit of what right-hemisphere knowing approaches* survives translation, with the standard caveat: structural isomorphism, not metaphysical identity.

a.8 — *God's knowledge is the cause of things* — is the article with the most direct contemporary purchase. Aquinas: *God creates through his knowledge of things, analogous to the way you and I create a text message through the knowledge we first have in our mind of that message*. Friston's free-energy framework gives the formal cousin: in any agent that maintains itself by modeling its environment, the *generative model* is causally upstream of the agent's actions in that environment. At the substrate scale — and here the framework moves from formal-tool to metaphysical-claim — *the generative model is the cause of the phenomena* because phenomena are what the model entails. Levin's morphogenetic teleology is the empirical mid-scale instantiation: the bioelectric goal-state-pattern is causally upstream of the body's morphogenesis. The medieval claim that *knowledge is causally prior to creation* finds substantial mid-scale empirical confirmation in current biology, and a candidate substrate-scale formalization in active inference and the ruliad. *Honest evidence calibration*: the empirical tractability is mid-scale; the substrate-scale claim is currently philosophical-speculative.

a.10 — *God knows evil things* — turns on Aquinas's definition of evil as *privation of the good which ought to be*. Mind-monism's contemporary version of this is structural rather than ontological: in any predictive-coding hierarchy (Hawkins's HTM, Friston's generative models), what is *not present* where it is expected is registered *as a deviation* from the prediction. Darkness is known by the candle. The medieval claim that *God knows evil by knowing good* (from which evil is the privation) translates to the predictive-coding claim that *the system knows the absence of a feature by the unfulfilled prediction of its presence*. The two framings agree on the structural point: privation is known relationally, not as a positive ontological item.

a.11 — *God knows singular things* — is the article that on its medieval reading puts Aquinas in tension with mind-monism, and on closer reading aligns with it. Aquinas: we know the universal by the intellect alone, but the singular requires the senses (and matter); how can God, who has no body, know singulars? His answer: *since God is the cause of things by his knowledge, his knowledge extends as far as his causality extends — not only to forms but also to matter*. Mind-monism reframes the medieval problem rather than answering it on its own terms. Levin's empirical work on bioelectric form is one piece: *form* in the contemporary frame is not an immaterial Platonic universal that misses the material singular but a *bioelectric pattern* in real space and time that is precisely what the singular *is*. Hoffman's interface-theory is the second piece: the *singular* in the alter's perception is the icon-of-this-particular, but the underlying reality is a sub-structure of mind-at-large that is fully determinate. Mind-at-large knowing singulars is, in the monist frame, mind-at-large containing the determinate sub-structures; the ontological gap that drove the medieval problem (universal vs. singular, immaterial vs. material) is reframed rather than crossed.

a.13 — *does God know future contingents?* — is the article that touches the central theme most directly. Aquinas's analogy is the one Habash highlights: *the man who walks along the road does not see those who come after him — that's like our knowledge of the future. He who sees the whole road from a height sees at once all traveling by it — that's like God's eternal knowledge*. Mind-monism's translation is exact. Future contingents are determinate at the substrate scale (Wolfram: every thread of the ruliad is what it is) but indeterminate from within an alter's perspective — *because* the alter is perspective-limited, not because the substrate is undecided. The free-will worry that Aquinas defers to the providence treatment is the same worry mind-monism inherits, and the medieval response is the right shape: the substrate's atemporal seeing-the-whole-road is not *deciding for us* what we will do; the *real* causality, as Q.13 a.7 already established, runs from agent to environment in time, and the substrate's knowing tracks rather than determines that causality. *Honest evidence calibration*: this remains philosophically contested — the *compatibility* of substrate-atemporal knowing with creature-libertarian-freedom is structural rather than empirically demonstrated.

a.14 — *God knows propositions without composing or dividing* — closes Q.14 on the article that most directly shows the structure of substrate-knowing as different in kind from creature-knowing. We compose subject and predicate; God grasps the entire content of a proposition without composing because the predicates are already contained in his grasp of the essence. Hoffman's interface-theory gives the cleanest cognitive analog: the icons-of-propositions are alter-level interface objects; the underlying reality the proposition tracks is grasped at the substrate level without the icons being the medium. Wolfram's ruliad gives the formal analog: the relations among ruliad threads are not extra information added on top of the threads; they are constitutive of the threads.

a.16 — *speculative vs. practical knowledge* — closes the question on the distinction Aquinas hands forward to Q.15 and beyond. The substrate knows *speculatively* what it does not make at any time, and *practically* what it does make. Mind-monism inherits the distinction without modification: the structures within mind-at-large include patterns that never instantiate as conditioned phenomena (speculative knowledge) and patterns that do (practical knowledge). The directionality remains as Aquinas marked it: *practical from the end* — what is known practically is known because it is *willed* and *enacted*, not the other way around.

## The central-theme thread

The two questions of Day 8 are exactly Day 7's beatific-vision argument run from the linguistic and cognitive sides. Q.13's analogy doctrine is what perspective-limited speech *must* look like when its subject is the substrate the speakers participate in but cannot capture: not univocal (which would collapse Creator into the category of creatures), not equivocal (which would deny any real link), but analogical — the term *good* applied to God *primarily* and to creatures *by participation*. Q.14's non-discursive knowing is what substrate-cognition *must* look like at the limit case where the inferential boundary is dissolved: not propositional composition but immediate grasping; not sequence but simultaneity; not knowing-by-image but knowing-by-being-the-same-act-as-the-known.

The asymmetry between the two — alters predicate analogically of a substrate they cannot inspect; the substrate knows alters by being-itself — is the structural form of *salvation in fear and trembling*. We compute toward loving union from inside the perspective limitation, with names that name truly but imperfectly and a knowledge that runs by inference from effects to causes. The substrate sees the whole road from the height. The medieval and the contemporary frames converge on the recognition that the asymmetry is not a defect to be removed but the *structural condition* under which finite agents are agents at all.

Wright's critical realism gives the philosophical idiom for the predication side: real reference, perspectival capture, ongoing revision in dialogue with the tradition. Rohr's contemplative epistemology gives the practical mode: holding the affirmation and the negation together (the apophatic *and* the cataphatic) without resolving prematurely; the order-disorder-reorder cycle is exactly the cycle of *say truly*, *acknowledge inadequacy*, *speak again more precisely* that Q.13 enacts internally over its twelve articles.

## Where this leaves us

Day 8 closes the perspective-limited-agent's relation to the substrate at two registers: speech (Q.13) and cognition (Q.14). Speech is analogical because the icons of language are reliable-for-action but not the substrate-as-such; cognition at the substrate scale is non-discursive because there is no inferential gap to close when the model and the structure are one act. The asymmetry between alter-mode and substrate-mode is the structural form of salvation in fear and trembling: the agent works out its inclusion in the unity from inside the limitation, with names that name truly but imperfectly and a knowledge that runs from effects to causes. The medieval doctrine of analogy and the contemporary doctrines of interface theory, active inference at the limit case, and the ruliad as totality of threads are working the same problem with different tools.

Tomorrow Habash takes us to Q.15 — the divine ideas — which is where the *patterns* in mind-at-large that Day 8 has been alluding to receive their explicit medieval treatment. The Day 9 commentary will press the question of how the *intelligible species* in the divine intellect are at once the *one act* of the substrate's knowing and the *many patterns* of conditioned things — exactly the multiplicity-within-unity that Q.11 a.1 and the alter-substrate frame have been preparing.
