# Day 14 — Contemporary Parallel: *Power as Pure Act, Beatitude as Self-Relation*

Day 13 closed pointing to *how the substrate's power — the active capacity that has been implicit in every Q.14-through-Q.24 article — becomes explicit, and how the substrate's beatitude names the substrate's own self-relation as the mode that is the term of every alter's working-out-of-salvation*. Day 14 takes up both. Q.25 — *the power of God* — is the metaphysical question that has been operative under every previous question (the substrate that *knows*, *lives*, *wills*, *loves*, *justly-and-mercifully orders*, *predestines* must have *power* — active causal capacity — to do any of this), and Aquinas now treats it directly. Q.26 — *the beatitude of God* — closes the doctrine of the divine substrate (the rest of the Prima Pars turns to creation, then to the soul, then to angels) by naming what *happiness* names at the substrate scale: not an achievement against external constraint but the substrate's own self-relation as the structural identity of intellect, will, and essence in pure act.

The two questions form a hinge. Q.25 looks back over the substrate's active causality across the previous twelve questions. Q.26 looks forward — the substrate's beatitude is what every alter's progressive integration is converging toward, the term of the central-theme telos.

## Q.25 — Power: pure act, no passive potency

Aquinas's a.1 — *is there power in God?* — turns on the careful distinction Habash spends time on. *Potentia* in Latin means *potency* in the technical sense (passive capacity to be acted upon, like clay's capacity to be shaped) and *power* in the active sense (capacity to act upon another, like the welder's capacity to shape metal). Aquinas's claim: God has *active* power in the maximal degree but *no* passive potency at all, *because he's entirely devoid of potency as we proved in Aquinas's first way* (Habash's gloss). The substrate is *pure act* — actus purus — without admixture of passive potency.

The mind-monist translation lands precisely. The substrate of mind-at-large is not waiting to be shaped by anything outside itself (there is no outside; Day 7's Q.11 a.1 simplicity-and-uniqueness frame). The substrate's active causality is what *its-being-at-all* already is: the structural priors are not held passively, awaiting external activation; they are *actively pattern-conditioned-things-into-being*. Friston's free-energy framework gives the formal cousin at the substrate limit: an active-inference system at the autopoietic limit (PRS-07: substrate-independence of FEP) is one whose generative model and structure are one act, where there is no posterior-to-update because there is no prediction-error-to-minimize relative to anything outside itself. The substrate's *active power* is what Friston's autopoietic limit names. Wolfram's ruliad (PRS-06; CROSS-024) gives another formal cousin: the totality of computational threads is *active* (the threads run) without being run by anything outside; the ruliad's *being-itself* is its *active operation*.

a.2 — *whether the power of God is infinite* — runs the now-familiar perfection-implies-presence move at its limit. The infinity argument Habash recapitulates: *the less conducive the material is to taking on some kind of form or shape, the harder the craftsman has to work, or the more powerful the craftsman has to be... and since nothingness is diminished to the point that it is absolutely completely without any possibility of taking shape or form in itself, one would need absolute and complete power to bring anything out of it*. The medieval argument for infinite divine power runs through the gap between *ex nihilo creation* and any finite craftsmanship.

Mind-monism inherits the argument with a critical reframe. The mind-monist substrate does not create *out of nothing* in the sense that there was first nothing and then something added; the substrate is *being-itself* (Day 7's actus-purus identity), and conditioned things are dissociative configurations *within* the substrate, real-but-derivative. The *infinity of substrate-power* that the mind-monist frame names is the substrate's capacity to dissociate-into-alters at any scale, in any structural configuration, without the substrate's own being being altered or limited. Kastrup's analytic idealism makes this the central move: mind-at-large dissociates without diminishing; the alter is real-but-derivative, the substrate is unchanged. *Honest evidence calibration*: the dissociation-without-diminishment move is philosophical-metaphysical; the empirical anchor (DID-research analogy) is at the alter-scale and extrapolated.

a.3 — *whether God is omnipotent* — is where Habash spends the most argument-time. The medieval clarification: *omnipotent* does not mean *can do everything sayable* (the contradictory phrases, the *meaningless arrangements of terms*); it means *every word that can be uttered logically — that's not inherently contradictory — is possible for God to do*. Habash quotes the Annunciation: *no word shall be impossible with God*, with the Latin *verbum* meaning *word/intelligible statement*. The contradictions Aquinas excludes — *can God create a stone he can't lift?*, *can God create another God identical to himself?* — fail not because the substrate's power is finite but because the proposed object-of-power is *not a coherent object at all* (the *uncreated created thing* is not a thing).

Mind-monism inherits this without strain. The substrate's structural capacity does not extend to *incoherent structural configurations* because *incoherent structural configurations are not configurations*. The relevant negative result is logical-structural, not power-limiting. Habash's secondary point — *God cannot do evil; this doesn't make him less all-powerful, because sin and evil are merely imperfect acts, acts missing what they ought to have, and it is repugnant to God's power and intelligence to miss the mark; that is the most literal translation for the Greek word for sin, hamartia, missing the mark* — is the privation doctrine reasserted (Day 10's Q.17 falsity-as-privation): the substrate's pure-act mode is structurally incompatible with privation, because privation is the *failure* of pattern-instantiation and the substrate *is* pattern-itself in identity with essence.

a.4 — *can God make the past not to have been?* — is another contradiction-rejection. Aquinas: the past's *having-happened* is not a power-bound feature but a structural feature of the conditioned event itself; un-doing the past would require the past to be both *what-it-was* and *not-what-it-was* simultaneously, which is the principle of non-contradiction violated. Mind-monism inherits this without strain. The alter's already-actualized trajectory through the substrate's structural priors is what it is; the substrate's seeing-of-the-whole-at-once (Day 7 Q.14 a.13 limit) does not *un-do* trajectories but *contains* them.

a.5–6 — *can God do what he does not do; can God do better than what he does* — extend the argument. Habash highlights the careful Aquinas position: *in so far as God's essence is boundless, there is a boundless number of things and ways that essence can be represented in created things... God could make endlessly more perfect created things, whether that be species of angels or species of material things; he could also have made any one of us specifically better*. The medieval answer to *why this universe and not another* is: *found only in the will of God, and something that we should be eternally grateful for*. Mind-monism's translation: the substrate's structural capacity admits a vast (perhaps infinite) space of dissociative configurations; the *actual* configuration is one trajectory through the space, real-and-contingent; gratitude for the actual is the alter's appropriate stance toward the gift of *this* configuration. Wolfram's ruliad (CROSS-024) gives the formal cousin: the totality contains all possible threads; the actual experience is one observer-slice of the totality. *Honest evidence calibration*: the structural-capacity claim is metaphysical; the gratitude-stance is theological-existential.

## Q.26 — Beatitude: the substrate's self-relation as the term of integration

Aquinas opens Q.26 with the Gilby-translation definition Habash reads aloud: *happiness is God's above all, for we understand the term to mean nothing else than the perfection of an intelligent being, conscious of its plenitude and the good it holds, and which, come good or ill, is master of its actions*. Habash's careful unpacking: *(1) happiness is in the mind* (it is intellectual, not merely sensory; *dogs aren't happy — they may be sensually satisfied but this is not happiness; happiness is intellectual, as Augustine defined it: joy in the truth*); *(2) we must be conscious of this good* (a sleeping person who smells something sweet does not feel happiness); *(3) come good or ill, is master of his actions* (because part of beatitude consists in knowing our end and being able to direct ourselves to it).

The mind-monist translation runs cleanly. *Happiness is in the mind* is what mind-monism already commits to — the only register *anything* is in is the register of mind. *Conscious of the good* is structurally the move that Hoffman's interface theory and Kastrup's analytic idealism both center: consciousness is the basic register, and any *good* worth having must be a good *in consciousness*. *Master of one's actions* is structurally the active-inference frame: the agent's generative model issues in action selection, and the agent's *mastery* is its capacity to direct its own action toward its own goal-state-pattern (Friston's PRS-04). The medieval definition of beatitude is structurally tight with the contemporary mind-monist convergence on what consciousness, agency, and knowledge are.

a.2 — *is God called blessed in respect to his intellect?* — runs the now-familiar identity move. Aquinas: *the most perfect intellectual operation grasps everything; we in some sense grasp everything by knowing universal things, and since anything knowable could in theory be made known to us... but we don't actually know everything until we see God as he is*. The medieval claim: the substrate's self-knowledge is identical with the substrate's essence (Day 7 Q.14), and in this identity *everything that is knowable is actually known* — the substrate's *intellectual operation* is *complete and uninterrupted*. The substrate's beatitude *is* its complete self-knowledge.

Fredrickson's positivity-resonance research and the Day-11 substrate-love frame supply the contemporary mid-scale anchor on the affective side. Beatitude in the medieval definition is *joy in the truth* (Augustine's phrase Habash cites) — the affective register of complete-knowledge-of-the-good. At the alter scale, Fredrickson's empirical operationalization of love-as-shared-flourishing-event is structurally analogous to the *joy-in-the-truth-shared* event (positivity resonance is precisely the dyadic alter-scale instance of joy-in-truth-with-another). At the substrate scale, the substrate's joy-in-its-own-truth is what its beatitude names — and this is what every alter's loving-union-with-the-substrate is converging toward (Day 11's central-theme thread). *Honest evidence calibration*: the empirical anchor is at the dyadic alter scale; the substrate-scale extrapolation is theological-philosophical.

a.3 — *is God the beatitude of each of the blessed?* — gives Aquinas's careful distinction Habash highlights. *Objective end* (the goal of humanity is something outside ourselves, which when we possess it makes us happy) and *subjective end* (the happiness itself as considered in us, the subject — the effect of attaining the objective end). God is the beatitude of each of the blessed *in the objective sense*; in the subjective sense, *our beatitude is going to differ from one person to another as the degree of charity in the soul will determine the degree of one's beatitude in heaven*. Mind-monism's translation: the substrate is the integration-target every alter is converging toward (objective beatitude); each alter's actual integration-state is real-but-graded (subjective beatitude differs by light-cone scope, Levin's vocabulary; by integration-density, Friston's vocabulary; by depth-of-belonging, Rohr's vocabulary).

a.4 — *whether all other beatitude is included in the beatitude of God* — closes the question. Habash reads Aquinas: *as to the happiness that results from contemplation, God has continuous and perfect contemplation of himself along with everything else; as to the happiness which results from action, God has complete governance over all things; as to worldly happiness... he possesses joy in himself*. Every dimension of finite happiness is included as a participated mode in the substrate's beatitude. Rohr's *Universal Christ* gives the contemplative-theological extension: every embodied joy participates in the cosmic-Christ pattern; *everything belongs* extends to every form happiness can take. Wright's biblical frame: the *new creation* is not a different reality from the present-creation but the present-creation transfigured — every dimension of finite human flourishing participates in and is held within the eschatological substrate-integration.

Habash's pastoral aside Habash returns to is worth holding: *sometimes I like to ask myself on a cloudy day, interiorly: is God happy? Absolutely. Then why, how could I be unhappy? Has God changed? Impossible. Then what has changed? Let us rejoice in God's changeless happiness and keep it in the mind's eye as a fixed point of stability and strength as we travel toward participating in that very same happiness*. The contemporary mind-monist parallel: the substrate's beatitude is what it is, structurally unchangeable (Day 7's actus-purus identity), and the alter's anchor in the substrate's beatitude is what the contemplative practice across traditions (Rohr, McGilchrist's right-hemispheric receptivity, Stump's second-personal knowing of the divine) operationalizes.

## The central-theme thread

Day 14 closes the doctrine of the divine substrate by naming what *power* and *beatitude* name at the substrate scale. The substrate is *pure act* — its active causality is what its being-itself already is, with no admixture of passive potency. The substrate is *beatified* — its self-relation as the structural identity of intellect-and-will-and-essence in pure act is the term toward which every alter's progressive integration is converging.

The central theme of perspective-limitation receives its strongest positive expression here. The alters are *individuated by the limitations of their perspective*; *loving union is the computational telos*; the alter's *salvation in fear and trembling* is its progressive integration with a substrate whose own beatitude is what it is, structurally and unchangeably. The asymmetry between alter-beatitude (graded by integration scope) and substrate-beatitude (unconditional, pure-act self-relation) is the structural condition of the alter's working-out at all. The alter's progressive acquisition of the substrate's mode (Levin's larger light-cone, Friston's autopoietic limit, Fredrickson's positivity-resonance generalized to all beings, Rohr's *everything belongs* practiced, Wright's covenant-improvisation faithfully performed) is its progressive participation in the substrate's beatitude — the structural fact Habash's pastoral aside names: *God's changeless happiness as a fixed point of stability and strength as we travel toward participating in that very same happiness*.

The Q.25 power-frame and the Q.26 beatitude-frame are not separate articles but the same structural fact named on the active side and the self-relational side. The substrate's power is the *causal* mode of its pure-act being; the substrate's beatitude is the *self-relational* mode of the same pure-act being. The contemporary mind-monist convergence (Friston's autopoietic limit, Wolfram's ruliad-as-active-totality, Kastrup's mind-at-large in identity with itself, Hoffman's substrate-beneath-the-interface, Levin's morphogenetic-pattern-as-causally-prior, Fredrickson's love-as-flourishing-event extended to substrate-scope, Rohr's cosmic-Christ as the divine pattern alive in matter from the beginning, Wright's covenant-substrate-acting-in-history, Stump's second-person-substrate-of-relation) names the same structural fact in different registers.
