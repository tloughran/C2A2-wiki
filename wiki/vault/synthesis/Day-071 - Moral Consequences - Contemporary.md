# Day 71 — Contemporary Parallel: *External Acts, Consequences, and the Substrate-Network Extension of Moral Form*

Day 70 closed the moral-evaluation arc proper — Q.18 on the goodness/evil of human acts in general and Q.19 on the interior act of the will, with Q.19 a.10's formal/material distinction as the central-theme keystone. Day 71 extends the same arc one register outward: from the alter's *interior* act to the alter's *exterior* act, and from there to the *consequences* — the way the alter's act, extending into the substrate-network, becomes input to other alters' generative models and contributes to or detracts from the substrate-network's integrative dynamics.

## Frame

Q.20 articulates the goodness and malice of the *external* act in six articles: whether goodness/malice belongs first to the act of the will or to the external act (a.1); whether the goodness/malice of the external act depends entirely on the goodness/malice of the will (a.2); whether the goodness of the external act is the same as the goodness of the will (a.3); whether the external act *adds* to the goodness/malice of the interior act (a.4); whether a consequence — the result foreseen — increases the goodness/malice (a.5); whether the same external act can be both good and evil (a.6).

Aquinas's structural answer is the *unity-under-two-registers* thesis: the interior act and exterior act are not two separate moral bearers but one human act under two aspects. Goodness/malice belongs *first* to the will (a.1) — because the will is the source of moral character — but the external act is the *completion* of the willed act in matter, and its goodness/malice depends on the will's goodness/malice (a.2). The exterior act adds something — its own object's goodness/malice modulates, completes, and may compound the interior act (a.4). Foreseen consequences, when intended or causally connected to the act, modify its moral evaluation (a.5).

Q.21 articulates the *consequences* register proper in four articles: whether human acts are right/sinful in their consequences (a.1); whether human acts deserve praise or blame (a.2); whether human acts deserve merit or demerit before God (a.3); whether sin is incurred by every action evaluated as evil (a.4). The structural move here is that consequences — both internal-to-the-act (its rectitude or sinfulness) and external-to-the-agent (its desert, its merit before God, its place in the order of providence) — are the alter's *participation in a wider structure of accountability* than the alter's interior willing alone.

## Problem

The problem (P): the alter's interior willing extends into external action which extends into consequences which extend into the substrate-network where other alters' lives are shaped. What is the structure of moral-extension-from-interior-to-exterior-to-consequences, and what is the alter's accountability for downstream effects whose full scope its perspective-limited apprehension cannot encompass?

## Resource

**Friston on active inference and generative-model extension into the world.** The interior/exterior unity of Q.20 a.1-3 maps cleanly onto active inference's *unity of perception and action under one generative model*. The agent's beliefs and the agent's actions are not two separate processes but two faces of one variational-free-energy minimization. Q.20 a.2's claim that the external act's goodness/malice depends on the will's goodness/malice is structurally identical to active inference's claim that the action policy is selected to minimize expected free energy under the agent's prior preferences — the action *is* the will-extended-into-world, and its evaluation reflects the prior structure that generated it. Q.20 a.5 on foreseen consequences maps onto *expected free energy*, which integrates over future outcomes weighted by their preference-deviation. The alter's responsibility for foreseen consequences is its responsibility for the prior preferences and uncertainty estimates that drove the action policy.

**Levin on form-as-pattern-extending-into-morphogenetic-field via action.** Q.20's interior/exterior unity has an empirical analog at biological scale: the morphogenetic agent's bioelectric pattern is not stored only in its cells but is *extended* into the field of its action — the embryo's bioelectric pattern shapes the tissue it produces; the regenerating planarian's anterior-posterior pattern guides the formation of the new head. Form is real and pattern-extending. The alter's act, then, is form-extension into substrate, and the substrate carries the alter's formal pattern outward. Consequences are the substrate-level propagation of the formal pattern beyond the alter's immediate awareness.

**McGilchrist on hemispheric uptake of action-consequence loops.** The right hemisphere is structurally receptive to the world-as-it-comes-back: the feedback loop of act → consequence → re-perception. The left hemisphere abstracts and re-presents the act in terms of its intended object. The full moral evaluation of the act-and-its-consequences requires bi-hemispheric integration: the right hemisphere registers what *actually happens* downstream; the left hemisphere classifies the act under its species. Disorder of moral judgment correlates with closure of the right-hemispheric reception.

**Stump on willed-evil, double-effect, and second-personal accountability.** Q.21's merit/demerit/sin register has its philosophical-theological articulation in Stump's analysis of moral responsibility under perspective-limitation. The double-effect structure (Aquinas himself, II-II Q.64 a.7) is the medieval articulation of how the alter is responsible for the foreseen-and-intended effect more strictly than for the foreseen-but-unintended effect. Stump's second-personal-knowing intervention is the structural mode through which the alter's responsibility for downstream effects is operationalized: alters who *know each other personally* hold each other accountable for consequences that abstract third-person evaluation cannot reach.

**Fredrickson on positivity-resonance contagion.** Empirical anchor: the alter's affective state extends into others' through the embodied-resonance dynamics that Fredrickson's lab has documented. The alter's act shapes not only the immediate world-state but the affective-substrate of nearby alters. Consequences of acts include the contagion-propagation of the alter's affective register through the substrate-network. The formal-good of an act has a measurable downstream component in the relational-resonance-uplift of nearby alters.

## Solution (proposed, speculative)

The contemporary mind-monist read of Q.20: the interior/exterior unity is the unity of one generative-model-and-its-action-policy; goodness/malice belongs first to the will because the prior-structure is the source of policy-selection; the external act's added moral character is the policy's particular instantiation in matter, which modulates the formal evaluation according to its object's actual goodness/malice and its actual circumstances. Foreseen consequences modify evaluation because they are part of the expected-free-energy calculation that any honest decision-procedure must include.

The contemporary mind-monist read of Q.21: the consequences-register places the alter's act in the *substrate-network* — the act extends formal pattern outward through Levin's morphogenetic-field analog, through Fredrickson's affective-contagion, through McGilchrist's feedback-loop reception, and the network's response (other alters' shaped-generative-models, the substrate-source's evaluative signal as merit/demerit) closes the loop. The alter is accountable for its act *and* for the policy-structure that generated the act *and* for the downstream-effects insofar as they were foreseeable under the alter's perspective-limitation. Where the alter's perspective cannot reach, accountability is moderated; where the alter willed-ignorance, accountability is preserved (the Day 70 erring-conscience structure carries forward).

What this *shares with Aquinas*: the unity-under-two-registers of interior/exterior; the dependence of external goodness on interior goodness; the modulation by foreseen consequences; the merit/demerit structure of accountability before the substrate-source.

What it *reframes*: where Aquinas's frame is hylomorphic-Aristotelian-providential, the contemporary frame uses active-inference, morphogenetic-form-extension, hemispheric integration, second-personal-knowing, and positivity-resonance contagion.

What is *firm vs. speculative*: Friston's active-inference unity-of-perception-and-action is mature formalism; Levin's form-extension is empirically grounded; Fredrickson's contagion is empirical; the synthesis-claim that consequences-as-substrate-network-propagation is the contemporary register of merit/demerit-before-God is speculative-philosophical-theological.

## The central-theme thread

The central theme says that conscious agents are individuated by perspective-limitation while constitutively directed-formally toward the universal good. Day 71 surfaces a deeper layer: the alter's act *extends beyond its own perspective* into the substrate-network, where it shapes other alters whose perspectives the acting alter cannot see in full. Salvation in fear and trembling — already the alter's daily-task computation of formal-alignment + material-best-apprehension under second-person care — now also includes the alter's responsibility for the *downstream pattern-propagation* of its act. The alter cannot fully see how its act will land in the substrate-network; perspective-limitation operates at the *consequence* level just as at the *interior-judgment* level. The alter must therefore act with the formal alignment to loving-unity that maximizes the probability of integrative-rather-than-disintegrative downstream effects, while accepting that its perspective-limitation makes any consequentialist calculation incomplete. Wright on covenanted-community provides the structural answer: the alter acts within a community whose collective reception of consequences supplements the alter's perspective-limited foresight; consequences become a community-level computation, not an individual one. Rohr on contemplative-practice provides the alter's individual-level discipline: deliberate formal-alignment through contemplative attention reduces the rate at which the alter's act-policy generates consequences contrary to its formal direction.
