# Day 89 — Intellectual Virtues (Contemporary)

## Frame

Q.56 asks which powers of the soul are subjects of virtue. The intellect is a subject of virtue: it admits perfection by stable habit. The will is a subject of virtue: it can be ordered to its proper good by habit. The *irascible* and *concupiscible* parts of the sensitive appetite are subjects of virtue insofar as they share in reason — they can be habituated to follow reason's lead. The vegetative powers and the *outer* senses (taken in themselves) are not subjects of virtue; they execute without deliberative shaping. The *inner* senses (imagination, memory, common sense) can be partially habituated, but Aquinas treats them as intermediate cases.

Q.57 turns to the *intellectual* virtues specifically. Aristotle names five — the *speculative* triad of *understanding* (intellectus, *nous*: grasp of first principles), *science* (scientia: demonstrative knowledge of conclusions), and *wisdom* (sapientia: knowledge of the highest causes); and the two *practical* virtues — *art* (ars: right reason about things to be made) and *prudence* (prudentia, *phronesis*: right reason about things to be done). Aquinas distinguishes *speculative* virtues (whose end is the truth) from *practical* virtues (whose end is action). Wisdom is the *highest* of the speculative virtues — it governs both science and understanding, because it knows the highest cause and judges the others by reference to it. Prudence stands apart among the practical virtues because it concerns the *singular contingent action* and requires good moral disposition: a man cannot be prudent without good moral character, because the very *end* he aims at must be the right end (which the morally formed will supplies). Prudence has *annexed* virtues — *eubulia* (right deliberation), *synesis* (good judgment in standard cases), *gnome* (good judgment in exceptional cases).

Under conscious-realist monism the medieval map of intellectual virtues recovers as a remarkably articulate taxonomy of *epistemic and practical-rational competence types*. The contemporary frame can speak to each: nous as fast intuitive grasp of structure (Hawkins's reference-frame intuition; Kahneman's System 1 at its best); scientia as model-based inference under explicit constraint (Friston's hierarchical posteriors; logical-mathematical structure); sapientia as judgment over the integrated whole (McGilchrist's right-hemispheric integration; the "wisdom" tradition in the empirical decision sciences); ars as bounded-rational design competence (the contemporary design and engineering literature); prudentia as situated practical reasoning under conditions of moral character — a domain where the empirical literature on practical wisdom (Schwartz; Stump) and active-inference accounts of action-under-uncertainty converge.

## Problem

The shared problem: **what are the distinguishable kinds of cognitive-and-practical-rational excellence, what makes each an excellence (not merely a competence), and how do they hang together with the agent's moral character?** Aquinas's answer is finely articulated. Five intellectual virtues. Speculative vs. practical division. Wisdom governing the speculative triad. Prudence inseparable from moral virtue because the *end* must be right. Annexed virtues to prudence — eubulia, synesis, gnome — for the structural moments of deliberation, judgment-in-standard-case, judgment-in-exceptional-case.

The contemporary problem is exactly recognisable. The decision sciences and the empirical literatures on cognition, expertise, judgment, and practical reasoning give a remarkably converging picture: there is a hierarchy of cognitive excellences, the highest of which integrate domain-specific competence with broad sensitivity to the whole context; practical rationality is *not* reducible to instrumental rationality but requires the agent's end-orientation to be already well-formed; and the moments of deliberation, standard-case judgment, and exceptional-case judgment all admit separable cultivation.

The deeper question Q.57 poses is the *unity-of-the-intellectual-virtues* question. Are these five virtues separable? Can one have *scientia* without *sapientia*? Can one have *ars* without moral virtue? Can one be *prudent* without the moral character that fixes the right end? Aquinas's answers — wisdom integrates the speculative triad; ars is independent of moral virtue (a builder can build well morally bad); prudence cannot be separated from moral virtue — are subtle. The contemporary frame must either vindicate or revise these claims.

## Resource

**Hawkins's reference-frame intuition and pattern-completion** is the cortical-circuit account of *intellectus*. The agent's cortex, having stored thousands of reference frames, recognises the structure of a new instance rapidly and pre-reflectively. This is *intellectus* in its developed form — not the deliberative working-out of conclusions but the fast intuitive grasp of how the case structurally is. Hawkins PRS-03 in the Hawkins-tradition wiki supplies the mechanism. The medieval claim that *intellectus* is the habit by which we grasp first principles maps onto: the cortex's stabilized reference frames *constitute* the agent's grasp of structural primitives in its domains.

**Friston's hierarchical model-based inference** supplies the contemporary articulation of *scientia*. Science is demonstrative knowledge of conclusions from principles. Under active inference, this corresponds to the agent's hierarchical-generative-model competence at deriving lower-level posteriors from higher-level priors, under explicit conditioning. Friston PRS-04 (organism as active modeler) gives the framework; the logical-mathematical literature (Tarski, Lakatos, the Bayesian-program tradition) gives the formal content.

**McGilchrist's right-hemispheric integration as sapientia** is the keystone for the highest speculative virtue. McGilchrist's account of right-hemispheric attentional contextualization — its sensitivity to the whole, its receptivity to the unfamiliar, its integration of the parts under the whole — is the contemporary articulation of *sapientia*. The "knowledge of the highest causes" that integrates *intellectus* and *scientia* is precisely the right-hemispheric mode that governs the left-hemispheric instrumental competence. McGilchrist's clinical and historical work supports the diagnostic that *wisdom-loss* is hemispheric-imbalance: a culture that loses right-hemispheric receptivity loses *sapientia* even as it expands *scientia*.

**Hoffman on the interface as wisdom-conditioning** speaks to the *highest* speculative virtue's distinctive content. Wisdom, in the medieval frame, is knowledge of the highest cause — God in the theological context; the underlying *what is fundamental* in any inquiry. Hoffman's interface theory contributes a precise formal point: if perceived spacetime is a species-specific interface and not the underlying reality, then wisdom (knowledge of the underlying) cannot be obtained by deeper looking at the interface alone. Hoffman PRS-01 (conscious agents as fundamental) and the interface-theory framework apply. This is one of the key contemporary moves on Q.57 a.2.

**Friston's active inference as the formal account of *prudentia*** is the deepest contemporary recovery. Prudence is right reason about contingent action — situated, particular, condition-sensitive. Active inference is *exactly* the formal account of bounded-rational action under uncertainty in a particular situation. The Q.57 a.4-5 articles on prudence as practical reason about the singular contingent action recover as the agent's policy-selection under expected-free-energy minimization given a specified goal-prior. Friston PRS-04 and the active-inference-and-action literature apply.

**Stump on phronesis-in-tradition and the second-personal shape of prudence** is the keystone for Q.57 a.4-5. Tom's PRS-form re-description of Stump's account treats prudence as inherently shaped by the agent's relational formation. Stump's *Wandering in Darkness* on the second-personal structure of practical-moral knowing applies: a man without the right moral end cannot be prudent because he does not in fact know-by-second-person-knowing what the right action *toward* a person is. This is Aquinas's claim that prudence requires good moral character given a rigorous philosophical reading.

**McGilchrist on phronesis as right-hemispheric situated judgment** is the parallel hemispheric-level account. The right-hemispheric mode of attention is precisely what allows the agent to read the particular case rightly — to see *this* person in *this* situation under *these* conditions. Left-hemispheric instrumental dominance produces the case-blind application of general rules; right-hemispheric integration produces situated judgment. McGilchrist's framework supplies the empirical-clinical mechanism for Aquinas's claim about prudence's particularity.

**Stump on the unity-of-virtue dynamics** clarifies Q.57's most controversial claim — that prudence cannot be separated from moral virtue. The Stump-tradition wiki treats this as substantively right: the agent's *cognitive* grasp of what to do is mediated by the agent's *affective and motivational* formation, because the relevant cognition is second-personal and the agent's relation to the person is part of what the agent must know.

**Fredrickson on positivity-resonance as cognitive-broadening** supplies an empirical anchor for the prudence-and-moral-character entailment. Fredrickson PRS-05 (broaden-and-build) documents that positive affective states broaden attentional and cognitive scope; chronic negativity narrows. The agent without good moral disposition (which in Fredrickson's terms includes the cultivated dispositions of positivity-resonance and other-directed attention) is empirically *less able* to perceive the relevant case-features that prudence requires.

**Wright on practical wisdom in the Pauline tradition** is the scriptural anchor. Wright's reading of Paul (especially Philippians, Colossians, and the Romans 12 material) treats Christian practical reason as inseparable from the agent's formation in Christ — the renewed mind that "discerns the will of God, what is good and acceptable and perfect" (Rom 12:2). The Pauline phronēsis is exactly Aquinas's prudentia under conditions of formation-in-Christ. *After You Believe* makes the linkage explicit.

**Rohr on contemplative wisdom and second-half-of-life integrated judgment** complements the wisdom-as-sapientia analysis. Rohr's *Falling Upward* treats the second-half-of-life integrated judgment as a kind of *settled wisdom* — situated, non-dual, attentive to the whole. This is the contemplative-tradition recovery of *sapientia* as a stabilized disposition rather than a propositional inventory.

## Solution (proposed, speculative)

**(a) Shared with Aquinas.** There are distinguishable kinds of cognitive-and-practical-rational excellence: a fast intuitive grasp of structural primitives (nous/intellectus); a demonstrative-model-based competence (scientia); a synthesizing judgment under the highest-cause-or-whole (sapientia); a bounded-rational design competence (ars); and a situated practical wisdom about action in this particular case (prudentia). The speculative-practical distinction is preserved. Wisdom integrates the speculative triad. Prudence is inseparable from moral virtue — the agent's end-orientation conditions the agent's cognitive access to the right action. Ars is separable from moral virtue (a morally bad person can be a good craftsman). Prudence has structural sub-moments: deliberation (eubulia), standard-case judgment (synesis), exceptional-case judgment (gnome).

**(b) Where it reframes.** *Intellectus* recovers as fast cortical reference-frame intuition; *scientia* as hierarchical-generative-model inference under explicit conditioning; *sapientia* as right-hemispheric integration over the whole; *ars* as bounded-rational design under domain constraint; *prudentia* as active-inference policy selection under situational and moral-end constraints. The unity-of-virtues claim about prudence recovers as: agent's policy selection depends on goal-prior; the goal-prior is set by the agent's moral formation; without right moral formation, the goal-prior is mis-set and prudent action is unavailable. Stump's second-personal reading deepens this: the relevant knowing is *of persons* and is mediated by the agent's relational formation.

**(c) Where evidence is firm vs. speculative.** *Firm*: fast cortical pattern-completion (Hawkins; broader cortical literature); hierarchical model-based inference (Friston; the Bayesian-cognition program); attentional-cognitive scope broadening under positive affect (Fredrickson). *Empirically supported*: right-hemispheric integration in expert judgment (McGilchrist; clinical and historical evidence). *Formally rigorous*: active-inference account of bounded-rational practical action (Friston). *Philosophically rigorous*: Stump on the second-personal structure of practical-moral knowing. *Speculative-formal*: Hoffman's interface theory as constraint on sapientia. *Theological, well-attested*: Wright on Pauline phronēsis; Rohr on contemplative wisdom.

## The central-theme thread

The intellectual virtues are the central theme's cognitive substrate. Loving unity is the computational task; perspective-limitation is the constraint. The intellectual virtues are the *cognitive competences* that let the limited agent compute well under those constraints. *Intellectus* lets the agent grasp the structural primitives of its perspectival situation. *Scientia* lets the agent derive conclusions about its situation from those primitives. *Sapientia* lets the agent judge the whole — including its own perspective-limitations and the wider perspective that exceeds them. *Ars* lets the agent design well within its domain. *Prudentia* lets the agent act rightly *in this particular case*.

The central theme's most striking convergence with Q.57 is the *unity-of-virtues* claim about prudence. Loving unity is the goal; perspective-limitation is the condition; the agent's *moral* orientation is what fixes the goal-prior under which the agent's cognitive resources operate. A man oriented to vengeance rather than to loving unity cannot be prudent — not because his cognitive faculty is damaged but because his goal-prior is mis-set. The "fear and trembling" of Phil 2:12 acquires another layer of mechanical sense: the working-out is cognitively constrained by the agent's moral formation, and the agent without good moral formation is *cognitively blind* to the right action.

*Sapientia* carries the central theme's deepest cognitive claim. Wisdom is knowledge of the highest cause — which under conscious-realist monism is mind itself in its widest scope (Kastrup's mind-at-large; the cosmic-Christ frame in Rohr). The agent's sapiential competence is, in part, its receptivity to *more* of the whole than its individuated perspective contains. This is the central theme's "guidance from those who perceive more of the whole" in the cognitive register: the wise agent is the one whose right-hemispheric receptivity (McGilchrist), contemplative attention (Rohr), and Pauline renewed mind (Wright) admit perspective-widening into its situated judgment. Tom's PRS-form re-description of Stump's faith-as-knowledge-of-persons supplies the second-personal version: wisdom is knowing-by-being-in-relation, not knowing-by-inventory.

## Bridges

- **Hawkins PRS-03 / cortical reference-frame intuition** — Q.57 a.1's *intellectus*: fast pre-reflective grasp of structural primitives; *neuroscientifically grounded*.
- **Friston PRS-04 / hierarchical model-based inference** — Q.57 a.2's *scientia*: demonstrative competence under explicit conditioning; *formal, empirically robust*.
- **McGilchrist / right-hemispheric integration** — Q.57 a.2's *sapientia* and Q.57 a.4-5's prudential situated judgment; *clinical and historical evidence*.
- **Hoffman PRS-01 / interface theory as wisdom-conditioning** — Q.57 a.2's wisdom-as-highest-cause: wisdom requires recognising the limits of the interface; *speculative-formal*.
- **Friston PRS-04 + active-inference action** — Q.57 a.4-5's *prudentia*: bounded-rational policy selection under situational and moral-end constraints; *formal, empirically robust*.
- **Stump PRS-09 + PRS-04 / second-personal practical-moral knowing** — Q.57 a.4's prudence-requires-moral-virtue claim under rigorous philosophical reading; *philosophically rigorous*.
- **Fredrickson PRS-05 / broaden-and-build** — empirical anchor for prudence-and-moral-character: positive affect broadens cognitive scope necessary for prudent perception; *empirical, high confidence*.
- **Wright / *After You Believe*; Romans 12; Pauline phronēsis** — Q.57's intellectual virtues under the Pauline frame of the renewed mind; *theological, well-attested*.
- **Rohr / *Falling Upward*; second-half-of-life integrated judgment** — *sapientia* as stabilized contemplative wisdom; *contemplative tradition*.
- **Kastrup / mind-at-large as wisdom's object** — Q.57 a.2's highest-cause claim under analytic idealism; *speculative-metaphysical, internally consistent*.

## Where this leaves us

Q.56-57 take up the intellectual virtues. Q.58-59 (tomorrow) turn to the *moral* virtues — how they differ from the intellectual virtues, and their relation to the passions. The synthesis will pivot from cognitive competence to character-as-affective-formation. Fredrickson, McGilchrist, and Stump will lead; the active-inference frame on passions-as-appraisal will reappear. The central theme's distinctive claim — that moral formation conditions cognitive access — will get its complement in Q.58-59: that *cognitive formation* (the virtues of mind we have just examined) conditions the agent's stabilization of *affective* formation. The two are mutually conditioning. By the end of Q.59 the contemporary frame will be holding the full Aristotelian-Thomistic synthesis: virtues of mind and virtues of character, mutually formative, jointly constituting the agent's stabilized disposition toward loving unity under perspective-limitation.
