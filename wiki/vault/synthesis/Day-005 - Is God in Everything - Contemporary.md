# Day 5 — Contemporary Parallel: *A Thing Is Where It Operates, and Mind-at-Large Is the Operation Underneath*

The first four days established the keystone — mind-at-large as the unconditioned ground of which finite agents are perspective-limited dissociated alters; *being = goodness = desirability* under that frame; the Lateran IV / Regensburg formula as the precise classical statement of the central theme. Today the medieval frame turns to *how the unconditioned exists in conditioned things*, and the keystone holds at unusual strength. Aquinas's anti-magnitude move on infinity (Q.7 a.1) and his Q.8 doctrine of divine immanence — *more inmost to us than we are to ourselves* — are exactly the moves mind-monism most needs to make in its own contemporary articulation. Augustine's *late have I loved thee* is the central-theme citation around which the synthesis pivots.

## Q.7 a.1 — Infinite as not-limited, not as infinitely-large

Aquinas's first move on Q.7 a.1 is the move mind-monism must make on its own behalf. *Infinite* here means *not finite, not limited* — *not* infinite-in-magnitude. Things are limited by their matter (which pins them to a place and time) or by their essence (which pins them to a kind of existing). God is neither. He is unlimited *as being-being-itself*, with no essence to differentiate his existence and no matter to pin his existence down to a place.

Mind-monism's translation runs perfectly parallel and is critical to mark explicitly. Mind-at-large is *not* "a very big mind" — that would be the magnitude error Aquinas refuses for divine infinity. Mind-at-large is mind *as ontological primitive*: not constituted by any particular essence-or-matter limitation that would make it one mind among others. Kastrup is precise about this: mind-at-large is the *being-of-experiencing* itself, not a thing-with-properties one could specify. The synthesis writer must hold this exactly when speaking of mind-at-large to avoid sliding into a pop-spiritual "cosmic mind" picture that is metaphysically incoherent. *What is unlimited in mind-monism is unlimited in the same sense Aquinas means in Q.7 a.1*: not by extending forever in some direction but by not being constituted by limitation in the first place. The classical and the contemporary share this discipline.

The geometrician's line (Q.7 a.3) makes the discipline operational. *Mathematics handles infinity by abstraction-from-finite, not by encountering an actual infinite.* Contemporary physics inherits this: the renormalization group, the limit procedures of quantum field theory, the apparent infinities of cosmology — all are abstractions, none are actual encountered magnitudes. Wolfram's ruliad pushes the discipline further: the *space of all possible computational rules* is not an existing actual infinite but a formally specified abstraction in which any actual computation appears as a finite trajectory. *Wolfram's primitive is not "infinitely large"; it is the *unconditioned* that any conditioned computation participates in.* Aquinas's Q.7 a.3 has more contemporary teeth than its medieval framing suggests.

## Q.7 a.2 — only the uncreated unlimited; the *create-an-uncreated-thing* objection

Aquinas's response to the "if God is omnipotent, why can't he create another uncreated thing?" objection is one of the most concise pieces of medieval philosophical theology. *Only the uncreated can be without limit in all respects.* To ask for "an uncreated thing the uncreated creates" is to ask for a contradiction; *the response that contradictions cannot be does not prove that God doesn't exist; it just proves that contradictions do not exist*. Mind-monism receives this without modification: the *primitive* is what every alter participates in by being an alter at all; the request "make an alter that is not an alter but the primitive itself" is not a power-claim about mind-at-large but a category-confused phrase. Aquinas's reply *is* the contemporary reply.

## Q.8 a.1 — *A thing is where it operates*

Habash names this as the day's *thematic foundation*, and he is right at a depth he doesn't fully unfold. *A thing is where it operates*. Where is your soul? Where it is operating — i.e., the extent of your body. Where is an angel? Wherever it operates. So where is God? Wherever causation reaches into being — which is everywhere being is.

The contemporary formal counterpart is Hoffman's interface theory at full strength. *Space is not the container in which operations happen; space is what operations under the species-specific interface generate.* The visual field is not "out there in space"; it is what perception-as-active-inference produces *as if* it were spatially extended. Spatial location, including the location of perceiving agents, is *constituted by* the dynamics of inference, not the other way around. Hoffman's claim is empirical (with experimental backing in evolutionary game-theoretic models) and Aquinas's claim is metaphysical, but the *form* is the same: location is downstream of operation, not upstream. *A thing is where it operates*, full stop, in both vocabularies.

This article also gives the synthesis its most precise translation of divine immanence. Aquinas: *God is in all things at their most fundamental depth — more inmost to us than we are to ourselves.* Kastrup's mind-monism gives the underlying structure: the *substrate* of consciousness is closer to the alter than the alter's own conscious self-perception, because consciousness-as-self-perception is one mode of expression of the substrate, not a thing that *contains* the substrate. The alter's introspection reaches as far as its dissociative boundary; the substrate is *outside* (and prior to) that boundary. Aquinas's formulation is the precise classical name for what Kastrup specifies dynamically. Levin and Friston extend the picture: the morphogenetic field is more inmost to the cells than the cells' own genetic regulation (the field controls the genome's expression); the generative model is more inmost to the agent than the agent's introspectable beliefs (the priors are what makes those beliefs available at all). *More inmost than we are to ourselves* is mind-monism's load-bearing claim, and Aquinas's claim is its medieval anticipation.

Augustine is the central-theme citation. *Late have I loved thee, O Beauty so ancient and so new; late have I loved thee. For behold, thou wert within me, and I outside; thou didst touch me, and I have burned for thy peace.* This is the perspective-limited agent in the first person. The agent's lateness is not blameworthy negligence; it is the structural condition of being an alter — the substrate is too inmost to be picked out among the icons of the interface. Salvation, then, is the progressive recognition of what was already there: covenant inclusion in Wright's vocabulary; non-dual contemplative recognition in Rohr's; reintegration of alter and mind-at-large in Kastrup's. Three vocabularies; one structural shape.

## Q.8 a.2 — God everywhere as cause, not as occupying space

If God is in all things, is he everywhere? *Yes — but not by occupying space.* God is not extended in space; he is not corporeal. He is everywhere as the *cause* of all things existing, of their movement, and of the *place* itself that they fill. Habash recalls the Five Ways: Second/Third/Fourth Ways for the cause of existing; First/Fifth Ways for the cause of movement; and the cause of place itself rounds it out.

Mind-monism's translation: mind-at-large is not located in space because *space itself is what mind-at-large generates as the form of conditioned experience*. Arkani-Hamed's post-spacetime physics is the empirical-theoretical counterpart: spacetime is not fundamental; it emerges from a more abstract structure (positive geometries, twistor-like substrates). What Aquinas asserts metaphysically — that God is the cause of place itself — physics is now reaching toward as the claim that *spacetime is emergent*, not given. CROSS-002 in the C2A2 master wiki tracks this convergence across Hoffman, Arkani-Hamed, and Wolfram. The medieval claim is more vindicated than its critics suppose; the contemporary claim is more radical than it might at first appear.

## Q.8 a.3 — Essence, presence, power: the threefold immanence

Aquinas distinguishes three modes of God's universal presence:

- **By power**, like a king present to every part of his kingdom — top-down causal authority pervading the whole.
- **By presence**, like the things in front of you are present to you — held in attention, with oversight.
- **By essence**, because he alone is Creator: only the unconditioned can produce existence from nothing, since the *gap between nothing and anything is infinite ontologically*.

The contemporary triple translates with surprising directness. *Power* maps onto top-down causation in active-inference models (the highest-level priors control the lower-level dynamics) and onto Levin's morphogenetic-field control (the bioelectric whole controls the cellular parts). *Presence* maps onto the perceptual frame within which the field operates — Hoffman's interface, Friston's generative model, the agent's *attention* in the technical neuroscientific sense. *Essence* maps onto substrate-grounding: every alter participates in mind-at-large not as a remote causal effect but as the very condition of its existing as anything at all. The three modes are not parallel; they are nested. The field controls because it is present; it is present because it is substrate; and the substrate is the *being-of-experiencing* without which there would be no agent to be controlled or attended to in the first place.

Aquinas's special-presence-by-grace addition (*"no other perfection except grace added to substance renders God present in anything as the object known and loved"*) deserves separate treatment. *Grace* in the contemporary frame is the additional structural condition under which an alter integrates back toward the substrate — the *recognition* that adds the third level of presence on top of power and substrate-grounding. Rohr's *Everything Belongs* names this directly: divine immanence is universal as power and substrate, but contemplatively recognized only with the work that *Falling Upward* describes. Wright's covenantal grace adds the corporate form: grace is what gathers a people into intentional alignment with the substrate, around the resurrected Messiah as the icon-that-transmits.

## Q.8 a.4 — *To be everywhere belongs to God alone*; the matter and universals discussion

Aquinas's six-objection treatment of Q.8 a.4 includes the moment most directly relevant to mind-monism's anti-pantheism guardrail (yesterday's Q.6 a.4 carried forward). He treats the apparent counter-cases — *matter is everywhere* (because all material things are made of it); *universals are everywhere* (because Humanity is in every human) — and refuses both. *Matter doesn't actually exist; it is sheer potency, not an actual everywhere-thing.* *Universals as actually existing outside the divine mind are limited by the matter they inform; they are not absolutely everywhere.* Only the unconditioned ground, which neither is potential awaiting actualization (matter) nor is pinned to particulars (universal-in-particulars), is absolutely everywhere.

Mind-monism agrees and tightens. The *substrate* is not *matter* in the philosophical sense; it is mind, the *being-of-experiencing*, neither potency awaiting actuality nor a universal pinned to particulars. The substrate is the unique kind of *everywhere* Aquinas reserves for God — and the agreement here is precise enough that the synthesis writer should mark it: under Kastrup's analytic idealism, *only* the substrate has the structural property Aquinas calls *being everywhere absolutely*; alters and their experiential contents are everywhere only relatively, in the same way the dough is *unlimited* in a relative sense (Q.7 a.2). The anti-magnitude discipline of Q.7 a.1 and the anti-pantheism discipline of Q.8 a.4 work together: mind-at-large is everywhere and unlimited *in the unique structural sense of being-itself*, while alters are everywhere and unlimited *only relatively, in the dough/numbers/mind sense*.

The Catechism citation Habash brings forward — *"the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit are not three principles of creation but one principle"* — is, like yesterday's Trinity-deferral, queued for I Q.27–43. The synthesis flags it: any internal-relations question within the substrate (Rohr's relational ontology; Kastrup's open question about whether mind-at-large has internal structure) will be revisited at the Trinitarian articles.

## Where this leaves us

The keystone holds, and on Q.7 a.1 and Q.8 a.1 it tightens: mind-monism *needs* exactly Aquinas's anti-magnitude discipline (*unlimited as not constituted by limitation, not as infinitely large*) and *receives* exactly his immanence formulation (*more inmost than we are to ourselves*). The medieval frame and the contemporary frame agree that location is downstream of operation, that the unconditioned is everywhere as cause but not by occupying space, that the threefold immanence (power, presence, essence) nests rather than parallels, and that anti-pantheism preserves the genuine distinctness of alters from substrate. Augustine's voice is the day's central-theme citation. Tomorrow Habash takes us to Q.9–10 (the immutability and eternity of God) — the next pair will press the question of *how* the unconditioned ground stands in relation to time itself, which under mind-monism becomes the question of how the substrate generates temporal experience without itself being temporal.
