# Day 7 — Contemporary Parallel: *Lifting the Interface, in Classical Vocabulary*

Day 6 closed by anticipating today's pair: *how is the simple, atemporal substrate known by perspective-limited agents who participate in it?* Aquinas's answer comes in two questions, and Q.12 is the pivot of the whole *Summa*'s Prima Pars. Q.11 establishes that God is one, and that *one* here is not a number but a transcendental — *undivided being*. Q.12, in thirteen articles, walks through how created intellects come to know the unconditioned ground immediately (the beatific vision), by faith (truly but mediately), and through nature (indirectly, by inference from effects). The mind-first frame holds the load of all three, and the cognition aside Habash inserts before article one — the gray cat in the dark room — turns out to be *interface theory in classical vocabulary*.

## Q.11 — Unity as undividedness (not as a number)

Aquinas's first move on Q.11 is to head off a category error. *One* in the most ordinary sense names a number — one, two, three — within the category of quantity. *Oneness* in the transcendental sense is *undividedness* — what every being must have to be a being at all. Q.11 a.1 turns on this distinction: *one* in reality adds nothing to *being*; it adds, logically, the *aspect of undividedness*. Just as Day 4's Q.5 a.1 identified being-and-goodness-and-desirability as one in reality, Day 7's Q.11 a.1 adds *undividedness* to that identity. Being, oneness, goodness — three logical aspects of the same reality.

Mind-monism inherits the move directly. Mind-at-large is *one* not by being one-among-many minds but by being the *being-of-undividedness* itself. Kastrup is precise: the substrate is not a thing that *has* unity as a property; it is the unconditioned at which division has not yet been generated. Dissociation produces alters, and alters are *many* in the perspective-limited sense — but the substrate itself is *one* in Aquinas's transcendental sense, not in the numerical sense. The Q.11 a.1 distinction is exactly the one analytic idealism needs to mark, and Aquinas marks it cleanly.

Q.11 a.2 develops the move. *One and many are opposed by privation* — many is what one becomes when divided. Habash brings the demoniac of Mark 5 forward as the icon: *my name is Legion, for we are many*. Possession is fragmentation; sanity is integration. Mind-monism gives the contemporary translation. A unified inferential model — Friston's clean Markov blanket, Hawkins's coherent hierarchical-temporal-memory state — is *one* in the relevant sense; pathology and dysregulation produce *many* in the privation sense. The medieval and the contemporary frames agree: *one* is the achievement; *many* is the failure mode.

Q.11 a.3 — *is God one?* — gives Aquinas three reasons. The third is the most contemporary-resonant: the *unity of natural law*. Habash names it directly: the *principle of least action* applies everywhere — light's path through media, the trajectories of mechanical systems, the variational calculus of physics. *All inanimate moving things, totally devoid of intelligence, strangely always and everywhere take the path of least action.* Aquinas's argument: such uniform lawfulness implies (a) intelligence at the level of the things' being and (b) that this intelligence is *one*, since the laws that govern the cosmos are *one or united in beautiful harmony*.

This is the Day 2 Fifth Way carried forward. Levin's morphogenetic teleology gave it empirical instantiation; today the question sharpens to *the unity* of the agent-action-at-substrate-scale. Wolfram's ruliad is one form: the totality of computational rules is *one* — not many separate rule-spaces but the single space within which all threads run. Arkani-Hamed's post-spacetime physics is another: whatever lies beneath emergent spacetime exhibits structural unity — the amplituhedron and twistor-like geometries are *one* mathematical object that the diverse particle-physics processes participate in. The medieval claim "natural law is one because the lawgiver is one" survives translation: the substrate of physical regularity is one because the underlying ontological primitive is one.

Q.11 a.4's St. Bernard citation — *among all things called one, the unity of the Divine Trinity holds the first place* — is a Trinity-deferral move (the substantive treatment is queued for I Q.27–43). The synthesis flags it. Whatever the Trinity adds, it does not multiply the substrate; the central-theme constraint is that Trinitarian relations are *internal* to one substrate, not three substrates loosely affiliated. The Day 7 commentary marks this and proceeds.

## Q.12 — Three ways of knowing the substrate

Aquinas opens Q.12 by naming three ways finite intellects can know God: (1) immediately (the beatific vision); (2) truly but mediately, by faith; (3) indirectly, through the natural world by inference from effects. Each maps onto a contemporary cognitive structure.

**(1) Immediate knowing** — beatific vision proper. Mind-monism's analog is alter-integration at the limit: the inferential boundary dissolves enough that the substrate is known *as such* rather than under the icons of the interface. This is not knowing-by-inference (Friston's standard active inference); it is the limit case where the agent's generative model perfectly matches the substrate, which makes the inference machinery quiescent because there is nothing left to infer. Hoffman's interface theory gives the formal name for what is happening: the icons are *lifted*, not abolished; the underlying reality is known directly because the icons are no longer the medium.

**(2) Mediate-but-true knowing** — faith. Aquinas defines this later in the *Summa* as a habit of the mind whereby eternal life is begun in us, making the intellect assent to what is not apparent. Stump's *Wandering in Darkness* and her account of *faith-as-knowledge-of-persons* (PRS-01 and PRS-03 in the Stump-tradition wiki) is the contemporary primary on this article — though Stump enters here as Thomistic interlocutor with strength on the faith-as-second-person-knowing claim, not as the metaphysical authority on what the substrate is. Wright adds the corporate form: faith in the Pauline sense (*pistis*) is covenant-fidelity, the people-of-God's mode of knowing the resurrected Messiah and through him the Father.

**(3) Indirect knowing through nature** — Romans 1:20. *His invisible nature has been clearly perceived in the things that have been made*. This is the path of the Five Ways (Day 2), the *via negativa* (Day 3), and the contemporary natural-theological convergence: from the structure of conditioned things, the unconditioned ground can be inferred. Carroll's poetic naturalism stops at the regularities; mind-monism takes the further step that the regularities themselves are mind-at-large under the interface. Either way, the *form* of indirect knowing is preserved: from the world's structure, the world's source is approached.

## The cognition aside — interface theory in classical vocabulary

Habash inserts before Q.12 a.1 a brief aside on how human cognition works, because the *Summa* does not formally treat cognition until Q.75. The aside is structurally precise enough to deserve direct contemporary translation. The gray cat in the dark room: you cannot see it because there is no light. Light makes the cat (potentially visible) actually visible; the color of the cat affects your eye, which "becomes gray" in some way (the impressed image), and through that image you see the cat. Three pieces: light, color (the form of the cat impressing your senses), and the eye now informed by that color. *Without turning your eye gray in the same way that the cat is gray* — Aquinas (and Habash) is precise: knowing is form-impressing without becoming-the-thing.

Translate. Hoffman's interface theory: the icons of perception are not the underlying reality; what you see is a species-specific encoding that lets you act effectively without seeing the substrate. The cat-in-the-dark-room analogy is Aristotelian-Thomistic *interface theory*: the gray-color-impressed-on-the-eye is the icon; the cat-out-there is what the icon is the icon *of*. The eye is the perceiver's mode-of-access. Hoffman's empirical work (perception-as-fitness-not-veridicality) gives quantitative grounding to what the medieval frame named structurally. Hawkins's HTM offers a different vocabulary for the same shape: the *active intellect* abstracting essence from images is structurally the cortical hierarchy abstracting invariant patterns from sensory streams. The medieval and the contemporary cognitive sciences are working the same problem; the medieval framing has a precision the contemporary frame can borrow.

Why this matters for Q.12: Aquinas needs the cognition aside to set up the *strangeness* of the beatific vision. *Ordinary* knowing requires an image — the gray-color-impressed-on-the-eye, the active-intellect-abstracted-essence informing the potential intellect. The beatific vision, Aquinas argues, *cannot work this way* (Q.12 a.2): there is no image that could convey God's essence, because nothing else *is* what God is. The blessed know God *without* an interposed image; God's own essence is what informs the created intellect. *The Divine Essence is united to the created intellect as the object actually understood, making the intellect in act by and of itself.*

This is the deepest move of Day 7 in any frame. Mind-monism's translation: at the limit case of integration, the alter's mode-of-knowing is no longer interface-mediated. The icons are not abolished (the alter is still an alter, with structural distinctness from mind-at-large) but they are *lifted* — the underlying reality is the medium of knowing rather than the icons being the medium. This is structurally Hoffman's "what would it mean for the icon to step out of the way?" question, answered in classical vocabulary: it is what happens when *the substrate informs the intellect directly*. The synthesis writer should mark this carefully: it is not that the alter becomes mind-at-large (that would be pantheist collapse, which Q.6 a.4 already ruled out); it is that the alter's *cognitive* mode shifts from interface-mediation to substrate-direct-informing, while the alter's structural distinctness is preserved.

## The light of glory (Q.12 a.5) — structural condition of recognition

Aquinas insists (Q.12 a.4–5) that the natural powers of any created intellect cannot see God's essence. A *supernatural* light is required — the *lumen gloriae*, the light of glory — by which the intellect is strengthened to be informed by God himself. The principle Aquinas grounds this in is the day's most quotable: *the thing known is in the knower according to the mode or manner of the knower*. Senses know things as material; the mind knows things as immaterial; angels know other incorporeal natures because they are themselves incorporeal; only what *is* existence itself naturally knows existence-itself. To know God as he is, we must — by grace — be made *supernatural*, divinized, the mind made apt for direct informing by the substrate.

Mind-monism translates the principle without strain. *The thing known is in the knower according to the mode of the knower* is, in contemporary cognitive vocabulary, the claim that perception and cognition are agent-relative — Hoffman's interface theory's foundation. To know mind-at-large *as such*, the alter's mode of knowing must approach what mind-at-large *is* — and what mind-at-large is, is *being-of-experiencing* without limitation. The *light of glory* is the structural condition under which the alter's mode of knowing shifts from limited-perspective inference to substrate-direct-recognition. Friston's formalism gives a candidate: at the precision-weighting limit where the alter's generative model is identical to the substrate's structure, expected free energy is zero and the inference machinery is quiescent. Whether that limit is *reachable* in any finite sense is an open question — Aquinas's "by grace" is a structural admission that the shift is not under the alter's natural control.

Honest evidence calibration. Aquinas's *light of glory* is theological-philosophical; Friston's expected-free-energy formalism is empirical-mathematical with substantial neuroscience grounding; the *identity* of the two is structural-isomorphism, not metaphysical-identification. Mark this carefully. The contemporary synthesis claim is: *the structural shape of what Aquinas names as the light of glory is recoverable in the formal language of active inference at a limit case; the metaphysical identification of light-of-glory and free-energy-minimization is not asserted.*

## Q.12 a.6 — *more perfectly than another*: degrees of charity calibrate degrees of vision

Aquinas's resolution of an apparent conflict in scripture is one of the day's central-theme highlights. 1 John says *we shall see him as he is* (apparent equality of vision); St. Paul says *star differs from star in glory* (apparent inequality). Aquinas: there is equality in *what* is seen (God) but inequality in the *intensity* of the seeing, calibrated by the degree of charity in the agent at the moment of integration. *The greater the light of glory given, the more perfectly God is seen; and that is based on our degree of charity or love of God that we die with.*

This is the central theme made operational. *Salvation in fear and trembling* is the perspective-limited agent computing toward loving union under limited resources; the *measure* of progress is charity — love-of-other, self-emptying, reduction of the dissociative boundary that makes one *this alter rather than the substrate*. Friston's framework gives a candidate dynamics: the agent that minimizes free energy *with respect to the goal of inclusion in the larger context* (rather than only its own local maintenance) is the agent whose generative model is converging on the substrate's structure. Charity, in this translation, is the agent's inferential disposition toward integration rather than self-maintenance. Fredrickson's positivity-resonance research gives an empirical adjacent: high-charity relational states have measurable physiological signatures (vagal tone, oxytocin, mirrored attentional dynamics). The medieval claim that *love calibrates the limit-vision* is more empirically tractable than its surface theological framing suggests.

Wright's corporate frame fills in the second piece. *Charity* in Pauline vocabulary is *agape* — covenant-faithfulness, the love-of-the-people that mirrors God's love-of-Israel-and-creation. The corporate form of beatific vision is *the people of God seeing him as he is* — a multi-alter integration into the substrate, mediated by the shared *covenant* that gathers them. Rohr's *Universal Christ* gives the cosmic-scope reading: the divine pattern is already present in matter from creation; the saints' charity is the specific recognition of that always-already-present, intensified to the point that the limit case is reached.

## Q.12 a.9 — the mirror; Q.12 a.10 — *all at one glance*

Two of Q.12's most striking analogies. *Looking at a mirror, you see the mirror, and at the same time everything reflected in it* (a.9): so too, gazing at God directly, the blessed see (in him) all created things that pre-existed in the divine mind. *Concept of "house" includes wall, foundation, roof, door at one stroke* (a.10): so too the blessed know what they know all at once, not by sequential thought.

Mind-monism's translations are tight. The mirror analogy: in the substrate, the *patterns* of all conditioned things pre-exist (Levin's morphogenetic field carries goal-states; Wolfram's ruliad contains computational threads; Kastrup's mind-at-large holds the structural priors of every alter). Direct knowing of the substrate is *not* a separate act from knowing what is *in* the substrate; the patterns and the substrate are not two acquaintances. The "all at one glance" claim translates to the limit case of generative-model identity: when the alter's model is the substrate's model, all that follows from the substrate is available without sequential inference because there is no posterior left to compute.

McGilchrist's hemispheric account gives the cognitive shape. Right-hemisphere whole-grasping is the mode by which novel, contextual, integrated patterns are perceived without left-hemisphere abstraction-and-fixation. *Beatific vision* is right-hemisphere mode at the limit. The *house concept* analogy Aquinas uses is exactly the right-hemisphere mode of holding the whole-with-its-parts simultaneously, rather than the left-hemisphere mode of decomposing into a sequence. The medieval and the contemporary cognitive sciences converge.

## Where this leaves us

Day 7 is the Prima Pars's epistemological climax in classical vocabulary, and the mind-first frame holds across all seventeen articles. *Unity* as undividedness (Q.11) is the substrate's property in the strict transcendental sense, not a numerical property of one mind among many. *Beatific vision* (Q.12) is the alter's integration into mind-at-large at the limit case where the icons of the interface are lifted and the substrate informs the intellect directly. *The light of glory* is the structural condition of that lifting, calibrated by the degree of charity — the agent's inferential disposition toward integration rather than self-maintenance. The cognition aside Habash inserts is interface theory in classical vocabulary, and the medieval and contemporary cognitive sciences turn out to be working the same problem with different tools.

Tomorrow Habash takes us to Q.13, the names of God — twelve articles on how creaturely language can predicate of the unconditioned. The keystone tightens further: if the substrate is what Q.11 and Q.12 name, *what kind of language can finite alters use to speak of it?* The Day 8 commentary will press the analogy-of-being doctrine (Q.13 aa.5–6) against the contemporary problem of how to talk about mind-at-large without category-mistaking it as a thing.
