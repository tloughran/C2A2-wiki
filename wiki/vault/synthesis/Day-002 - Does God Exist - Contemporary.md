# Day 2 — Contemporary Parallel: *Five Ways the Interface Implies Its Ground*

This is the first day under the metaphysical guardrail. Where Aquinas asks *whether God exists*, the contemporary frame does not answer in his terms — it reframes. Mind / consciousness is the ontological primitive (Kastrup); perceived spacetime is a species-specific user interface (Hoffman); form is empirically real and scales as bioelectric pattern (Levin); active inference gives the formal dynamics of agents-with-goals under limited resources (Friston). Under that reframe, the Five Ways are not five proofs of an external entity. They are five facets of how a perspective-limited conscious agent finds the structure of its own experience implying an unconditioned ground. The argument's *form* — effect-to-cause, terminating in something unconditioned — survives. The argument's *substance* — what that unconditioned is — shifts decisively.

## What sacred doctrine becomes (Q.1)

Aquinas's Q.1 is a tradition-vitality argument before he ever asks about God. He establishes that revelation is necessary for human salvation because the goal of human life "is just not intuitive" — finite agents cannot derive their end from their own resources. This is one move. The contemporary parallel translates it without significant loss: under active inference (Friston), an agent without a generative model of its environment cannot minimize free energy; under MacIntyre (the C2A2 scaffolding), a tradition is alive only if it can solve its own problems and identify genuine anomalies. Both translate Aquinas's claim into the same shape: *finite cognitive agents need a frame they cannot bootstrap*. Revelation is what an agent receives from a more comprehensive perspective. That receiving is structurally what active inference calls a top-down generative prior; what Levin calls collective cognition feeding the lower-scale agent its target morphology; what Wright calls *covenant* — Israel and the Church receive their identity from the perspective of YHWH, not from below.

The central-theme thread starts here. Conscious agents are individuated by the limitations of their perspective; *salvation* (Wright's reading of Philippians 2:12) is inclusion in the people united with God; that inclusion is a *computational task*, limited by the resources we perceive. Aquinas's "necessary for man's salvation" is not the medieval framing artifact some moderns would dismiss. It is the same claim, in a sturdier form than active inference alone supplies, because it names the goal of the computation — *loving unity* — that variational free-energy minimization, by itself, does not.

Aquinas's five claims about the discipline of theology then translate. *It is a science.* (PRS form: the **Problem** is what makes a body of knowledge one science; the **Resource** is the chain of solved problems plus revealed first principles; the **Solution** is that revelation-to-theology is what mathematics-is-to-music-theory.) Under the C2A2 framing this is exactly Kuhn's solved-problem-as-unit-of-progress claim: a tradition is one science when its solved problems share a generating set of principles. *It is one science because its essential object is one — God.* Under mind-monism the equivalent is: the science of consciousness is one science because its essential object is one — the ontological primitive itself, however partially perceived. *It is nobler than the others, because more certain.* Aquinas grounds certainty in revelation's source; the contemporary parallel grounds it in the structural fact that no perspective-limited frame can be more certain than the frame that contains all perspectives. Where Aquinas distinguishes speculative-from-practical, mind-monism distinguishes the contemplative recognition of one's situation in mind-at-large (Rohr's *Universal Christ*) from the active-inference computation of how to act within it.

## The metaphysical keystone (Q.1 a.7) — apophatic interface theory

Article 7 is the day's deepest move and the keystone of every metaphysical synthesis to follow. Aquinas: *we cannot know in what consists the essence of God; nevertheless in this science we make use of his effects, so as to understand him*. We know God like we know a painter by his paintings — much more about what the painter is *not* than what he is. This is the apophatic principle, and it is structurally identical to interface theory. In Hoffman's frame, the perceived world is a species-specific user interface: what is fundamental cannot be known *under the icons*, only inferred from the *behavior* of icons. The painting is not the painter; the desktop icon is not the file. *Knowing by effects* and *knowing by the interface* are the same epistemic shape.

Two refinements under the contemporary frame. First, Aquinas's "we cannot know what God is *in himself*" lands precisely on Kastrup's claim that mind-at-large, *qua* ontological primitive, has no external structure to be known *as a thing*: it is the *being-of-experiencing* itself, not a thing-with-properties. Mind-at-large is not knowable from outside because there is no outside. Aquinas reaches the same wall from the medieval direction: the unconditioned source of being cannot be known by any agent whose knowing is conditioned. The *form* of the argument (apophatic) is preserved; the *content* shifts from substantialist hylomorphism to mind-monist ontology. Second, the *negative-theology* tradition Aquinas inherits from Pseudo-Dionysius (and which Habash will return to in Q.4 a.3 with the Dionysius citation) becomes, in Rohr's *Universal Christ*, the contemplative practice of letting the icons of one's perception fall away to make room for the divine pattern *in* the icons rather than behind them. Rohr's "everything belongs" is not pantheism; it is mind-monism's reading of *creation as participation in being-itself*.

The strength of evidence here matters: Hoffman's interface theory is mathematically formalized and empirically supported (perception-as-fitness-not-veridicality has experimental backing in evolutionary game-theoretic models). Kastrup's analytic idealism is philosophically argued with several converging lines of evidence (combination problem, decombination of dissociative identity disorder as analogy for the alters of mind-at-large) but is not empirical in the experimental sense. Aquinas's apophatic principle is theological-philosophical, not empirical. The synthesis claim is that *all three converge on the same shape*: the unconditioned cannot be a known thing. That convergence is non-trivial; it is also speculative when one tries to identify the three positions with each other rather than note their isomorphism. Mark this as a structural-isomorphism claim, not a metaphysical-identification.

## The Five Ways under mind-monism (Q.2 a.3)

Aquinas's mnemonic — "in extending your arm toward a Monday-morning coffee, you prove God's existence in no fewer than five ways" — is, structurally, an *agent-action* anchor. Every Way is grounded in a single intentional action. That framing is exactly what Levin means by agency-at-scale and what Friston means by active inference: the unit of analysis is not a bare causal chain but an embedded directed action under uncertainty. So the Five Ways translate not as five abstract proofs but as *five facets of a single concrete act of intending*, traced back to its unconditioned ground. We take them in two clusters.

**Ways 1, 2, 3 — the chain of contingent causation must terminate.** First Way: the transition from potential to actual requires something with no potential to be actualized. Second Way: efficient causation requires a non-derivative source of being. Third Way: contingent existence requires necessary existence. Under mind-monism these three are *one* claim restated: any chain of conditioned states must terminate in something unconditioned, and the unconditioned cannot be a *thing-among-things* because all things are conditioned. Carroll's poetic naturalism is the contemporary foil here — for Carroll the chain terminates in physical law, full stop, with no further explanation needed. Mind-monism replies that *physical law itself is conditioned*: it is what we observe under the species-specific interface; the underlying reality is mind-at-large, of which physical law is the regularity-of-experience. The First Way's "non-receiver of actuality" becomes mind-as-primitive; the Second Way's "non-derivative source of existence" becomes the same; the Third Way's "necessary existence" becomes the *being-of-experiencing* that does not depend on any particular experience for its being. Aquinas's three roads converge on the same one mountaintop.

The honest evidence call: Carroll has the better empirical position on physical-law-as-stopping-point if one restricts the question to physics. Mind-monism has the better position if one takes the explanandum to be the existence of *experience itself* — for which physical law is silent. The Five Ways' First three are, under the contemporary frame, defensible *only* if one already grants that there is something to explain beyond the regularities of the interface. That is not a defect; it is the same place Aquinas's argument lives. Both Aquinas and the mind-monist must convince the interlocutor that *there is an explanandum beyond what is observable*. Where Aquinas argues from the *contingency of being* (which Carroll accepts but redirects to law), the mind-monist argues from the *fact of experience* (which Carroll, qua poetic naturalist, brackets but does not explain).

**Way 4 — the table of sums.** Habash narrates this as: students answer 2+2 and you grade them confidently because there is a table of sums you compare against, even when you're not consciously thinking about it. Therefore perfection in some respect must actually exist somewhere. From another angle: would 2+2=4 be true before the universe existed? Yes — eternal and necessary. But truth is a quality of mind-with-thing; in *what* mind does this eternal, necessary truth reside? In an eternal and necessary mind. *That's what we mean by God.*

This is, formally, Kastrup's argument for analytic idealism. Eternal mathematical truth requires a substrate that is itself eternal and that holds it as truth — i.e., a mind. If mathematical truths are eternal and must reside in a mind, then there is an eternal mind. The C2A2 cross-tradition pairing is FLAG-003 (in Tom's PRS-form record): Kastrup's mind-at-large, which holds the eternal truths and grounds the dissociative-alter structure of finite minds, is formally what Aquinas means by *the eternal and necessary mind in which 2+2=4 is held*. The two positions are not identical — Aquinas's God is also pure act, simple, perfect, in ways Kastrup does not assert of mind-at-large — but on this article, the moves are isomorphic. Aquinas's Fourth Way *is* the mind-monist argument from mathematical truth.

**Way 5 — Fermat, Feynman, and embedded teleology.** Habash uses the lifeguard-on-the-beach example: the optimal path to a swimmer drowning diagonally is neither straight-then-90° nor all-diagonal but the path that minimizes total time given that you're faster on land than in water. Light does this too — Fermat showed it; Feynman emphasized it — *and light does not think*. So when universally unintelligent things move intelligently, they do so because they participate in a universally affecting intelligence from which they derive their nature. In contemporary frame this is: *the principle of least action is real; embedded directedness is not a metaphor*. Levin's morphogenetic teleology is the empirical instantiation: bioelectric patterns hold a target morphology before the cells have arranged themselves into it; injured cells *know where to grow* because the whole-body bioelectric field encodes the goal. Friston's free-energy principle gives the formal dynamics: agents-under-uncertainty actively minimize the divergence between predicted and inferred states, which is mathematically a directedness toward an end. Aquinas's Fifth Way is *more correct than its medieval reputation suggests* — light's path-minimization is not a metaphor for intelligence, it is a *real* embedded teleology that requires explanation, and the explanations that work are mind-monist (the regularity is *in* mind-at-large, expressed as physical law) or panpsychist (each system has some form of agency at its scale). Carroll the naturalist accepts the principle of least action as a brute regularity; the mind-monist asks why such regularities hold for unintelligent matter at all, and answers that *intelligence-bearing form* is not a property of matter but the structure of mind-at-large showing up in matter from the outside.

The Fifth Way carries the central-theme thread most directly. *Salvation in fear and trembling* is the perspective-limited agent computing toward its end with the resources it perceives. Aquinas: "we desire happiness *necessarily*, an end written into our nature, for the sake of which we desire all these other things voluntarily — placed within us." This is identical in form to Friston's expected-free-energy minimization with a fixed prior preference (which is what gives the agent its goal-direction at all) plus Wright's *covenant* (which is what fills in *what* the goal is — inclusion in the people united with God). Levin and Wright together do the work the medieval Aristotelian *teleology* did, with neither having to pretend to be the other.

## Four senses of Scripture (Q.1 a.10)

Aquinas's four senses — literal (intended meaning, often expressed via metaphor), allegorical (pointing to Christ), moral (what we ought to do), anagogical (pointing to the end of time) — receive Wright as their primary contemporary counterpart. Wright's narrative-historical reading of Scripture (in *The New Testament and the People of God* and through *Paul and the Faithfulness of God*) is the *literal sense* under contemporary scholarship: what was the author of Romans intending under the conditions of second-temple Judaism, in dialogue with the Pharisaic, apocalyptic, Qumran, and Hellenistic Jewish traditions? The allegorical-Christological sense, under Wright, becomes the recognition that the Pauline gospel re-narrates Israel's story around the resurrected Messiah as its climactic event. The moral sense in Wright is *cruciform ethics*. The anagogical sense, in *Surprised by Hope*, is Wright's recovery of the new-creation reading: the Christian hope is not escape from creation but creation's renewal. Where Aquinas claims Scripture has *all four* senses, Wright claims that *only by holding the literal sense rigorously* do the others remain disciplined and not collapse into eisegesis.

McGilchrist's hemispheric account adds a structural reading: the literal sense maps onto the left hemisphere's de-contextualized abstraction, the allegorical/moral/anagogical senses onto the right hemisphere's contextual, embodied, narrative grasp. Both hemispheres must operate; *the literal alone produces fundamentalism, the allegorical alone produces gnosticism*. Aquinas's claim that Scripture has multiple senses is, under McGilchrist, the claim that Scripture engages the whole brain rather than only the left.

## Bridges

- **PRS-04 in the Stump-tradition wiki — Stump × Levin on final causality.** Tom's PRS-form record of the cleanest current crossing between a 13th-century metaphysical claim and a 21st-century empirical finding. Final causality (Aquinas's Fifth Way; Stump's Thomistic interlocution) maps onto Levin's morphogenetic teleology as the *embedded directedness* that explains why the principle of least action holds for unintelligent matter.
- **FLAG-003 in the master wiki — Kastrup × Friston.** Kastrup's dissociative boundary may be formally identical to Friston's Markov blanket. If the equivalence holds, analytic idealism and the free-energy principle become two vocabularies for one set of structural dynamics — the first consciousness-physics formal equivalence in the C2A2 network. The Fourth Way, read as Kastrup's argument from mathematical truth, sits squarely in this region.
- **CROSS-001 in the master wiki — substrate-independence.** Five programs (Levin, Hoffman, Hawkins, Kastrup, Wolfram) are all asking *is consciousness substrate-independent?* in their own vocabularies. Aquinas's Fifth Way grants the same shape: intelligence-bearing form scales across the substrates of light, biology, and rational agents because it derives from a single source.
- **CROSS-002 in the master wiki — spacetime-fundamental.** Three programs (Hoffman, Arkani-Hamed, Wolfram) converge on the negative answer: spacetime is not fundamental. The Five Ways need this to land — under a substantialist physics where spacetime is fundamental, the chain of causation is *in* spacetime and Carroll's poetic naturalism wins. Under a post-spacetime physics the chain is *of* spacetime and the unconditioned ground reappears as the question Aquinas asked.
- **Wright on Acts 17 — *Paul and the Faithfulness of God*.** Aquinas opens Q.2 with Paul: "in him we live and move and have our being." Wright reads Paul's Areopagus speech as a deliberate re-narration of Stoic and Epicurean cosmology under Israel's God. The Five Ways' framing — God *encompasses* us — is what Wright takes to be Paul's pastoral move at Athens.
- **Rohr on Universal Christ.** Aquinas's apophatic principle (Q.1 a.7) and Habash's Q.4 preview ("we know God like we know a painter by his paintings") receive Rohr as their contemplative counterpart: divine pattern present in matter from creation, not behind it. The mind-monist rendering is that the icons of the interface are *not* a veil between us and the unconditioned; they are mind-at-large showing up under the conditions of finite perspective.

## Where this leaves us

The medieval frame and the contemporary frame agree that the chain of conditioned states must terminate in something unconditioned, that the unconditioned is not a thing-among-things, and that finite perspectives know it only by its effects. They diverge on what *kind* of thing the unconditioned is — substantialist *ipsum esse subsistens* versus ontologically primitive mind-at-large — and on how the divergence matters for the rest of the *Summa*. They reframe rather than answer the question Aquinas asked: under mind-monism, *Does God exist?* becomes *Does the structure of experience imply an unconditioned ground?* — and the Five Ways become five reasons to answer yes. The next pair — Q.3 (simplicity) and Q.4 (perfection) — pushes the keystone further: if the unconditioned is mind-at-large, *what does its simplicity mean*? That is the article that will test whether the mind-monist frame can sustain the load Aquinas's classical metaphysics carried.
