# Day 83 — Daring (Contemporary)

## Frame

Q.44 details the effects of fear: bodily contraction, skewed judgment, trembling, action either aided (moderate fear) or hindered (excessive fear). Q.45 introduces *daring* — the irascible passion contrary to fear, oriented to the same future evil but under the aspect of *resistible*. Q.45 a.4 then introduces the distinction Aquinas uses across the rest of the *Summa*: **antecedent vs. consequent passions** — those that follow sense-apprehension simply vs. those that follow intellect-and-will.

Under conscious-realist monism the day's content reads as the affective interface of *action selection* under a perspective-limited agent. Yesterday (Q.42-43) gave us the structure of fear's object and causes; today gives us fear's *somatic-cognitive* effects and the contrary affective register that engages the same future-evil with daring rather than withdrawal. The Q.45 a.4 distinction is the keystone: it is exactly the contemporary distinction between fast/predictive affect and slow/deliberative affect, between embodied appraisal and reflective endorsement — a distinction that organizes the whole modern science of emotion.

## Problem

The shared problem: **what does fear *do* to the agent, and what is the contrary policy when the future evil is judged resistible?** Aquinas's a.4 of Q.44 ("moderate fear aids action, excessive fear hinders it") states the empirical problem the modern literature on the Yerkes-Dodson curve and stress-performance interactions takes up. Q.45 a.4's antecedent/consequent distinction is the deeper problem: how does the deliberative system interact with the predictive-processing system that produces fast affect? This is *the* central problem of contemporary cognitive science — the relation of System 1 to System 2, of habit to deliberation, of model-free to model-based reinforcement learning.

The contemporary frame supplies a unified answer. Fear and daring are the affective signatures of contrary policy-precisions on the same future-evil prediction. The antecedent passion is the fast-affective output of the predictive-processing system; the consequent passion is the slow-affective output of the deliberative system once the intellect and will have engaged. Moderate fear is the affective state in which the fast system's signal is preserved as evidence but not allowed to override the deliberative policy — exactly what is meant by *calibrated precision*. Excessive fear is the over-precision case in which the fast signal does override.

## Resource

**Friston's active inference and the precision-weighting account** is the load-bearing resource for the contrary-precision structure of Q.45 a.1 (daring opposite to fear). Same future-evil, contrary aspects of resistibility = same prediction, contrary precision on the agency parameter. The active-inference literature on agency-and-precision (Friston, Daunizeau, Kiebel, Klaas 2010) is the formal home. Q.45 a.2 (daring ensues from hope) is the policy-feasibility cascade: high precision on the policy-toward-good (hope) raises the precision on the policy-against-obstacle (daring); affective registration follows the precision update. This is precisely what active-inference policy selection does.

**Damasio's somatic-marker hypothesis and embodied-cognition literature** is the empirical foothold for Q.44 a.1 (fear causes contraction) and a.3 (fear causes trembling). The body is part of the affective computation, not its passive vehicle. Damasio (1994, 2010) and the broader interoception literature (Critchley, Garfinkel, Seth, Tsakiris) demonstrate that bodily states are *informational inputs* to affective and decisional processing. Aquinas's claim — bodily change resembles the essence of the passion — is the medieval anticipation of this structure. The contraction-of-vital-spirits maps onto sympathetic activation, peripheral vasoconstriction, and the somatic markers Damasio names.

**The dual-process literature (Kahneman, Stanovich, Evans)** is the contemporary articulation of Q.45 a.4's antecedent/consequent distinction. The antecedent passion is *fast, automatic, embodied, evidence-driven* (System 1, model-free RL, habitual control); the consequent passion is *slow, deliberate, intellect-and-will-engaged, model-based* (System 2, model-based RL, deliberative control). The relation between the two is the central computational problem the modern cognitive sciences are working on. Aquinas's distinction anticipates the structure with surprising precision, and his moderate-fear claim (Q.44 a.4) is the empirical observation that the deliberative system's effectiveness *requires* but is not identical with the fast affective signal.

**Fredrickson and the broaden-and-build / positive-affect literature** is the empirical foothold for Q.45 a.2 (hope causes daring). The action-broadening effect of positive affect (PRS-01) is precisely the mechanism by which hope spills into daring: broader action repertoire, expanded perceived feasibility, willingness to engage obstacles. Fredrickson PRS-08 / PRS-09 (high-quality connection, weak-tie social trust) gives the social-resource correlate: the daring of the agent embedded in community is empirically higher than that of the isolated agent. Aristotle's "wealth, strength, and a multitude of friends drive fear away" (Q.43 a.2) cashes out here as the broaden-and-build mechanism extended into the daring register.

**McGilchrist's hemispheric account** sharpens the antecedent/consequent distinction. Left hemisphere: narrow instrumental focus, the executive of the planned engagement. Right hemisphere: contextual openness, the executive of the gestalt assessment. *Mature* daring (consequent passion of the brave) is left-right integration — the gestalt assessment confirms the obstacle is engageable, and the planned engagement is selected. *Pathological* daring (Q.45 a.3's drunken case) is left-hemispheric over-precision without right-hemispheric contextual buffering — the obstacle's actual magnitude is not perceived. McGilchrist's clinical correlations (intoxication, mania, certain personality disorders) match the structural claim.

**Friston / Pezzulo on epistemic vs. pragmatic value** sharpens Q.44 a.2 (fear motivates seeking counsel). The fearful agent has a high-precision aversive prediction with low-precision policy efficacy. Under expected-free-energy minimization, the agent's optimal move is information-seeking — exactly what "seeking counsel" is. Aquinas's article is the affective shadow of the epistemic-value calculation: when the policy space is too uncertain to select within, the rational move is to acquire more information, often from another agent. The community-embedded computation of the central theme appears here mechanistically.

**Stump on antecedent vs. consequent passions and the Thomistic will** belongs to this synthesis. Q.45 a.4's distinction is Stump's home territory: how the intellect-engaged will interacts with the sensitive appetite is *exactly* the question her analytic-Thomist work clarifies. Stump's reading preserves the structural Aquinas — antecedent passions are not yet morally voluntary, consequent ones are — and supplies the contemporary articulation in terms of higher-order endorsement. The synthesis cites Stump as the most rigorous contemporary articulator of the distinction; the conscious-realist-monist reframe places her account inside the active-inference structure (antecedent = fast model output; consequent = endorsed slow policy).

**Wright on Philippians 2:12** is the centerpiece. Aquinas himself quotes Phil 2:12 ("work out your own salvation with fear and trembling") at Q.44 a.4. Wright's reading of Philippians: salvation as *participation in Christ*'s shape of life, worked out by the community-in-Christ under genuine eschatological tension. Moderate fear is the affective signature of disciplined eschatological participation — neither presumption (which would be the no-fear case Aquinas implicitly excludes) nor despair (which would be the excessive-fear case that hinders action). The two-Wright moves: (i) salvation is not yet completed and requires worked-out engagement; (ii) it is community-embedded, not individual. Both match Aquinas's claim at Q.44 a.4 with theological depth Aquinas presupposes.

**Rohr on consequent daring and the contemplative shift** carries the synthesis into the deeper register. Rohr's order-disorder-reorder pattern (PRS-02) is exactly the structure of Q.45 a.4's brave-man-who-becomes-eager-on-reflection: the initial passion (whether fear or daring) is *ordered* in the agent's first-half default; encountering the obstacle is the *disorder*; the consequent passion that emerges on reflection is the *reorder* that contains the prior order within a larger frame. Rohr's contemplative reframe of moral action is the contemplative endorsement of the consequent-passion register.

## Solution (proposed, speculative)

**(a) Shared with Aquinas.** Daring is contrary to fear, same object, contrary aspect. Daring follows hope. Fear causes somatic contraction, skewed judgment, and trembling. Moderate fear aids action, excessive hinders it. Defect (in two specific modes) can cause daring. The brave are sometimes more eager at first, sometimes after reflection — the antecedent/consequent distinction sorts the cases.

**(b) Where it reframes.** Aquinas's "essence of the passion" structure that resembles its bodily counterpart is reframed under embodied-cognition: the body is not a passive resemblance-vehicle but an informational input to the affective computation. The antecedent/consequent distinction is reframed as the predictive/deliberative dual structure of modern cognitive science. The "moderate fear" of Q.44 a.4 is articulated as *calibrated precision* on the aversive prediction — the precision that preserves the fast signal as evidence without overriding the deliberative policy. The Phil 2:12 keystone is articulated, with Wright's help, as the shape of community-embedded salvation worked out under genuine perspective-limitation.

**(c) Where evidence is firm vs. speculative.** *Firm*: dual-process literature (Kahneman, Stanovich); somatic-marker / embodied cognition (Damasio, Seth); broaden-and-build (Fredrickson); active-inference dual-precision structure (Friston, Pezzulo); information-seeking under uncertainty as expected-free-energy minimizer. *Empirically supported*: hemispheric account (McGilchrist). *Speculative metaphysical*: the conscious-realist-monist reading of antecedent/consequent as alter-level fast vs. dissociative-boundary-engaged slow processing. *Theological, well-attested*: Wright on Phil 2:12; Rohr's order-disorder-reorder.

## The central-theme thread

Q.44 a.4's citation of Philippians 2:12 is the day's high point. *Work out your own salvation with fear and trembling.* The central theme's keystone scripture is here in Aquinas's own text, with Aquinas himself defending the role of moderate fear in the worked-out salvation. The synthesis can lean explicitly on the alignment: under perspective-limitation, the agent's fear is the affective signal that the goal is arduous; moderate fear preserves this signal as evidence; the consequent daring (Q.45) is the deliberative engagement of the obstacle in the strength of hope.

The antecedent/consequent distinction at Q.45 a.4 is structurally identical to the central-theme distinction between (i) what the limited agent feels from the inside under its current model and (ii) what it endorses under the larger view available to it through community, contemplation, and tradition. The first is the alter's surface affective state; the second is the alter's engaged-and-endorsed action under the wider available perspective. The synthesis reads the consequent-passion register as the affective shadow of the central theme's "guidance from those who perceive more of the whole than we do" — the deliberative endorsement is the agent's engagement of perspectives larger than its first-fast model.

The community-embedded shape of Wright's Phil 2:12 reading and the seeking-counsel motivation of Q.44 a.2 align tightly. The agent under fear is, on Aquinas's reading and on the active-inference reading, *driven toward community* — toward the resources of intellect, advice, and embedded support that expand the perspective. The central theme's "those who perceive more of the whole than we do" is operationally present in Aquinas's article: when fear engages, the wise move is to seek counsel.

## Bridges

- **Friston PRS-01, PRS-04 + Pezzulo on epistemic value** — Q.45 a.1 contrary-precision; Q.44 a.2 information-seeking under fear; *formal, empirically supported*.
- **Damasio somatic-marker / interoception literature** — Q.44 a.1 (contraction) and a.3 (trembling) as informational inputs to affective computation; *empirical, well-established*.
- **Kahneman / Stanovich dual-process literature** — Q.45 a.4 antecedent vs. consequent passions; *behavioral evidence, robust*.
- **Fredrickson PRS-01, PRS-08, PRS-09 / broaden-and-build, high-quality connection** — Q.45 a.2 (hope causes daring), Q.43-44 community-embedded fear-reduction; *empirical*.
- **McGilchrist hemispheric account** — left-right integration in mature daring; *clinical evidence*.
- **Stump on antecedent vs. consequent passions and Thomistic will** — rigorous contemporary articulation of Q.45 a.4; *analytic-Thomistic*.
- **Wright on Philippians 2:12** — community-embedded eschatological salvation as the theological articulation of "moderate fear" in Q.44 a.4; *historical-theological, well-attested*.
- **Rohr PRS-02 / order-disorder-reorder** — contemplative reading of the consequent-passion register; *contemplative tradition*.

## Where this leaves us

Q.44-45 close the fear/daring pair and introduce the antecedent/consequent distinction that will be load-bearing through the rest of the passions and the habits. Tomorrow's Day 84 (Q.46-47) takes anger — the irascible passion oriented to *present* injury rather than future evil — and its causes. Day 85 (Q.48-49) takes the effects of anger and opens habits in general. The five-day arc now turns from the *aversive-prospective* passions (fear and despair) and the *engaged-prospective* passions (hope and daring) to the *retrospective-aversive* passion (anger), and then to the structural turn from transient affective states to stabilized dispositions (habits). The central theme — calibrated precision in fear-and-trembling toward loving unity — continues to organize the affective economy across the arc.
