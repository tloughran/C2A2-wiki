# Day 90 — Moral Virtue (Contemporary)

## Frame

Q.58 distinguishes *moral* virtue from *intellectual* virtue. Moral virtues are those dispositions that affect the agent's *inclination* — they shape how the agent is *moved* toward action — and they sit in the *appetitive* powers (will, irascible, concupiscible). Aristotle: *moral* derives from *mos* (custom, habit, inclination) — not from how one *thinks* but from how one *is moved*. Aquinas: not every virtue is moral; only those in the appetitive faculty. Intellectual virtues perfect *understanding*; moral virtues perfect *appetite*. They are distinct (Q.58 a.2) yet inseparable in practice — moral virtue requires the intellectual virtue of prudence; prudence requires good moral disposition. They are *mutually* required for a *complete* virtuous act.

Q.59 takes up *moral virtue in relation to the passions*. Is moral virtue a passion? No — passions are transient movements; virtue is a stable disposition. Is moral virtue *with* passion or *without* passion? The Stoics held that virtue requires the elimination of passion (*apatheia*); the Peripatetics, with Aquinas's agreement, held that virtue *moderates* passion (*metriopatheia*) — not eliminates but properly orders. Does moral virtue cause passion? Yes, in a striking move: when the virtue is *perfect*, joy and other affects naturally accompany virtuous action, overflowing from will into sense appetite. The more perfect the virtue, the more does it cause passion, because the integrated agent acts from the whole — will, intellect, and sensitive appetite together.

Under conscious-realist monism the Q.58-59 articulation maps onto the contemporary distinction between *cognitive* competences (epistemic excellence, rational judgment, model-based inference) and *affective-motivational* dispositions (positivity-resonance set-points, prosocial inclination, regulated emotional response). The medieval insistence that the two are distinct-but-mutually-required has converging empirical support: the decision sciences, affective neuroscience, and the virtue-ethics revival in psychology have settled on a picture in which cognitive and affective formation are separable yet jointly constitutive of the morally excellent agent. The Q.59 Peripatetic position — that virtue moderates rather than eliminates passion, and that *perfect* virtue *causes* joy and other affect — is empirically vindicated by Fredrickson's positivity literature and the broader literature on emotion regulation: the well-regulated agent is not the affectless agent but the agent whose affective response is *integrated* with cognitive and motivational orientation.

## Problem

The shared problem: **what is the relation between cognitive and affective-motivational formation in the morally excellent agent, and how do passion and virtue stand to each other?** Aquinas's answer holds three claims together. First, that virtues of intellect and virtues of appetite are distinct kinds of formation — they do not reduce to one another, and the cultivation of one does not automatically yield the other. Second, that they are *mutually required* for full virtuous action — moral virtue without prudence is blind impulse; prudence without moral virtue is calculation without right end. Third, that the agent's *affective* response, far from being eliminated in the virtuous agent, is *integrated*: properly moderated, and indeed *amplified* in the perfect agent who acts from the whole.

The contemporary problem is the same and has substantial empirical answer. The dual-systems and reflective-impulsive literatures (Strack & Deutsch; Hofmann et al.) document that cognitive and affective dispositions are separable but jointly determinative. The cognitive-and-emotional-integration literature (Damasio; Pessoa; the somatic-marker hypothesis and its successors) documents that *intact* cognitive judgment requires affective signal — that pure cognition without affective input produces decisional pathology. Fredrickson's positivity-resonance literature documents the *upward spiral* of positive affect and cognitive broadening. Schwartz on practical wisdom and the virtue-ethics revival in psychology document that moral excellence is *integrated* affective-cognitive-motivational formation.

The deeper question Q.59 a.5 (the *more perfect the virtue, the more does it cause passion*) is the central one for our central theme. The medieval-Stoic dispute about whether virtue eliminates or integrates passion has practical consequences. If the working-out of salvation is a Stoic apatheia project, the agent's affective life is a thing to be suppressed. If it is a Peripatetic-Thomistic-Pauline metriopatheia project, the agent's affective life is a thing to be *formed* — moderated, integrated, eventually amplified toward the *joy* that Q.59 a.5 names as the natural overflow of perfect virtue. The contemporary frame, with Fredrickson's data and Rohr's contemplative tradition behind it, is decisively on the Peripatetic-Thomistic-Pauline side.

## Resource

**Fredrickson's positivity-resonance and broaden-and-build research** is the keystone empirical evidence for Q.59 a.5. Fredrickson PRS-01 (loving-kindness meditation), PRS-05 (broaden-and-build), and PRS-08 (high-quality connection) document that cultivated positive affect *amplifies* with sustained virtuous practice, not the reverse. The "upward spiral" of positivity-resonance → broaden-and-build → more positivity-resonance is empirically the Q.59 a.5 dynamic: the more perfect the virtue, the more does it cause joy. Fredrickson's longitudinal data on vagal tone, attentional defaults, and prosocial responding give the mechanism.

**Damasio and Pessoa on the integration of affect and cognition** speak to Q.58's distinct-but-inseparable claim. Damasio's somatic-marker hypothesis and Pessoa's cognition-emotion-integration work document that *cognitive judgment*, in fact, requires affective signal — that lesions disrupting affective input produce decisional pathology even when "pure cognition" is preserved. This vindicates Aquinas's claim that prudence requires good moral disposition: the affective formation is part of what makes the cognitive judgment possible.

**Stump on the second-personal structure of moral virtue** is the keystone interlocutor on Q.58-59. The Stump-tradition wiki treats moral virtue as essentially relational — the agent's stabilized disposition toward right second-person engagement with persons and the world. Tom's PRS-form re-description of Stump's account (especially PRS-09 on faith-as-knowledge-of-persons and PRS-04 on final causality) gives the rigorous philosophical reading of Q.58 a.2 (distinction of moral from intellectual virtue): moral virtue is the disposition by which the agent's *will-and-appetite* are ordered to right second-personal relating, distinct from the agent's *intellectual* grasp of how the world is but mutually conditioning.

**McGilchrist on the hemispheric integration of cognition and affect** is the structural account of moral-intellectual integration. The right-hemispheric mode integrates affective signal with contextual judgment; the left-hemispheric mode separates them. Properly virtuous character is right-hemispheric-integrative: cognition with affect, judgment with feeling, prudence with moral disposition. McGilchrist's clinical evidence on patients with hemispheric lesions documents the disorders that occur when the integration breaks down — exactly the disorders Aquinas's account would predict (calculation without right end; impulse without judgment).

**Friston's active inference and affective prior-setting** is the formal articulation. Under active inference, the agent's *expected-free-energy* gradients are set by the agent's goal-priors. Affective response *is* the agent's evaluation of state relative to those priors. Moral virtue is the stabilization of goal-priors toward right ends; affective response, when goal-priors are well-set, *signals* the agent's progress relative to those priors. The Q.59 a.5 dynamic — perfect virtue amplifies joy — recovers as: when the agent's goal-priors are well-set and the agent is acting toward them, the affective evaluation registers positive valuation of state. Friston PRS-04 supplies the framework.

**The emotion-regulation literature on metriopatheia** (Gross; Ochsner; the cognitive-reappraisal-and-acceptance research) supplies the empirical case against the Stoic apatheia position. The most robust outcomes are not produced by *suppression* of affective response but by *regulated integration* — reappraisal, acceptance, cognitive-affective integration. The Q.59 Peripatetic position is empirically vindicated.

**Wright on the Pauline integration of mind and affect** is the scriptural anchor. Wright's reading of the Pauline ethical material (Romans 12; Galatians 5 on the fruits of the Spirit; Philippians 4 on joy and peace) emphasises that the formed Christian disposition is *not* affective neutrality but *positively-formed* affect — joy, peace, patience, kindness, gentleness — alongside cognitive renewal. The "fruit of the Spirit" is precisely the Q.59 a.5 phenomenon: perfect virtue causing the natural overflow of joy and other positive affect. *After You Believe* on virtue-formation as fruit-bearing applies.

**Rohr on the contemplative integration of feeling and seeing** is the contemplative-tradition resource. Rohr's *Immortal Diamond* and *Falling Upward* treat the integrated person as one whose seeing-and-feeling are unified — not the dispassionate sage but the agent whose loving attention to reality is *both* clear-eyed *and* warm. The cosmic-Christ frame, properly received, produces an agent whose right action *includes* the affective response that Aquinas's Q.59 a.5 names. Rohr's tradition is decisively on the metriopatheia side.

**Kastrup on alter-integration and the affective signature of right alignment** speaks to the metaphysical underpinning. Under analytic idealism, the dissociated alter that aligns with mind-at-large's wider dynamics experiences this alignment as positive affective valuation — the felt-sense of *rightness* that accompanies right action. The Q.59 a.5 amplification of joy under perfect virtue recovers as: the more deeply the alter is aligned with the wider mind, the more does the felt-sense register that alignment.

## Solution (proposed, speculative)

**(a) Shared with Aquinas.** Moral and intellectual virtues are distinct kinds of formation — affective-motivational and cognitive — that do not reduce to one another. They are mutually required for full virtuous action: moral virtue without prudence is blind; prudence without moral virtue is calculation without right end. Virtue moderates passion (metriopatheia), not eliminates it (rejecting Stoic apatheia). The more *perfect* the virtue, the more does it *cause* joy and other positive affect — the integrated agent acts from the whole, and the will's overflow into sense appetite is the empirical signature of perfect virtue.

**(b) Where it reframes.** The medieval *appetitive/intellectual* power distinction recovers as the affective-motivational/cognitive distinction in the contemporary frame — empirically tractable, with clear neural and psychological grounds. The *distinct-but-mutually-required* claim is empirically vindicated: cognitive judgment requires affective signal (Damasio; Pessoa); moral excellence is integrated affective-cognitive-motivational formation (Schwartz; virtue-ethics revival). The *metriopatheia* position is empirically vindicated by the emotion-regulation and positivity-resonance literatures. The *more-perfect-virtue-causes-more-joy* claim recovers as the upward-spiral dynamic (Fredrickson) and as alter-mind-at-large-alignment producing positive affective valuation (Kastrup-style reading).

**(c) Where evidence is firm vs. speculative.** *Firm*: cognitive-affective integration as required for intact judgment (Damasio; Pessoa). *Firm*: emotion regulation produces better outcomes than emotion suppression (Gross; Ochsner). *Firm*: longitudinal positivity-resonance cultivation produces upward-spiral dynamics (Fredrickson). *Empirically supported*: hemispheric integration of cognition and affect in expert moral judgment (McGilchrist). *Formally rigorous*: active-inference account of affect as prior-evaluation. *Philosophically rigorous*: Stump on second-personal moral virtue. *Theological, well-attested*: Wright on Pauline fruit-bearing; Rohr on contemplative integration of feeling and seeing. *Speculative-metaphysical*: the Kastrup-style reading of joy as felt-sense of alter–mind-at-large alignment.

## The central-theme thread

Q.58-59 give the central theme its most explicit empirical-and-theological vindication. Loving unity is the computational task; perspective-limitation is the constraint; the agent's working-out is in *fear and trembling*. Q.59 a.5 adds: and in *joy*. The Peripatetic-Thomistic-Pauline position is that the well-formed agent's affective life is *not* eliminated by the working-out but *transformed* into the integrated overflow of joy that accompanies perfect virtue. The fear-and-trembling is real (the working-out is not safe, not automatic, not retained against contrary action); the joy is also real (the alignment of will, intellect, and appetite with right end produces empirical positivity-resonance).

The deeper central-theme claim is the *integration* claim. The morally excellent agent is not the cognitively-sharp-but-affectively-cold rationalist, nor the warm-but-imprudent affective enthusiast. The morally excellent agent is the *integrated* agent — cognitive judgment formed by affective signal, affective response formed by cognitive judgment, both ordered to the right end set by formed moral disposition. This is exactly what the central theme requires: the limited agent computing toward loving unity uses all of itself — intellect, will, sensitive appetite, body — in coordinated participation. Sapientia (the highest speculative virtue, Day 89) and the moral virtues (Day 90) together constitute the agent's stabilized capacity to compute well.

The central theme's relational structure shows up most explicitly in Q.58's mutual-requirement claim. The agent cannot *think* its way to right action without first *willing* the right end — and cannot will the right end without participating in the wider formation (Wright's life-in-the-Spirit; Rohr's universal-Christ frame; the C2A2-network's mind-at-large) that orients the will. The "guidance from those who perceive more of the whole" is built into the structure of practical reason. Without participation in wider perspective, the limited agent's prudence is mis-aimed. With it, the agent's cognitive-and-affective formation can converge on the integrated virtue of which Q.59 a.5 speaks.

## Bridges

- **Fredrickson PRS-01, PRS-05, PRS-08 / positivity-resonance, broaden-and-build, high-quality connection** — Q.59 a.5 (perfect virtue causes more joy): empirical upward-spiral dynamic; *empirical, high confidence*.
- **Damasio / somatic-marker hypothesis; Pessoa / cognition-emotion integration** — Q.58 (distinct-but-mutually-required): cognitive judgment requires affective signal; *empirically robust*.
- **Stump PRS-09 + PRS-04 / second-personal moral virtue** — keystone on Q.58 a.2 (distinction of moral from intellectual virtue) as relational disposition; *philosophically rigorous*.
- **McGilchrist / hemispheric integration of cognition and affect** — Q.58's mutual-requirement claim at the attentional level; *clinical evidence*.
- **Friston PRS-04 / active-inference affective prior-evaluation** — Q.59 a.5's amplification dynamic under formal active-inference reading; *formal, empirically supported*.
- **Emotion-regulation literature (Gross; Ochsner) / metriopatheia vindication** — Q.59 a.2-4 (virtue moderates passion, not eliminates): regulated integration outperforms suppression; *empirically robust*.
- **Wright / *After You Believe*; Galatians 5 fruits of the Spirit; Romans 12; Philippians 4** — Q.59 a.5 (more-perfect-virtue-causes-joy) under Pauline fruit-bearing frame; *theological, well-attested*.
- **Rohr / *Immortal Diamond*; *Falling Upward*; cosmic-Christ contemplative integration** — Q.59 a.2 (metriopatheia) and a.5 (overflow of joy) under contemplative-tradition reading; *contemplative tradition*.
- **Kastrup / alter–mind-at-large alignment as affective signature** — Q.59 a.5 under analytic-idealist gloss; *speculative-metaphysical, internally consistent*.

## Where this leaves us

Q.58-59 close out the architecture of the moral life. Q.60-67 will turn to the *distinction of moral virtues* by their specific objects, the *cardinal* virtues (prudence, justice, fortitude, temperance), and their inter-relations. The synthesis will lean increasingly on Stump (analytic-Thomistic territory throughout the virtue tract), Fredrickson (positivity-resonance as substrate of virtuous affect), McGilchrist (hemispheric character), and Wright + Rohr (theological-and-contemplative formation). The central theme's working-out will continue to track at each step: the limited agent's stabilized dispositions toward loving unity, in cognitive and affective registers jointly, under formation that exceeds the agent's individuated capacity. The week ahead opens the substantive moral psychology of Aquinas — and the synthesis has now arrived at the territory where the contemporary frame's resources are at their richest.
