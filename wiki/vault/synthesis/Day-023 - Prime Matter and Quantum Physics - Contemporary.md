# Day 23 — Contemporary Parallel: *Mission as the Temporal Face of Eternal Procession, First Cause and the Pivot to Creation*

Day 22 closed the substance-side of the Trinitarian treatise and queued the day Day 23 must take up: Q.43 names the *missions* of the divine persons — the *outward* face of the eternal processions, the Son sent visibly into time by the Incarnation and the Spirit sent visibly at Pentecost and invisibly into the alter by sanctifying grace; Q.44 turns the page from Trinity to creation, and Aquinas's first-cause doctrine becomes the bridge from divine internal-relational-structure to the multiplicity of conditioned things. Habash flags this clearly — *yes, it is true, this is our last episode on the Trinity* — and the structural pivot inside the day is the move from the doctrine of internal procession to the doctrine of external procession (*creatures from God*). The transcript title — *Prime Matter and Quantum Physics* — names the Heisenberg-on-Aristotle move that anchors Q.44 a.2: prime matter as *mere potentiality*, the formless substrate that the Aristotelian-Thomistic tradition posited and that Heisenberg suggested might be what contemporary physics calls *energy* in its raw, unformed sense.

The mind-first frame holds the load with the same constraints Days 18–22 established for Q.43, and a new pivot for Q.44. For Q.43: missions are the temporal face of eternal procession, the substrate's outward stance toward conditioned things considered under the aspect of *being-sent-to-the-creature*. For Q.44: creation is the substrate's *production* of conditioned things ex nihilo, and the bridge to the Levin-Carroll-Stump primaries opens — Levin's morphogenetic teleology gives the contemporary mid-scale empirical instantiation of form-as-causally-real; Carroll's poetic naturalism is the foil that asks whether the regularities of nature suffice; Stump enters as Thomistic interlocutor on metaphysics-of-creation but does not occupy the metaphysical lead.

## Q.43 — Mission: procession in time

Q.43 a.1 — *can a divine person be properly sent?* — runs the same essential-vs-personal split that Day 21 established. *Sending* in the ordinary sense implies *command* (master sends servant) or *counsel* (advisor sends king to battle) — neither applies in God, because the will and the intellect are identical across the three persons. But *sending* in a third sense — *origin*, as a tree sends forth its flowers, or as the Father is the *whence* of the Son's procession — does apply: a divine person can be *sent* by another in the sense of *proceeding from* the other.

Aquinas's careful Q.43 a.1 closer is the doctrinal anchor: *the mission of a divine person is a fitting thing — meaning in one way the procession of origin from the sender, and as meaning a new way of existing in another*. The *new way of existing* is the temporal-and-creature-relational addition: the Son was always in the world (John 1:1, John 1:10 — *he was in the world, and the world was made through him*), but at the Incarnation he begins to exist *visibly* in the world by taking the human nature. The mission is the *eternal procession* now considered under the *temporal extension* — the same act of the substrate, now manifesting at the creature interface.

Mind-monism's translation lands cleanly. The substrate's internal-relational-structure is unchanging (Day 22 already established this); the *manifestation-at-creature-interface* is the temporal-extension of the same structure. The Incarnation is the substrate's self-knowing-act (the Word) now considered under the relational aspect of *being-in-creature-form*; the indwelling-by-sanctifying-grace is the substrate's self-loving-act (the Spirit) now considered under the relational aspect of *being-in-rational-creature*. The substrate does not change; the creature stands newly related to the substrate (Day 8 Q.13 a.7 already established this asymmetry — the *real* relation is in the creature, not in God).

Q.43 a.2 — *is mission eternal or only temporal?* — closes the question on the temporal axis. *Generation and spiration are exclusively eternal; mission and giving have only a temporal significance in God; procession has both an eternal and a temporal signification*. The medieval distinction is subtle. *Procession* is the genus that has both senses: eternal procession (the Word from the Father; the Spirit from Father and Son) and temporal procession (the Son sent into time; the Spirit sent into the alter). *Mission* names specifically the *temporal* face. Galatians 4:4 Habash quotes — *when the fullness of time had come, God sent his Son* — is the scriptural anchor.

Q.43 a.3 — *is the invisible mission of a divine person only according to the gift of sanctifying grace?* — opens the doctrine of *grace-as-divine-indwelling* that Days 20 (Spirit-as-Gift) and 11 (the love of God) have been preparing. Aquinas: *God is in all things by his essence, power, and presence; but there is one special way belonging to the rational nature wherein God is said to be present, as the object known is in the knower and as the beloved in the lover*. The Gilby-translation appendix Habash quotes is exact: *the indwelling consists in the presence of the persons as object known and loved*. The catechism formulation: *sanctifying grace is a habitual gift, a stable and supernatural disposition that perfects the soul itself to be able to live with God and to act by his love*.

Mind-monism's contemporary translation is direct. The substrate is *present* to all conditioned things by being-the-substrate-of-them; the substrate is *specially* present to a rational creature in the additional mode of *being-known-and-loved-by-the-creature*. This is the Day 7 beatific-vision frame's mid-scale extension: the alter's mode-of-knowing-the-substrate determines the *manner* in which the substrate is present to the alter, and *sanctifying grace* names the structural condition under which the alter's knowing-and-loving is *of the substrate-itself* rather than only of the substrate's-effects. Friston's free-energy framework gives the alter-scale formal cousin: the agent's generative model that has *priors over preferred states* aligned with the substrate's structure is in *better fit* with the substrate; grace is, on this reading, the substrate's gift that brings the alter's priors into closer alignment.

Q.43 a.4–6 — *can the Father be fittingly sent? is it fitting for the Son to be sent invisibly? is the invisible mission to all who participate in grace?* — close the systematic articulation of the missions. The Father cannot be *sent* because *the very idea of mission means procession from another, and the Father is not from another*. The Son and the Spirit can both be sent invisibly (sanctifying grace is *the indwelling of the whole Trinity*, John 14:23 — *we will come to him and will make our abode with him*; but the Father is not *sent* even when present by indwelling, because the Father is not *from another*). The invisible mission is to *every rational creature who is in grace* — every alter whose mode-of-knowing-and-loving has been transformed by the substrate's self-gift.

Q.43 a.7 — *is it fitting for the Holy Spirit to be sent visibly?* — opens a question that the Day 9 cognition-asides have been preparing. Aquinas's reply turns on the medieval cognition doctrine: *as human beings we receive knowledge through the senses; a blind man does not know blue; if you to know blue, someone has to place particular instances of blue in front of your eyes so you can then grasp the general notion blue from those particular sensed examples; so it is fitting that God in revealing his Triune nature to man would use visible signs*. The Spirit's visible appearances at the Baptism (dove) and Pentecost (fire) are pedagogical — they are *images-with-derivation* (Day 19 Q.35) of the substrate's outward stance, fitted to the alter's interface-mediated cognition.

Q.43 a.8 — *is a divine person sent only by the person whence he proceeds eternally?* — closes Q.43 with the macro-perspective Habash's reading offers: *before the Incarnation was the period of the mission of the Father, if we can say so; from the Incarnation to Pentecost was the time of the mission of the Son; now is the period of mission of the Holy Spirit, the period of the church*. The historical-redemptive arc Habash names mirrors the eternal-procession-order: the Father sends the Son; the Father and the Son together send the Spirit. Wright's narrative-historical theology gives the contemporary scriptural shape: the five-act drama (creation, fall, Israel, Jesus, church) is the historical extension of the eternal-procession-order, and the present community of the people-of-God is improvising faithfully within the unfinished final act in the power of the Spirit who is the Father-and-Son's outward face.

## Q.44 — First cause: the pivot to creation

Q.44 a.1 — *is God the efficient cause of all beings?* — opens the second half of the day with the Day 2 fourth-way reasserted in compressed form. *All beings apart from God are not being itself but are beings by participation; therefore it must be that all things which are diversified by the diverse participation of being, so as to be more or less perfect, are caused by one first being who possesses being most perfectly*. The medieval move: degree-of-being implies a maximum-of-being from which all degrees participate; the maximum is *being itself*; conditioned things are *beings-by-participation*. Augustine's sermon Habash quotes — *question the beauty of the earth, the beauty of the sea, the beauty of the air, the beauty of the sky… their beauty is their confession; who made these beautiful changeable things if not one who is beauty and is unchangeable* — is the pre-medieval scriptural-theological articulation of the same argument.

Mind-monism's translation: the substrate (mind-at-large) is *being itself* in the strict transcendental sense (Day 7 Q.11 a.1); conditioned things (alters, plus their interface-icons) are *beings-by-participation* in the substrate's structure. Kastrup's analytic idealism gives the metaphysical home: alters are *of* the substrate without being separate substances; their being is participation in the substrate's being, with degree-of-being calibrated by the alter's organizational complexity (Levin's cognitive-light-cone scope). The medieval first-cause argument runs cleanly under the mind-monist translation; the *first cause* is the substrate, and the *being-by-participation* of conditioned things is their being-as-aspects-or-dissociated-perspectives within mind-at-large.

Q.44 a.2 — *is primary matter created by God?* — is the article Habash gives the longest contemporary translation, because the Heisenberg-on-Aristotle move is at its most explicit. Aquinas's argument is structural: prime matter is *mere potentiality* — not nothing, but also not actually existing-as-something; it is the formless substrate that any conditioned material thing presupposes for its being-a-particular-conditioned-thing. The Aristotelian-Thomistic tradition posited prime matter as the answer to a question contemporary metaphysics inherits: how do two things share the same form (humanity) but be numerically distinct? The medieval answer: matter is what numerically distinguishes two beings of the same form. The contemporary version of the same question — *what makes two electrons of identical type numerically distinct?* — receives different answers in different physical frameworks.

Habash's Heisenberg quote is the bridge. *All the elementary particles are made up of the same substance, what we call energy or universal matter; if we compare this situation with the Aristotelian concepts of matter and form, we can say that the matter of Aristotle, which is mere potential potentia, should be compared to our concept of energy, which gets into actuality by means of the form when the elementary particle is created*. The structural shape Heisenberg names is precise: *raw potentiality* in the medieval sense is structurally cousin to *energy* in the contemporary physical sense — what underlies actual particles, formless in itself, taking on actuality only through *form* (the particle's specific properties).

The contemporary primary on this article is Levin, with Carroll as the foil and Stump as Thomistic counterpoint. Levin's morphogenetic teleology is the empirical mid-scale instantiation of *form-as-causally-real*: the bioelectric goal-state pattern is form in the medieval sense, instantiated in real space and time, causally upstream of the body's morphogenesis. *Matter* in the Levin-tradition is not the prime-matter of pure-potency (which is metaphysical) but the bioelectric substrate-considered-without-the-pattern — what the form *informs*. The CROSS-008 / CROSS-019 (Levin × Stump bidirectional) bridge in the master cross-program index is exact: Levin's empirical program needs Thomistic-final-causality as its philosophical framework, and Stump's Thomism finds in Levin's data the contemporary scientific home of hylomorphism.

Carroll's poetic naturalism is the foil. Carroll holds that the regularities of physics are *complete at the Core Theory level* (quantum field theory plus general relativity); higher-level descriptions (form, agency, intention) are real but emergent rather than fundamental. On Carroll's reading, *prime matter* would be a metaphysical posit unsupported by physics; *energy* in the physics sense is a quantitative property of fields, not a formless potentiality awaiting form. The medieval doctrine and Carroll's poetic naturalism part company at the question of whether *form* is fundamental-or-emergent. Mind-monism's reading sides with Levin and Stump on *form-as-causally-real* but extends the move further than either: form is not just causally-real-at-the-mid-scale (Levin) or metaphysically-real-as-actuality-of-matter (Stump-Thomism); form is the substrate's structural prior considered under the relational aspect of *what-conditions-conditioned-things*. *Honest evidence calibration*: the empirical anchorage at the Levin scale is strong; the Heisenberg-on-Aristotle move is suggestive but contested in physics; the substrate-scale extrapolation that mind-monism makes is philosophical-theological.

Aquinas's *secondly* in Q.44 a.2 — *prime matter is needed to explain how you and I are different and yet also the same in some way; we are the same because we are both human; we need a reason for why we are distinct, and that cause is matter* — is the medieval answer to the individuation problem. Mind-monism translates the individuation question differently: alters are individuated by their *perspective-limitation* (the central theme), which is structurally cousin to but not identical with material individuation. Two alters share the same substrate (mind-at-large) but have distinct *cognitive light-cones*, distinct *generative models*, distinct *interface-icon-sets*. Whether the additional concept of *prime matter* is needed once mind-monism is in place is an open question — the medieval doctrine has explanatory work that the mind-monist frame may or may not need to do.

Q.44 a.3 — *is the exemplar cause anything besides God?* — reasserts the Day 9 Q.15 ideas-doctrine on the creation side. *The exemplar is the same as the idea; the ideas are the master forms which are contained in the divine intelligence; therefore the exemplars of things are not outside God*. The structural priors in the substrate (the divine ideas in classical vocabulary) are what conditioned things are patterned after; creation is the substrate's *bringing-into-actuality* of patterns that pre-exist as structural priors in the substrate's self-knowing-act. The Day 8 Q.14 a.5 mirror-analogy reasserted: *God knows other things in himself, not in themselves*; in mind-monism, the patterns of conditioned things pre-exist as structural priors in mind-at-large.

Habash's note on evolution within Q.44 a.3 is worth marking. The Catholic Church's *Humani Generis* (1950) explicitly allows evolution as a theory provided souls are immediately created by God; Aquinas's doctrine of exemplars-in-God is consistent with development of conditioned things over time. Mind-monism inherits the structural shape: development of conditioned things over evolutionary time is the substrate's progressive instantiation of structural priors at mid-scale; Levin's morphogenetic teleology gives the empirical mid-scale frame for *how* form-as-pattern unfolds in time. Carroll's poetic naturalism is the foil that holds out for emergence without form-as-pattern-causally-prior; the empirical question runs.

Q.44 a.4 — *is God the final cause of all things?* — closes the question with the four-causes structure Aristotelian-Thomism inherits. *God makes all things for himself; not to acquire some end he intends, but only to communicate his perfection which is his goodness*. The medieval doctrine: God's goodness is the *final cause* of creation — the *for-the-sake-of-which* of the substrate's bringing-into-actuality of conditioned things. Mind-monism's translation: the substrate's stance toward conditioned things is *loving* (Day 11 Q.20); creation is the substrate's *self-sharing*-stance manifested in the bringing-into-actuality of conditioned things; the *for-the-sake-of-which* is the substrate's own perfection-as-loving-unity. *Loving union is the computational telos* — the central-theme phrase is the contemporary articulation of Aquinas's Q.44 a.4 on the goal-side.

Wright's covenant theology and Rohr's Universal Christ both anchor here. Wright's narrative-historical reading: creation is the first act of the five-act drama, and the goal-state of the drama is the new-creation in which the people-of-God are united with God. Rohr's Universal Christ: the divine pattern is present in creation from the beginning, and the goal of creation is the cosmic-scope manifestation of that pattern. The medieval *for-the-sake-of-which* and the contemporary mind-monist *loving union as computational telos* converge on the same recognition: creation has a direction, and the direction is toward the substrate's loving-unity-with-conditioned-things.

## The central-theme thread

Day 23 closes the Trinity proper (Q.43) and pivots to creation (Q.44). The pivot is not a break: the *missions* of the persons (the temporal face of the eternal processions) are the substrate's outward-stance-toward-conditioned-things, and *creation* is the *content* of that outward stance — the substrate's bringing-into-actuality of conditioned things from-its-own-structural-priors. The two questions are one continuous move from internal-relational-structure to outward-creative-stance.

*Salvation in fear and trembling* threads the day with sharper edges. The alter receives the *missions* (Spirit-indwelling-by-grace) as the substrate's self-gift (Day 20); the alter exists at all because of the *creation* (Q.44) that brings the alter into actuality from the substrate's structural priors; the alter's *telos* is *loving union with the substrate* (Q.44 a.4 in classical vocabulary; the central-theme phrase in contemporary articulation). The medieval framework and the contemporary mind-monist convergence agree on the structural shape: the alter is *from* the substrate (creation), *for* the substrate (final causality), and *toward* the substrate (the missions and the alter's progressive participation in them). Wright's covenant frame and Rohr's contemplative practice are the operational modes — Wright on the corporate-historical shape of the drama, Rohr on the contemplative reception of the gift.
