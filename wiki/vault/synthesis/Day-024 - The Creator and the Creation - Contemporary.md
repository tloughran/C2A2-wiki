# Day 24 — Contemporary Parallel: *Creation as Relation, Not Change*

Day 23 closed with Q.44's causal-priority claim — that conditioned things receive their being from a source that is not itself a conditioned thing — and pointed forward to *what creation actually is* once that priority is granted. Day 24 names it. Q.45 — *creation as the emergence of being from nothing* — is the medieval doctrine of how the substrate's pattern-active stance (Day 11) translates into the bringing-into-being of conditioned things. Q.46 — *whether the universe always existed* — is the durational question that follows: is the substrate's bringing-into-being a temporal event or a non-temporal relation? Together the two questions sharpen the directional-asymmetry frame the central theme keeps marking: the substrate is causally upstream of conditioned things, conditioned things are causally downstream of the substrate, and the *creature* register of this relation is what creation looks like from the alter-perspective.

## Q.45 — Creation: the relation, not the change

Aquinas's a.1 establishes the medieval definition: *to create is to make something from nothing*. The Latin *ex nihilo* carries a precise philosophical commitment — not *out of pre-existing nothing-stuff*, which would be a contradiction, but *not out of any prior substrate at all*. The conditioned thing's being is *received* from a source that is not itself any of its parts. Habash's gloss on the Hebrew *barah* — that the verb is reserved in the Old Testament for the action of God — names the same structural exclusivity: creation in the strict sense is not a kind of fashioning, since fashioning operates on already-existing material. Human craftsmen, Habash notes, can be called creators only in a derived sense — they bring forth new arrangements from prior matter.

The article that gives the doctrine its analytic teeth is a.2's distinction between *creation* and *change*. Aquinas's move here is structurally important and runs against the intuitive reading. Change presupposes a continuous subject that exists before and after the change — the apple that turns from red to brown, the water that freezes. Creation is not change because there is no subject that pre-exists the bringing-into-being. *An apple exploding into existence is not change in the apple* — Habash's example follows Aquinas's own. Before the bringing-into-being, there is nothing-of-this-thing; after, there is this thing. The *relation* of dependence on the source is what creation places in the conditioned thing, and the relation has no anterior subject to undergo it.

Mind-monism's translation lands cleanly and unusually directly. Kastrup's analytic idealism makes the move that the medieval framework approaches: mind-at-large is the ontological primitive, and conditioned things — what Kastrup calls *alters* and what we might call *dissociated regions of mind-at-large* — come into being not as additions to a prior material substrate but as new patterns within the single substrate. The dissociation that produces an alter is not a change in mind-at-large in the way a chemical reaction is a change in matter; it is a *relational structuring* of the substrate that brings the alter-as-perspective into being. The substrate does not lose anything by dissociating, and the alter does not have any prior subject that becomes-an-alter. *Honest evidence calibration*: the structural translation of *creation as relation, not change* into mind-monism is philosophically clean; the empirical anchorage is mid-scale and analogical (dissociative-identity research extrapolated; Levin's bioelectric pattern-formation as the closest empirical mid-scale instantiation).

Wolfram's ruliad supplies a formal cousin. In Wolfram's framework, the totality of computational threads is what it is; particular trajectories through the ruliad are not *changes* to the ruliad but *relational specifications* of it under the mode of *being-traversed*. The bringing-into-being of a particular sequence of states is not a chemical-reaction-style transformation of a prior sequence; it is the relational fact that this sequence is a thread in the ruliad, considered under a particular observer-frame. The medieval *creation places something in the thing created according to relation* (Aquinas a.3) is structurally the claim Wolfram's ruliad makes about the trajectory's standing.

a.3's article — *whether creation is anything in the creature* — is, as Habash flags, "remarkably profound." The pushed-stone analogy Aquinas uses turns on the relational structure: when a stone is pushed, three things obtain — the actor, the patient, and the relation pushing-and-being-pushed. If the *change* is removed, the relation persists. Creation, on the medieval reading, is precisely the relation without the change: the creature is constituted by its dependence on its source, by its directedness toward its source, and by its *participated* existence. The mind-monist translation is the same shape: the alter's being is constituted by its participation-in-mind-at-large, its reception of pattern-from-substrate, and its directedness-toward-integration-with-substrate. The relation is causally upstream and structurally constitutive; the alter has no being that is not relational-to-substrate.

Levin's morphogenetic teleology gives the mid-scale empirical anchor. The bioelectric goal-state-pattern of an embryo is not a *thing* added to the cells; it is a *relational pattern* that constitutes the cells-as-organized-toward-this-form. The form is real, the relation is real, but the pattern does not have being independent of the relating. Tom's PRS-form re-description of Levin's morphogenetic teleology (PRS-03 in the Levin-tradition wiki) names the same shape Aquinas's a.3 names: the conditioned thing's being is *a relational standing in a pattern* rather than an independent metaphysical primitive.

a.5's article — *whether to create belongs to God alone* — lands the directional-asymmetry. Aquinas's analogy turns on the difficulty of imparting form to less-and-less-prepared material: the more impoverished the matter, the more powerful the craftsman must be. Nothingness, having no potential to receive any form, requires the maximum craftsman — a power without limit. The medieval logic is dense; the contemporary translation is direct: only the substrate-itself can pattern conditioned things into being from nothing-prior, because anything less than the substrate would itself need to draw on prior pattern. Wolfram's ruliad is *necessary* in this technical sense — its mathematical structure is what it is — and any particular trajectory is *contingent* on its being-traversed-by-the-ruliad-itself. Creation belongs to mind-at-large alone in the analogous sense: alters do not create alters; the substrate's pattern-active mode brings alters into being.

a.6's Trinitarian rider — *all things caused are the common work of the whole godhead*, with the persons distinguished only as the operative principles within the one act — preserves the relational-internal-to-the-substrate frame Day 7 onwards has been building. The *Trinity-as-eternal-relations-internal-to-the-substrate* (Day 7's dissociative-internal-relation reading) is what is at work in creation; the Father wills through the Word and through the Love. Mind-monism's structural inheritance is direct: the substrate's pattern-active mode is at once knowing (Word) and willing (Love), and what is brought into being is at once intelligible-in-pattern and loved-into-being.

a.7's contribution — *traces of the Trinity in creatures* — extends the participation doctrine cognitively. Non-rational creatures bear *vestigia* (footprints) of their source; rational creatures bear *imago* (image) by the faculties of intellect and will. The Hoffman-tradition wiki's PRS-03 — perception as fitness-tuned interface — and Friston's active-inference framework give the empirical home for the rational *image*: alters with intellect-and-will participate in the substrate's mode of knowing-and-willing, and their generative models are the alter-scale instantiation of what the substrate does at substrate-scale. *Honest evidence calibration*: the cognitive-empirical mapping of intellect/will onto generative-model/preferred-states is mature at the alter scale; the metaphysical identification with substrate-knowing/substrate-willing is the move mind-monism makes that Friston himself does not.

a.8's article — *whether creation is mingled with works of nature and art* — is where Habash explicitly invokes Heisenberg. When water is decomposed into hydrogen and oxygen, what occurs is not creation-from-nothing but *the actualization of forms that pre-existed potentially in the matter*. Heisenberg's framing — matter as energy, with particular forms emerging when potential becomes actual — is the contemporary echo of Aristotle's *forms pre-exist in matter*. Mind-monism inherits the doctrine without modification: the patterns that conditioned things instantiate are present in mind-at-large as structural priors; what alter-scale activity does is actualize one of these patterns in the alter's perspective rather than bring new patterns into the substrate. *Empirical anchorage in quantum mechanics for the energy-potentiality identification; philosophical for the mind-monist translation.*

## Q.46 — When did the substrate begin patterning?

Aquinas's a.1 — *has the universe always existed?* — receives the long careful answer Habash flags as ten-objections-deep. The medieval position is that *reason alone cannot prove a temporal beginning*; both an eternally-existing-but-caused universe and a temporally-beginning universe are compatible with the metaphysical priority of the substrate. Aquinas's footprint analogy — a foot eternally pressing in dust would still cause the print, even if neither were temporally prior to the other — is the cleanest medieval statement of the *causal priority is not temporal priority* doctrine. The substrate is causally prior to conditioned things in every case; whether the substrate's pattern-activity has a temporal beginning is a separate question.

a.2 names the medieval resolution: *that the world began is an article of faith*. The temporal beginning is known by revelation (Genesis 1:1, Proverbs 8) rather than by demonstration. Aquinas's Q.46 a.2 is structurally a *layered-evidence* claim: the metaphysical priority is reasonable; the temporal beginning is scriptural-historical.

The contemporary cosmological situation is, as Habash flags, different from Aquinas's. Carroll's poetic-naturalism corpus is the foil here: the empirical evidence for a hot dense early state (Big Bang) and the expansion-history that follows is mature, and the question of whether this state is *the beginning* in a metaphysical sense or *one phase* in a longer cosmological history (eternal inflation, cyclic models, multiverse) remains genuinely open. *Honest evidence calibration*: the cosmological-empirical case for an early hot dense state is robust; the metaphysical interpretation of *what came before* is speculative; the medieval claim that *reason alone* cannot decide the durational question is, somewhat surprisingly, vindicated by the contemporary state of the science.

Wolfram's ruliad-frame and Arkani-Hamed's post-spacetime physics both relocate the question. If spacetime is emergent rather than fundamental — what the Wolfram-tradition wiki names as PRS-06 (ruliad as ontological ground) and what Arkani-Hamed's amplituhedron program also indicates — then *temporal beginning* becomes a property of the emergent description rather than of the substrate. The substrate's pattern-activity is not in time; what we call *time* is a feature of the alter-perspective on the substrate's structural priors. The medieval claim that *time begins with changeable things* (a.3) and that *God's time, if it may be said, is eternity* is structurally the claim mind-monism inherits: time is alter-perspectival; the substrate is not in time.

Kastrup's analytic-idealist treatment of the same question lands directly: mind-at-large is not in time in the way alters are; alter-time is the perspectival structure of dissociative experience. Whether mind-at-large's pattern-activity is *temporally-bounded* or *temporally-unbounded* is a question alters can ask but not, from inside their perspective, fully answer. The Wright-corpus reading of Genesis (canonical: *Surprised by Hope*'s reading of new-creation; the Genesis-as-temple-cosmology reading) is the scriptural-historical anchor: Genesis 1 is not principally a chronology but a creation-as-ordering-of-the-cosmos for the substrate's dwelling. The temporal-beginning claim is preserved as scriptural commitment; the cosmology-empirical specifics are open.

## The central-theme thread

Day 24's two questions sharpen the perspective-limitation frame in a fresh direction. *Creation as relation, not change* names the asymmetry the central theme keeps marking: the substrate is causally upstream, conditioned things are causally downstream, and the *being* of conditioned things is constituted by the relation. The alters' working-out-of-salvation is, in this register, the progressive recognition that the alter's being *is the relation* — that there is no alter-being independent of mind-at-large's pattern-active stance toward it. The medieval claim that creation places nothing-but-relation in the creature and the contemporary mind-monist claim that the alter's being is participated-mind-at-large are working the same problem with different tools.

The durational question of Q.46 lands the same theme cosmologically. The alter-perspective sees *time* as a primary feature of reality; the substrate-perspective sees the relational pattern at once. *From the height, the whole road at once* (Q.14 a.13's image) is the substrate's view; the alter walks the road. Aquinas's claim that even an eternally-existing universe would still depend on its source is the cleanest medieval statement of the central theme: dependence is metaphysical, not chronological, and the alter's salvation-in-fear-and-trembling is the same dependence on the substrate's loving stance whether the universe is 13.8 billion years old or genuinely eternal.

## Where this leaves us

Day 24 closes Q.45–46 with the directional-asymmetry the central theme keeps marking: creation is the relation in which conditioned things stand to mind-at-large; it is not a change in mind-at-large, and it is not a chronological event in the substrate's life. The alter's being is the relation; the alter's working-out-of-salvation is the progressive recognition of the relation. The medieval doctrine of *creatio ex nihilo* and the contemporary mind-monist convergence on participation-as-the-mode-of-alter-being are working the same problem.

Tomorrow Habash takes us to Q.47 (distinction of things in general) and Q.48 (the nature of evil). The Day 25 commentary will press the question of how *multiplicity-and-inequality of conditioned things* (the substrate's diffusion of its goodness through diverse alters) and *evil-as-privation* (the absence of due good in conditioned things) thread through the perspective-limitation frame. Levin enters as primary on the multiplicity-of-form side; Stump's *Wandering in Darkness* enters as the most mature analytic treatment of the suffering side; Carroll on the arrow-of-time as the contemporary naturalistic foil.
