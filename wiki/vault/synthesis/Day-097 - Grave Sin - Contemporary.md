# Day 97 — Grave Sin (Contemporary)

## Frame

Q.72 and Q.73 — nineteen articles in a single day — are the *structural* questions about sin. Q.72 asks how sins are *individuated*: by their objects, by spiritual vs. carnal, by the order they violate, by thought / word / deed, by excess vs. deficiency. Q.73 asks how sins are *comparable*: are they all equal (no), what makes one graver than another, can one sin host another? The medieval frame asks: *how do we tell mis-aligned policies apart, and how do we measure how mis-aligned a given policy is?*

In the contemporary frame the question becomes one of *agent-level diagnostics*: given an agent whose policy is failing to align with its constitutive goal, what is the right partition over policy-failures, and what is the right scaling on their severity? Aquinas's answer — *species by intended end, gravity by ultimateness of perverted end* — turns out to be very close to what a contemporary mind-monism / active-inference frame would propose.

## Problem

**P1**: action-individuation. The same physical motion can be many different acts. A man kills another man — that is the matter. Is it self-defense, soldierly duty, robbery, or fratricide? The matter does not decide; the *end* does. Aquinas states this as Q.72 a.3's central insight: *objects in relation to external acts have the character of matter about which, but in relation to the interior act of the will they have the character of end, and it is owing to this that they give the act its species.* This is *species-by-end*. The contemporary problem is identical: given a sequence of observable behavior, what is the policy that generates it? Behavior is multiply-realizable; the same trajectory can arise from very different policies under very different preferred-outcome distributions.

**P2**: severity-scaling. Given two failed policies, which is the more severe failure? Aquinas's answer: graver as the perverted end is more *ultimate*. Sins against God > sins against human nature > sins against external goods. The contemporary problem is again identical: what is the scaling that captures how *deep* a policy failure runs?

**P3**: the legion problem. Aquinas says *vices fragment the agent into many appetites; virtue unifies the agent under reason* — the demoniac is called Legion, the virtuous man is *one*. This is the *individuation-by-perspective-limitation* claim of this project's central theme, stated in 13th-century vocabulary.

## Resource

**Active inference on policy individuation** (Friston-tradition wiki, PRS-04 *organism as active modeler*). In the FEP frame, an action is individuated by its *preferred outcome distribution* — the prior over states that the policy is trying to bring about. The same surface behavior can implement very different policies because the *prior* differs. This is *exactly* Aquinas's species-by-end claim, expressed in measure-theoretic vocabulary instead of Aristotelian-final-cause vocabulary. **Confidence: high** (FEP substrate-independence is formally established, PRS-07).

**Precision-weighting as the voluntariness-scaling parameter**. Q.73 a.6 — gravity tracks willfulness — fits cleanly with Friston's account of policy precision. An action is "more willed" when the high-level model retains high precision over the lower-level priors that produced it. Ignorance reduces precision over the truth-evaluating prior; fear, violence, weakness reduce precision over the agent's high-level model. *Involuntariness drops precision to zero, at which point the act is no longer hostable to sin* — which is exactly the limit Aquinas himself names. **Confidence: medium-high** (the formal apparatus is solid; the identification with voluntariness is interpretive bridging.)

**McGilchrist on cold-rational pathology** (McGilchrist-tradition wiki, PRS-01). Q.73 a.5's most counter-intuitive claim — *spiritual sins are graver than carnal sins because they are colder, more diabolical* — has a contemporary echo in McGilchrist's account of left-hemispheric pathology: when the analytic-manipulative mode operates without the integrative-receptive mode's grounding, it can generate elaborate, unimpassioned, *unified* policies that nonetheless miss the whole that the agent is part of. Aquinas's *intellectus alone can sin most coldly* and McGilchrist's *left-hemisphere dominance generates the most disconnected pathology* converge — same finding, different vocabulary. **Confidence: medium** (the hemispheric account is empirically robust at the brain level; the cultural-scale extension is contested.)

**Stump on suffering and divine permission** (Stump-tradition wiki, PRS-05; *Wandering in Darkness*) — Aquinas's Q.73 a.10 (excellence of the sinner aggravates the sin) finds its contemporary deepening in Stump's account: virtue and grace are *cooperatively* held; rupture of that cooperation when one has been graced more deeply is rupture of *more*. The framework is theological but the structural claim has a counterpart in mind-monism: an agent integrated more deeply into the wider conscious-agent network has *more to lose* when its policy diverges. **Confidence: medium** (Stump's account is rigorous within analytic Thomism; the mind-monism transposition is exploratory.)

## Solution (proposed)

**Species-by-end is correct under both frames.** Aquinas's analytic move — that the formal kind of an act is fixed by the end intended, not by the matter — is structurally identical to the active-inference claim that policies are individuated by their preferred-outcome priors. This is a place where the medieval analysis and the contemporary formalism *converge* with high confidence. The synthesis can state this directly: the *species* of a sin is the policy's target distribution; the *matter* is the trajectory that the policy traverses to get there.

**Gravity tracks two things: ultimateness of perverted end, and precision of the willing.** Aquinas's a.3 (gravity by ultimateness) and a.6 (gravity by willfulness) are not two separate dimensions but the two parameters of a single function. *Ultimateness* names how high in the prior-stack the perversion sits — a turn-against-charity (the highest prior) is graver than a turn-against-temperance (a lower prior) because the prior governs more downstream policy. *Willfulness* names how much precision the agent's high-level model is putting on the policy. The product of "how deep in the stack" times "how much precision the agent put on it" is the *gravity*. Aquinas treats these as two articles of one question; the active-inference reading treats them as two factors in one product.

**Aquinas's counter-intuitive ranking — spiritual sins graver than carnal — survives the translation.** Under the active-inference reading, the explanation is that carnal sin is typically a *low-precision precision-failure at the policy level* (the agent's policy is hijacked by a strong but local appetitive signal). Spiritual sin is a *high-precision policy-level commitment to a disordered end without appetitive coupling*. The latter is more pathological because the prior itself is mis-set, not just a local override. Aquinas's "colder, more diabolical" lands, in the contemporary frame, as "the failure is at the level of the prior, not the level of the noise."

**The Legion / unity claim is the central theme.** Q.73 a.1 — vices fragment the agent into many appetitive sub-agents, virtue unifies the agent under reason — is one of the most direct articulations of the project's central frame found in I-II. Each appetite is, in Hoffman-style interface terms, a partial perspective on what the agent ought to pursue. Without an integrating prior (charity / loving unity), the multiple perspectives do not coordinate; they fragment the agent. The unified agent is the one whose perspective-multiple is held together by the highest prior. Aquinas's Latin for this — *the virtuous man is one* — and the central theme's English — *loving unity is the goal toward which the limited agent computes* — say very nearly the same thing.

**What's reframed**: Aquinas treats the unification as effected by *charity* understood as supernatural infused virtue. The contemporary frame treats unification as the *high-precision integrative prior that couples the agent's policy to the wider conscious-agent network*. These are not identical claims — the metaphysical anchor differs — but they are *compatible*, and the contemporary frame is in the position of being a *partial* mind-monist gloss on what Aquinas was naming theologically.

**Evidence strength**: species-by-end as policy-by-prior — formal, high confidence. Gravity = depth × precision — formal interpretation, medium-high. Spiritual-graver-than-carnal as prior-level vs. policy-level failure — medium, interpretive. Legion-vs-unity as multi-perspective-without-integration — speculative bridge to mind-monism, but theologically Aquinas-internal.

## The central-theme thread

The deepest hit to the central theme today is Q.73 a.1 — *all sins are not connected, because vice fragments the agent into multiple appetites*. The agent is *individuated* not by its substance but by its *perspective-cohesion*. A unified agent is the one whose multiple partial perspectives — toward self, neighbor, body, world — are held together by a single integrating prior. Vice does not merely make the agent *bad*; it makes the agent *plural in the wrong way*, multiplying perspective-bound sub-policies that no longer coordinate. The way back is the same as Day 96: *charity* — the prior that integrates all the partials into one. Aquinas says it; mind-monism re-says it.

## Where this leaves us

Today's question-pair is the structural anatomy of sin. We have made the analytic alignment — species-by-end as policy-by-prior, gravity as depth × precision — and located one of the central-theme's clearest medieval articulations. Tomorrow's Q.74-75 take this structural account into *etiology*: which *power* of the agent is the seat of sin (the will, primarily; reason and sensuality secondarily) and what *causes* sins (internal: reason/will; external: influences that influence but do not compel). The hinge from *what sin is* to *what causes it* — Day 98 — is where the active-inference framework will earn the most ground.
