# Day 67 — Contemporary Parallel: *Intention as Hierarchical Terminus and Choice as the Conclusion of Practical Inference*

Day 66 articulated the necessity-and-freedom structure of will-movement and the substrate-direct-rest phenomenology of enjoyment. Day 67 takes Aquinas through Q.12 (intention — 5 articles) and Q.13 (choice — 6 articles), the bridge between willing-the-end and willing-the-means. The contemporary mind-monist frame engages this as the *hierarchical-prior structure* of intention and the *practical-syllogism conclusion* structure of choice, with active inference and hierarchical predictive coding as the leading resources.

## Frame

Q.12 a.1: intention is *to tend toward something* — it is the will's act in moving toward the end. Q.12 a.2: intention can be of the last end *or* of midpoint termini (the *A → B → C* analogy: B is a terminus though not the last). Q.12 a.3: one cannot intend two last ends (Mt 6:24), but one can intend several proximate ends ordered to a single ultimate end. Q.12 a.4: intention of end and volition of means are *one act* when the means is willed under the end's formal aspect (analogy: light and color are distinct objects but in one act of sight I see both). Q.12 a.5: irrational animals have *built-in tendings* (instinct) but not intention strictly speaking, because they cannot understand the goal as goal.

Q.13 a.1: choice is primarily an act of the will (it is *the desire of things in our power*). Q.13 a.2: irrational animals do not choose (they are determined by instinct). Q.13 a.3: choice is *primarily* of the means; conclusions of one practical-syllogism can become principles of another (the chosen-mean-of-being-a-doctor becomes an end requiring further means). Q.13 a.4: choice is only of things done by us. Q.13 a.5: choice is only of possible things. Q.13 a.6: man chooses freely — necessity attaches only to the perfect good (happiness), and choice is of the means, not the perfect good.

## Problem

The problem (P): how does the alter's volitional architecture coordinate the higher-order *intention-of-end* with the lower-order *choice-of-means* under perspective-limitation? The medieval frame articulates this as the bridge-act of intention plus the conclusion-act of practical syllogism. The contemporary frame meets this as the hierarchical-generative-model architecture of policy-selection.

## Resource

**Friston on hierarchical generative models and practical syllogism.** Active inference's hierarchical structure: high-level priors set terminal goals; lower-level priors compute mean-policies that minimize expected free energy under the terminal-prior constraint. The medieval *terminus-of-movement* structure (Q.12 a.2) — end-as-stopping-point and end-as-midpoint-of-larger-movement — is precisely the hierarchical-prior structure: every prior is a terminus relative to its sub-policies and a way-station relative to higher priors. Q.13 a.3's *conclusions-of-one-syllogism-become-principles-of-another* is the hierarchical-prior chain. The medieval *choice as the conclusion of practical syllogism* (Q.13 a.3 — *I intend to be at work by 8:00am; the best way is to drive; therefore I choose to drive*) is the formal correlate of policy-selection at the bottom of the hierarchical chain.

**Hoffman on single-act-multi-object structure.** Q.12 a.4's analogy — light-and-color are distinct objects but one act of sight sees both — preserves under interface theory: the perceiver's interface integrates multiple substrate-pattern channels into single perceptual acts.

**McGilchrist on right-hemispheric integration and left-hemispheric analysis.** Right-hemispheric integration sees the end-as-gestalt; left-hemispheric analysis works the means as practical-syllogism. The medieval intention-and-choice distinction maps onto the hemispheric-coordination structure.

**Stump on will-as-faculty-of-the-good and second-personal-knowing.** The will is constitutively directed toward the formal good; the second-personal mode of intention-toward-other-persons is the will's full mode. The medieval *intention as the will's tending-toward* preserves under Stump's frame as the will's directedness in its second-personal mode.

**Levin on multi-scale agency and instinctual tending.** Q.12 a.5's *animals have built-in tendings, not intentions* — the bee toward the beehive, the deer fleeing the wolf — preserves under multi-scale agency: cellular and tissue-level systems exhibit goal-directedness without representing the goal as goal. *Tending without representing* is exactly Levin's diverse-intelligences research at sub-personal scales.

## Solution (proposed, speculative)

The contemporary mind-monist read of Q.12: intention is the alter's *hierarchical-prior commitment* to a terminal-or-midpoint-terminus, with the higher levels of the hierarchy carrying the *as-ordered-toward-the-perfect-good* formal character even when the immediate intention is a midpoint terminus. The single-act structure of Q.12 a.4 is the integrated-hierarchical-prior structure: when the means is computed *under* the end-prior, the policy-selection is a single integrated act spanning multiple hierarchical levels.

The contemporary mind-monist read of Q.13: choice is the *conclusion of practical inference* under hierarchical priors. The medieval *necessity-only-attaches-to-the-perfect-good* preserves: terminal-prior necessity exists only at the top of the hierarchy; mid-and-lower priors leave latitude. The alter's freedom is the latitude-among-mean-policies that the partial-good structure leaves open.

What this *shares with Aquinas*: the hierarchical structure of intention-and-choice; the freedom-only-among-particulars commitment; the animal-tends-but-does-not-choose distinction.

What it *reframes*: Aquinas's frame is faculty-psychological and Aristotelian; the contemporary frame uses hierarchical generative models, active inference, hemispheric coordination, and multi-scale agency. The structural commitments are preserved.

What is *firm vs. speculative*: Friston's hierarchical-generative-model frame is mature; Levin's *tending without representing* is empirically robust at biological scales; Hoffman and McGilchrist contribute mature philosophical-conceptual frames; Stump's second-personal-knowing extension is theological-philosophical strong. The synthesis-claim that the medieval bridge-act of intention is the hierarchical-prior-commitment is speculative-formal.

## The central-theme thread

Today's day articulates the *practical-rationality* register of the central theme. *Salvation in fear and trembling* under perspective-limitation is, structurally, the alter's hierarchical-prior commitment to the perfect-good as formal-terminus, plus the alter's iterated practical-syllogism work to compute mean-policies under that commitment. The alter is constitutively free *because* its perception of the means is under-determined; the alter is constitutively bound *because* its terminal-prior is the formal universal good.

This is the *computational task* dimension of the central theme. The alter is engaged in a real computation — under limited resources, with limited perceptual interface, under priors shaped by passion-and-habit-and-substrate-network-pedagogy — to compute mean-policies that align actually with the terminal-prior the alter formally holds. The computation is the daily-task work of becoming.

Rohr on contemplative-prayer as the alter's deliberate alignment of computation under the substrate-source's primary causation. Stump on tradition-bound rationality as the inherited resource for practical-syllogism-conclusion.
