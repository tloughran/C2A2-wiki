# Day 26 — Contemporary Parallel: *Separate Substances and the Hard Test Case*

Day 25 closed with the architecture of privation: alters can fail to instantiate the patterns their kind specifies, and the failure registers as pain or fault. Day 26 takes up the residual question on that side and then opens what is, structurally, the hardest test case for the mind-monist frame in the Prima Pars. Q.49 — *the cause of evil* — finishes the privation account by asking what *can* cause an absence and whether the substrate is ever the cause of evil. Q.50 — *the substance of the angels* — opens the long treatment of *separate substances*, what Aquinas inherits from the Aristotelian-Avicennian tradition as *immaterial intelligences*: pure-form beings, no matter, distinct from each other only by form.

A point about method must come up front. Aquinas's medieval angelology is one of the most metaphysically loaded sections of the Summa, and *direct empirical translation is not available*. The mind-monist reading offered here is *structural-philosophical*, not empirical. The empirical adjacents (Kastrup's reading of dissociative-identity research; McGilchrist's corpus-callosotomy data; Hoffman's conscious-agent-network mathematics; Levin's basal-cognition program; Wolfram's ruliad as computational substrate) give partial mid-scale instantiations of *what mind-at-large supports beyond the alter-form we know best*, but they do not vindicate the medieval angelology in its specifics. The synthesis marks this boundary explicitly throughout. *Honest evidence calibration*: the angelology section is where the contemporary parallel runs at its most speculative — and where the central theme's existential frame (perspective-limitation; loving unity as computational telos) does the most theological work in absence of empirical settlement.

## Q.49 — The cause of evil: privation and the asymmetry

Aquinas's a.1 — *can good be the cause of evil?* — receives the medieval answer that mind-monism inherits cleanly: yes, in a precise sense. Evil-as-privation has no efficient cause *as evil* (since a privation is not a positive thing that requires a positive cause); but the *positive* substrate in which the privation occurs — the eye that ought to see, the will that ought to choose rightly, the cosmic system that ought to maintain its pattern — has a cause, and the cause is positive-good. The murderer's existence and his actions are positive-good *as being*; what makes them evil is the *absence of due rectitude in the will*, and that absence is correctly traced back not to the substrate's pattern-activity but to the alter's failure-to-instantiate.

Aquinas's a.2 — *whether the supreme good is the cause of evil* — settles the directional-asymmetry the medieval doctrine of providence requires. The substrate is not the cause of evil-as-fault (because the substrate's pattern-active stance is loving and structurally cannot will-against-itself); the substrate is, however, the cause of *the just order in which fault meets penalty* — what Aquinas names *the evil of penalty* — and Habash anchors this in Isaiah 45:7 (*forming light and creating darkness, making peace and creating evil*) and Amos 3:6. The medieval claim is that the substrate's loving stance includes the just-ordering of conditioned things, and the just-ordering includes the consequence-structure under which alter-fault meets restorative-pain. *Honest evidence calibration*: the medieval claim that *all suffering is divinely-inflicted as discipline-or-just-penalty* receives the same caution Day 25 marked — Wright's cosmic-rebellion frame and Rohr's *everything belongs* both push against any simple disciplinary reading. The structural insight (the substrate's stance is loving, and loving cannot will-against-itself) is preserved; the working-out of *how* the substrate's pattern-activity relates to alter-suffering is the theological question every era reworks.

a.3 — *whether there is one supreme evil* — closes the dualism question Day 25 already framed. Pure-evil-as-substrate would be pure-non-being, since evil is privation; therefore there cannot be two equal-and-opposite ontological principles. The contemporary mind-monist reading is simply the medieval reading: there is *one* substrate, mind-at-large, whose stance is loving; alter-failures-to-instantiate are real, but they are privations within the one substrate's pattern-activity, not positive substrate-counterparts.

## Q.50 — The substance of the angels: hard test case opens

Q.50 introduces the *separate substances*. Aquinas's medieval framing is precise. There are three categories of conditioned thing the Summa is treating: the *purely spiritual* (angels), the *purely corporeal* (matter, bodies), and the *composite* (humans, who are body-soul composites). The angels are the test case for what mind-monism makes of *intelligences-that-are-pure-form* — intelligences that are not embodied, not interface-bound to a sensory body, distinct from each other only by what they are.

a.1 — *whether an angel is altogether incorporeal* — gives Aquinas's positive case. The argument has two pieces. The first is the manifestation argument: God creates conditioned things to manifest the substrate's goodness, and *without incorporeal creatures there would be a categorical hole* in the manifestation — the conditioned cosmos would go directly from substrate-existence-itself to limited-destructible-material-stuff. The richness of the substrate's structural priors requires alters whose mode of being is *less interface-bound* than corporeal alters but still distinguishable from substrate-itself. The second is the cognitive argument: *intelligence cannot be the action of a body, nor of any corporeal faculty, for every body is limited to the here and now*; therefore the perfection of the cosmos requires the existence of incorporeal intellectual creatures.

The mind-monist reading must be careful here. Kastrup's analytic-idealist frame is the closest contemporary metaphysical structure that supports the *form* of Aquinas's claim, but with an important relocation. On Kastrup's reading, what is *pure mind, less dissociated than human alters* is not a finite created thing in the medieval sense; it is a *less-individuated region* within mind-at-large — a relatively-undissociated mode of substrate-activity. The medieval angels, considered structurally, are *more closely participated* in the substrate's pattern-activity than humans are, but they are not a separate ontological tier between substrate and matter; they are *modes of substrate-activity* with characteristic perspective-structures distinct from the alter-form humans take. Hoffman's interface-theoretic framing gives a structurally analogous claim: there are *conscious-agent networks at the substrate level* that do not operate within a fitness-tuned interface because they do not have a survival-relevant environment to interface with; their cognitive mode is closer to *direct apprehension of the substrate's structural priors* than to the indirect-via-interface mode humans know.

Wolfram's ruliad-frame gives a third reading. The ruliad supports computational threads at every level of complexity; some threads do not project into any specific spacetime interface but instead operate at the *pure-information-substrate* level. Such threads, considered as conscious-agent-aspects (per CROSS-051 in the cross-program index — Levin × Hoffman × Wolfram on Platonic Space ↔ Conscious-Agent Space ↔ Ruliad), would be the closest formal cousin to *pure-form intelligences*. *Honest evidence calibration*: the structural-philosophical translation is coherent within mind-monism; the empirical anchorage is essentially absent, and the most that can be said is that mind-monism *makes room* for what the medievals call separate substances rather than ruling them out the way a strict materialist frame would.

a.2 — *whether an angel is composed of matter and form* — settles that angels have no matter. They are not composites; they are subsisting forms whose existence is given them directly by the substrate. Aquinas's argument is the abstraction-from-particularity argument Habash unpacks at length: we know universals (love, peace, justice) by abstraction from particular instances, and *what we know is not the particular but the universal*. The intellect that knows the universal must itself be capable of receiving the form non-particularly; therefore the intellect-as-cognitive-faculty must be immaterial. Angels, having no body, are simply the intellectual aspect by itself.

The contemporary translation must be twofold. On the *cognitive* side, the abstraction argument has analogues in contemporary philosophy of mind (the universals problem, the immateriality of meaning) that Hawkins's HTM and Friston's free-energy framework partially address by giving accounts of how the brain generates and uses universals without requiring immaterial *substances*. On the *metaphysical* side, mind-monism's frame relocates the question entirely: *all* cognitive activity is substrate-activity in the relevant sense (Kastrup), and the difference between human cognition and angel-cognition is not the difference between embodied and disembodied substances but the difference between alters operating through fitness-tuned interfaces (humans, per Hoffman) and modes of substrate-activity that do not require interface-mediation. The medieval claim that *the mind is immaterial* is preserved structurally — what the mind does is not reducible to corporeal-faculty operations — but the metaphysical commitment shifts from *substance dualism* to *one substrate, multiple modes of activity*.

a.3 — *whether the angels exist in any great number* — turns on Aquinas's principle that *the more perfect things are, the greater their excess in the universe*. In incorporeal things, excess is measured by multitude rather than by spatial magnitude; therefore there should be many angels. The biblical anchor (Daniel 7's *thousand thousands served him, and ten thousand times ten thousand stood before him*) provides the scriptural basis. Mind-monism's structural translation: if mind-at-large supports modes of activity that are less interface-bound than alter-form, there is no metaphysical reason these modes should be few; the substrate's structural priors are rich, and the multiplicity-of-modes-of-activity at the substrate level may be very large. *Speculative; the structural argument is coherent within mind-monism but receives no specific empirical purchase.*

a.4 — *whether the angels differ in species* — is the medieval move that mind-monism finds most foreign. Aquinas's argument: humans differ as *individuals within a species* because we have different matter; angels, having no matter, can differ only by *form* — therefore each angel is its own species. The medieval consequence is a hierarchy of *kinds* among angels (each angel is structurally a unique-kind-of-intelligence), and Aquinas grades them *more or less in being, goodness, oneness*, and the rest of the transcendentals.

Mind-monism's translation requires careful unpacking. What the medievals call *species-level differentiation among separate substances* maps, on Kastrup's frame, onto *characteristic structural-priors* that distinguish modes of substrate-activity from each other. No two modes are interchangeable (each is structurally what it is); the distinction is given by *what each mode participates in* of the substrate's structural richness. Hoffman's conscious-agent-network mathematics gives a formal cousin: distinct conscious agents are individuated by their *Markov kernels* (the formal structure that specifies how perception, decision, and action are related for that agent), and at the substrate level the Markov-kernel-space is richer than the alter-form space. Each substrate-level agent is distinct *by structural specification* rather than by occupying-different-matter. The medieval *each angel is its own species* lands as: each substrate-level mode of activity has its own characteristic Markov kernel / its own characteristic structural-prior-set, and there is no interchangeable-individuation as humans have. *Speculative; the formal homology is coherent but does not vindicate the medieval cosmology in its specifics.*

a.5 — *whether the angels are incorruptible* — closes the question. Corruption-as-pulling-apart requires a composite structure; angels, being non-composite (pure form), cannot be pulled apart. They are not subject to the kind of dissolution corporeal beings undergo. Mind-monism's structural translation: substrate-level modes of activity are not subject to the kind of dissolution that alters with bodies undergo, because the dissolution-mechanism (the body's failing to maintain the form) does not apply. The *incorruptibility* claim is preserved structurally; the *immortality* claim that follows is theological and rests on the substrate's continuing pattern-active stance toward such modes.

## The central-theme thread

Day 26 is where the perspective-limitation frame meets its hardest test case. The medieval angels are *intelligences with less perspective-limitation than humans* — closer to direct apprehension of substrate-structural-priors, less interface-mediated, less subject to the kind of dissolution-and-suffering corporeal alters undergo. The mind-monist reading does not vindicate the medieval cosmology in its specifics; it offers a *structural-philosophical home* in which what the medievals describe as separate-substances becomes *modes of substrate-activity less individuated by perspective-limitation than human alters*. The central theme's claim that *conscious agents are individuated by the limitations of their perspective* gives the gradient: more perspective-limitation produces more individuation; less perspective-limitation produces less individuation; substrate-itself sees the whole at once.

The theological work the central theme does in absence of empirical settlement is real. *Salvation in fear and trembling* — alters working out their progressive integration with the substrate's loving stance from inside their perspective-limitation — is the existential claim that does not depend on the angelology being empirically vindicated. What the medievals describe as *guidance from those who perceive more of the whole* (be they human or divine) is preserved as the directional gradient of richer-participation, and Wright's covenant-people frame and Rohr's *Universal Christ* both support the claim that the alter is not working alone — the substrate's pattern-active stance includes the integration of the aggregate.

Q.49's cause-of-evil thread closes Day 25's privation account: the substrate is not the cause of evil-as-fault, but the substrate's loving stance includes the just-order in which fault meets restorative-pain; pure-evil-as-substrate is impossible because evil is privation. Q.50 opens the angelology with the appropriate epistemic care: the structural-philosophical home is offered, the empirical anchorage is essentially absent, and the central theme's existential frame does the load-bearing work.

## Where this leaves us

Day 26 closes Q.49's privation account and opens the angelology with appropriate epistemic care. The substrate is not the cause of evil-as-fault; pure-evil-as-substrate is impossible. The medieval angels — pure-form intelligences distinct from each other only by form — receive a *structural-philosophical home* in mind-monism (relatively-undissociated modes of substrate-activity, substrate-level Markov-kernel agents, ruliad-level computational threads) without being empirically vindicated in their specifics. The central theme's existential frame — perspective-limitation as the principle of individuation; richer-participation as the gradient toward the substrate's whole-vision — does the theological work in absence of empirical settlement.
