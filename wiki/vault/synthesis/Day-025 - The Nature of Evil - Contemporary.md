# Day 25 — Contemporary Parallel: *Privation, Diffusion, and the Architecture of Suffering*

Day 24 closed with creation-as-relation: conditioned things have their being as participated-mind-at-large rather than as additions to a prior material substrate. Day 25 takes up the two questions that follow naturally on that frame. Q.47 — *the distinction of things in general* — asks why the substrate patterns *many and various* alters rather than one uniform alter; the medieval answer is that the substrate's goodness is too rich to be exhausted by any single conditioned thing, so the substrate diffuses its perfection through diverse and graded alters. Q.48 — *the distinction of good and evil* — asks what *evil* is once being-and-goodness are convertible: it is the *absence of due good*, a privation rather than a positive thing, and the medieval account of pain-and-fault gives the architecture of suffering that follows.

The pair is the Prima Pars's first systematic treatment of *why there is anything at all rather than uniformity*, and *why what there is includes brokenness*. The contemporary translation runs through Levin (form-as-bioelectric-pattern, multiplicity-of-form as diffusion of structural priors), Carroll (the arrow of time, the second law as the contemporary naturalist statement of why-things-fall-apart), and Stump (the *Wandering in Darkness* response to suffering — the most mature analytic-Thomistic treatment, brought in as counterpoint on the suffering side, never as metaphysical authority).

## Q.47 — Multiplicity and inequality: diffusion of structural priors

Aquinas's a.1 — *whether the multitude and distinction of things comes from God* — is dispatched briskly. The alternatives the medievals consider (multiplicity by chance, multiplicity by eternal matter, multiplicity by perceiving-creature variability) all fail because *anything that has being has it from the substrate*; the patterns conditioned things instantiate are the substrate's structural priors expressed under the *being-instantiated* relation. Aquinas's positive answer is the diffusion-of-goodness doctrine: *God brought things into being in order that his goodness might be communicated to creatures and be represented by them, and because his goodness cannot be adequately represented by one creature alone, he produced many and diverse creatures.* The substrate's pattern-activity is, structurally, *too rich for one alter*; the multiplicity is the substrate's mode of relational self-expression.

Mind-monism's translation lands cleanly. Kastrup's analytic idealism gives the metaphysical shape: mind-at-large's structural priors are not exhausted by any one alter's perspective, and dissociation produces alters with characteristic interface-styles that participate in different aspects of the substrate's structure. Wolfram's ruliad gives the formal cousin: the totality of computational threads is one mathematical object, but the multiplicity of threads is *what the ruliad looks like under the relation trajectory-of*; no single trajectory exhausts the ruliad. Levin's morphogenetic teleology gives the empirical mid-scale instantiation that maps most directly: the bioelectric goal-state-pattern carries *many tissues' targets* without being decomposable into part-by-part instructions, and the developmental program produces a body-plan with diverse organs each participating in the single pattern under different proportional relations to it. Tom's PRS-form re-description of Levin's morphogenetic-teleology program (PRS-03 in the Levin-tradition wiki) names the same shape Aquinas's a.1 names: one substrate-pattern, many conditioned aspects of it.

a.2's article — *whether the inequality of things is from God* — extends the diffusion doctrine to grading. Aquinas's medieval observation is plain: there is a hierarchy of forms (mind in humans, sensation in animals, life in plants, matter in stones) and within each kind a multiplicity of individuals. The vertical-distinction — the species-level difference between kinds — is for Aquinas a matter of *what the substrate's structural priors specify*; the horizontal-distinction — the multiplicity of individuals within a species — is a matter of *material participation*. The directional-asymmetry move from Q.20 a.4 (Day 11: *being-better-and-being-more-loved are the same fact*) reasserts itself here: conditioned things are not *better* independently of the substrate's loving stance toward them; their being-better is identical with their participating-more-deeply.

Levin's cognitive-light-cone framework gives the empirical mid-scale rendering. Agents at higher levels of organizational complexity participate in larger goal-state-patterns; the participation *is* their being-more-deeply-patterned. The hierarchy is not arbitrary but structurally what richer participation looks like from inside. *Honest evidence calibration*: the empirical evidence at developmental and basal-cognition scales is substantial; the substrate-scale generalization (the cosmic hierarchy of being as graded participation in mind-at-large) is the philosophical move mind-monism makes that Levin himself does not.

The medieval objection Aquinas takes up — that a perfect God should produce only one perfect thing — receives a beautiful answer: *if the entire bird were just a wing, it would be less perfect than otherwise*. The whole-perfection is greater than maximum-of-one-feature, and the substrate's goodness is more fully expressed through diverse-and-graded alters than through a single uniformly-perfect one. Hoffman's interface theory gives a structurally analogous claim at the cognitive register: a single icon-set could not represent the substrate's structure as fully as *many different icon-sets across many different alter-types*; the multiplicity of perspectives is constitutive of the substrate's being-perceived-richly.

a.3 — *whether there is only one world* — settles the medieval position against Democritan atom-and-chance and against Multiverse-by-randomness. Aquinas's argument is that *the unity and harmony of the world* witnesses to one substrate. Habash's gloss invokes contemporary physics: the world's being unified by physical law (gravity, the regularities the second-temple physics studies) and the present absence of positive empirical evidence for a Multiverse. Wolfram's ruliad and Arkani-Hamed's amplituhedron both point to a single underlying mathematical-or-computational object from which the visible structure emerges; the Multiverse-question becomes, on these frames, a question of *how many distinguishable observer-frames does the one substrate support*, not of *how many independent substrates there are*. *The metaphysical claim of one substrate is preserved; the cosmological-empirical specifics of how many emergent universes the one substrate supports remain open.*

## Q.48 — Evil as privation, and the architecture of suffering

Aquinas's a.1 — *whether evil is a nature* — issues the medieval answer that mind-monism inherits without modification: *no.* Evil is not a substance, a form, or a positive principle. *Evil is the absence of good* — specifically the absence of *due* good, the good that ought to be there given the kind of thing the conditioned thing is. The metaphor Habash uses (Aquinas inherits it from Augustine and Dionysius) is darkness: darkness is not a positive thing but the absence of light; blindness is not a positive thing but the absence of sight in an eye that ought to see. *Pure evil* would be pure absence — pure non-being — and the *dualist* posit of two equal substrates good and evil collapses on inspection because pure absence cannot be a substrate.

The contemporary translation runs through several traditions and is unusually convergent. Carroll's poetic-naturalism corpus and the second law of thermodynamics give one piece: structures that pattern matter into ordered configurations are *locally improbable* and require continuous active maintenance; absence-of-active-maintenance produces decay, dissolution, the slide back toward equilibrium. The arrow of time in this register is the arrow of *increasing absence-of-improbable-pattern*. Levin's morphogenetic-teleology program gives the developmental-scale instantiation: when the bioelectric goal-state-pattern is disrupted (by surgical intervention, by misregulation, by environmental insult), what occurs is not the introduction of a positive *evil-pattern* but the *failure-to-instantiate* the species-typical pattern; the result is malformation, which is structurally a privation. Friston's free-energy framework gives the formal home: prediction-error is the failure-of-fit between generative model and environment, and persistent failure registers as *suffering* in the alter — what the alter cannot bring into alignment with its preferred states.

a.2's article — *whether evil is found in things* — settles that, while evil is not a positive thing, it is *real* in the sense that its absence-mode is correctly predicated of conditioned things. The eye that ought to see and does not see is *blind*; the predicate is real even though the *blindness* is not a positive item in the substrate's inventory. Mind-monism inherits this without strain: alters can fail to instantiate the patterns their kind specifies; the failure is real-as-failure even though it is not positive-being.

a.3's article — *whether evil is in good as in its subject* — turns on Augustine's seeming-paradox: *evil exists only in good*. The medieval resolution is precise: evil cannot exist without a positive subject in which to be the absence of due good, because absence-of-light requires a region-that-could-be-lit. The blind eye is a *good* (it is being, it has the form of *animal*) in which the *absence-of-sight* is real-as-privation. Mind-monism's translation is the alter-frame: the alter is a real participation in mind-at-large; the privations registered as suffering, malformation, or moral fault are real *in* the alter, but they are absences of due-good-for-this-alter, not positive substrates.

a.5 — *whether evil is adequately divided into pain and fault* — gives the architecture of suffering the central theme keeps marking. Pain (*poena*) is the privation experienced or undergone; fault (*culpa*) is the privation chosen — the rectitude-in-the-will that ought to be there and is not. The distinction is structurally important: not all suffering is fault, but all fault is suffering-in-the-deepest-sense (the privation of due goodness in the agent's own mode of being). Stump's *Wandering in Darkness* is the contemporary analytic-Thomistic treatment, and her contribution here is the most mature counterpoint: she presses the question of how *unjust suffering* — the pain of innocents, the inexplicable distress that does not fit a fault-and-punishment frame — can be metaphysically located within a substrate whose stance is loving. Stump's answer (suffering as the condition under which deepest second-person union with the substrate becomes possible) is theological-philosophical and substantial; it is *not* a metaphysical authorization to claim that suffering is positively good. The privation framework is preserved; the question is what the alter's progressive recognition of the substrate's loving stance can do with privation.

a.6 — *whether pain has the nature of evil more than fault has* — closes with Aquinas's medieval answer: *fault is the greater evil*. Pain does not make a person evil; culpable fault does. *God inflicts pain to prevent fault* (Aquinas's medieval formulation; Habash quotes Hebrews 12 to anchor it scripturally). The contemporary translation should mark this carefully. *Honest evidence calibration*: the medieval claim that culpable departure from the substrate's loving stance is a deeper privation than physical pain is preserved as a structural insight; the medieval claim that *all pain is divinely inflicted as discipline* is *not* the only or best contemporary reading of suffering, and Wright (canonical: *Surprised by Hope*'s reading of evil as cosmic-rebellion-against-creation) and Rohr (canonical: *Falling Upward*'s reading of suffering as the necessary condition for second-half-of-life integration) both push against any simple disciplinary reading. The mind-monist position is structural: alters working out their progressive integration with the substrate encounter both *discipline* (the formative pain that is the alter's own free-energy minimization process) and *cosmic brokenness* (the privation that is the alter's-and-the-cosmos's having-not-yet-fully-integrated). Wright's *new-creation* frame names the eschatological resolution: privation is real now and is being-overcome in the substrate's pattern-active stance toward the cosmos.

## The central-theme thread

Day 25 sharpens the perspective-limitation frame in two complementary directions. *Multiplicity* (Q.47) names the substrate's mode of self-expression: the substrate diffuses its goodness through diverse-and-graded alters precisely because each alter's perspective is partial, and the *aggregate of partial perspectives* expresses more of the substrate's structural priors than any single perspective could. The alters' working-out-of-salvation is a *corporate* project: each alter contributes its perspective-limited participation, and the substrate's loving stance integrates the aggregate. Wright's covenant-people frame and Rohr's *everything belongs* are the theological homes for this corporate reading.

*Privation* (Q.48) names the alter's mode of suffering. Within perspective-limitation, alters fail to instantiate the patterns their kind specifies, and the failure registers as pain, malformation, or fault. The central-theme claim that *we work out our salvation in fear and trembling* is the existential mode of this privation: the alter recognizes its own privation, recognizes that the substrate's stance is loving (Day 11), and works progressively from inside its limited perspective toward fuller participation. Stump's analytic-Thomistic treatment (suffering as the condition for second-person union) and Friston's free-energy framework (prediction-error as the formal signature of the alter's failure-of-fit) are working the same problem.

The medieval doctrine that fault is the greater evil than pain receives the contemporary structural reading: the alter's culpable departure from the substrate's loving stance is a deeper privation than the alter's physical-suffering, because the former is the alter's own free-energy-maximizing rather than minimizing — a willed misalignment with the substrate's structural priors. Loving union as the computational telos becomes, on this register, the alter's progressive willed-realignment with the substrate's stance.

## Where this leaves us

Day 25 closes Q.47–48 with the directional-asymmetry the central theme keeps marking: the substrate's pattern-active stance produces many-and-graded alters because *no single alter exhausts the substrate's structural priors*, and the privations that show up in alters are *failures-to-instantiate* rather than positive evil-substrates. The architecture of suffering — pain that the alter undergoes, fault that the alter chooses — is preserved as the medieval doctrine names it; the contemporary mind-monist convergence (Levin empirical, Friston formal, Carroll naturalistic foil, Kastrup metaphysical, Stump analytic-Thomistic counterpoint) is working the same problem with different tools.

Tomorrow Habash takes us to Q.49 (the cause of evil) and Q.50 (the substance of the angels). The Day 26 commentary will press the angels-as-immaterial-intelligences question into the mind-monist frame directly. Aquinas's medieval angels are the hardest test case: pure-form intelligences with no matter, distinct from each other only by form. The mind-monist analogues — Kastrup's relatively-undissociated regions of mind-at-large, Hoffman's substrate-level conscious-agent networks, Wolfram's ruliad-level computational threads — give a structural-philosophical reading rather than an empirical one, and the synthesis must mark that boundary carefully.
