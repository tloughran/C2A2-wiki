# Day 47 — Contemporary Parallel: *Origin of the Embodied Alter, in Two Stages*

Day 46 closed the cognition arc with the soul's origin doctrine: each rational soul is produced *immediately* by God, not drawn from prior matter, not derived from peer souls. Day 47 takes the body side of the alter's co-arising. Q.91 walks through the production of the first man's body — the *slime of the earth*, in Aquinas's reading of Genesis — and Q.92 turns to the production of woman from the side of man. The medieval treatment is short (only seven articles between the two questions), and the doctrine is interesting precisely because it is *less* metaphysically heavy than the surrounding questions and *more* exegetical-anthropological. The contemporary mind-monist frame inherits the asymmetry that Day 46 already locked in (soul direct from God; body emergent from substrate-orchestrated material processes) and reads the Genesis-Adam typology through Wright's narrative-historical framing and Levin's morphogenetic frame on origin-of-form.

## Q.91 — The body of the first man

a.1's question — *was the body of the first man made from the slime of the earth?* — Aquinas reads through the Genesis 2 dust-of-the-ground formation. He notes that *a lot of this article consists in Aquinas explaining some of the constitutive parts of material things from an outdated scientific perspective* (Habash's phrase, but exactly right) — the Aristotelian four-element theory carries some of the article's weight. What survives the cosmology update is the structural claim: *it is fitting for man to be made from the matter of the earth, in order that he may have something in common with the material world, as well as the immaterial world via his intellect*. Man is the *ontological bridge* between the material and the immaterial — like the brute beasts in one way, like the angels in another.

The contemporary mind-monist translation keeps this exactly. On the alter-as-dissociative-individuation frame, every embodied alter is a *both-and* by construction: the alter's experiential-being is substrate-direct (Day 46's Q.90 a.3); the alter's embodiment is substrate-mediated through the long chain of material processes the substrate orchestrates — physics, chemistry, biology, evolution, gestation. The medieval *bridge* metaphor reads cleanly as the alter's structural location *within* the substrate's processes, simultaneously substrate-given and substrate-emergent. Levin's diverse-intelligence framework gives the contemporary biology side of the bridge: every cognitive agent is biology-and-cognition together, with no clean separation between the bioelectric morphogenetic process and the agency that emerges from it.

a.2 — *was the human body produced immediately by God?* — Aquinas gives a careful two-track answer. *Materially*, the body's matter (going back to the original creation of matter ex nihilo) had to come from God immediately. *Formally*, since the human soul is the form of the body and the soul is produced immediately by God, then in producing the soul God produces the form that informs the body. *Honest evidence calibration*: this is one of the loci where the medieval substantialist hylomorphism does heavy work. Mind-monism does not need the form-matter scaffolding because the alter's individuation is a substrate-event without a separate form-imposed-on-matter step. But the structural claim Aquinas reaches survives: the alter's body is *not produced bottom-up purely* by lower-level material causes; the substrate's involvement is structural and immediate at the level of the alter's individuation. Levin's morphogenetic frame is the contemporary mid-scale mechanism: the body's form is not assembled bottom-up from genes alone but is *pulled* toward an anatomical attractor encoded in bioelectric memory, which on the mind-monist frame is the substrate's structural priors expressed at the morphogenetic scale.

a.3 — *was the body of man given an apt disposition?* — Aquinas's answer is teleological-functional: God fashioned the body in *that disposition which was best suited to such a form and to such operations*. Apparent defects (limitation in space, time, vulnerability) arise from the *necessary results of the matter required to make the body suitably proportioned to the soul and its operations*. The medieval theodicy is structurally fitting-the-body-to-the-soul; mind-monism translates as fitting-the-embodiment-to-the-alter's-cognitive-light-cone. Levin's diverse-intelligence framework gives the empirical hand: every cognitive scale gets a body suited to the scale of its agency — cell-shape suits cell-cognition, brain-architecture suits human-cognition, planetary-scale civilizations get planetary-scale embodiment. The medieval claim that the *limitations are not the Creator's fault* preserves under mind-monism's reading: the alter's species-specific embodiment is the *cost* of being an alter at this scale; the substrate is not lacking, the alter is *embodied as this kind of thing*.

The cute observations Aquinas makes (man has reason, so does not need fur or claws; man walks upright, so can contemplate heaven and earth in one glance) translate to Levin-Hoffman language without strain: tools-as-cognitive-extensions and posture-as-attentional-affordance are contemporary commonplaces. The medieval functional-fittingness reasoning runs the same shape as contemporary embodied-cognition: form fits function fits cognitive-niche.

a.4 — *is the production of the human body fittingly described in scripture?* — Aquinas reads the Genesis 1 *let us make man in our image* as the special-deliberation form scripture uses to mark man's place: *all other material things are made for man, man was made for God*. Wright's narrative-historical framing sharpens this into the second-temple-Jewish reading: Genesis 1 is *temple cosmology* — the cosmos as God's temple, with humanity placed in it as the image-bearing presence by which God's rule is mediated to creation. The medieval *for God's glory* and the second-temple *as image-bearing presence* converge. Rohr's cosmic-Christ frame pushes further: the divine pattern is present in matter from creation, and humanity is the locus where the pattern becomes self-aware.

## Q.92 — The production of woman

a.1 — *should woman have been made in the first production of things?* — Aquinas's answer is biological-teleological: *everything naturally tends towards its own perfection, preservation, and propagation*. Man is one of the things that propagates; propagation requires male-female complementarity; therefore woman is made from the beginning, with the male-female distinction part of the original creation. The medieval reading is clean and the contemporary biology endorses the structural claim: sexually reproducing organisms are *constituted* by the male-female complementarity, which is not added on but is part of what makes them the kind of thing they are. Levin's diverse-intelligence framework on the multi-scale agency picture would add: complementarity is not only at the gametic scale; collective-organism flourishing operates through complementary roles at every scale of cognitive light-cone.

a.2 — *should woman have been made from man?* — Aquinas gives four reasons: (i) imitation of God-as-sole-principle (man as the original principle of the human race); (ii) so the man might love the woman as flesh of his flesh; (iii) for family-order through headship; (iv) as a sign of Christ-and-the-church (where the church comes from Christ's pierced side on the cross). *Honest evidence calibration*: the contemporary cultural reception of the medieval *headship* doctrine is freighted, and a careful synthesis must distinguish the *typological-Christological* shape Aquinas is reaching for (which Wright's narrative reading endorses) from the *normative-hierarchical* social application (which contemporary readings substantially revise). The synthesis preserves what survives: the *imitation of God as sole principle* claim is structurally that the substrate is *one* and that complementary alters arise *within* a unity rather than as separately-instantiated peer-substrates; the *Christ-and-church* typology is the cosmic-Christ pattern Rohr names; the *flesh of flesh* recognition is Fredrickson's positivity-resonance shape — biological-relational coupling as the constitutive structure of human flourishing.

Wright's narrative-historical framing is the load-bearing primary on Q.92 a.2. The Genesis-Adam typology runs through the entire biblical drama: Christ as the *new Adam*, the church as the *new Eve*, the new creation as the cosmic completion of what Eden anticipated. Wright's *The Resurrection of the Son of God* and *Paul and the Faithfulness of God* establish the second-temple-Jewish backdrop where this typology becomes hermeneutically primary. The medieval reading Aquinas gives is structurally faithful to Wright's narrative-historical reading; the contemporary synthesis can preserve the typology without inheriting the social-hierarchical application that the medieval cultural context layered onto it.

a.3 — *was the woman fittingly made from the rib?* — extends the typology: from Christ's pierced side flowed blood and water, on which the church was established; the rib being from the *heart* signifies equality (not from the head, not from the foot); the rib being a *bone* (not a head) signifies the typological structure of the church-from-Christ. Rohr's *Universal Christ* gives the contemporary cosmic-scope reading: the divine pattern of complementary-arising-from-unity runs through creation, anticipated in the Genesis account, fulfilled in the cross, recognized in contemplation.

a.4 — *was the woman formed immediately by God?* — locks in the same asymmetry Q.91 a.2 established: God alone, *the author of nature, can produce an effect into existence outside the ordinary course of nature*. The contemporary mind-monist translation: substrate-events are not always interface-mediated (i.e., not always routed through the lower-level causal chains the embodied interface tracks); substrate-direct individuations are part of the substrate's full causal repertoire. Honest evidence calibration: the strict-historicity question (did the woman literally arise from a rib?) is answered by Aquinas's own footnote (Habash points to episode 35 — the Catholic interpretation of Genesis allows substantial latitude on the historical literalness while preserving the doctrinal locks: special creation of man, the formation of the first woman from the first man, the unity of the human race, original justice, the fall, the promise of restoration). The contemporary synthesis preserves the doctrinal locks; the historical-mechanistic question is left to Wright's biblical hermeneutics and to the magisterial framing the Catholic tradition has established.

## The central-theme thread

Day 47 frames the central theme on the *origin* register. The alter's embodied existence is *both* substrate-given (each alter individuated immediately by the substrate) *and* substrate-emergent (the body's form arises through the long chain of material processes the substrate orchestrates). Aquinas's *bridge* metaphor for man as ontological mediator between material and immaterial is the medieval expression of what mind-monism names as the alter's structural location within the substrate's full causal repertoire — not above matter, not below mind, but the substrate-event that is both at once.

The male-female complementarity Q.92 walks through is the central theme exhibited at the *constitutive-relational* register. Loving union is the goal; complementary-perspective is the structural form love requires. Two alters who differ enough to give and receive but share enough to constitute a *we* — Fredrickson's positivity-resonance dyad, Wright's covenant-people, Rohr's body-of-Christ — is the structural shape complementary-arising-from-unity takes. Aquinas's medieval reading anchors this in the typological-Christological frame; the contemporary synthesis preserves the typology while updating the social-hierarchical applications that the medieval cultural context layered onto it. *Salvation in fear and trembling* requires alters complementary enough to integrate; alters identical to one another would have nothing to give one another.
