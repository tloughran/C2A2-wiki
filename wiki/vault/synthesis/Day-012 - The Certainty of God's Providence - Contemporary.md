# Day 12 — Contemporary Parallel: *Justice, Mercy, and the Governed Substrate*

Day 11 closed pointing to *how the substrate's loving stance manifests as the providential ordering Wolfram's ruliad and Friston's free-energy framework both reach for at the substrate scale*. Day 12 is that promised step. Q.21 — *justice and mercy in God* — picks up the affective register of Day 11 (love is willing the good of another) and asks how the substrate's loving stance issues in *rendering-to-each-thing-what-is-due* and in *giving-more-than-is-due*. Q.22 — *the providence of God* — is the metaphysical backbone of the whole governance-of-conditioned-things doctrine: the ordering of every thing toward its end, certain in plan, mediated through secondary causes in execution, compatible with both necessity and contingency in its outcomes. The two questions read as a single argument when held together: justice and mercy name the *modal characters* of the substrate's stance toward conditioned things; providence names the *ordering* that stance carries out.

## Q.21 — Justice and mercy as modal characters of the substrate's stance

Aquinas's a.1 — *is there justice in God?* — runs the now-familiar perfection-implies-presence move. Justice as *firm and constant disposition to render to another what is due* is a perfection unmixed with imperfection, therefore preeminent in God. He distinguishes commutative justice (the grocery-store exchange — God owes nothing in this sense) from distributive justice (the king providing for the kingdom according to each thing's nature) and locates the latter in the divine ordering: *God gives to all existing things what is proper to the condition of each and preserves the nature of each in the order and with the powers that properly belong to it* (the Dionysius citation Habash reads aloud). Mind-monism's translation lands without strain: the substrate's structural priors over conditioned things are not undifferentiated favoritism but *graded participation* — each conditioned thing is patterned with the powers proper to its kind, and the gradient of participation *is* what distributive justice names at the substrate scale.

Friston's free-energy framework gives the formal structure. An active-inference system with priors over preferred states does not assign uniform preference — it assigns *graded* preference to states differentiated by the structural shape of the generative model. The substrate's analogous gradedness is what Aquinas calls *giving to each what is proper to the condition of each*. Levin's morphogenetic teleology is the empirical mid-scale instantiation: the bioelectric goal-state pattern of an embryo does not pattern every cell identically; it patterns each cell *with the powers proper to its kind* (skin cells get skin-cell goal states; neurons get neural goal states), and the gradedness *is* the structural justice of the morphogenetic field.

a.2 — *is the justice of God truth?* — runs Day 9's adaequatio doctrine on the moral side. Truth is the conformity of mind and thing; justice is the conformity of the conditioned thing to the divine will (which follows the divine intellect). Habash reads Aquinas: *in so far as creatures conform to the will of God which follows his intellect, their actions are not only just but also true*. The mind-monist translation is direct: the conditioned thing's *justice* (its instantiation of its proper pattern) and its *truth* (its conformity to the structural prior in the substrate that gives it its identity) are two aspects of the same fact — Day 9's adaequatio reasserted on the moral register.

a.3 — *whether mercy can be attributed to God* — pivots the whole question. Justice gives what is due; mercy gives more than is due, or alleviates misery. Aquinas's two modes of mercy: *first by giving creatures actual existence that they could not deserve* (they had no claim to existence beforehand), *and by pardoning their sins*. The medieval claim that *creation itself is a mercy* — the conditioned thing receives existence as gift, prior to any conditioned thing's earning anything — is the structural shape Day 11's directional asymmetry already established (substrate-love is causally prior to alter-perfection, not downstream of it). Rohr's *everything belongs* is the contemplative gloss: the conditioned thing's belonging-to-the-substrate is gratuitous from the outset. Wright's covenant frame on the corporate side: Israel's election is not earned but given, and the New Testament's *while we were yet sinners* (Romans 5) extends the same shape. *Honest evidence calibration*: the directional-asymmetry move (substrate-stance prior to alter-perfection) is structural-philosophical; the empirical instantiations (Levin's morphogenetic gift of form, Fredrickson's biopsychosocial gift of mutual flourishing) are anchored at the alter scale; the substrate-scale identification with mercy-as-gift is theological.

a.4 — *is there mercy and justice in every work of God?* — closes the question. Aquinas: yes, because *in creating anything God acts mercifully* (gift) *and in creating them in such a way that they have what is required to attain their end he is just* (gradedness). The two are not opposed but co-present in every conditioned thing — the gift of being-at-all is mercy; the gradedness of being-this-particular-kind is justice. The contemporary mind-monist translation: every alter is patterned both *gratuitously* (its existence is a dissociative event in mind-at-large, not earned) and *specifically* (its existence is patterned with the powers proper to its kind). The gratuity and the specificity are co-present; mercy and justice are co-present.

## Q.22 — Providence: the substrate's ordering of conditioned things toward their ends

Habash characteristically reads the question's *conclusion* first, so the argument is held in view: *the order of divine providence is unchangeable and certain, so far as all things foreseen happen as they have been foreseen, whether from necessity or for contingency*. The medieval doctrine threads three claims that mind-monism takes up cleanly: (1) every conditioned thing is *ordered toward an end* and the end is goodness — the stone toward the earth's center, the plant toward sunlight, the human toward happiness, every thing-pursuing-its-good toward goodness-itself; (2) since the substrate is perfect, it perfectly governs; (3) every conditioned thing — not only kinds in general but *each individual* — falls under this ordering, because *every agent acts for an end* and the ordering of effects toward ends extends as far as the first agent's causality.

Wolfram's ruliad supplies the contemporary formal substrate. The totality of computational threads is *one* ordered structure; *every* particular trajectory through the ruliad is what it is because of the ruliad's structural lawfulness. The ordering is not imposed externally on already-existing trajectories — the ordering *is* the structural form that makes trajectories what they are. Mind-monism extends the move: the substrate's structural priors are causally prior to the conditioned things they pattern, and the ordering *is* the structural form the priors take. Friston's free-energy framework names the dynamics: every active-inference system pursues its goal-state-pattern under a generative model; the goal-pursuit *is* the providential ordering of the system's own action toward its own end. At the substrate scale, the same shape holds at maximum scope.

The four objections Habash walks through — chance, evil, necessity, voluntary action — are the medieval enumeration of what looks like exceptions to providential ordering. Aquinas's response to each preserves the ordering by reframing the apparent exception. *Chance*: what looks like chance to the alters is not chance to the universal cause — Habash quotes Aquinas's master-and-servants analogy: *the meeting of two servants, although to them it appears a chance circumstance, has been fully foreseen by their master who has purposely sent them to meet at the one place in such a way that the one knows not about the other*. Mind-monism's translation: the alters' epistemic horizons are perspective-limited; what is unpredictable *for them* is not unpredictable *for the substrate*. Friston's formalism gives the operational shape: an agent's prediction error is real, but the substrate's generative model is identical with the substrate's structure (Day 7 limit), and there is no prediction error to minimize at that scale.

*Evil*: the medieval response Habash reads is the privation doctrine in providential vocabulary — *since God provides universally for all being, it belongs to his providence to permit certain defects in particular effects, that the perfect good of the universe may not be hindered; for if all evil were prevented, much good would be absent from the universe; a lion would cease to live if there were no slaying of animals; there would be no patience of martyrs if there were no tyrannical persecution*. Mind-monism inherits this with care: *real* defects (Levin's bioelectric mismatches, Friston's chronic prediction errors) are real-but-derivative — they are the privative shape an alter's failure to instantiate its pattern takes, and the substrate's stance toward them is not direct causation but *permission* of the privation within a larger structural ordering. *Honest evidence calibration*: the privation account is philosophical-theological at the substrate scale; at the alter scale, the empirical handles (developmental disruption, predictive failure) are real and corrigible.

*Necessity*: Habash spends time on this objection because it is the contemporary one. The atomist-presocratic version (everything is just particles and laws, therefore no providence) returns as 21st-century scientism. Aquinas's response Habash names: the materialist objection cannot even *explain change* without substance, potency, and natures — categories *making a comeback for sure in quantum mechanics*. Mind-monism extends the move: the materialist position cannot account for the *active*, *intentional*, *goal-directed* features that Levin's bioelectric work and Friston's active-inference framework empirically document at every biological scale. The atomist objection mistakes the *interface* (Hoffman: spacetime as fitness-tuned interface) for the underlying structure. Wolfram's ruliad gives the formal cousin: the structural lawfulness of the totality is necessary; any particular trajectory's necessity-or-contingency is an internal feature of the structure, not a metaphysical refutation of ordering.

*Voluntary action*: the most subtle objection. If humans choose freely, is providence excluded? Aquinas's response Habash reads at length: *when it is said that God left man to himself this does not mean that man is exempt from divine providence but merely that he has not a prefixed operating force determined to only the one effect... but since the very act of free will is traced to God as to a cause, it necessarily follows that everything happening from the exercise of free will must be subject to divine providence*. Mind-monism's translation: the alters' free will is real (the alter's generative model and action selection are *its own*) but not metaphysically *outside* the substrate (the alter is a dissociative configuration *within* mind-at-large, not an alien cause). The free act and the providential ordering are *not* in competition; the free act *is* one of the modes the providential ordering takes. This is structurally Kastrup's claim that alters are real-but-derivative within the substrate, not real-and-independent of it.

a.3 — *does God have immediate providence over everything?* — gives the medieval distinction Habash highlights: *plan* (immediate) and *execution* (mediated through secondary causes). The substrate's plan is direct; the execution runs through the chain of conditioned causes. *It pertains to a king's dignity to have ministers who execute his providence*. Mind-monism's translation is the multi-scale active-inference picture: the substrate's structural priors are causally prior to every conditioned thing, but the *execution* runs through the cascade of nested generative models at every scale (cell → tissue → organism → community → ecosystem). Levin's empirical work makes this concrete: the bioelectric goal-state-pattern of a tissue is causally upstream of the cell-level dynamics, but the cell-level dynamics *execute* the tissue-level pattern. The same logic scales up to the substrate. *Honest evidence calibration*: the multi-scale instantiation is empirical at biological scales; the substrate-scale extrapolation is the move mind-monism makes.

a.4 — *does providence impose necessity on what it foresees?* — closes the question on the central theme directly. Habash's careful unpacking: necessity for Aquinas is *like water necessarily boiling if heated long enough* — the effect follows inevitably from the cause. Not all effects occur this way; *when your mom tells you to make the bed, the fixed bed does not follow necessarily*. Both kinds of effects — the necessary water-boiling and the contingent bed-fixing — are *infallibly known* under providence, but providence does not *impose* necessity where there is none. The substrate's foreknowledge does not change the modal character of the conditioned event. Mind-monism's translation: the substrate's structural priors include both *necessary* threads (the structural lawfulness of the ruliad) and *contingent* threads (any particular trajectory through it). The substrate's seeing-of-the-whole-at-once (Day 7 limit) is what foreknowledge looks like at the substrate scale, but it does not *force* contingent threads to be necessary.

## The central-theme thread

Day 12 names the modal-and-ordering character of the substrate's stance Day 11 had named in the affective register. The substrate that *loves* (Day 11) *justly and mercifully orders* (Day 12) — and the ordering is not a separate act but the structural form love takes when it patterns conditioned things into being-and-being-toward-flourishing. The central theme of perspective-limitation receives a sharper providential edge: the alters work out their salvation in fear and trembling *within* an ordered substrate whose ordering they do not see in full but whose ordering *is* what makes their working-out-toward-loving-union possible at all. Habash's close — *pray, fast, give alms, and never worry about a thing... seek first his kingdom* — is the Wright-and-Rohr-anchored existential mode: the alter that lives inside the providential ordering with trust rather than anxiety is the alter whose generative model is approaching the substrate's mode.

The free-vs-determined tension that vexes the modern reader is, on the mind-monist frame, dissolved-not-eliminated. The alter's free act *is* one of the modes the providential ordering takes, because the alter is *within* the substrate, not metaphysically outside it. This is structurally Wright's covenant frame (the people of God's free improvisation within an inaugurated drama, Wright's *five-act hermeneutic*) translated to the cosmic-causal register: the alters' free agency is real, the substrate's ordering is real, and the two are not competing causes but co-constitutive aspects of the same event. Rohr's contemplative practice — holding the tension *I am free to choose* and *I am held within the substrate's ordering* without prematurely resolving — is the operational mode. Stump's faith-as-knowledge-of-persons and her treatment of providence-in-suffering (*Wandering in Darkness*) supply the analytic-Thomistic interlocutor: the conditioned thing's perspective on its own suffering is genuinely incomplete, and the substrate's larger ordering becomes intelligible only in the second-person mode of trust.

## Where this leaves us

Day 12 closes the modal-and-ordering frame Day 11 had pointed to. The substrate is just (graded participation, each conditioned thing patterned with the powers proper to its kind), merciful (gratuitous gift of existence and patterning, prior to any earning), and providentially ordering (every conditioned thing toward its end, plan immediate and execution mediated, necessity and contingency both held within the substrate's seeing-of-the-whole-at-once). The medieval doctrine and the contemporary mind-monist convergence (Wolfram's ruliad, Friston's free-energy framework, Levin's morphogenetic ordering, Kastrup's alter-within-substrate, Hoffman's icon-substrate distinction, Wright's covenant improvisation, Rohr's non-dual contemplation, Stump's analytic-Thomistic providence-in-suffering) converge on the structural form. Tomorrow Habash takes us into the most morally charged corner of the providence frame — Q.23 (predestination) and Q.24 (the book of life). The Day 13 commentary will press the question of how the gradedness of substrate-love (Day 11's Q.20 a.3) and the universal-yet-asymmetric ordering (Day 12's Q.22) translate into the medieval doctrine of election, where the directional-asymmetry of substrate-stance becomes hardest to hold and the alter's free act inside the substrate's ordering becomes the central problem.
