# Day 99 — Lusts of the World (Contemporary)

## Frame

Today's questions extend the cause-of-sin analysis into two of the three classical etiologies. Q.76 takes up *ignorance* — when, and how, can an agent be culpable for not knowing what shaped its act? Q.77 takes up *the passions* — when, and how, do appetitive signals draw an otherwise-aligned agent off-policy? Day 100 will close the etiology with *malice*. Today's question is: what are the two great pre-malice modes by which an agent's policy fails, and what does the contemporary mind-monism / active-inference frame have to say about them?

Q.77 a.5's Johannine triad — concupiscence of the flesh, concupiscence of the eyes, pride of life (1 John 2:16) — gives Aquinas the chance to deploy his most architectural account of how *self-love* deforms into specific kinds of disordered desire. The triad is exhaustive (Aquinas argues) of the levels at which appetitive disorder can take hold. This is one of the densest single articles in the whole I-II for the central theme.

## Problem

**P1**: epistemic responsibility. When is an agent culpable for ignorance? Aquinas's careful answer: when the ignorance concerns *what the agent is bound and able to know*, and when the agent's *failure to dispose itself* toward the available evidence is itself voluntary. Vincible (culpable) vs. invincible (not culpable). The contemporary problem: what is the substrate-level account of *culpable ignorance*? In an active-inference frame, the agent's *priors* are shaped by what the agent attends to over time — ignorance is *the policy-of-not-attending-to* certain evidence streams, and that policy is itself an active choice.

**P2**: capture-by-passion. When does an appetitive signal hijack policy? Aquinas distinguishes two mechanisms (Q.77 a.1-2): (a) *fixating attention* on a particular object (the smell of food drawing thought to the restaurant); (b) *diluting focus*, degrading the deliberation that would otherwise have selected the better policy. Both are precision-effects in modern vocabulary. **The contemporary problem**: under what conditions does an appetitive precision-spike override the agent's high-level prior?

**P3**: the structure of self-love. Q.77 a.4-5 is the architectural climax: all sin proceeds from inordinate self-love, and self-love deforms in three canonical modes (the Johannine triad). The contemporary problem: *why* would self-love be the universal cause of sin, and *why* would it deform along these three axes specifically?

## Resource

**Friston on active inference and curiosity** (PRS-04 in the Friston-tradition wiki; the FEP-on-epistemic-action literature). Active inference accounts for *what an agent looks at* as policy — agents allocate their inferential resources, and what they attend to becomes their prior. Culpable ignorance, in this frame, is the prior-shaping consequence of a policy-of-not-looking. Where the wider conscious-agent network *makes evidence available* to the agent (Aquinas's "what one is bound and able to know"), the agent's failure to look is a failure of curiosity-policy — and curiosity is, on the FEP frame, *constitutive* of healthy agency. **Confidence: high** on the curiosity-as-active-inference formalism; **medium-high** on the identification with Aquinas's epistemic responsibility.

**Hawkins/Friston on appetitive capture** (Hawkins-tradition wiki on HTM-cortical-prediction; Friston on precision-weighting). Aquinas's two mechanisms of passion-affecting-the-will (Q.77 a.1) — *fixation* and *dilution* — map cleanly: fixation is *precision-spike on the appetitive prior*, dilution is *precision-loss across competing priors*. Q.77 a.2's account of misapplication-of-universals-under-passion translates as *the agent's policy momentarily uses a high-precision low-level prior that overrides the high-precision high-level prior*. The agent does not stop knowing the universal; the agent stops *acting from* the universal because the local appetitive signal has higher precision in the moment. **Confidence: high** on the formal architecture; the moral-theological identification with Aquinas's "sin of weakness" is interpretive.

**The Johannine triad as three-level architecture deformation** (Levin-tradition wiki on agency-at-scale; Hawkins-tradition wiki on hierarchical predictive architecture). The triad — concupiscence of the flesh (animal-level appetites), concupiscence of the eyes (social/cognitive goods — wealth, prestige), pride of life (the *arduous* spiritual good of self-excellence) — maps onto a three-level architecture: lower-level body-bound priors, mid-level reward-bearing socio-cognitive priors, upper-level self-modeling-and-excellence priors. Each level has its own characteristic deformation when self-love mis-prioritizes from it. **Confidence: medium**. The architectural mapping is clean; the *exclusivity* of the triad (Aquinas claims these three exhaust the categories) is harder to defend on substrate-architectural grounds.

**Stump on narrative cognition** (PRS-07, PRS-08 in the Stump-tradition wiki). Q.76 a.2's claim that *the articles of faith come through hearing* yet *one can still be culpable* for not having them — because of failure to dispose oneself — pairs deeply with Stump's narrative-cognition account. Some knowledge is irreducible to propositions; it requires *encounter* — including encounter with narratives that train one's attention. Failure to *enter* such narratives is failure of epistemic disposition. **Confidence: medium-high** within analytic Thomism; the substrate-level identification (training of priors through narrative engagement) is speculative.

**Wright on the divided self under sin** (Wright wiki not yet on disk; canonical works: *Paul and the Faithfulness of God*; the Romans 7 reading). Aquinas's Q.77 a.4 — *self-love as the source of every sin* — has a Wright-compatible reading in the Pauline account of the divided self: "I do not do what I want, but the very thing I hate" (Rom 7:15). The divided self is the self whose perspective-bounded local optimization keeps overriding its higher-level commitment. Wright reads Paul's account as the *anatomy of life under sin* prior to the new-creation re-integration available in Christ. Theological evidence-class; converges directly with the central-theme account of perspective-limited individuation.

## Solution (proposed)

**Ignorance is policy-of-attention.** Aquinas's vincible/invincible distinction (Q.76 a.2-3) survives the translation completely. *Invincible* ignorance is the agent's inability-in-principle to know — the evidence is unavailable, or the cognitive apparatus to receive it is absent. *Vincible* ignorance is the agent's *active failure to dispose itself* — a curiosity-policy choice. Under active inference, this is not metaphorical: what one attends to *is* what one comes to know, and choosing not to attend is choosing not to know. The Cornelius example (Acts 10, the devout pagan given a vision) carries: the agent who was disposing itself was met with the evidence. The agent who was not disposing itself is the agent whose ignorance is *culpable* — not because ignorance per se is the failure, but because *the policy that produced it* was the failure.

**Passion-as-capture is precision-as-policy-hijack.** Aquinas's Q.77 a.1-2 gives a structurally precise account of how appetitive signals override the high-level prior: *fixation* (the local prior captures attention), *dilution* (the high-level prior loses precision relative to competitors), *bodily change* (the substrate itself loses the high-level prior's signal-to-noise capacity). All three are precision-control failures. Q.77 a.6's "passion before / passion after" asymmetry — passion *before* the act diminishes culpability (capture), passion *after* expresses it (commitment) — is then the principled difference between *the agent being moved* and *the agent moving from* its now-disordered prior.

**Self-love is perspective-bias.** This is the central-theme convergence of the day. Aquinas's Q.77 a.4 — *the inordinate love of self is the cause of every sin* — and the central theme of this project — *perspective-limitation individuates the conscious agent; loving unity is the goal* — say the same thing. The limited rational agent has a perspective. Its local optimizations are *for itself* — *amor sui* in Augustine's vocabulary. Every disordered policy proceeds from inordinate weight on the agent's own perspective at the cost of the wider whole the agent is part of. Aquinas reaches this through philosophical-theological analysis. The contemporary frame reaches it through the structural observation that *any* agent individuated by perspective-limitation will, absent the integrating prior, optimize for its perspective. The cure, in both frames, is the same: *charity* / *loving unity* / the high-precision integrative prior that couples the agent's perspective to the wider network of conscious agents.

**The Johannine triad as three-level deformation.** The triad survives translation: concupiscence of the flesh is mis-prioritization at the body-bound level; concupiscence of the eyes is mis-prioritization at the socio-cognitive level; pride of life is mis-prioritization at the self-modeling-and-excellence level. Whether *exactly* these three exhaust the levels is open — but it is striking that the medieval analysis arrives at a three-level decomposition compatible with both Levin-style multi-scale agency and Hawkins-style hierarchical predictive architecture.

**Where this reframes Aquinas**: the contemporary frame is more substrate-aware but does not contradict. **Where evidence is firm**: precision-as-policy-hijack is empirically robust; curiosity-as-active-inference is formally clean; the Pauline divided-self account converges textually with Q.77 a.4. **Where speculative**: the exclusivity of the Johannine triad as the canonical three-level deformation; the precise identification of *the* integrating prior with *charity* across all the relevant traditions.

## The central-theme thread

Today is one of the days where the central theme lands hardest. *Self-love is the source of every sin* (Q.77 a.4) and *conscious agents are individuated by the limitations of their perspective* (the central claim) are *the same statement* in different vocabularies. The individuating perspective bias *is* what Augustine called *amor sui*. The cure, in both frames, is *charity* — the integrating prior that, by re-coupling the agent's perspective to the wider whole, *changes what the agent optimizes for*. Aquinas's wager — that this re-coupling is *participation in divine charity* — is the theological commitment; the mind-monism frame is, at high confidence, agnostic-or-compatible. The *fear-and-trembling* working-out-of-salvation that this project's central theme invokes is *this very process*: the limited agent recognizing its perspective-limitation, seeking the wider whole it is part of, and slowly retraining its priors under the guidance of those who perceive more of the whole.

## Where this leaves us

Day 99 places us at the threshold of *malice*. We have walked through ignorance (Q.76) and passion (Q.77) — the two pre-malice modes by which an agent's policy can fail. We have noted that under both Aquinas's frame and the mind-monism frame, *self-love is the deepest substrate of the failure*, and that the cure is the integrating prior of charity / loving unity. Day 100 — *the cause of sin which is malice* — is the question of what happens when an agent fails *not* through ignorance or passion-capture but through deliberate choice of an evil *known as evil*. That is the morally weightiest case in the whole sin treatise, and it will require us to be careful: *deliberate choice of an evil known as evil* is, on the mind-monism frame, a particularly hard pattern to model. We will face that tomorrow.
