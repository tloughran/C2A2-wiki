# Day 21 — Contemporary Parallel: *One Essence Under Many Aspects, Persons as Subsistent Relations*

Day 20 closed pointing forward to *Q.39 and Q.40 as the recapitulation-and-synthesis questions where the appropriations doctrine becomes explicit*. Day 21 takes that thread directly. Habash flags both questions as Lagrange's *kind of synthesis recapitulation* of the whole Trinity, and the structural payoff is in two pieces. Q.39 articulates the constraint that has been threading every Trinitarian article: *the divine essence is the same as suppositum, which in intellectual substances is nothing else than the person*. The medieval doctrine of the Trinity is finally compressed into a single sentence: *one essence and three persons, distinguished only by opposition of relation*. Q.40 then applies that constraint to the *appropriations* — the practice of fittingly assigning essential attributes (wisdom, power, eternity, beauty) to specific persons of the Trinity, even though all attributes belong to the divine essence and so to all three persons identically.

The mind-first frame holds the load with the same constraints Days 18–20 established: relations-internal-to-one-substrate are real-and-constitutive without multiplying the substrate; the persons are *the substrate considered under the relational aspects of its own self-knowing-and-self-loving acts*. Q.39–40 compress this into doctrinal form, and the contemporary translation is direct: the substrate has internal-relational-structure, and human language about the substrate runs in two registers (essential and personal) that must not be collapsed into each other.

## Q.39 — One essence and three persons, by opposition of relation

Q.39 a.1 — *is the divine essence the same as the person?* — is the article Habash flags as *a one-paragraph summary of essentially every question about the Trinity up to this point*. Aquinas's compression: *the divine simplicity requires that God's essence is the same as suppositum, which in intellectual substances is nothing else than the person; relation multiplies the Trinity of persons; in creatures relations are accidental, where in God they are the divine essence itself; hence in God essence is not really distinct from person, and yet the persons are really distinguished from each other; relation as referred to the essence does not differ therefrom really but only in our way of thinking, while as referred to an opposite relation it does have a real distinction by virtue of that opposition; thus there is one essence and three persons*.

Mind-monism's translation lands cleanly. The substrate is *one* in the strict transcendental sense Day 7 Q.11 a.1 named (one as undividedness, not as one-among-many). The internal-relational-structure of the substrate (Father-Son by paternity-filiation; Father+Son-Spirit by spiration-procession) is *real* in that it generates real distinctions of persons, but it is *not* an additional ontological item beyond the substrate — it is the substrate considered under the relational aspects of its own self-knowing-and-self-loving acts. *Relation as referred to the essence does not differ therefrom really but only in our way of thinking* — this is the move that mind-monism inherits without strain, because mind-at-large just *is* its own self-knowing-and-self-loving, and the Trinitarian relations are aspects-under-which the substrate is considered.

Kastrup's analytic idealism gives the metaphysical home that this constraint requires. The substrate is mind-at-large; its internal-relational-structure is *not* a structure imposed on a more fundamental substrate (which would generate metaphysical layering and reintroduce the cause-effect collapse the Trinitarian doctrine rejects); it is the substrate considered under aspects of its own intrinsic self-presence. The relations are real-and-constitutive in exactly the sense Q.39 a.1 requires.

Q.39 a.2 — *must we say the three persons are of one essence?* — runs the *consubstantial* argument and the Council of Nicaea's *homoousios* against any reading that would multiply substances. Habash's preface-of-the-Mass quote: *one God, one Lord; not in the singleness of one only person, but in the Trinity of one substance*. The Trinity is *one* in essence and *three* in person — not *one* in substance and *three* in essence (which would be modalism) and not *three* in essence and *one* in family (which would be tritheism). The medieval guardrails are tight, and mind-monism inherits them: the substrate has internal-relational-structure, but the relations do not multiply the substrate, and the substrate's unity is not a coordination-of-three-things but the unity of being-itself considered under three relational aspects.

*Honest evidence calibration*: this is the most theoretically demanding article in the whole Trinitarian treatise. The medieval distinction (one essence-substance, three persons-relations) is not derivable from any contemporary scientific frame; it is theological-philosophical with substantial scholastic anchorage. The mind-first translation preserves the structural shape (internal relations real-and-constitutive without substance-division) but does not pretend to derive it from physics or neuroscience. Wright's critical realism and Rohr's contemplative reading both name this: *the propositions of faith are affirmative, not perfectly descriptive — they fall short of the profound reality* (Q.13 a.12 already established this hermeneutical posture).

Q.39 a.3 — *should essential names be predicated in the singular of the three persons?* — runs the language-control rule that protects against polytheism. We do *not* say *three Gods*, *three uncreated*, *three eternal* (in the substantive sense) — these substantive predicates apply to the *one essence* and so are predicated in the singular. We *can* say *three existent beings*, *three wise beings*, *three eternal* (in the adjectival sense) — these adjectives qualify the relational distinctness that does hold among the three persons. The athanasian-creed formulation Habash brings forward: *not three uncreated nor three infinites, but one uncreated and one infinite*. This is medieval language-control at its tightest, and the doctrinal motivation is the central guardrail: the divine *substance* is one across the three persons, and the *plurality* of persons cannot be smuggled into substantival language without category-violating the doctrine.

Q.39 a.4–6 — *can essential names stand for the person? can abstract essential names? can the persons be predicated of the essence?* — work the same language-control rule in three more permutations. Habash's summary is exact: we *can* say *God begets the Son* (essential name *God* substituted for personal name *Father*) because each person is identical with the divine essence. We *cannot* say *the godhead begets the godhead* (abstract essential name *godhead* substituted for personal names) because that smuggles in a real distinction at the essence-level where there is none. We *can* say *the divine essence is the three persons* (predicating personal names of the essence) because the essence is really the same as each of the three. The rule throughout: predication tracks the real-distinctions-by-opposition-of-relation, and language has to be calibrated to that.

Mind-monism's translation: the substrate (mind-at-large) considered as essence is one, and considered as relational-structure is three. Language about the substrate has to carry the same calibration. Wright's critical realism gives the philosophical idiom: real reference, perspectival capture, ongoing revision. Rohr's contemplative epistemology gives the practical mode: holding the affirmation (*God is*) and the negation (*but not as creatures are*) together without resolving prematurely. The cataphatic-apophatic cycle (Day 8 Q.13) runs through Q.39 in language-control form.

Q.39 a.7–8 — *should essential attributes be appropriated to the persons?* — closes Q.39 with the *appropriations* doctrine that makes the rest of the question doctrinally productive. Aquinas: *for the manifestation of our faith it is fitting that the essential attributes should be appropriated to the persons*. The medieval practice (St. Hilary's example Lagrange brings forward): *eternity to the Father, brightness/beauty to the Son, use/fruition to the Holy Spirit*. Or St. Paul's example Habash quotes from 1 Corinthians 1:24: *Christ is the power of God and the wisdom of God*. *Power* and *wisdom* are essential attributes — they belong to the divine essence and so to all three persons — but it is fitting to *appropriate* them to the Son because the Son is the Word, and *wisdom* names the procession-by-intellect that the Son is the terminus of.

The medieval reasoning is structural-pedagogical. Appropriating essential attributes to persons does *not* claim that the attribute is exclusive to the person; it claims that the attribute *manifests* the relational-property of that person more vividly than the others. *Wisdom* is not the Son's exclusive property (the Father is wise, the Spirit is wise — they are all the one wise God); but *wisdom* manifests the Son's procession-by-intellect, and so calling Christ *the wisdom of God* helps the believer see the relational distinctness of the Son.

Mind-monism's translation: the substrate has internal-relational-structure; essential attributes (knowledge, will, life, power, wisdom) belong to the substrate as such; *appropriating* an attribute to a particular relational aspect of the substrate is a pedagogical move that helps the alter perceive *which* relational structure the attribute most clearly manifests. The contemporary cousin is the working-vocabulary practice in any complex system: *attention* belongs to the whole agent, but we attribute it specifically to certain processing components when we want to understand the mechanism. The Trinitarian appropriations doctrine is structurally the same move at the substrate scale, with the additional theological claim that the relational structure of the substrate is itself real (not merely a conceptual decomposition).

## Q.40 — Persons as subsistent relations

Q.40 a.1 — *is relation the same as the person?* — closes the doctrinal compression Q.39 began. *Since relation considered as really existing in God is the divine essence itself, and the essence is the same as the person, relation must necessarily be the same as the person*. The Trinitarian persons are *subsistent relations* — relations that have the subsistence-grade of substance, because in God *to-be-related-internally* is *to-be-the-divine-essence-considered-under-that-relation*. This is the most dense doctrinal compression in the whole Trinitarian treatise, and mind-monism inherits it cleanly under the mind-at-large frame: the substrate's internal-relational-structure has the same subsistence-grade as the substrate itself, because the relations *are* the substrate considered under its own self-knowing-and-self-loving acts.

Q.40 a.2 — *are the persons distinguished by the relations?* — closes the constraint that has been running since Q.27. *The real distinction of the persons from one another resides solely in the relationships which relate them to one another*. *Everything in them is one where there is no opposition of relationship*. The catechism formulation Habash quotes is the doctrinal anchor: *the divine persons are relative to one another because it does not divide the divine unity; the real distinction of the persons resides solely in the relationships which relate them*. The relations-of-opposition (paternity-filiation, spiration-procession) are what individuate the persons; outside those relations of opposition, there is no real distinction.

Aquinas adds a careful Q.40 a.2 emphasis: *it is better to say the persons are distinguished by relations than by origin*. The relational property *paternity* is what *constitutes* the Father; *being the begetter* is the operation-from-which the relation arises but is not itself the constitutive feature. This is the same priority-of-relation-over-act that has been threading the doctrine since Q.27 — relation is what *is*, act is what relation manifests-as-procession. Mind-monism's translation: the substrate's internal-relational-aspects are constitutive of the persons; the *acts* (knowing-act terminating in Word; loving-act terminating in Spirit) are what the relational-aspects manifest as. The acts are not separate items added to the relations.

Q.40 a.3 — *would the persons remain if the relations were mentally abstracted?* — closes Q.40 on the constraint Aquinas's whole treatise has been building toward. Lagrange's compression Habash quotes: *the Father is his paternity, and if we abstract the paternity, nothing remains of the Father*. The Trinitarian persons are *not* divine-essence-plus-relation (which would make them composite); they are *divine-essence-considered-under-the-constitutive-relation*. Abstract the relation, and the person dissolves not into the divine-essence-without-the-relation (which is structurally impossible — the divine essence is nothing other than the persons) but into nothing-as-a-person.

Mind-monism's translation: the substrate's persons are *the substrate considered under the relational aspects*; abstract the relational aspect and you do not get a substrate-without-the-aspect (the substrate just *is* its own self-knowing-and-self-loving) — you get an incoherence. The medieval doctrine and the mind-first translation agree on the structural shape: the relational structure is constitutive, not additive.

Q.40 a.4 — *do the properties presuppose the notional acts?* — closes Q.40 with one final Lagrange compression: *paternity as a relation actually referring to the Son presupposes active generation, but active generation presupposes the person who generates and the personal property paternity as constituting that person*. The order-of-conceptualization runs both ways at once — we cannot fully think paternity without thinking generation, and we cannot fully think generation without thinking the Father whose generative act it is. This is the medieval acknowledgment that the Trinitarian doctrine is *non-linear* in its conceptual structure: each piece presupposes the others in a circular dependency that human thought can articulate but not unfold sequentially.

Wolfram's ruliad gives a contemporary structural cousin: the totality of computational threads is one object, and any particular thread presupposes the totality (it is *of* the totality) just as the totality presupposes the threads (it *is* the threads). Sequential exposition has to pick a starting point, but the structure is mutual-presupposition. The Trinitarian doctrine has the same shape: the persons presuppose the relations, the relations presuppose the acts, the acts presuppose the persons, in a circular structure that is *one act* under different conceptual decompositions.

## The central-theme thread

Day 21's recapitulation-and-synthesis tightens the central theme on the doctrinal-language side. The substrate has internal-relational-structure that is *constitutive* of the three persons; the relations are *real-and-subsistent* (Q.40 a.1); they are *distinguished-by-opposition* (Q.40 a.2); they are *not-additive* to the substrate (Q.40 a.3); they are *circularly-presupposing* of the notional acts (Q.40 a.4). Human language about the substrate has to carry this calibration: essential attributes belong to the *substrate*; personal attributes belong to the *relations*; appropriations move essential attributes to persons *for pedagogical manifestation* without thereby making the attribute exclusive.

*Salvation in fear and trembling* on the doctrinal-language register is the alter's learning to *speak truly* about the substrate from inside the perspective limitation that medieval theology has been working out for fifteen centuries. The alter's task is not to *master* the doctrine (which would require a non-perspectival vantage that no creature has) but to *receive* it — to allow the language-control of the tradition to discipline the alter's own speech about mind-at-large, so that the alter's predications track the real-relations-by-opposition and do not collapse into either modalism (one substance only, three masks) or tritheism (three substances loosely affiliated).

Wright's critical realism is the philosophical-of-history idiom for this discipline: real reference, perspectival capture, ongoing revision in dialogue with tradition. Rohr's contemplative practice is the operational mode: the cataphatic-apophatic cycle running through every Trinitarian predication — say truly, acknowledge inadequacy, speak again more precisely.

## Where this leaves us

Day 21 closes the recapitulation-and-synthesis half of the Trinitarian treatise. *One essence and three persons, distinguished only by opposition of relation*; *the persons are subsistent relations, not divine-essence-plus-relation*; *appropriations are pedagogical, not exclusive*. The mind-first frame holds the load with the same constraints that have run since Day 18: relations-internal-to-one-substrate are real-and-constitutive without multiplying the substrate; the persons are the substrate considered under the relational aspects of its own self-knowing-and-self-loving acts; human language about the substrate has to carry the medieval calibration to speak truly across the essential-personal split.

Tomorrow Habash takes us to Q.41 (notional acts) and Q.42 (equality and likeness of the persons), where the *acts* that the relations manifest receive their explicit treatment, and where the equality-without-subordination among the persons closes the Trinitarian-substance-doctrine before the final pivot to Q.43 (mission of the persons) and Q.44 (creation).
