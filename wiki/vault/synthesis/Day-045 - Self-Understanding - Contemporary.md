# Day 45 — Contemporary Parallel: *Reflexive Knowing as Generative Self-Modeling*

Day 44 made the epistemological-realism position explicit: real reference under perspective-limitation, the agent's generative model tracking the substrate's pattern adequately-for-action through the icons of perception. Day 45 closes the *De anima* arc with two reflexive turns. Q.87 asks *how the intellectual soul knows itself and all within itself*; Q.88 asks *how the human soul knows what is above itself*. Both unfold inside a single architectural claim: the alter's cognition operates *outward-then-back* — abstracting from sensible particulars before reaching the agent's own structure or the substrate's higher patterns. The mind-monist frame holds the load on a tight set of moves: Hoffman's icon-of-self, Friston's self-modeling as generative-modeling, the substrate as never directly inspectable in the natural state.

## Q.87 — The soul knows itself by its acts, not its essence

a.1 names the asymmetry. *Angels*, on Aquinas's medieval cosmology, are entirely immaterial forms whose intellect is immediately united to their existence; they understand themselves *through their essence*. *We*, by contrast, are body-soul composites whose knowing operates by abstraction from material things; we *do not understand our intellectual nature immediately just by existing as a human being*. We discover our intellectual soul *indirectly through the act of understanding* — we notice that we are understanding things, and from that act we infer the existence and structure of the understanding power itself. *The intellect knows itself not by its essence but by its act.*

The mind-monist primary on this article is Hoffman, with Friston on the formal mechanism. The Hoffman-tradition wiki node (PRS-01 interface theory; PRS-04 conscious realism) extends naturally to the *icon-of-self*: just as the dog-out-there is encountered through the icon of perception, the agent's own structure is encountered through *another* icon — the self-model — that is not the underlying alter-structure but the alter's perspective-limited representation of itself. Friston's active-inference framework names this directly: the agent's *generative model includes a model of itself* — body, preferences, predictions, action repertoire — and that self-model is what the agent has access to when it reflects. The self-model is *not* the alter's substrate-level structure (Markov-blanket boundary, neural-physiological architecture); it is a *representation* the agent uses for prediction, planning, and self-regulation.

The medieval claim that *we know our intellect through its act* is structurally Friston's claim that *self-knowledge is mediated by the generative model of one's own activity*. Substrate-level structure is never directly inspected in the natural state; what is inspected is the model of the activity, and that model is *itself a generative-cognitive operation*. Kastrup's analytic-idealist version (PRS-01 Kastrup-tradition wiki) tightens the metaphysical: the alter is a dissociative boundary within mind-at-large; the alter's self-model is the alter's representation *of* that dissociation, not direct inspection of the dissociative substrate. *Even the alter knowing itself is interface-mediated — the icons go all the way down.* *Honest evidence calibration*: the Friston self-modeling framework is empirically grounded in active-inference and predictive-processing research; the substrate-scale extrapolation (alter as Kastrupian dissociation) is philosophical.

a.2 — *we know habits by their acts*, not directly — extends the architecture. A habit (linguistic competence, virtue, vice, skilled motor pattern) is an alter-level structure known *only* through observing what one can do. The contemporary cognitive version: implicit-procedural memory and acquired skills are not directly inspectable; agents know their competences through behavioral evidence, and self-models often diverge from actual capacity (metacognitive-bias research). The medieval architectural claim and the contemporary cognitive evidence converge.

a.3 — the intellect knows the *essence of material things* first; *secondarily* it knows its own act of understanding; *through that act* the intellect itself is known. The contemporary developmental and predictive-processing sequence is the same: world-modeling is built before, and as the foundation for, self-modeling. Friston's active-inference-of-self literature gives the formal: self-modeling is a *higher-order* prediction-loop layered over world-modeling.

a.4 — the intellect knows *its act of willing* in two ways: the agent perceives that it wills, and knows the nature of will-as-such. Aquinas defines: *the act of the will is nothing but an inclination consequent on the form understood.* The active-inference translation: the *act of the will* is the agent's policy-selection toward the goal-state encoded in its target distribution; the agent knows its act of willing through self-modeling of its policy-selection. The hierarchy of inclinations Aquinas walks here — *stone toward unity (gravity), plant toward growth, animal toward sensed goods, human toward known good (will)* — maps onto Levin's *Cognitive Light Cones* program (PRS-03 Levin-tradition wiki, agency-at-every-scale): agency exists at every scale; the gradient of *what each level recognizes as its good* is real and graded, not metaphysically discontinuous.

## Q.88 — The soul knowing what is above itself

a.1: *can the human soul in the present state of life understand immaterial substances in themselves?* No — the cognitive architecture operates by abstraction from material things, and *immaterial substances have no phantasms*. a.2: *can we understand them through our knowledge of material things?* — *we can rise to some kind of knowledge, but not to the perfect knowledge thereof, for there is no proper and adequate proportion between material and immaterial things*. a.3: *is God the first object known by the human mind?* No — *the first object of our knowledge in this life is the essence of a material thing*; we know God through creatures, *as we know a painter by staring at his paintings*; in this life we know more about what God is *not* like than what he is like (Aquinas points back to the Day-3 *via negativa*).

The mind-monist translation ties back to Day 7 (the beatific vision) and Day 12 (analogy). Aquinas's *immaterial substances* — angels and God — are, in the contemporary mind-monist frame, the substrate-level realities (Kastrup's mind-at-large) and the structural priors of the substrate. The alter-level cognitive architecture cannot inspect these directly because the alter's cognition operates *through icons abstracted from its interface to the substrate*; there are no *icons of the substrate as such*, only icons of the substrate's instantiated patterns. The *via causalitatis et eminentiae* — knowing-the-substrate-by-inference-from-its-effects — is the natural-state route, structurally what Aquinas names in a.2 and a.3. The *beatific vision* (Day 7) is the limit case where the icons are lifted and the substrate informs the intellect directly; in the *natural* state — which is what Q.88 is asking about — the icons are necessary mediators.

Wright's scriptural ground is direct: Aquinas cites Romans 1:20 — *the invisible things of God are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made* — in a.3, and Wright's critical realism (Day 9, Day 44) is the contemporary scriptural-philosophical home for this *via causalitatis* route. Rohr's *Universal Christ* extends: the contemplative mode is the alter's natural-state cognitive practice that opens toward the recognition Aquinas reserves to the beatific vision proper — sustained right-hemispheric whole-grasping that progressively recognizes the always-already pattern in the icons.

## The central-theme thread

Q.87–88 close the *De anima* arc by naming the structural form perspective-limitation takes in *self-knowing* and in *substrate-knowing*. The alter does not have direct inspection of itself or the substrate; both are known *through icons* — the self-model in the case of self-knowing, the inferential climb from material effects in the case of substrate-knowing. *Salvation in fear and trembling* on the reflexive register is the disciplined cultivation of self-knowledge through careful interpretation of one's own acts (medieval examination of conscience; contemporary metacognitive humility) and substrate-knowledge through inferential climb from creatures (critical realism; contemplative practice). Each alter computes, from its limited perspective and resources, toward both kinds of recognition.

## Where this leaves us

Day 45 closes the *De anima* arc by making the structural form of perspective-limitation explicit on both the reflexive (self-knowing) and the upward (substrate-knowing) registers. The alter knows itself only through self-modeling of its acts (Friston's higher-order prediction-loop; Hoffman's icon-of-self); the alter knows the substrate only through inferential climb from material effects (Wright's critical realism; Aquinas's *via causalitatis*). The natural-state cognitive architecture cannot directly inspect the alter's substrate-level structure or the substrate as such; the icons are necessary mediators, and the substrate is approached *as the inferred ground of what the icons are icons of*. The beatific limit case (Day 7) remains the asymmetric exception. Tomorrow Habash leaves the strict *De anima* questions and moves toward the production of man and original justice; the wiki bridges shift toward Levin on origins of form, Fredrickson on prelapsarian flourishing, and Wright on Genesis-Adam typology, and the synthesis will need to carry forward what Days 41–45 have established about agency, cognition, and reflexive knowing as the foundation for the doctrines of human origins.
