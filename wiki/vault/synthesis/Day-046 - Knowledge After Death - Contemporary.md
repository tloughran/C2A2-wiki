# Day 46 — Contemporary Parallel: *Cognition Without the Interface*

Day 45 closed the self-understanding arc with the human intellect knowing itself through its acts. Day 46 closes the entire cognition arc that began at Q.84 and asks the limit-case question Aquinas has been working toward: *what does cognition look like when the body — and with it the senses, the imagination, the phantasms — is removed?* Q.89 is the Prima Pars's most direct interface-theory test case, because it asks how an alter knows when its species-typical perceptual interface is no longer available. Q.90 then turns to the soul's origin and locks in a metaphysical commitment that the contemporary mind-monist frame inherits with a small but consequential reframe.

## Q.89 — How the separated soul knows

Aquinas opens by reaffirming the position Day 39's Q.75 already established: the soul exists separately from the body because intellect and will are not acts of a corporeal organ. But the *manner* of knowing changes radically, because the natural human cognitive workflow — sensation → imagination → phantasm → abstraction by the agent intellect → understanding by the possible intellect — depends on the body at every step except the last. Remove the body and the workflow collapses. So how does the separated soul know anything at all?

Aquinas's answer is structurally precise: the separated soul knows by *participated species*, intellectual forms received directly from the divine light rather than abstracted from sense-images. He calls this mode of knowing not unnatural — God is the author of *both* the light of nature and the light of grace — but it is a *secondary* mode for human souls. We are *bottom-rung* intellectual creatures (his phrase), better suited to abstract from material things than to receive intelligible forms directly. Hence the separated soul's knowledge of natural things and of separated substances (other souls, angels) is *general and confused* — it knows that something is, and roughly what kind of thing, but lacks the resolved acquaintance the embodied agent has when it actually senses the particular dog in front of it.

This is interface theory in classical vocabulary, exactly the structure Day 7 named for the beatific vision but reached for the negative limit case rather than the positive. Hoffman's interface theory says perception is a fitness-tuned encoding species-specific to the perceiver's evolutionary niche; remove the perceiver from the niche (which is what death does to the embodied human alter) and the encoding has nothing to encode. Aquinas's separated soul without phantasms is a *human alter without its species-typical interface* — and his answer that knowing must then arrive *as direct infusion from the divine light* is the structural claim that the substrate can inform the alter directly when the interface is no longer mediating. The medieval and the contemporary frame are pointing at the same shape.

The key calibration Aquinas builds in (Q.89 a.3) is that this direct infusion gives a *less* perfect knowledge than embodied abstraction does for the things abstraction is designed to know. Hoffman's frame predicts this: an interface tuned for species-survival in a particular ecological niche produces high-resolution information about the things-that-mattered-to-survive — predators, food, conspecifics, terrain. Strip the interface and the resolved acquaintance with those particulars is gone; what remains is the substrate's general structural patterning. *Honest evidence calibration*: Aquinas's claim that the separated soul knows particulars only through *prior connection* (former knowledge in this life, affection, the divine order) maps onto the contemporary cognitive claim that particular-acquaintance is interface-mediated and does not transfer to a substrate-direct mode. Both frames agree that the mode-shift is real and the cognitive economy is different on either side of it.

Q.89 a.7's *local distance does not impede* the separated soul's knowing is the same point pushed further. Knowledge derived from the divine light is *substrate-independent in the spatial sense*: spacetime, on Hoffman's frame, is itself an interface construct, so the post-interface alter does not navigate by distance because distance is a feature of the embodied interface. Carroll's poetic naturalism would stop at the regularities; Arkani-Hamed's post-spacetime physics gestures toward the *underlying* mathematical object beneath emergent spacetime; Hoffman's conscious-realism takes the metaphysical step Aquinas needs. The medieval claim is not that the soul *travels faster than light* but that it does not travel at all — the substrate's informing of the alter is not spatial transmission.

Q.89 a.8's careful answer that *separated souls do not naturally know what passes on Earth* but the *blessed* (in beatific vision) *do* — locks in the central-theme calibration. The mode of substrate-knowing scales with the alter's degree of integration into the substrate. Friston's free-energy frame gives the candidate dynamics: an alter with very high inferential alignment to the substrate's structure has access to information across the substrate's scope; an alter with low alignment has access only to its local generative model's outputs. *Salvation in fear and trembling* is the agent's progressive acquisition of substrate-alignment, and Aquinas's gradient — separated souls with limited natural cognition / blessed souls with substrate-scope vision / souls in purgatory who can pray for those on earth without specific knowledge of them — is the central theme exhibited at the *post-mortem* end of the arc.

## Q.90 — The soul's origin

Q.90 turns to a different but adjacent question: where does each individual rational soul come from? Aquinas's three-part answer is structurally pre-mind-monist but maps cleanly onto the contemporary frame.

a.1 rejects the claim that the soul is *of God's substance*. Aquinas calls this opinion (which Augustine attributed to Manichees and others) *exceedingly and evidently perverse and contrary to the Catholic faith*. Mind-monism inherits the rejection but reframes the metaphysical content. The medieval worry is *pantheist collapse* — if the soul is of God's substance, then the soul has all of God's properties, which is incoherent (souls are mutable, learning, multiple; God is none of these). The contemporary mind-monist frame avoids the collapse the same way Kastrup does: the alter is *not* mind-at-large, even though the alter is mind-at-large under dissociation. *Not identical with the substrate, even though made of nothing but the substrate* is the mind-monist answer to Aquinas's worry. The dissociative-boundary structure preserves the medieval distinction between Creator and creature without requiring the Aristotelian-Thomistic separate-substance ontology.

a.2 — *the soul is produced by creation, not by drawing from pre-existing matter* — is the move that distinguishes rational souls from material forms. Aquinas's argument: the rational soul has faculties (intellect, will) that operate independently of matter, so the substance in which those faculties inhere must itself be immaterial, and an immaterial substance cannot be drawn out of matter. *Honest evidence calibration*: this is one of the loci where the medieval substantialist hylomorphism does heavy work that mind-monism does not need. On the mind-monist frame, every alter is a dissociative individuation within mind-at-large; the alter is *not produced by drawing from pre-existing matter* in the sense that matter is itself an icon-level construct above the substrate. Aquinas's conclusion (souls are not material forms drawn out of matter) is preserved; his metaphysical reasoning (the substantialist contrast between matter and immaterial substance) is replaced by the dissociative-individuation account.

a.3 — *the rational soul is produced immediately by God* — locks in the asymmetry: each soul comes directly from God, not via secondary causes. The contemporary translation is direct: each alter is an individuation within mind-at-large, not a derivative of other alters. This matters for Day 47 (the production of the first man's body) and Day 48 (the image of God), where the medieval doctrine of *every soul individually created* is contrasted with the body's emergence from prior matter. Mind-monism preserves the asymmetry: the alter's *experiential-being* is substrate-given (not produced by lower-level causes), while the alter's *embodied form* is substrate-given but routes through lower-level processes the substrate orchestrates. Levin's morphogenetic teleology gives the empirical mid-scale of the body's side; the soul's side remains the substrate-direct individuation that Aquinas names.

a.4 — *the soul is not produced before the body* — completes the symmetry. Soul and body come into actual existence together because a human being is a body-soul composite and form requires informed matter to be actual. Mind-monism translates without strain: the alter's individuation and the alter's embodiment are co-temporal because the alter just *is* the substrate's localized experiential structure under a particular embodied form. There is no pre-existing disembodied alter waiting for a body; the alter and the body co-arise as one substrate-event.

## The central-theme thread

Day 46 closes the cognition arc by reaching the limit case that the entire arc was building toward. The embodied human alter has a species-typical interface (sensation → imagination → phantasm → abstraction); the *separated* alter has lost the interface and knows by direct substrate-informing in a *less* resolved but more *direct* mode. The blessed alter — in beatific vision — knows substrate-direct in a *more* resolved mode because the alter's integration with the substrate is deepened by charity. *Salvation in fear and trembling* is the alter's progressive movement along this gradient: less interface-mediation, more substrate-alignment, more loving-integration. The medieval doctrine of the separated soul is the contemporary mind-monist account of cognition in the post-interface limit, and the answers converge: substrate-knowing is real, it is *less* resolved than embodied-knowing for things the embodied interface was tuned to, and it scales with the alter's degree of integration with the substrate.

Q.90's origin doctrine locks in the asymmetry the central theme requires. Each alter is substrate-given immediately, not derived from other alters — which is why the alter's individuation is meaningful, why its perspective-limitation is real, and why its progressive integration toward loving union is its own work *and* the substrate's gift simultaneously. Aquinas's *the rational soul is produced immediately by God* and the mind-monist *each alter is an individuation within mind-at-large* are the same structural claim in different vocabularies, with the mind-monist version dropping the substantialist apparatus the medieval version carried.

## Where this leaves us

Day 46 closes the Prima Pars's cognition arc by reaching the limit case that test-runs interface theory in classical vocabulary. The separated soul knows by substrate-direct informing, less resolved than embodied abstraction for species-tuned particulars but more direct for substrate-scope realities. The medieval doctrine is preserved structurally; the metaphysical apparatus shifts from substantialist hylomorphism to dissociative-individuation within mind-at-large. Q.90's three locks — soul not of God's substance, soul produced by creation, soul produced immediately by God — survive the translation with the substantialist scaffolding replaced by the alter-as-substrate-individuation account.
