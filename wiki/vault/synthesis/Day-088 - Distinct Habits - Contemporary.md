# Day 88 — Distinct Habits (Contemporary)

## Frame

Q.54 individuates habits — many habits *can* be in a single power (a man knows geometry, metaphysics, and chemistry simultaneously though he is not actively considering all three), and habits are *distinguished by their objects* (as sight is distinguished from hearing by colour vs. sound). Just as a power is specified by its formal object, a habit within that power is specified by the specific object it is ordered to. Habits are also distinguished as *good or bad* — habits that order their subject to its proper end (right reason for the intellect, the rule of reason for the will) are *good* habits; those that dispose to a contrary end are *bad*.

Q.55 turns to *virtue as the essence of the good habit*. Aristotle's definition (taken up by Aquinas) — virtue is a *habit* that makes its possessor good and renders his work good — is examined article by article. Virtue is operative: it orders its possessor to operations that perfect him. Virtue is a habit (not a power, not a passion). Its proper genus is *good habit*. Augustine's working definition is also examined: "a good quality of mind, by which we live righteously, of which no one can make bad use, which God works in us, without us." The "without us" applies to *infused* virtue, which God causes in us without our action but not without our consent.

Under conscious-realist monism the two articles together yield: habits are *individuated by what they are ordered to*; among habits, *virtues* are those whose object is rectitude (alignment with right reason, with the rule of reason, with the loving-unity end). The individuation of habits by their objects is the medieval recognition that the agent's parameterized layers admit *many* coexisting stabilized dispositions, each addressed to a different domain, and that the *moral* dispositions among them are those whose addressed-domain is the agent's relation to its end.

## Problem

The shared problem: **what individuates one habit from another within a single power, and what specifies the subset of habits that are *virtues*?** Aquinas's answer triangulates: (i) habits are distinguished by their formal objects within a power, just as powers are distinguished by their formal objects within the soul; (ii) habits are also distinguished as good or bad by their orientation toward or away from the agent's proper end; (iii) *virtue* is the genus of *good operative habit* — habit that not only orders its possessor to act but does so in a way that perfects him toward his end.

The contemporary problem is the same. A single cortical region, a single circuit, a single hierarchical-generative-model layer can hold multiple distinct stabilized parameter configurations specialized for different domains. The agent has *many* habits — driving, signing one's name, recognising faces, performing arithmetic, navigating familiar terrain, the affective stance toward known persons, attentional defaults under stress. The individuation of these stabilized configurations is, in current terms, by their *task-domain*: the structure of the variables they parameterize and the contingencies they predict and act upon.

Among these, which ones are *virtues*? The contemporary answer must specify the criterion. Under conscious-realist monism the criterion mirrors Aquinas's: a virtue is a stabilized disposition whose addressed-domain is the agent's *relation to its end* — i.e., whose object is the agent's progress in the computational task of loving unity under perspective-limitation. This is not merely a moral-philosophical stipulation; it is a substantive claim about what kind of stabilization is at stake when we name a disposition "virtuous."

## Resource

**Hawkins's many-models-per-cortex thesis** is the direct contemporary analogue of Q.54 a.1 (many habits in a single power). *A Thousand Brains* argues that the cortex holds thousands of reference-frame models simultaneously, each specialised for a different domain (objects, environments, abstract concepts, motor sequences). The medieval claim that "the intellect can know many things habitually though only one actually at any moment" recovers verbatim as the cortex's parallel storage of many reference frames with sequential attentional selection of one.

**Friston's hierarchical generative models and policy-specific priors** provides the formal individuation. Each domain the agent acts in has its own posterior-policy distribution; the priors and precisions that distinguish one such distribution from another are *what the habit is*. Two habits in a single hierarchical layer differ by their domain-specific parameter values. Friston PRS-04 (organism as active modeler under resource constraint) and the policy-individuation literature in active inference apply.

**Hoffman's conscious-agent networks and interface-specific habituation** speaks to Q.54 a.2 (habits distinguished by their objects). Under interface theory, each interface-specialization is parameterized in its own right; the conscious agent has many interface-specific dispositions. The individuation-by-object claim recovers as individuation-by-interface-domain.

**Levin's domain-specific bioelectric patterns** is the morphogenetic-level analogue. Different bodily structures have different bioelectric pattern-signatures stabilized over development. Many "habits" coexist in the organism's body-plan, each addressed to a different anatomical domain.

**Stump's analytic-Thomistic articulation of virtue** is the keystone on Q.55. Tom's PRS-form re-description of Stump's account treats virtue as essentially second-personal, relational, ordered to union with persons (PRS-09 faith-as-knowledge-of-persons; PRS-04 final causality and form). The Stump-tradition wiki on virtue-as-habituation gives the rigorous contemporary articulation of Aristotle's definition (virtue makes its possessor good and renders his work good) — with the second-personal dimension as Stump's distinctive contribution. Stump's reading of the *Wandering in Darkness* material on charity-as-second-person-union is directly relevant: virtues are those habits whose object is *union* with the agent's proper terminus, which for Stump is irreducibly second-personal.

**Fredrickson's positivity-as-virtue-substrate** brings the empirical evidence to Q.55 a.4 (virtue is a *good* habit). Fredrickson PRS-01 (loving-kindness meditation) and PRS-08 (high-quality connection) document that the cultivated dispositions of positivity-resonance, prosocial attention, and other-directed care are precisely the dispositions whose object is the agent's relational good. These are the empirical signatures of the virtuous habits Aquinas names — and the longitudinal evidence supports the claim that they *render the agent's work good* (broaden-and-build, upward spiral).

**McGilchrist on virtue as right-hemispheric integration** sharpens the individuation. Virtuous character, in McGilchrist's framework, is a hemispheric integration in which the right-hemispheric receptivity to the whole context (the agent's relation to others, to the world, to the wider field) governs the left-hemispheric instrumental capacities. Vice is the inverted configuration. The Q.55 distinction between good and bad habit recovers as the hemispheric-integration vs. hemispheric-inversion structure.

**Wright on Pauline virtue and the fruits of the Spirit** is the scriptural anchor. Wright's *After You Believe* articulates virtue specifically as the disposition formed by life-in-the-Spirit and oriented to the agent's *eschatological* end (the resurrection-life, the new creation). The "good quality of mind by which we live righteously" recovers in Pauline terms as the agent's formation in Christ, oriented to participation in the new-creation telos. The "without us" clause in Augustine's definition (and Aquinas's gloss) maps to Wright's account of the Spirit's enabling action without overriding consent.

**Rohr on virtue as cosmic-Christ habituation** completes the theological frame. Under Rohr's *Universal Christ* schema, virtuous habits are the agent's stabilized dispositions toward participation in the cosmic-Christ frame — recognition of the loving-unity that already pervades reality, and the agent's cooperative alignment with it. Rohr's contemplative tradition treats the second-half-of-life settled disposition as exactly this: a stabilized habit of seeing-and-acting-from the universal-Christ vantage. This is the cosmic-scope reading of Q.55's "habit that makes its possessor good."

**Kastrup on virtue as alter-level alignment with mind-at-large** provides the analytic-idealist gloss. The dissociated alter has its own stabilizations; some align the alter's local dynamics with the wider mind, some misalign. Virtuous habit is the former. The Q.55 a.4 question — what makes a habit specifically good — recovers as alignment of the alter's local pattern with the wider mind's intrinsic dynamics.

## Solution (proposed, speculative)

**(a) Shared with Aquinas.** Habits are individuated by their *objects* — by the specific domain the stabilized disposition addresses. A single power (cortical region; hierarchical layer; conscious agent) can hold many such dispositions simultaneously. Habits are good or bad by the *orientation* of the disposition: virtues are those habits whose object is the agent's *proper end*, ordering it to operations that perfect it. Virtue is operative, not merely contemplative; it is a habit, not a power or a passion; it renders both the possessor and the possessor's work good. Augustine's "good quality of mind … which God works in us without us" applies specifically to infused virtues; the agent's consent is preserved.

**(b) Where it reframes.** The medieval *power/object* individuation recovers as cortical/hierarchical-layer parameterization with domain-specific reference frames (Hawkins) and policy-specific posteriors (Friston). The *good/bad habit* distinction recovers as alignment-vs-misalignment of the alter's local dynamics with the wider mind's intrinsic dynamics (Kastrup) — or, equivalently, as hemispheric-integration vs. inversion (McGilchrist), or as positivity-resonance-cultivating vs. positivity-resonance-eroding stabilization (Fredrickson). The *infused* clause in Augustine's definition recovers as participation-mediated stabilization (Rohr's cosmic-Christ; Wright's life-in-the-Spirit; the Kastrup-tradition de-dissociative widening) — with consent preserved at the alter level.

**(c) Where evidence is firm vs. speculative.** *Firm*: parallel domain-specific stabilizations within a single cognitive substrate (Hawkins; cortical literature). *Firm*: policy-specific priors and precisions (Friston; active-inference literature). *Firm*: positivity-resonance and prosocial-cultivation dispositions as empirically tractable (Fredrickson; Davidson). *Empirically supported*: hemispheric-integration accounts of character (McGilchrist; though framed clinically rather than experimentally). *Philosophically rigorous*: Stump on virtue as second-personal disposition. *Theological, well-attested*: Wright on Pauline virtue-formation; Rohr on cosmic-Christ habituation. *Speculative-metaphysical*: the conscious-realist-monist reading of good-habit as alter-mind-at-large alignment.

## The central-theme thread

Q.54-55 individuate the central-theme-relevant dispositions. The agent has many habits — not all of them are about loving unity. Some are domain-skills (signing one's name, recognising faces). Some are bodily stabilizations (the gait, the breath-pattern). Among them, *virtues* are the dispositions whose object is the agent's progress in the computational task of loving unity under perspective-limitation.

The central-theme reading sharpens Q.55's "what makes a habit good." A good habit is one that *advances* the agent's computation toward loving unity — that aligns the alter's local dynamics with the wider mind, that integrates left-hemispheric instrumentality under right-hemispheric receptivity, that cultivates positivity-resonance and other-directed attention, that abides in the participation that exceeds the agent's individuated capacity. This is what Stump means by virtue as second-personal; what Wright means by formation in Christ; what Rohr means by stabilized participation in the universal Christ.

The Augustinian "which God works in us without us" — applied to infused virtue — is the central theme's structural-form statement that some virtuous habits are *not built by the limited agent's own resources*. They are participations in wider perspective. The agent's part is consent and ongoing cooperation, not the generation of the disposition from within. This is exactly the central theme's "guidance from those who perceive more of the whole" in the habit-essence register.

- **Hawkins PRS-03 / *A Thousand Brains*; thousand-models-per-cortex** — Q.54 a.1 (many habits in one power): parallel domain-specific reference-frame storage; *neuroscientifically grounded*.
- **Friston PRS-04 + policy-specific priors and precisions** — Q.54 a.2 (habits distinguished by object): formal individuation of stabilized dispositions by their parameterized domain; *formal, empirically robust*.
- **Hoffman / interface-specific habituation** — Q.54 a.2 (habits distinguished by their objects): interface-domain-specific stabilization; *speculative-formal*.
- **Levin / domain-specific bioelectric patterns** — morphogenetic-scale analogue of multiple coexisting habits; *empirical at growing scales*.
- **Stump PRS-09 + PRS-04 / virtue as second-personal disposition** — keystone on Q.55: virtue's relational telos as Stump's distinctive contribution; *philosophically rigorous*.
- **Fredrickson PRS-01, PRS-08 / cultivated positivity-resonance** — empirical evidence for Q.55 a.4's good-habit claim; longitudinal data on virtuous disposition; *empirical, high confidence*.
- **McGilchrist / hemispheric integration as virtuous character** — Q.55's good/bad habit distinction at the attentional level; *clinical evidence*.
- **Wright / *After You Believe*; Pauline virtue, fruits of the Spirit** — Q.55 a.2 (virtue is operative; orders to proper end): scriptural anchor on virtue as life-in-the-Spirit ordered to new-creation telos; *theological, well-attested*.
- **Rohr / *The Universal Christ*; stabilized participation in the cosmic-Christ frame** — Q.55 a.4 (good habit, God's working without us): cosmic-scope reading of infused virtue; *contemplative tradition*.
- **Kastrup / alter-mind-at-large alignment** — Q.55's good/bad habit distinction under analytic idealism; *speculative-metaphysical*.

## Where this leaves us

Q.54-55 individuate virtues among habits. Q.56-57 (tomorrow) turn to the *subject* of virtue (which powers of the soul are subjects of virtue) and to the *intellectual virtues* specifically — wisdom, science, understanding, art, prudence. The synthesis will shift its center of gravity from generic habit-individuation toward the specific structure of the *cognitive* virtues, with Friston, Hawkins, McGilchrist, and Stump leading. The central theme will sharpen as the inquiry turns to what specifically counts as *cognitive* perfection under perspective-limitation: not merely knowing-correctly but knowing-in-a-way-that-orders-the-agent-toward-loving-unity, which is precisely Stump's territory (faith-as-knowledge-of-persons) and Wright's (the wisdom-tradition under Pauline reading).
