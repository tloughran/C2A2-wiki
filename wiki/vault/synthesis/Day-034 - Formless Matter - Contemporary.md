## Q.65 — Work of creation of corporeal creatures

a.1 — *are corporeal creatures from God?* — receives the medieval *yes* against various dualistic notions that would attribute matter to a separate evil principle. Aquinas's response cites Psalm 146 and reinterprets the New Testament texts that *seem* to identify the devil with the *god of this world*: the devil is called *god of this world* because *worldlings serve him*, not because he created it. Mind-monism's translation runs through Kastrup's analytic idealism (PRS-01, PRS-02 in the Kastrup-tradition wiki) and Levin's morphogenetic realism: *matter* in the contemporary frame is what experience looks like from the outside (Kastrup) *and* is the locus of empirically real bioelectric pattern (Levin); both readings make matter a feature of the substrate's structure rather than an independent ontological item. The medieval claim that *corporeal creatures are from God* (the substrate's act of conditioning) survives the contemporary translation cleanly. Wright's covenant-creation framing adds the scriptural shape: Genesis 1's repeated *and God saw that it was good* is the affirmation that matter is the substrate's good work, not a fall from spirit.

a.2 — *were corporeal things made on account of God's goodness?* — receives the medieval *yes*: *the entire universe with all its parts is ordained towards God as its end, in as much as it imitates and shows forth the divine goodness to the glory of God*. Vatican I's framing Habash quotes — *not with the intention of increasing his happiness nor indeed of obtaining happiness, but in order to manifest his Perfection by the good things which he bestows on what he creates by an absolute free plan* — preserves Day 11 Q.20's substrate-stance-toward-conditioned-things doctrine: love is the substrate's relational-stance, and creation is the substrate's loving-conditioned-things-into-being-and-flourishing. Mind-monism's reading: the substrate's structural priors pattern conditioned things because the patterning is what it is for the substrate to be loving-toward-its-alters; Rohr's *Universal Christ* extends this — the divine pattern is *in* matter from the beginning, not imposed on inert stuff later. Honest evidence calibration: the empirical adjacents are at the level of fine-tuning arguments and the philosophy of cosmology; the substrate-scale claim is theological-philosophical.

a.3 — *were corporeal creatures produced by God through the medium of the angels?* — receives the medieval *no*: matter (the *prima materia*, the *raw sheer potential* at the bottom of all material things) must be created directly by God, because *only what bridges the infinite difference between nothing and anything at all* can produce it, and that is God alone. The angels are creatures and cannot create *ex nihilo*. Mind-monism's translation: the substrate's act of conditioning is the substrate's own act, not delegated to alters. Kastrup's framework holds: dissociation is mind-at-large's own structural process, not something an alter does to other alters. The medieval claim that *prima materia* requires direct divine creation maps onto the contemporary claim that the *lowest layer of pattern* is the substrate's own structural prior, not a derivative of any alter-scale process. Habash's gloss: *prima materia is so fundamental that the only thing beneath it in the order of being is nothingness*. Heisenberg's *physics and philosophy* connection (the matter of Aristotle as *mere potential* compared to *energy*) is the contemporary identification: whatever the *most fundamental thing* is in physics, it is at most one step above nothingness, and that one step is the substrate's act.

a.4 — *are the forms of bodies from the angels?* — receives the medieval *no*. *Forms* — that-which-informs-matter-allowing-it-to-actually-exist-as-tree-or-rock-or-water — exist *potentially* within the *raw potency* of matter and *come into actual existence with some particular material by the action of some external agent* (Aquinas's example: the lab-agent who decomposes water into hydrogen and oxygen — *the raw underlying matter is now under the form of hydrogen and oxygen, distinctly*). The medieval form-doctrine is precise here: forms are *not* angelic emanations; they are *patterns the substrate's act actualizes within matter via secondary agents*. Mind-monism's translation lands directly on Levin's empirical work. Levin's *Cognitive Light Cones* program (PRS-01, PRS-03 in the Levin-tradition wiki — morphogenetic control; morphological attractor formalism) gives the empirical mid-scale demonstration that *form-as-bioelectric-pattern* is empirically real and causally active: cells and tissues organize toward target morphologies under bioelectric goal-state-patterns; *form* in the medieval Aristotelian sense is a *real causal feature*, not a Platonic abstraction. The medieval doctrine and the contemporary biology agree on the structural shape *form is real and causally active in matter*. Heisenberg's gloss Habash quotes — *we can say that the matter of Aristotle, which is mere potential, should be compared to our concept of energy, which gets into actuality by means of the form when the elementary particle is created* — preserves the medieval distinction at the quantum level: the pattern that distinguishes one elementary particle from another is the *form*, and the underlying *energy* is the *matter*. CROSS-008/-019 in the master cross-program index (Levin × Stump on Thomistic teleology) gives the cross-tradition convergence: the medieval Aristotelian-Thomistic form-doctrine and the contemporary morphogenetic-teleology biology are working the same problem with different vocabulary, and the bridge is now formal (CROSS-006/007 — Levin × Friston: morphogenesis as free-energy minimization). *Strong empirical anchorage at developmental and quantum scales; substrate-scale identification structural-philosophical.*

## Q.66 — Order of creation toward distinction

a.1 — *did formlessness of created matter precede in time its formation?* — receives the careful medieval answer Aquinas pushes back on. He rejects the reading that God created *prima materia* first as raw-formless-stuff and *later* gave it forms; an *imperfect effect* would imply *imperfection in the agent*, and God is absolutely perfect. So *primary matter was not created altogether formless* but *under distinct forms*. Yet Aquinas notes the alternative reading that other holy writers held — that *formlessness* refers not to the absence of all form but to *the absence of that beauty and comeliness which are now apparent in the corporeal creation*. Mind-monism's translation runs through Carroll on emergence (PRS-01, PRS-04 — ontological closure vs. emergence; downward causation) and Levin on morphogenetic teleology. The contemporary cosmological story: the early universe was high-symmetry, low-entropy, and structurally simple; the structures we now see (galaxies, stars, planets, life, mind) emerged through symmetry-breaking, gravitational clustering, chemical and biological complexification. *Formless* in Aquinas's preferred sense maps onto *high-symmetry, low-complexity*; the *forms now apparent* map onto the differentiated structures the universe's emergent dynamics have produced. Honest evidence calibration: the empirical case for emergent structure is strong (cosmological, astrophysical, biological); the medieval-cosmological framework Aquinas inherits is dated, but the structural intuition (*formlessness* as low-determination, *form* as high-determination) survives. Wright's *Genesis-as-temple-cosmology* reading (his work on Genesis 1 in *Surprised by Hope* and the broader narrative-theological framework) is the corrective to literalism: Genesis 1 is not a six-day young-earth scientific cosmology but a theological reading of cosmic emergence that preserves the claims about creation's goodness, ordering, and purpose. Day 35's transcript will press this further; the corrective is already in play here.

a.2 — *is the formless matter of all corporeal things the same?* — Aquinas says *no* (his cosmology distinguishes the matter of celestial bodies, which he held to be incorruptible, from terrestrial matter). Habash flags the dating directly: *if Aquinas was writing today, the answer to this question would be yes* — terrestrial matter and stellar matter are made of the same elementary particles, and any contemporary Catholic philosopher would affirm this. Mind-monism's translation: the lowest layer of pattern in the substrate is *one* — Wolfram's ruliad (PRS-06 in the Wolfram-tradition wiki — ruliad as ontological ground) gives the formal cousin: the threads of the ruliad are sub-structures within a single computational totality. The medieval *prima materia* is *one* (in the substrate's own structure) even though the *forms* it manifests under are many. Carroll's poetic naturalism (PRS-01) explicitly affirms this: there is one underlying physical reality, and *multiple legitimate ontologies describe the same physical reality* — the medieval form-doctrine and the contemporary particle-physics doctrine are both legitimate ways of speaking about the one underlying pattern.

a.3 — *was the empyrean heaven created at the same time as formless matter?* — Aquinas accepts the medieval cosmology (a highest-place at the top of the universe). Habash flags this is dated. Mind-monism's reading: the *empyrean* in the medieval framework was the dwelling-place of angels and the location of the cosmological hierarchy's top; in the contemporary frame, the *highest place* is a category-mistake at the cosmological level (there is no preferred direction in the cosmos), but the structural intuition (the substrate's structure has a *highest scope of operation* corresponding to mind-at-large itself) is preserved at the metaphysical rather than cosmological level. The angelic *location by operation, not by containment* (Day 32 Q.61 a.4) covers the same structural ground.

a.4 — *was time created simultaneously with formless matter?* — receives the careful medieval *no*: *time is the measure of change*; *creation out of nothing is not change* (because change requires a pre-existing subject which becomes-different, and nothing is the privation, not the subject, in *creation*); therefore time *begins when change begins, which must happen after the creation of formless matter*. Mind-monism's translation runs through Carroll's poetic-naturalism cosmology directly. Carroll's work on *The Big Picture* and on the arrow of time (PRS-01, PRS-07 — observer-centric quantum gravity) names the contemporary version: *time* is an emergent feature of the universe's low-entropy past and the second-law dynamics that follow from it; cosmological time *begins* with the structures that exhibit irreversible change. Arkani-Hamed's post-spacetime physics gestures at the same point: spacetime is emergent, not fundamental. The medieval claim that *time begins with the first changeable creature* maps onto the contemporary claim that *time emerges with the universe's low-entropy initial state and its dynamical evolution*. Aquinas's careful distinction between *creation* (not a change) and *the changes that follow on creation* (the locus of time) is preserved structurally — the substrate's act of conditioning is *atemporal* in the medieval sense (Day 7 Q.11 — divine eternity as transcendental) and time is the alter-mode-specific feature of the conditioned things' dynamics. Wright on biblical eschatology gives the corporate-theological frame (Romans 8 and 1 Corinthians 15): the *end* of time is *new creation*, in which the old-creation's irreversibility is taken up into the substrate's renewed conditioning — not erased but transformed.
