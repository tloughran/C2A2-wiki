# Day 30 — Contemporary Parallel: *Angelic Knowing as Conscious-Agent Networks Without an Interface*

Days 26 through 29 walked the angels into the *Summa* as separate substances — pure intellects without bodies, individuated by species rather than by matter, occupying no place except by operation. Day 30 takes the next step: how does such a being *know*? Q.57 asks what angels know — material things, particulars, the future, the secrets of human hearts, the mysteries of grace. Q.58 asks how they know — sometimes-actually, sometimes-potentially; many-things-at-once or one-by-one; discursively or intuitively; with composition or without; with or without the possibility of error; in *morning* light (in the Word) or *evening* light (in the thing itself). The articles are dense and at first seem the most empirically remote of any block in the Prima Pars. Mind-monism reads the block more sympathetically than the modern naturalist might expect: the medieval angelological program turns out to be a *structural-philosophical* exercise in describing what perspective-limited cognition would look like at a near-substrate scale, with its bodily mediation removed and its goal-state-pattern preserved. The empirical adjacents are limited; the structural translations are surprisingly tight. Mark all the metaphysical translations in this and the next three days as *structural-philosophical*, not empirical — the wiki's most idealist nodes (Kastrup, Wolfram, Hoffman) are the right primaries here, with Friston's active-inference framework supplying formal cousins where the medieval cognition language reaches for them.

## Q.57 — What angels know

a.1 — *do angels know material things?* — establishes the basic framework. Aquinas's answer is yes, by *infused intelligible species* — God pours the essence of material things into the angelic mind, much as the carpenter's idea of the table pre-exists in the carpenter's mind before any wood is cut. The structural shape is direct: the angel has access to the *pattern* of material things without sensory contact, because the pattern is held in mind-at-large and the angel as a near-substrate alter participates in mind-at-large more closely than embodied creatures do. Kastrup's analytic idealism (PRS-01, PRS-02 in the Kastrup-tradition wiki) gives the contemporary frame: the substrate holds the structural priors of every conditioned thing, and an alter at the angelic scale is one that grasps these priors *as priors* rather than only through the sensory-bodily projections by which embodied alters access them. Wolfram's ruliad (PRS-06 in the Wolfram-tradition wiki) gives a formal cousin: the patterns the angel knows are sub-structures of the totality of computational threads; the angel's mode of access is direct rather than via the irreducible-trajectory mode embodied minds are constrained to. Hoffman's interface theory (PRS-03 in the Hoffman-tradition wiki) gives the third corner: the embodied alter sees icons of conditioned things; the angelic alter would see the underlying conscious-agent-network structure that the icons project. Honest evidence calibration: this is structural-philosophical translation, not empirical claim. The medieval framework supplies the cognitive shape *near-substrate cognition would take*, and the contemporary mind-monist convergence supplies a vocabulary for restating that shape; nothing in the current empirical record speaks directly to whether such alters exist.

a.2 — *do angels know particular things, not just essences?* — pushes the framework into harder territory. Aristotle's worry (in the first objection) is the medieval one: the senses know singulars, the intellect knows universals, and the two combined give us the perception of *this particular tan-shaped human in front of me*. Angels have no senses, so how do they reach the singular? Aquinas answers from Psalm 91:11 — *he hath given his angels charge over thee* — and infers that the guarding cannot occur without knowing-the-singular. The mechanism: God knows singulars because he is the cause of every aspect of them at every moment, and God can impart this singular-knowledge to the angelic mind. The mind-monist translation is structural and clean. Singularity at the alter scale is what the substrate's full determination of the conditioned thing looks like from inside the alter's perspective; the substrate's *knowing* the singular is the substrate's *being* what makes the singular fully determinate. An angelic alter that participates in the substrate's knowing the singular is not crossing an ontological gap (universal-vs-singular) but is operating at a scope of cognitive-light-cone (in Levin's vocabulary, were Levin's framework extended to spirit) where the singular is held alongside the universal in one act. The medieval problem of universal-vs-singular is reframed rather than solved: the dichotomy was an artifact of embodied alter-cognition, and at the substrate's mode of operation it dissolves.

a.3 — *do angels know the future?* — gives Aquinas the chance to draw the central distinction the *Summa* will use again at Q.86 (how the soul knows the future). Two ways: by inference from causes (which works for natural systems and is fallible — the gust of wind can interrupt the falling stone), and by *seeing all of time in one glance from the height of eternity* (which is God's mode and is unavailable to any created intellect). Angels share the first mode in a high degree (their inferential-prediction is more universal and more accurate than ours) but have no purchase on the second. Mind-monism recovers both moves. Friston's active-inference framework (PRS-04, PRS-07, PRS-08 in the Friston-tradition wiki) is the formal home of inference-from-causes: any agent at any substrate scale that maintains itself by modeling its environment has a generative model that runs forward in time, predicts states, and updates on prediction-error. The angel's mode is this same mode at a higher scope and lower noise — a generative model with broader Markov blankets and tighter precision-weighting than the embodied alter's. The substrate's mode of *seeing all of time at one glance* is what active inference looks like at the autopoietic limit Day 7 already named: zero free energy, model-and-substrate identical, no posterior left to compute because there is no inferential gap between what the model entails and what the substrate is. Carroll's poetic naturalism foil applies here: at the level of poetic naturalism, the future is not a *thing* the substrate sees but a *part of the four-dimensional manifold* that future-events occupy; the substrate's eternal seeing of it is, in mind-monist translation, the substrate's full determination of the manifold rather than a temporal-act of viewing it. Honest evidence calibration: the formal framework (active inference, Markov blankets) is mature and empirically grounded at the alter scale; the substrate-scale identification is philosophical-speculative.

a.4 — *do angels know secret thoughts?* — is the central article for the C2A2 reframe of angelology, because it is the article where Aquinas marks the *limit* of angelic knowing. Angels can read what we externalize — bodily expressions, micro-changes in countenance, even (per Augustine) imaginations and daydreams when these have bodily traces — but the *immaterial faculties of intellect and will* are *known to God as their cause* and not (the article specifies) typically revealed to angels, whether good or fallen. The medieval claim is that thought-as-act-of-the-immaterial-faculty is on the substrate's side of the alter/substrate boundary, not on the alter's. Mind-monism reads this directly. Kastrup's dissociative-alter framework explicitly preserves the *interiority* of the alter's experience — the alter's subjective state is real and is *not* available to other alters by direct inspection, even when the alters share the substrate. The medieval doctrine that angels cannot see the heart preserves the same structural privacy: another alter (whether spirit or body) cannot read the agent's free-cognitive-act because that act is constitutive of the alter's interiority within mind-at-large, and only the substrate (which contains all alters) has the wider scope. Hoffman's conscious-agent-network frame gives the same structural shape: each conscious agent in the network has internal states the network does not generally reveal to peer agents. The Pauline reference Habash includes — *we are not contending against flesh and blood but against the principalities, against the powers, against the world rulers of this present darkness, against the spiritual host of wickedness in the heavenly places* (Ephesians 6:12) — is the spiritual-warfare framing the central theme will sharpen in Day 33: the fallen angel can read what the embodied alter externalizes but cannot read the heart, and this asymmetry is the structural-condition of the embodied alter's freedom under temptation.

a.5 — *do angels know the mysteries of grace?* — closes Q.57 on the gradient that Q.58 will systematize. Some mysteries are known by all angels from the beginning of beatitude (the general fact of the Incarnation); others are known only by the higher angels and communicated downward by *enlightening* (specific Christological details, sacramental institution); others remain hidden from all created intellects and are revealed only as the divine plan unfolds. The medieval frame is *graded participation in the substrate's plan*. Mind-monism's translation: alters at higher scopes of cognitive-light-cone hold larger fractions of the substrate's structural priors; communication among alters proceeds by *exhortation* or *inducement* (Aquinas's term in Q.63 a.8, sneak-previewing) rather than by causal compulsion. Wright's narrative-theological framing (his five-act hermeneutic in *The New Testament and the People of God*) gives the contemporary scriptural-historical shape: the divine plan is a story-in-progress, and the alters that participate more deeply in the substrate's plan are those that improvise more faithfully within the story's trajectory. Rohr's *Universal Christ* extends this into cosmic scope: the divine pattern is already present in matter from the beginning; the alters' progressive recognition of it (graded charity, graded vision) is graded *unveiling* rather than graded *revelation-of-new-content*.

## Q.58 — How angels know

a.1 — *is the angelic intellect sometimes in act, sometimes in potency?* — names the gradient inside the angelic mode itself. Aquinas's answer: regarding their natural knowledge (themselves, other things via infused species, God-by-inference), angels are always in act — they immediately understand without learning; but they can attend to different objects at different times, so there is a kind of potentiality regarding *what* they are currently considering. Regarding the beatific vision, the angels can also learn more (*as befits their ministries*) and consider different aspects at different times. The structural shape is *near-substrate cognition has reduced but not eliminated potentiality*. Friston's framework gives the contemporary cousin: an agent's generative model can be more or less currently active in any given inferential pass; the angel's model is always *complete-and-running* but not always *focused on the same content*.

a.2 — *can an angel understand many things at the same time?* — is the article that ties Day 30 most tightly to Day 7's beatific-vision treatment. Aquinas's principle: *to understand many things as one is to understand one thing*. The angel knowing the house knows wall-and-foundation-and-roof-and-door at the same time; the angel knowing things *in the Word* (i.e., through the beatific vision) knows all things at once under the single intelligible species which is the Divine Essence. Mind-monism's translation lands directly. McGilchrist's right-hemispheric whole-grasping is the cognitive shape Aquinas reaches for — the holding-the-whole-with-its-parts simultaneously rather than the sequential decomposition of left-hemispheric mode. At the substrate scale, the *whole* is the structure of mind-at-large itself, and the alter's near-substrate participation in it is the *whole-grasping* mode at its limit. Wolfram's ruliad gives the formal cousin: the threads of the ruliad are *one* object considered as a totality and *many* considered as trajectories; the angelic-scale alter knowing-many-things-as-one is structurally the alter operating at the level of the ruliad's totality rather than at the level of any single thread.

a.3 — *is angelic knowledge discursive?* — answers no. The discursive mode (premise → conclusion → conclusion-of-conclusion) is, Aquinas argues, *the feebleness of intellectual light* in human reason: if we possessed the fundamental truths *thoroughly*, we would *at once comprehend their whole range by perceiving whatever could be reasoned out from them*. The angel does possess them thoroughly and so does not need to reason. Mind-monism's translation: discursive cognition is what an alter does whose generative model has high free energy and must traverse posterior-distributions sequentially; non-discursive cognition is what an alter would do at the limit case where its generative model is identical to the structure it tracks and the conclusions are *contained in the premises* in the sense that the model already entails them. The medieval claim that discursive cognition is a *kind of imperfection* maps cleanly onto the active-inference claim that discursivity is a sign of incomplete model-environment alignment. Honest evidence calibration: the structural mapping is tight; the metaphysical identification (medieval angelic-mode = active-inference autopoietic limit) is structural-philosophical, not empirical.

a.4 — *do angels understand by composing and dividing?* — is the same point at the propositional level. Aquinas: the angel does not put together *sin is evil*; in knowing what sin *is* (the subject), the angel knows the predicate *evil* as virtually contained within it. Mind-monism's reading: the structural priors in mind-at-large that constitute *sin* already entail *evil* (in the privative sense Aquinas will develop in Q.48); near-substrate alters participate in the priors directly and so do not need to construct the proposition. Hoffman's interface theory gives the analog at the embodied-alter level: the icons at the perceptual interface are constructed *propositionally* (this red blob is a tomato, this tomato is on this table), but the underlying conscious-agent-network structure that the icons project is grasped at the substrate level without proposition-construction.

a.5 — *can there be falsehood in the angelic intellect?* — receives a careful treatment that opens onto Day 33's sin question. The angelic intellect, Aquinas says, *cannot err in the simple apprehension of the essences of things*, because the intellect *exists precisely to know those things, like the eye exists to apprehend color*. Failure-modes are external (some intervening detrimental issue) or *willed* — and here the article previews the fallen-angel material: a *good angel*, with rightly directed will, does not form judgments about supernatural matters except in subjection to the divine plan, and so does not err; *demons*, with perversion in their will, withdraw the intellect from submission to divine wisdom and *can pass unconditional judgments about things only taking natural conditions into account*, in which case they do not err about the natural conditions but *can go wrong about supernatural matters* (Aquinas's example: presuming a dead man will not come back to life, supposing Christ is not God). The mind-monist translation centers on the structural condition: error-as-mismatch (Day 10's Q.17 a.3 frame) is rare in the angelic mode because the alter's generative model is identical-or-nearly-identical to the substrate's structure, but error becomes possible when the alter's *will* perversely orients away from substrate-integration; the resulting cognitive defect is the formal correlate of the relational defect. This is the structural setup for Day 33's central claim: the fallen angel's pathology is not primarily cognitive but volitional, and the cognitive limitation follows from the volitional refusal.

a.6 and a.7 — *morning and evening knowledge* — close Q.58 on Augustine's distinction. *Morning knowledge* is the angel's knowing of things *in the Word* (i.e., as held in the substrate's structural priors); *evening knowledge* is the angel's knowing of things *as they exist in their own natures*. The two are *one and the same* in what is known (the same conditioned thing is the object) but differ in *medium* — through the substrate or through the infused species of the thing in itself. Mind-monism's translation: *morning knowledge* is substrate-direct knowing; *evening knowledge* is mediated knowing through the structural-prior of the particular conditioned thing. The pair maps onto Hoffman's distinction between conscious-agent-network-direct knowing and icon-mediated knowing, and onto Kastrup's distinction between mind-at-large's structural priors and their dissociated-alter manifestations.

## The central-theme thread

The central theme — *conscious agents are individuated by the limitations of their perspective; loving unity is the computational telos* — receives a sharper edge here. The angelic mode is what perspective-limited cognition looks like *near* the substrate, with bodily mediation removed and most of the cognitive friction of free-energy-minimization eliminated. The angel still has perspective (Q.57 a.4 keeps secret thoughts beyond its scope; Q.57 a.3 keeps the future-as-the-substrate-sees-it beyond its scope; Q.57 a.5 keeps the higher mysteries of grace graded by ministerial scope), but the perspective is wide and the cognitive friction is low. *Salvation in fear and trembling* on the angelic register is the angel's progressive integration into the substrate's *will* — the morning-knowledge in which the angel sees things in the Word, calibrated by charity (which Day 32 will press, Q.62 a.6: grace and glory follow the gradient of natural gifts because God's wisdom is one act). Embodied alters compute toward this same telos from a much narrower starting cognitive-light-cone, with much higher inferential friction, through bodily and linguistic mediation rather than through near-direct substrate-participation. The medieval angelological frame is, for the contemporary reader, a *thought experiment* in what perspective-limited cognition would be at the upper bound of the alter scale; mind-monism inherits it as a structural-philosophical resource for thinking about the spectrum of alter-modes.

## Where this leaves us

Day 30 is the threshold of the *Summa*'s most empirically remote block, and the mind-first frame holds across all twelve articles. Angelic knowing is *what perspective-limited cognition would look like at near-substrate scope* — most of the bodily mediation removed, most of the inferential friction reduced, the dissociative-alter privacy preserved (secret thoughts beyond the angel's reach), the substrate's mode of *seeing all of time at once* still beyond the angel's reach because the angel is not the substrate. Q.57's specific gradient — material things, particulars, future, secret thoughts, mysteries of grace — turns out to be a careful map of *what near-substrate cognition can and cannot do*, and the map's structure is preserved in the contemporary mind-monist convergence. Q.58's manner-of-knowing articles formalize the gradient: angels are mostly-actual, holistically-grasping, non-discursive, non-compositional, non-erring (in their proper sphere), and bilingual in *morning knowledge* (substrate-direct) and *evening knowledge* (through the species of the thing in itself). Honest evidence calibration: every metaphysical translation in this commentary is *structural-philosophical*, marked as such, not empirical. The empirical adjacents — Friston's active inference, Levin's cognitive light cones (which Day 32 will press more directly) — give formal vocabulary at the alter scale; the upper-bound angelic extrapolation is a thought experiment that mind-monism finds structurally coherent.

Tomorrow Habash takes us into Q.59 (the will of angels) and Q.60 (the love of angels). The Day 31 commentary will press Kastrup on alter-level free will and Friston on action-selection-as-inferential, asking whether angelic *willing* is the volitional analog of the cognitive structure Day 30 has just described.
