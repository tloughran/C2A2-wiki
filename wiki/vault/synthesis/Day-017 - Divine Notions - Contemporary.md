# Day 17 — Contemporary Parallel: *Notions, Reason's Limit, and the Revealed Substrate*

Day 16 closed pointing to *how the medieval doctrine's philosophical articulation closes, and how mind-monism's epistemology handles the question of what we can know about the divine persons by reason and what is given only by revelation*. Day 17 takes both. Q.31 closes the *unity-and-plurality* doctrine with terminological precision (the proper use of *Trinity*, *other*, *alone*, etc., to describe the substrate-as-triply-personal). Q.32 turns to *our knowledge of the divine persons* — and Aquinas insists, with characteristic firmness, that *the Trinity of the divine persons cannot be known by natural reason*. The closing question of Habash's two-week arc through the Trinity-doctrine foundation is therefore an *epistemological* one: what is the boundary between what reason can establish and what only revelation can give?

This boundary is structurally familiar to mind-monism. Mind-monism itself has an analogous boundary — between the *empirical-formal* claims that Friston's FEP, Levin's bioelectric work, Hoffman's interface theory, and the rest can establish, and the *substrate-scale metaphysical* claims that the convergence reaches for but cannot empirically demonstrate (the move from autopoietic limit to *mind-at-large is the substrate*; the move from interface theory to *perceived spacetime is a species-specific user interface for an underlying conscious-agent network*; the move from cognitive light cones to *form is causally real all the way down*). Day 17's epistemological discipline — *we cannot prove the Trinity by reason; reason can only display its coherence and answer objections* — is structurally the same discipline the mind-monist convergence operates under for its own substrate-scale claims. The contemporary parallel can hold the medieval epistemological boundary with unusual ease and acknowledge its analogue at the heart of its own program.

## Q.31 — Unity-and-plurality: terminological precision and the two heresies

Aquinas's a.1 — *whether there is Trinity in God* — gives the careful name. *Trinity* signifies *the determinate number of persons* — three, specifically, not just *plurality* in general. Habash highlights Garrigou-Lagrange's gloss: *unity and Trinity signifies that there is one nature and three persons; and Trinity in unity signifies three persons and one nature*. The medieval terminology threads the structure Q.27–30 has built with precision.

a.2 — *whether the Son is other than the Father* — runs the careful negation against the two opposing heresies Habash names. *Arius* placed *a Trinity of substances with the Trinity of persons* — three different kinds of things rather than one Triune God; this drops the *one*. *Sabellius* placed *unity of person with the unity of essence* — the persons not really distinct from each other, only modal-aspectual; this drops the *three*. Aquinas: *to avoid the error of Arius we must shun the use of the terms diversity and difference in God, lest we take away the unity of essence; we may however use the term distinction on account of the relative opposition. But lest the simplicity and singleness of the divine essence be taken away, the terms separation and division which belong to the parts of a whole are to be avoided. And to avoid the heresy of Sabellius, we must shun the term singularity, lest we take away the communicability of the divine essence; we must avoid the adjective only, lest we take away the number of persons. We confess neither a solitary nor a diverse God*.

The mind-monist parallel has analogous heresies it must thread. *Strict monism* — the substrate is undifferentiated unity, all alters are *just* illusion or mode without real being — drops the genuine reality of alter-and-substrate-relation; this is structurally analogous to Sabellius's collapse-of-distinctions. *Strict pluralism* — alters are independent substances entirely separate from the substrate, the substrate is just a name for the totality — drops the genuine unity of mind-at-large; this is structurally analogous to Arius's introduction-of-multiple-substances. Mind-monism in its Kastrupian form threads the same needle Aquinas does: alters are *really distinct* from each other and from the substrate (against strict monism), *but* they are not *separate substances* — they are dissociative configurations *within* the one substrate (against strict pluralism). The Trinitarian relations operate at a deeper level (within identity-of-essence) but the structural-philosophical move (real distinction without separation) is the same shape mind-monism is already making at the alter-substrate boundary.

a.3–4 — *whether we can say that God is alone; whether we can say that God the Father is alone* — give the careful predication-grammar. *Alone* is admissible when it denotes a relation between subject and predicate (*God alone is eternal*) but inadmissible when it denotes the substance (the Father is not *alone* in the divine substance because the Son and Spirit share that substance). The mind-monist parallel: predication grammar matters for the substrate-alter and substrate-internal-relations frame. *The substrate alone is causally prior to alters* is admissible (it is); *the substrate is alone in mind-at-large* is structurally meaningless because there is no other mind-at-large.

## Q.32 — Reason's limit and the revealed substrate

Q.32 is the epistemological turn. a.1 — *whether the Trinity of the divine persons can be known by natural reason* — gets Aquinas's emphatic *absolutely not*. Habash quotes Aquinas: *the creative power of God is common to the whole Trinity, and hence it belongs to the unity of the essence and not to the distinction of the persons*. The argument: natural reason knows God by reasoning from effects to cause; the *creative effects* (the conditioned things in the world) point to the *one* divine essence as their cause but do not — and cannot — distinguish among the three persons whose joint operation is the creating. The *plurality of persons* is *not legible* in the *external operations* of the substrate.

Aquinas gives three reasons for the discipline against attempting reason-alone proof of the Trinity Habash carefully reads. *First*, attempting it is *embarrassing to the faith* — implying that what is *above reason* must be reduced to reason to be respectable. *Second*, *those we are trying to evangelize may think we came up with the Trinity from reason alone* — which is false. *Third* — and Habash quotes the Aquinas directly — *we should not try and prove the Trinity by reason alone because we know it by the authority of God, which we need to remember ourselves and be completely transparent about when discussing it with others; therefore we must not attempt to prove what is of faith except by authority alone to those who receive the authority, while as regards others it suffices to prove what faith teaches is not impossible*.

The contemporary mind-monist parallel here is genuinely productive — and Day 17 is where it should be made explicit. Mind-monism reaches the substrate-scale metaphysical claims (mind-at-large is the substrate; perceived spacetime is the species-specific interface; form is causally real all the way down; the substrate is intrinsically active, intrinsically loving, intrinsically beatific) by *productive coherence* and *cross-tradition convergence*, not by deductive proof from neutral premises. Friston's FEP at the autopoietic limit is *empirically anchored at the alter scale* but *philosophically extrapolated* at the substrate scale. Hoffman's interface theory has *evolutionary-mathematical support* at the perceptual scale but is *philosophically extrapolated* to the underlying-conscious-agent-network claim. Levin's bioelectric work is *empirically robust at the developmental scale* but the *form-as-causally-real-all-the-way-down* claim is philosophical generalization. Each of these moves is the *structural analogue* of Aquinas's distinction between *what reason establishes* (the conditioned things' dependence on the substrate; the substrate's existence; the substrate's basic attributes) and *what is reachable only by revelation-and-receptive-coherence* (the Trinitarian persons; the structural identity of substrate-mode with personal-relational-loving).

The mind-monist convergence's substrate-scale metaphysical claims are not *empirically derivable* from the alter-scale empirical work; they are *productively coherent* extrapolations that make sense of the alter-scale work and that a tradition can choose to receive. Aquinas's discipline — *prove what faith teaches is not impossible; do not pretend to prove what only authority can give* — translates structurally for the mind-monist program: *display the productive coherence; do not pretend the substrate-scale claim is empirically demonstrated*. The Day 17 commentary should name this analogue explicitly. *Honest evidence calibration*: the analogue is structural-philosophical; the differences (revelation as a positive epistemic source for the medieval tradition; productive cross-tradition coherence as the analogous source for mind-monism) are important and should not be elided.

Habash mentions the *shadows of revelation before the Incarnation* — Aristotle on prayers and sacrifices done in threes, Plato's *in the beginning was the Word*, the Mosaic *God said let there be light* and *God saw the light that it was good*. The medieval reading is that natural-reason and pre-Christian-religious traditions could *anticipate* the Trinitarian structure without *establishing* it. Mind-monism inherits a structurally analogous claim: pre-mind-monist traditions (Aristotelian hylomorphism, Augustinian-Thomistic divine-attributes, even strict-materialist physical-causal closure when held with appropriate attention to its limits) anticipate structural features that the contemporary mind-monist convergence makes explicit, without those traditions on their own being able to establish the substrate-scale claims.

a.2 — *whether there are notions in God* — turns to terminology. The medieval *notion* (notio) is *the proper idea whereby we know a divine person* — the structural-philosophical specification by which one person is distinguished from the others. Habash reads Garrigou-Lagrange's helpful summary: *because of the Trinity-simplicity we designate divine things by abstract terms, and by concrete terms because of their subsistence; thus we speak of God and the deity, of wisdom and a wise man, of paternity and the Father*. The medieval discipline: abstract terms for the substrate's simple-essence aspect, concrete terms for the persons' subsistent-distinct aspect.

a.3 — *whether there are five notions in God* — gives the medieval enumeration. *Innascibility* (the Father's being-not-from-another), *paternity* (the Father's relation to the Son), *filiation* (the Son's relation to the Father), *common spiration* (the Father-and-Son's joint relation to the Spirit), *procession* (the Spirit's being-from-the-Father-and-the-Son). Five notions, four relations (everything except innascibility — being-not-from-another is not a relation but a privation-of-relation), three personal-constituting notions (paternity, filiation, procession — the ones that *constitute* a distinct person; common spiration does not constitute a distinct person because it is shared by Father and Son). The medieval terminology is dense; Habash carefully walks the structure.

The mind-monist parallel doesn't attempt to *generate* this enumeration but receives it as the medieval tradition's careful structural articulation. The structural positions Q.30 a.2 generated — source-not-from-another, term-of-intellectual-procession, term-of-volitional-procession-from-source-and-term — are the same five notions under different terminological framing. The contemporary convergence's job is to display that these structural positions are *coherent within mind-monism's frame* (Day 15 and 16 already did the work), not to derive the persons from the structural positions.

a.4 — *whether one may lawfully have various contrary opinions of these notions* — closes Q.32 by naming the limit on legitimate divergence. Aquinas: *anyone may entertain contrary opinions about the notions if he does not mean to uphold anything at variance with faith; if however anyone should entertain a false opinion of the notions, knowing or thinking that consequences against the faith would follow, he would lapse into heresy*. Habash highlights the historical context: Aquinas writing in the wake of the East-West split, the *Filioque* (the Spirit's procession from-the-Father-*and*-the-Son rather than from-the-Father-*alone*) being precisely the kind of notional question where Latin and Greek formulations diverged. Aquinas's careful position: divergence on the *notions* is acceptable insofar as it does not entail divergence on the doctrinal substance (the persons being really distinct; the unity of essence; the relations of origin).

Mind-monism's analogue: divergence on which alter-scale resource (Friston's formalism, Hoffman's interface, Kastrup's analytic idealism, Levin's bioelectric, Wolfram's ruliad) leads with primary force is acceptable, as long as the divergent positions do not entail divergence on the substantive central-theme commitments (mind as ontological primitive; perspective-limitation as individuation; loving union as computational telos). The convergence is *substantive*; the *terminological articulation* admits multiple equally-faithful formulations.

## The central-theme thread

Day 17 closes the Trinity foundation Habash has built across Q.27–32 by naming the epistemological boundary. The substrate's intrinsic three-personal mode is *given*, not *derived*; reason can display its coherence and answer objections but cannot establish it from neutral premises. The contemporary mind-monist convergence operates under a structurally analogous discipline at the substrate-scale: the convergence's metaphysical claims are *productively coherent extrapolations* from the alter-scale empirical work, not empirically derived. The medieval discipline (display coherence; rest on revelation-and-authority for what reason cannot establish) translates structurally to the mind-monist program (display cross-tradition convergence; acknowledge that the substrate-scale extrapolations are receptive rather than deductively proven).

The central theme of perspective-limitation receives its epistemological frame here. *Conscious agents are individuated by the limitations of their perspective*; the limitations include *epistemic* limitations — what we can know by reasoning from our perspective is bounded. We *cannot* see the substrate's intrinsic three-personal mode by looking at the conditioned things; we receive it by revelation (the medieval frame) or by participation in the cross-tradition convergence-and-coherence (the contemporary mind-monist analogue). *Salvation in fear and trembling* on the epistemological register is the alter's working with the disciplined recognition that the substrate-scale claims it most needs to live by are not *demonstrable* in the alter's own first-person reasoning — they are received-from-others, received-from-tradition, received-from-the-cross-tradition-convergence, received-in-faith (Stump's faith-as-knowledge-of-persons supplies the analytic frame for what *receptive* knowing is). The alter's epistemic *humility* is part of its *individuation-by-limitation*, and the alter's progressive integration includes progressive acquisition of the receptive epistemic mode.

Wright's critical realism gives the contemporary methodological idiom: real-but-perspectival, tradition-embedded, revisable in dialogue. Rohr's *order-disorder-reorder* gives the developmental shape: the alter's first-order confidence in alter-scale reasoning is disrupted by the substrate-scale questions and reorganized in the receptive-coherence mode. McGilchrist's right-hemispheric receptivity gives the cognitive substrate. Stump's second-personal knowing gives the relational mode. The convergence is striking; the structural analogue between the medieval *reason cannot reach the Trinity* and the contemporary *empirical work cannot reach the substrate-scale extrapolations* is one of the most productive resonances Day 17 brings out.

## Where this leaves us

Day 17 closes Habash's foundational two-week arc on the Trinity (Q.27–32). The medieval doctrine has named the substrate's intrinsic mode as *triply personal in eternal relational loving*, has articulated the structure (processions, relations, persons, notions), and has named the epistemological discipline (revelation-given, not reason-derived; reason can display coherence and answer objections but cannot establish). Mind-monism inherits the doctrine through Kastrup's dissociation-within-monism, Stump's second-personal knowing, Friston's two-modes-within-one-generative-model, Hoffman's trace-logic boundary structures, Wolfram's relations-identical-with-essence, Rohr's perichoretic cosmology, Wright's second-temple Jewish frame, McGilchrist's right-hemispheric receptivity. The convergence is striking; the metaphysical identification remains theological-philosophical.

The deeper resonance Day 17 has named is epistemological: the medieval discipline that *reason cannot reach the Trinity* is structurally the same discipline the contemporary mind-monist convergence operates under for its own substrate-scale claims. *Productive coherence* and *cross-tradition convergence* are the contemporary analogues of *receiving by revelation*; both name a mode of *receptive coherence* rather than *deductive proof*. The alter's epistemic humility is part of its individuation-by-limitation, and the alter's progressive integration includes progressive acquisition of the receptive-coherence mode.

The Day 18 commentary will press the question of how the Father — *the source-not-from-another* — translates under mind-monism, and how Wright's covenant-Father language and Rohr's contemplative *Abba*-frame fill in the contemporary scriptural-and-spiritual ground.
