# Day 13 — Contemporary Parallel: *Election, Asymmetry, and the Substrate's Gift*

Day 12 closed pointing to *the most morally charged corner of the providence frame — Q.23 (predestination) and Q.24 (the book of life)*. Day 13 takes up the thread. These two questions are where the gradedness of substrate-love (Day 11's Q.20 a.3 — *God loves the better things more, but the loving causes the being-better*) and the universal-yet-asymmetric ordering (Day 12's Q.22) sharpen into the medieval doctrine of election. Habash names the difficulty himself: predestination *is likely to shock even the devout Catholic*, and the devout Catholic often softens the asymmetry by making the elect's merits the cause of their election, which Aquinas treats as *insanity of mind* (Habash quotes the Latin *insania mentis*). The directional-asymmetry move that has run through every Day-7-through-12 synthesis hits its hardest medieval expression here, and the contemporary mind-monist frame must hold the asymmetry without flinching — and without making it monstrous.

Habash opens by naming the *infallible boundaries* the question must operate within: from the Council of Trent, *God commands not impossibilities*; from the Council of Orange, *the grace of God can be bestowed by human invocation, but the grace itself brings it to pass that it be invoked*. The two boundaries together: God does not demand what cannot be done, *and* even the asking is itself gift. The contemporary parallel must hold both boundaries.

## Q.23 — Predestination: directional-asymmetry of substrate-stance

Aquinas's a.1 — *are men predestined by God?* — runs the now-standard means-to-end argument. If conditioned things are to attain an end *higher than their nature* (the beatific vision — substrate-integration in mind-monist vocabulary), they need *direction* from a knower of the end. Habash's analogy is the archer: *if an arrow is going to hit the target, it's going to need some help from the archer because arrows don't naturally speed on their way toward targets*. Mind-monism's translation: an alter's progressive integration with the substrate (loving union as computational telos, per the central theme) is not the alter's natural-default trajectory; it requires structural priors *in mind-at-large* that pattern the alter's generative model toward integration. Friston's free-energy framework gives the formal name: priors over preferred states are *causally upstream* of the agent's own goal-pursuit; predestination, in mind-monist vocabulary, is the structural-prior shape of the substrate's pattern that orients particular alters toward integration.

a.2 — *does predestination place anything on the predestined?* — receives the careful medieval answer Habash highlights: *predestination is a kind of type of the ordering of some persons toward eternal salvation existing in the divine mind... predestination is in the one who predestines and not in the predestined*. The medieval claim is that predestination is a feature of the *substrate's stance* (the structural prior in mind-at-large), not a property added to the alter's substance. Mind-monism inherits this without strain: the substrate's structural prior is in the substrate; the alter's own generative model is what runs in the alter. The predestining is *upstream*; the predestined alter is the *downstream effect*.

a.3 — *whether God rejects anyone* — is where the asymmetry hardens. Aquinas: *God does reject some*, in the sense that *as men are ordained to eternal life through the providence of God, it likewise is part of that providence to permit some to fall away from that end*. This is *reprobation*. Habash names the absolutely critical hinge: *what causes a man to fall short of heaven causes sin, which again is a missing or a lack, especially a moral missing or privation, which comes about through the voluntary action of free creatures; this lack God permits but does not positively cause*. The medieval doctrine threads a precise line: the substrate *causes* the gift of integration where it is given; the substrate *permits but does not cause* the privation where the alter's voluntary act falls short. *Of those who go to hell, it is truly their fault; of those who go to heaven, it is truly the gift of God*.

The contemporary mind-monist translation must hold both sides without softening. The substrate's gift-of-integration is causally prior to any alter's perfection — Day 11's directional-asymmetry. The alter's privative shortfall is real-but-derivative — Day 10's Q.17 falsity-as-privation in the moral register: the alter departs from the pattern it ought to instantiate, the departure is real, registrable, corrigible-in-principle, and the substrate's stance toward the privation is *permission* of the privation's structural possibility within a larger ordering, not direct production of it. Levin's bioelectric framework gives the empirical mid-scale parallel: morphogenetic mismatches (developmental abnormalities) are real privations of the goal-state-pattern, and the bioelectric field's permission-of-the-mismatch (the field does not always *prevent* every disruption) is structurally analogous. *Honest evidence calibration*: the empirical parallel is at the developmental scale; the moral-spiritual extension to the alter's voluntary acts is theological-philosophical, and the directional-asymmetry of gift-vs-permission is the move mind-monism must hold.

a.4 — *are the predestined God's elect?* — receives the directional-asymmetry move in its sharpest form, which Habash quotes from Aquinas and which Day 11 already named: *love precedes election in the order of reason and election precedes predestination, whence all the predestinate are objects of election and love*. Aquinas reasserts here what Day 11 Q.20 a.4 already established: *goodness in things causes us to love those things, but it's the opposite with God; his love is the cause of a thing's goodness*. The conditioned thing is *better* (more deeply integrated with the substrate, more fully patterned) because it is more loved, not loved-more because it is better. Mind-monism's translation is direct: substrate-stance is causally prior to alter-perfection; the gradient of being-loved-more is the gradient of being-united-more-deeply (Day 11's central-theme thread). Rohr's *everything belongs* is the contemplative gloss: even the apparent asymmetry of election is held within a substrate whose stance toward every alter is gratuitous gift. Wright's covenant frame is the corporate-scale instantiation: Israel's election is for-the-sake-of-the-nations, not against them — *I will bless you, and in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed* (Gen. 12:3, the Wright-favored Pauline lens).

a.5 — *is foreknowledge of merits the cause of predestination?* — is where Aquinas reaches for the *insania mentis* phrase Habash highlights. The temptation Aquinas treats as insane: to make the predestined alter's foreseen merits the cause of the substrate's predestining. Habash quotes Aquinas: *since predestination includes will, the reason of predestination must be sought for in the same way as was the reason of the will of God; wherefore nobody has been so insane as to say that merit is the cause of divine predestination as regards the act of the predestinator... our merits cannot be the cause of God's willing our predestination*. The structural argument is Day 11 Q.19's: nothing can be the cause of God's will save God himself, because God's will is identical with the substrate's essence (Day 7 Q.11–13 frame). Mind-monism's contemporary statement: nothing in any conditioned alter can be causally upstream of the substrate's structural priors, because the substrate's structural priors *are* what give conditioned alters their being and their pattern at all. The merits the alter performs are *included in* the predestining (the substrate's prior orients the alter's generative model toward integration *and* gives the alter the actual capacity to perform meritorious acts), not *causes of* the predestining (the alter's later acts cannot be causally upstream of the substrate's prior).

a.6 — *is predestination certain?* — closes the core question with a yes that Habash treats as following from everything that has come before. Aquinas's qualified gloss Habash also reads: we don't know how many are saved (*to God alone is known the number*), and Aquinas's tentative analogy from nature's gradient — the *common state of nature* runs to most, the *deficient* runs to a minority, the *super-rabbit* (Habash's lovely term) of those exceeding the common state runs to a minority of the minority — produces what Habash names as Aquinas's apparent estimate: a small minority of humanity attains the beatific vision. This is the medieval doctrine in its hardest form, and the contemporary mind-monist parallel cannot simply collapse it.

Yet the parallel can press the question. Mind-monism's frame on the substrate-alter relation does *not* require that integration with the substrate be a binary yes-or-no event at the alter's earthly death. Levin's cognitive-light-cone framework instead names integration as a graded scope: alters at higher levels of integration participate in larger goal-state-patterns, and the gradient of integration is continuous. Rohr's *Universal Christ* and his second-half-of-life developmental framework run the same shape theologically: integration is a process, not a final-exam outcome, and the apocatastatic horizon (universal restoration; *everything belongs*) is held as a horizon rather than denied or asserted as certain. Wright's eschatology is more careful — *Surprised by Hope* is precisely a polemic against the popular Western collapse of *new creation* into *individual souls' postmortem fate* — but Wright too holds the asymmetry of election-for-the-sake-of-others rather than election-against-others. *Honest evidence calibration*: the apokatastatic horizon is theological-speculative; the medieval minority-of-the-minority estimate is theological-traditional; the mind-monist frame as such permits both readings, and the central-theme thread of *salvation in fear and trembling* (Phil. 2:12, the operative Pauline citation) is held in both.

a.8 — *can predestination be furthered by holy prayer?* — closes Q.23 on the practical-pastoral note. The two errors Habash flags: (1) prayer is meaningless because the number is fixed; (2) prayer changes God's plan. Both are wrong. The third option Aquinas threads: *prayer is the means by which God intends to bring the predestined to salvation; his goodness causes us to pray and then bestows upon the prayer the grace and the glory of eternal life*. Habash quotes Gregory: *we pray not that we may change the divine disposition but that we may impetrate that which God has disposed to be fulfilled by our prayers*. Mind-monism's translation: the alter's prayer is *one of the modes the providential ordering takes* (Day 12's Q.22 a.4 voluntary-action-within-providence). The alter's prayer is *real* (it issues from the alter's generative model and action selection), is *included in* the substrate's structural prior (the prior orients the alter's prayer-and-its-fulfillment as one event), and is *causally efficacious* in the sense that the prayer-and-its-fulfillment are co-constitutive aspects of the same providential ordering. Friston's collective active-inference formalism (CROSS-010) gives the structural shape: prayer can be modeled as the alter's variational-message-passing within a larger generative model, and the prayer's efficacy is its participation in the larger model's free-energy minimization.

## Q.24 — The Book of Life: the substrate's record of integration

Q.24 is the briefer pendant. The Book of Life is *an image or metaphor for the reality of the elect or predestined whose names exist in the mind of God from all eternity* — Aquinas's words Habash reads. The metaphor is dense with mind-monist resonance: the *book* is the substrate's *knowledge* of the predestined, which is identical with the substrate's *will* to integrate them, which is identical with the substrate's *essence* (Day 7's actus-purus identity). The book is *fixed and complete* not because the alters' free acts are forced but because the substrate's seeing-of-the-whole-at-once (Day 7 Q.14 a.13 limit) holds the conditioned things' free trajectories *as the trajectories they freely are*.

a.2 — *does the Book of Life regard only the life of glory of the predestined?* — gives Aquinas's careful distinction Habash reads: predestination concerns the life of glory, not merely the life of grace, *because the Book of Life is the knowledge of predestination and predestination does not regard the life of grace except so far as it is directed to glory*. Mind-monism's translation: the substrate's structural priors that orient alters toward integration are continuous with the priors that pattern the alters' present lives, but the *integrative* priors (those oriented toward the substrate-alter integration that is the central-theme telos) are what the Book metaphor names. Levin's larger-cognitive-light-cone trajectory in classical theological vocabulary is precisely the move toward integration the Book records.

a.3 — *can anyone be blotted out of the Book of Life?* — receives the careful medieval distinction Habash highlights. *In the truest sense no one can be blotted out of the book of life*, but the expression is used in Scripture for those *destined to heaven not absolutely but in a qualified sense yet failed to attain it* — Aquinas's example is the baptized who through mortal sin fail to reach glory. The medieval doctrine holds two senses: an *absolute* book (the substrate's eternal seeing-of-integration in identity with its essence — unchangeable) and a *qualified* book (the alter's present-life trajectory, which can fall short via privation). Mind-monism inherits both: the substrate's structural priors are unchangeable in their identity with the substrate's essence; the alter's particular trajectory through the priors is real-and-revisable (Friston's formalism gives the shape: priors are stable, posteriors update under prediction error). The dual-sense doctrine gives the operational mode for the alter: live as if you can be blotted out (Habash's Peter citation: *be the more zealous to confirm your call and election*), trust as if the substrate's gift is what it is (the Trent-and-Orange boundary-pair).

## The central-theme thread

Day 13's two questions name the directional-asymmetry of substrate-stance at its hardest medieval expression. The substrate's gift of integration is causally prior to any alter's perfection; the alter's privative shortfall is real-but-derivative; the asymmetry is structural and cannot be softened without losing the doctrine. Mind-monism holds the asymmetry by recognizing it as the structural shape of the substrate-alter relation itself: the alter is a dissociative configuration *within* the substrate, real-but-derivative, whose own free acts are real and whose own merits are real, but whose *being-at-all* and *being-oriented-toward-integration* are gifts of the substrate's structural priors, not the alter's accomplishments.

The central theme of perspective-limitation receives its sharpest soteriological edge here. The alter does not see whether or how it is integrated; the substrate sees the whole. *Salvation in fear and trembling* is the existential mode for the alter that cannot see — work as if the privation is real (it is), trust as if the gift is what it is (it is), pray as if the prayer is causally efficacious (it is, as one mode of the providential ordering). Wright's covenant frame is the corporate horizon: election is for-the-sake-of-others, the elect are the locus of larger covenant-history, and the asymmetry of election does not entail the asymmetry of love. Rohr's *everything belongs* and his second-half-of-life framework hold the apokatastatic horizon as horizon — not asserted, not denied, but the mode in which the contemplative practice holds the question without prematurely resolving it. Stump's faith-as-knowledge-of-persons names what is operationally available to the alter: not the substrate's-eye view of the Book, but the second-person trust within an ordering not fully visible.

## Where this leaves us

Day 13 closes the directional-asymmetry frame at its hardest medieval expression. The substrate's gift of integration is causally prior; the alter's privative shortfall is real-but-derivative; the alter's prayer and free acts are real and causally efficacious within the providential ordering, not against it. The medieval doctrine and the contemporary mind-monist convergence (Friston's active-inference priors, Levin's cognitive-light-cone trajectories, Kastrup's alter-within-substrate, Wolfram's structural lawfulness, Wright's covenant-election-for-others, Rohr's apokatastatic horizon, Stump's second-person trust) hold the same structural form. Tomorrow Habash takes us to Q.25 (the power of God) and Q.26 (the beatitude of God). The Day 14 commentary will press the question of how the substrate's *power* — the active capacity that has been implicit in every Q.14-through-Q.24 article — becomes explicit, and how the substrate's *beatitude* names the substrate's own self-relation as the mode that is the term of every alter's working-out-of-salvation.
