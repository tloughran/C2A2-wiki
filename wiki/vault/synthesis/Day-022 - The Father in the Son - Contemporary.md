# Day 22 — Contemporary Parallel: *Acts Without Change, Equality Without Subordination*

Day 21 closed the doctrinal-language compression of the Trinity (Q.39: essence and person; Q.40: persons as subsistent relations) and queued the question Day 22 must take up: if the persons are constituted by relations of opposition, and the relations manifest as notional acts (generation, spiration), what *kind* of acts are those? And once the acts are characterized, the second question follows: are the persons *equal* to one another, or does the relation-of-procession introduce subordination? Q.41 walks the notional-acts question through seven articles. Q.42 closes the substance-side of the Trinitarian doctrine with the equality-and-likeness articles — the medieval guardrails against any reading that would make the Son or the Spirit *less* than the Father.

The mind-first frame holds the load with two constraints Day 22 sharpens. First: the substrate's internal acts are *changeless* — the substrate does not move from potency to act in the procession, because there is no potency in the substrate. Second: the substrate's internal-relational-structure is *equality-preserving* — the relational distinctness does not introduce a hierarchy of being, because all three persons are the divine essence itself.

## Q.41 — Notional acts: relation, manifested

Q.41 a.1 — *are notional acts to be attributed to the persons?* — runs the constraint that has been threading the Trinitarian doctrine since Q.27. *Generation* (Father generates Son) and *spiration* (Father+Son spirate Spirit) are *notional acts* in the medieval sense — acts that *note* (mark, distinguish) the persons. The objection Aquinas heads off: acts in the ordinary sense involve change-from-potency-to-act; God has no potency; therefore God has no acts. Aquinas's careful reply: *since in God no change exists, the personal action of the one producing a person is only the condition of being principle to the person who is from the principle, which condition are the relations or the notions*. The notional acts in God *are* the relations themselves, considered under the aspect of *manifestation-of-the-relation*. The Gilby translation Habash quotes is exact: *the reality expressed is one; the differences are exclusively in the way of signifying between the relations and the notional acts*.

Mind-monism's translation lands cleanly. The substrate's self-knowing-act and self-loving-act are *not* changes from potency to act; they are the substrate's intrinsic self-presence considered under the aspects of self-knowing and self-loving. *Generation* and *spiration* are the same internal-relational-structure considered under the aspect of *what-the-relation-manifests-as-procession*. Friston's free-energy framework has a structural cousin at the alter scale: in the autopoietic limit (the system whose generative model is identical to its self-maintaining structure), the *act* of self-modeling is not a change but the system-being-itself; the inferential machinery is quiescent because there is no posterior to compute. At the substrate scale Aquinas reaches for, the *acts* of the Trinitarian persons are not changes but the substrate's intrinsic self-relational-structure.

Q.41 a.2 — *are the notional acts voluntary?* — runs a careful three-way distinction. *By will* in the sense of *something the will could do otherwise* — no, the Father does not generate the Son the way you and I might choose to do or not do something. *By necessity* in the sense of *external compulsion* — also no, the Father does not generate the Son under any external constraint. *By nature*, in a third sense — yes, the Father generates the Son the way you naturally will your own happiness: not because it could be otherwise (your nature is such that you cannot but will your good), but also not because something external forces it (it follows from your being-the-kind-of-thing-you-are). Aquinas's *faith of Damasus* formulation Habash quotes: *the Father begot the Son not by will nor by necessity, but by nature*.

Mind-monism's translation: the substrate's internal-relational-structure is not a *choice* (which would imply a substrate-prior-to-the-relations choosing them) and not an *external constraint* (which would imply something more fundamental than the substrate forcing the structure); it is the substrate-being-what-it-is. The relations are *constitutive* of the substrate considered as Trinitarian; abstract them and you do not get a more-fundamental substrate but an incoherence (Day 21 Q.40 a.3 already established this). Friston's autopoietic-limit gives the formal cousin: the generative model that is identical to the system's self-maintaining structure is *neither chosen nor forced* — it is the system-being-itself. The *naturalness* Aquinas's Q.41 a.2 reaches for has the same structural shape.

Q.41 a.3 — *do the notional acts proceed from something?* — closes a subtle question. The Son proceeds *from the Father* (not from nothing, not from pre-existing matter, not from anything outside the divine substance); the *from* here designates *consubstantial generating principle* (Aquinas's exact phrase), not *material principle*. *That which is produced from matter is made by a change of form, and whence it is produced; but the divine essence is unchangeable and is not susceptive of another form*. The medieval move that mind-monism inherits: the procession-from-the-Father is not creation-from-something (which would require a more-fundamental substrate from which the substance is taken), and not creation-from-nothing (which would put the Son outside the divine substance, making the Son a creature). It is *consubstantial procession* — the Son shares the Father's nature without any change-of-form or addition-of-substance.

Q.41 a.4–5 — *is there in God a power respecting the notional acts? does the power of begetting signify a relation?* — close Q.41 with the structural compression Aquinas needs to set up the Q.42 equality-articles. *Power of begetting* — *yes, there is in God a power of begetting* — but the power *signifies the divine nature directly, the relation indirectly*. The Father begets *via the divine nature* (which is identical with the Son's nature, by which the Son is *like in species* to the Father); the relation *paternity* is constitutive of the Father's distinctness, but the *acting-power* is essential, not personal. Habash's human-father analogy: *my dad does not beget me via his person, or I would be identical in person; my dad begets me via his human nature, which is why I am identical to his human nature*. The medieval analogy is structurally precise: the *power* runs in the essential register; the *distinctness* runs in the relational register.

Q.41 a.7 — *can several persons be the term of one notional act?* — closes Q.41 by tightening the trinity-not-quaternity guardrail. The medieval argument is structural: the persons are distinct by relation-of-origin; relations of origin admit only three structures (without origin, from one origin, from two origins via a third); therefore there are only three persons. Aquinas adds the omnipotence-defense: the *non-ability to generate multiple sons* is not a limit on God's power but a structural feature of the immateriality of the divine substance — *forms of one species are not multiplied except in respect of matter*. Mind-monism's translation: the substrate's internal-relational-structure has the shape it has by virtue of what the substrate is; multiplicity-by-matter is the alter-scale feature that does not transfer to the substrate.

## Q.42 — Equality without subordination

Q.42 a.1 — *is there equality in God?* — opens the equality-articles by addressing two scriptural passages that, on a surface reading, seem to imply subordination. *I go to the Father, for the Father is greater than I* (John 14:28) and *Christ Jesus, who though he was in the form of God, did not count equality with God a thing to be grasped* (Philippians 2:6). Aquinas walks the medieval guardrails. *The divine nature of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit is one; their glory is equal; their majesty is co-eternal* (athanasian creed). Inequality could only enter if one person were *greater in magnitude* (impossible — God is immaterial, no quantity), or *cause-of-another* in the efficient-causal sense (impossible — Day 18 Q.33 a.1 already established that *principle* in the Trinity is wider than *cause*), or *prior in time* (impossible — the processions are eternal), or *more perfect / more in act* (impossible — all three persons are identical with the divine essence, which is absolute act).

The Augustine formulation Aquinas closes the article with: *we understand equality to be in the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, inasmuch as no one of them either precedes in eternity, or excels in greatness, or surpasses in power*. Mind-monism's translation: the substrate considered as essence is one and absolute; the substrate considered under any of its three relational aspects is the same substrate considered under one aspect; therefore none of the three relational-aspects can be greater-or-lesser than the others. The relational distinctness is real-and-constitutive of the personal distinctness; the relational distinctness does *not* introduce a hierarchy of being.

Q.42 a.2 — *is the person proceeding co-eternal with his principle?* — runs the same constraint on the temporal axis. The Lagrange compression Habash quotes: *the proceeding persons are co-eternal with their principle because they proceed from a principle whose active power is always perfect by instantaneous action in the one unique instant of eternity; the intellect and the will of God are of course always in act; therefore the divine intellect is never without the Word, nor is the divine will ever without personal love or the Holy Spirit*. The Aquinas closer: *eternity excludes the principle of duration but not the principle of origin; thus the Son can have his origin from the Father and yet be co-eternal with the Father*.

Mind-monism's translation: the substrate's self-knowing-act and self-loving-act are *intrinsic* to the substrate — there is no temporal sequence in which the substrate first exists without them and then comes to have them. The substrate just *is* its own self-knowing-and-self-loving, and the persons (which are the substrate considered under those relational aspects) are co-eternal with the substrate-as-whence. Wolfram's ruliad gives a structural cousin: the totality and its threads are not temporally ordered; the threads are *of* the totality without coming-after-the-totality. The medieval *eternity-excludes-duration-not-origin* distinction is the ruliad's structure in classical vocabulary.

Q.42 a.3 — *is there an order of nature among the persons?* — closes a subtle question. The Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are equal in being, equal in glory, equal in power; but the medieval doctrine maintains that there is an *order of nature* among them — Father is unbegotten, Son is begotten, Spirit proceeds from both. *Where plurality exists without order, confusion exists; in the divine persons there is no confusion; therefore in God order exists*. The order is *of nature* (relational-structural), *not of essence* (because the essence is one). Mind-monism's translation: the substrate's internal-relational-structure is not symmetric (paternity is not the same relation as filiation; spiration is not the same relation as procession), and the asymmetry is real; but the asymmetry is *of relational aspect* and not of substance, so it does not introduce subordination.

Q.42 a.4–6 — *is the Son equal to the Father in greatness? is the Son in the Father, and conversely? is the Son equal to the Father in power?* — close Q.42 with the equality-doctrine applied across three more registers (greatness, mutual indwelling, power). The medieval pattern is the same: surface-reading of certain Johannine and Pauline passages can suggest subordination; careful reading shows that the apparent subordination is either Christological-as-incarnate (*the Father is greater than I* — said with respect to the Son's humanity, not his divinity) or relational-as-by-procession (the Son's *I do nothing of my own accord* refers to the relational-derivation of his nature from the Father, not to any inequality of being).

Aquinas's closer at Q.42 a.5 — the *mutual indwelling* — is the doctrinal anchor for the rest of the Tertia Pars. *The Son and the Father are in each other according to essence, according to relation, and according to origin*. Essence: Father and Son are identical with the divine essence. Relation: paternity and filiation mutually presuppose one another. Origin: the Word proceeds from the Father, but the Word *remains* in the Father (the procession is *immanent*, not transitive — Day 7 Q.14 a.4 already established the immanence-of-divine-act). Mind-monism's translation: the substrate's internal-relational-aspects are mutually constitutive — the Father-aspect cannot be considered without considering the Son-aspect (and vice versa), because the relations of opposition are what constitute the persons. The *mutual indwelling* is the medieval name for what mind-monism's *one-substrate-considered-under-multiple-relations* names structurally.

Hillary's formulation Aquinas brings forward at Q.42 a.6 is the doctrinal anchor: *the unity of the divine nature implies that the Son so acts of himself that he does not act by himself*. The Son's act is not a separate act from the Father's act (because the divine nature is one, and the operative power belongs to the nature); but the Son's act is *of himself* in the sense that the Son is the *whither* of paternity-relation and so is the proper terminus of the act-as-relation. The catechism formulation Habash quotes: *as the Trinity has only one and the same nature, so too does it have only one and the same operation*.

## The central-theme thread

Day 22 closes the substance-side of the Trinitarian doctrine with the constraints that have been gathering since Q.27: relations real-and-constitutive (not additive); persons distinguished by opposition of relation (not by substance); acts manifesting relations (not changing the substrate); equality preserved across all three persons (no subordination of being); order-of-nature without order-of-essence (asymmetric relations within one substance); mutual indwelling (the persons are in each other by essence-relation-origin). The mind-first frame holds the load: the substrate has internal-relational-structure that is constitutive of the personal distinctions, the structure is asymmetric (not all relations are reversible), but the asymmetry does not introduce hierarchy of being.

*Salvation in fear and trembling* on the equality-doctrine register has a sharper edge here. The alter's progressive participation in the substrate's life is *not* a participation in a *higher* part of the substrate at the expense of a *lower* part — there is no hierarchy of being among the persons, and so the alter's union with the Son does not exclude the Father or the Spirit. Wright's covenant theology gives the corporate-and-Christological frame: *the Spirit of his Son crying, "Abba, Father"* (Galatians 4) places the alter within the *whole* Trinitarian relation, not within a hierarchy. Rohr's contemplative practice is the operational mode: holding the equality-without-collapse and the relational-distinctness-without-subordination together without resolving prematurely.

## Where this leaves us

Day 22 closes the substance-side of the Trinitarian treatise. *Notional acts are relations manifested* (Q.41), *equality is preserved across all three persons* (Q.42), *order-of-nature exists without order-of-essence* (Q.42 a.3), *mutual indwelling* is the relational-anchor of the whole Trinitarian-personal doctrine. The mind-first frame holds the load with the same constraints that have run since Day 18: relations-internal-to-one-substrate are real-and-constitutive without multiplying the substrate; the asymmetry of the relations does not introduce hierarchy of being.

Tomorrow Habash takes us to Q.43 (the mission of the Divine persons — closing the Trinity with the *outward* face of the eternal processions) and Q.44 (the first cause of beings — pivoting from Trinity to creation). The Day 23 commentary will mark the pivot: the *missions* of the persons (the Spirit and the Son sent into time, the indwelling by sanctifying grace) are the *temporal* face of the eternal processions, and the *first-cause* doctrine begins the treatise on creation that Levin and Carroll become the contemporary primaries on. The transcript title — *Prime Matter and Quantum Physics* — flags Habash's Heisenberg-on-Aristotle move as the bridge.
