## Triplets

PRS-01:
  Label: P1 — The Enlightenment failure
  Problem: Modern moral philosophy cannot ground moral claims in tradition-independent reason; the result is emotivism (moral claims are merely expressions of preference)
  Resource: The Aristotelian-Thomistic tradition of virtue ethics, which grounds morality in practices, narrative identity, and teleological human nature
  Solution: Recovery of virtue ethics as a rationally superior alternative to the failed Enlightenment project; moral reasoning requires tradition, not escape from tradition
  Date Added: 2026-04-29
  Source: After Virtue (1981), Chapters 1-9 diagnostic argument
  Confidence: High

PRS-02:
  Label: P2 — Incommensurability without relativism
  Problem: If rational inquiry is tradition-constituted, how can traditions be rationally compared without collapsing into relativism?
  Resource: The concept of epistemological crisis — moments when a tradition's internal resources fail, forcing it to reconstruct itself by drawing on rival traditions
  Solution: Traditions can be rationally evaluated by their capacity to survive epistemological crises, solve their own problems, and render rivals intelligible; this is rational comparison without a neutral standpoint
  Date Added: 2026-04-29
  Source: Whose Justice? Which Rationality? (1988), Chapters 18-20
  Confidence: High

PRS-03:
  Label: P3 — Practice-virtue-institution
  Problem: How do intellectual and moral communities sustain themselves and transmit standards of excellence?
  Resource: The triad of practices (cooperative activities with internal goods), virtues (character traits that sustain practices), and institutions (social structures that house practices)
  Solution: Communities flourish when institutions serve practices and practitioners cultivate virtues; corruption occurs when institutional goods (money, power, status) displace the internal goods of practices
  Date Added: 2026-04-29
  Source: After Virtue, Chapter 14
  Confidence: High

PRS-04:
  Label: P4 — Narrative identity
  Problem: Modern philosophy treats the self as an atomistic rational agent; this makes moral development and accountability unintelligible
  Resource: The concept of narrative unity — a human life is a story with beginning, middle, and end, embedded in the stories of communities and traditions
  Solution: Personal identity is constituted by the narrative one inhabits; moral reasoning requires understanding oneself as a character in an ongoing story, not as an abstract rational agent
  Date Added: 2026-04-29
  Source: After Virtue, Chapter 15
  Confidence: High

PRS-05:
  Label: P5 — Second-first-language acquisition
  Problem: How can an outsider to a tradition come to understand it on its own terms rather than merely translating it into their own categories?
  Resource: The concept of learning a tradition's language as a "second first language" — acquiring not just vocabulary but the evaluative standards and perceptual habits internal to the tradition
  Solution: Genuine inter-tradition understanding requires inhabiting the rival tradition well enough to experience its internal coherence; this is what C2A2 agents attempt when they process a thinker's work
  Date Added: 2026-04-29
  Source: Whose Justice? Which Rationality?, Chapter 19; Three Rival Versions, Chapter 5
  Confidence: High
