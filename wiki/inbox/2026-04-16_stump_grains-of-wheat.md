## Summary
Stump's latest major work (Oxford University Press, 2024) examines seven biblical narratives—Job, Samson, Abraham, Mary of Bethany, the temptations and passion of Christ, and Ecclesiastes—arguing they yield a single emergent story in which human suffering is "defeated" by a good that outweighs it and could not be obtained without it. The book extends her narrative methodology from *Wandering in Darkness* and centers on non-propositional knowledge transmitted through stories.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
This is a direct sequel to *Wandering in Darkness* and represents the most significant extension of Stump's theodicy program since 2010. It deepens the claim that narrative knowledge is philosophically irreducible—a claim with immediate implications for how the C2A2 project structures its pedagogical tools and inter-tradition dialogues.

## Candidate PRS Triplets

PRS-CANDIDATE-01:
  Problem: Propositional philosophy of religion cannot capture the full meaning of suffering narratives — something is lost in abstraction
  Resource: Stump's theory of "Franciscan knowledge" (non-propositional, second-person) applied systematically to seven biblical narratives
  Solution: Narrative-mediated knowledge is philosophically rigorous and irreducible to propositions; it provides a form of understanding suffering that propositional theodicy cannot
  Confidence: High
  Evidence: The book argues that the emergent story across seven narratives reveals a good for suffering that "could not be gotten without the suffering, not even in a world without the Fall"

PRS-CANDIDATE-02:
  Problem: The problem of evil remains philosophically unresolved when treated only through analytic argument
  Resource: A unified reading of Hebrew Bible and New Testament suffering narratives as a single emergent story
  Solution: Suffering is "defeated" — not justified but redeemed — through a telos of personal union that requires vulnerability and loss as preconditions
  Confidence: High
  Evidence: The book examines Job, Samson, Abraham, Mary of Bethany, Christ's temptations and passion, and Ecclesiastes as variations on a single theme

## Cross-Tradition Signals
- **McGilchrist connection**: Stump's insistence on non-propositional, narrative knowledge aligns directly with McGilchrist's right-hemisphere mode of knowing — both argue that the left-hemisphere's propositional reduction misses what matters most
- **Fredrickson connection**: The telos of suffering in Stump is personal union — structurally identical to Fredrickson's positivity resonance as the biological basis of love-as-connection
- **Kastrup connection**: Suffering as a condition for deeper consciousness-integration maps onto Kastrup's dissociative idealism — suffering as the tension point where dissociated alters begin to re-integrate
