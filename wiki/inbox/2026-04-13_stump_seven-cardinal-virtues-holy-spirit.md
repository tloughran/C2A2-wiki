## Summary
A January 2026 Thomistic Institute podcast episode in which Eleonore Stump presents on the relationship between the seven cardinal virtues and the indwelling of the Holy Spirit within a Thomistic framework. The talk draws on Aquinas's account of the virtues as perfections of the soul's powers (intellect and will) and connects this to the theological claim that the Holy Spirit's indwelling transforms the agent's capacity for virtue — not by bypassing human powers but by perfecting them from within. This represents Stump's ongoing project of making Aquinas's philosophical psychology intelligible to contemporary audiences while preserving its theological depth.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
This talk advances Stump's distinctive contribution: showing that Thomistic virtue theory is not merely a historical curiosity but a living philosophical psychology with contemporary explanatory power. The connection between virtue, the soul's powers, and divine indwelling is central to Stump's argument that Aquinas offers a unified account of intellect, will, and transformation — the same Thomistic architecture that the C2A2 wiki tracks as potentially convergent with cognitive science accounts of agency and self-organization.

## Candidate PRS Triplets

PRS-CANDIDATE-01:
  Problem: Contemporary virtue ethics typically treats virtues as stable character traits or habits, but Aquinas's account embeds the virtues within a metaphysics of the soul's powers and their transformation by grace — this richer framework is underexplored in analytic philosophy
  Resource: Stump's exposition of Aquinas's account of the seven cardinal virtues as perfections of specific powers of the soul (practical reason, will, appetitive powers), integrated with the claim that the Holy Spirit's indwelling operates through — not around — these natural capacities
  Solution: A philosophical psychology of virtue that is simultaneously naturalistic (virtues perfect natural powers) and theological (grace operates within the natural order) — providing a non-reductive account of moral transformation that neither collapses into pure naturalism nor pure supernaturalism
  Confidence: Medium
  Evidence: Thomistic Institute podcast, January 30, 2026; builds on Stump's published accounts in "Aquinas" (2003) and subsequent work on Thomistic philosophical psychology

PRS-CANDIDATE-02:
  Problem: How does an agent move from moral deficiency to virtue — what is the mechanism of moral transformation? Purely voluntarist accounts (just try harder) and purely externalist accounts (grace alone) both fail to explain the phenomenology of genuine change
  Resource: Stump's Thomistic account of divine indwelling as a form of interior cooperation: the Holy Spirit perfects the soul's own powers, enabling the agent to will and act virtuously in a way that is genuinely the agent's own — Aquinas's synthesis of divine causality and human agency
  Solution: Moral transformation as cooperative causality — the agent's natural powers are genuinely operative but are elevated and directed by grace working within them. This offers a model of transformation that preserves agency while accounting for the inadequacy of unaided will
  Confidence: Medium
  Evidence: Stump's talk title directly links cardinal virtues to indwelling, suggesting a cooperative model; consistent with her published treatment of Aquinas on will and grace
