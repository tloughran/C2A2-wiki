## Summary
Fotowat, Levin, and colleagues at Tufts and the Wyss Institute created "neurobots" by implanting neural precursor cells into xenobot-style Xenopus ectodermal explants. The neural cells self-organized into functional nervous systems within the engineered living systems, producing organisms with altered morphology (more elongated), distinct gene expression patterns (including activation of visual perception genes), increased spontaneous activity, and more complex behaviors than non-neural biobots. This is the first demonstration of an engineered synthetic organism with a self-organizing nervous system.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
This paper extends Levin's research program from basal cognition in non-neural substrates (xenobots) to a direct bridge between non-neural and neural collective intelligence. It demonstrates that when you add neural tissue to an engineered living system, the resulting organism doesn't just gain reflexes — it self-organizes into qualitatively new behavioral and genetic repertoires, including unexpected activation of visual perception genes. This is empirical evidence for the continuity thesis (PRS-10): neural cognition grows out of the same substrate-independent collective intelligence that non-neural cells already exhibit.

## Candidate PRS Triplets

PRS-CANDIDATE-01:
  Problem: Can engineered living systems develop functional nervous systems that self-organize without evolutionary templates — and if so, what new capabilities emerge?
  Resource: Neurobots — xenobot-derived organisms with implanted neural precursor cells that self-organize into functional neural networks, altering anatomy, behavior, and gene expression
  Solution: Yes — neural tissue self-organizes within synthetic organisms, producing more complex behaviors, elongated morphology, and unexpected gene activations (including visual perception genes), demonstrating that nervous systems are not hard-wired evolutionary products but emergent properties of cellular collective intelligence
  Confidence: High
  Evidence: Published in Advanced Science (2026); direct experimental demonstration with controlled comparison between neural and non-neural biobots

PRS-CANDIDATE-02:
  Problem: The gap between non-neural basal cognition (xenobots) and neural cognition (brains) appears to require evolutionary scaffolding to bridge — is there a developmental continuum?
  Resource: The neurobot as a transitional form — a living system where neural tissue integrates with non-neural collective intelligence in real time, without evolutionary history
  Solution: Neurobots provide an empirical model for studying the emergence of neural cognition from non-neural substrates in a controlled setting, supporting the claim that neural and non-neural cognition sit on a continuum rather than being categorically different
  Confidence: Medium
  Evidence: Neurobots exhibit behavioral complexity intermediate between xenobots and natural organisms; gene expression patterns show activation of neural-specific programs without full evolutionary context

## Cross-Tradition Signals
**Friston (strong):** If neurobots self-organize neural networks that produce more complex predictive behavior, they may represent the emergence of higher-order active inference from lower-order FEP-satisfying systems. The transition from xenobot to neurobot could be modeled as an increase in the depth and complexity of the generative model — a direct empirical test of Friston's scale-free active inference (RGM framework).

**Kastrup (strong):** If consciousness is implicit in active inference (per the minimal consciousness paper already in pending), and neurobots represent a step-change in the complexity of self-organizing predictive systems, then neurobots may be empirical test cases for the emergence of richer forms of minimal consciousness from simpler substrates — relevant to Kastrup's dissociation model.

**Hawkins (moderate):** The self-organization of neural networks in neurobots without evolutionary templates parallels Hawkins' claim that cortical columns are general-purpose predictive units. Neurobots may show that columnar-like organization is an attractor state for neural tissue, not an evolved specialization.

**Wolfram (speculative):** The unexpected activation of visual perception genes in neurobots without eyes suggests that gene expression programs may be computational attractors in morphospace — resonant with Wolfram's view that complex behavior emerges from simple computational rules.
