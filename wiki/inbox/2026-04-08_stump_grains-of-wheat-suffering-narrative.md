## Summary
Stump's major 2024/25 OUP monograph examines a series of Hebrew Bible and New Testament narratives — Job, Samson, Abraham, Mary of Bethany, the Passion — to demonstrate that, read philosophically, these stories converge on a single argument: there is a possible world in which a good for suffering human beings outweighs their suffering and could not be obtained without it, even in a pre-Fall world. The book extends the theodicy project of *Wandering in Darkness* from abstract argument to detailed narrative exegesis, treating the stories themselves as a form of philosophical evidence about the structure of love, suffering, and union. The method is explicitly narrative-theological: Stump argues that propositional knowledge alone cannot capture what the stories teach — second-personal knowledge through narrative is the vehicle.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
This book directly advances PRS-05 (Suffering and Divine Permission) beyond the abstract theodicy of *Wandering in Darkness* into narrative epistemology — showing that suffering is not merely intelligible through final causality but is *known* through narrative encounter, a claim that bridges Stump's analytic Thomism with her emerging work on second-personal knowledge. It is a major new primary source not yet captured in the wiki.

## Cross-Tradition Signals
**McGilchrist connection (HIGH):** Stump's claim that narrative engages a mode of knowing inaccessible to propositional argument maps precisely onto McGilchrist's thesis that the right hemisphere's holistic, contextual, person-oriented mode of knowing is suppressed when only left-hemisphere propositional reasoning is permitted. Stump's "second-personal narrative knowledge" may be the theological name for what McGilchrist calls right-hemisphere knowing. This is a strong candidate for a cross-tradition dispatch.

**Fredrickson connection:** The claim that union and love are best understood through *experienced narrative* (not argument) resonates with Fredrickson's finding that love is a micro-moment of shared attentional synchrony — both resist being fully captured in propositional terms and require second-personal encounter.

**Kastrup connection:** Stump's possible-world argument implies that suffering occurs within a framework where love and union are the terminal conditions of consciousness — structurally parallel to Kastrup's model in which dissociation and re-integration are the fundamental dynamics of universal mind.

**MacIntyre connection (STRONG):** Stump's narrative epistemology is a direct implementation of MacIntyre's argument that tradition-constituted rationality operates through narratively structured inquiry — *Grains of Wheat* shows that the Thomistic tradition's deepest resources are precisely *narrative* resources, exactly what MacIntyre's account of tradition requires.
