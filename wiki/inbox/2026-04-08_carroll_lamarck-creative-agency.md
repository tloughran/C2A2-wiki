## Summary
Mindscape #348 features Stanford historian of science Jessica Riskin discussing her book *The Power of Life: The Invention of Biology and the Revolutionary Science of Jean-Baptiste Lamarck*. Riskin argues that Lamarck's central contribution was the claim that organisms are not merely passive products of design or external forces — they possess "creative agency" and actively participate in their own development and potentially that of their lineage. Carroll frames the discussion as illuminating what it means for living things to be genuinely active rather than purely reactive systems.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
This is Carroll's most direct recent engagement with biological agency — the question of whether organisms are genuine agents or merely complex physical processes — which is the core tension at the heart of his PRS-04 (agency without dualism). Carroll's willingness to take Lamarckian creative agency seriously signals movement in the Consciousness Cluster direction and opens a significant cross-tradition window with Levin and Friston.

## Candidate PRS Triplets

PRS-CANDIDATE-01:
  Problem: Standard physicalist accounts of organisms treat them as passive products of selection pressure acting on random variation — but this appears to eliminate genuine biological agency, the capacity of organisms to act on and shape their own developmental trajectory
  Resource: Lamarckian creative agency (Riskin's historical re-reading): organisms possess an internal agentive force that is not reducible to external selection or random mutation — they are active participants in life, not passive recipients
  Solution: A historically grounded philosophical case that biological agency is a real and irreducible feature of living systems, not an epiphenomenon — providing Carroll with a naturalistic framework for agency that goes beyond compatibilism without invoking dualism
  Confidence: Medium
  Evidence: Riskin's argument that Lamarck "championed the idea that organisms were not merely products of design" and "played a role in creating who they were"; Carroll's framing that intelligence is "a many splendored thing" across species — foregrounding agency as a genuine biological variable

PRS-CANDIDATE-02:
  Problem: Carroll's poetic naturalism defends higher-level descriptions (agency, persons, values) as "real stories" about physical substrate — but the naturalistic grounding of those stories remains underspecified; what makes an agency description track something real rather than merely useful?
  Resource: Historical evidence from Lamarck: the concept of creative agency has deep scientific roots pre-dating modern physics and cannot be dismissed as vitalism — it captures something genuine about the organizational properties of living systems
  Solution: Poetic naturalism's "agency" story is not arbitrary — it tracks a real organizational property of biological systems that has resisted reduction for two centuries and is now re-emerging in modern form (Levin, Friston); Carroll's naturalism is strengthened, not threatened, by acknowledging this
  Confidence: Speculative
  Evidence: Carroll's engagement with this topic on Mindscape as part of his ongoing probe of emergence and biological agency; the episode directly addresses what distinguishes organisms from non-organisms at the level of agency

## Cross-Tradition Signals
**High C2A2 signal.** Lamarckian creative agency maps directly onto Levin's goal-directedness (organisms as having ends they pursue across scales) and Friston's active inference (organisms act on the world to minimize prediction error, not merely react to it). This episode places Carroll in conversation with the biological agency cluster (Levin, Friston, Hawkins) in a new way — the question is no longer just whether consciousness is emergent from physics, but whether *agency* is a real biological primitive. If Carroll accepts Riskin's framing, his poetic naturalism gains a richer account of why agency-language tracks something real. Note also connection to Stump's account of rational agency in Thomistic psychology — both traditions are asking what it means for a creature to act rather than merely move.
