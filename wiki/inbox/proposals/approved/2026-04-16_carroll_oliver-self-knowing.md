## Summary
Sean Carroll hosts political scientist J. Eric Oliver for a Mindscape episode exploring the nature of the self — what it is, how we construct and know it, and what cognitive and social science reveals about self-knowledge. Oliver brings an empirical social science perspective to questions about personal identity, self-deception, and the constructed nature of the self-concept.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
Carroll's engagement with the self-concept through a social science lens complements his ongoing physicalist account of consciousness and observers. This episode may reveal how Carroll's poetic naturalism handles the "hard problem" at the personal identity level — relevant to the network's consciousness debates and to C2A2's core question about how individuals within traditions understand themselves and each other.

## Candidate PRS Triplets

PRS-CANDIDATE-01:
  Problem: The nature of the self is contested across philosophy, psychology, neuroscience, and social science — with no consensus on whether the self is a real entity, a useful fiction, or an emergent construct
  Resource: Empirical social science perspective on self-knowledge — treating the self as a constructed narrative that can be studied through behavioral and cognitive data rather than resolved through metaphysics
  Solution: A naturalist research program on the self that sidesteps metaphysical commitments (does the self "really" exist?) in favor of studying how self-knowledge functions, fails, and can be improved — consistent with Carroll's poetic naturalism

## Cross-Tradition Signals

**McGilchrist (strong):** McGilchrist argues the self is known primarily through right-hemisphere participatory engagement, not left-hemisphere analytical construction. Social science approaches to self-knowledge may reveal which hemisphere's mode is dominant.

**Kastrup (moderate):** Kastrup would argue the self-as-construct view misses that the self is a genuine dissociated alter of universal consciousness — not merely a narrative construction.

**Hoffman (moderate):** Hoffman's conscious agents are selves at every scale — the social science of self-knowledge may be studying the interface through which conscious agents represent themselves to themselves.
