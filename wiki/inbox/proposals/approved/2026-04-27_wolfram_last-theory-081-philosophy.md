## Summary
Mark Jeffery interviews Stephen Wolfram on the relationship between physics and philosophy. The episode's headline finding: Wolfram, despite his ongoing Templeton-funded "Computational Metaphysics" project and his new Salonnière series with professional philosophers (Jago, Seiberth), publicly voices a working skepticism about analytic-philosophical method — saying he is "much more at home in the concrete world of computer experiments than in the arguments of philosophers" and that when he looks at standard philosophical arguments he often "can't tell if they make sense or not. There's too much wiggling." The episode positions this not as dismissal but as a methodological tension Wolfram is actively living with as he engages traditional philosophy.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
This is a meta-level update on Wolfram's program: it tells us how Wolfram himself reads the engagement with professional philosophy that the wiki has been tracking through PROP-2026-04-18-002 (Seiberth dialogue), PRS-10 (Mark Jago dialogue), and PRS-06/PRS-07 (the "Metaphysics and the Ruliad" essay). Wolfram is signaling that he wants the philosophical scrutiny but does not concede the philosophers' methodological framing — he is bringing computational/empirical method *into* philosophy rather than translating his work *into* analytic philosophy's terms. This is a programmatic stance that has not yet been recorded in the wiki and that will shape how the Master Agent reads the entire computational-metaphysics dispatch line.

## Candidate PRS Triplets

PRS-CANDIDATE-01:
  Problem: Wolfram's computational metaphysics has been challenged to engage rigorously with analytic philosophy (PRS-10, the Jago dialogue). But there is an unstated meta-question: must the computational program adopt analytic philosophy's methodology to count as having engaged it, or can it engage from outside that methodology?
  Resource: Wolfram's stated methodological self-description (Last Theory #081): the philosopher's mode of argument feels under-determined ("too much wiggling") relative to the discipline of running computational experiments and observing what they actually do. His engagement with philosophers is a salonnière-style hosting, not an internal adoption of philosophical method.
  Solution: A reframing of the computational-metaphysics program as proposing a *new* methodology to philosophy — empirical ruliology applied to ontological questions — rather than as an attempt to satisfy analytic philosophy on its own terms. This is consequential: it converts the Jago/Seiberth dialogues from "compliance with philosophical scrutiny" into "method-export from computation to philosophy."
  Confidence: Medium
  Evidence: Direct quotes from Last Theory #081 host's framing: Wolfram "much more at home in the concrete world of computer experiments than in the arguments of philosophers"; "When I look at the arguments, I can't tell if they make sense or not. There's too much wiggling."

PRS-CANDIDATE-02:
  Problem: How does the Wolfram program adjudicate between competing philosophical accounts of its own central objects (the Ruliad, observers, emes) when those accounts use the formal vocabulary of analytic philosophy (modal logic, possible worlds, truth-makers)?
  Resource: Wolfram's implicit criterion in #081 — a philosophical formulation is taken seriously to the extent that it can be cashed out in or constrained by computational experiment. Pure verbal/formal manipulation without computational cash-out is treated as the "wiggling" he distrusts.
  Solution: A two-tier evaluation: (i) does the philosophical formulation produce a different prediction inside ruliology / observer theory? If yes, treat it as substantive. (ii) If no, treat it as one of many equivalent verbal frames over the same underlying computational structure. This sets an operational test for what counts as a genuine metaphysical disagreement vs. a mere notational dispute.
  Confidence: Speculative
  Evidence: Inferred from the contrast Wolfram draws between philosophical argument and computational experiment in #081, combined with the structure of his Mark Jago and Seiberth dialogues (he repeatedly returns the conversation to what computations would actually have to do).
