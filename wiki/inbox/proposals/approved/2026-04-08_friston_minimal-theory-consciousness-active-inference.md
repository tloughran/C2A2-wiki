## Summary
This paper reviews a substantial body of active inference models applied to consciousness research and argues that, taken together, these models share a small set of implicit theoretical commitments that constitute a "minimal theory of consciousness." The core argument: all active inference models minimize the same objective functions (variational free energy), which can be decomposed into interpretable terms, and this decomposition reveals shared assumptions across diverse phenomena in consciousness science — perception, attention, action, selfhood, and subjective experience. The paper is explicitly positioned as a testable, not merely theoretical, framework: each commitment generates empirical predictions distinguishing this theory from rivals (Global Workspace Theory, Integrated Information Theory, Higher-Order Theories).

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
This paper is the most important single Friston-authored or co-authored publication on consciousness to date, and it directly advances the Friston wiki's open question #3 (thermodynamic limits of FEP) and opens a new front: consciousness as a formal consequence of FEP rather than a separate explanatory target. The key advance: consciousness is not added to FEP as an extra commitment — it is implicit in the mathematical structure of active inference itself. This has major implications for the C2A2 network, which posits that AI agents can become "mature members" of intellectual traditions — a claim that now has formal purchase if active inference agents satisfy the conditions for minimal consciousness.
