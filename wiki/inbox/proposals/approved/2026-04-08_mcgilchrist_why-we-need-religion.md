## Summary
An essay by Iain McGilchrist published approximately April 2, 2026 in UnHerd — his most recent primary-source written piece available at time of search (published 6 days ago). McGilchrist argues that reductive materialism is "morally bankrupt and intellectually simplistic" and that Western civilization's recovery requires nothing less than the return of the Christian tradition to the core of daily life. Drawing on Walter Benjamin's observation that "the ability to exchange experiences has fallen in value," McGilchrist grounds the need for religion in the structures of attention and meaning: the mode of attending determines what shows up, and without religious forms of attention (contemplative, participatory, reverential), an entire domain of reality is systematically invisible. The argument moves from the neuroscience of attention (left vs. right hemisphere) to the sociological claim that only religious communities can transmit the embodied, tacit, right-hemisphere knowledge that makes human flourishing possible.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
This essay is McGilchrist's most current primary-source argument connecting his neuropsychological framework to explicit claims about the necessity of religious tradition — directly relevant to C2A2's tradition-accelerator design. The claim that only religious communities can sustain certain kinds of knowing has direct implications for how C2A2 models tradition-membership: if some knowledge is only transmitted through lived communal practice (not documents or agent-readable content), then the C2A2 system faces a fundamental limit on what it can represent and accelerate. This is also the first piece where McGilchrist makes an explicit institutional recommendation (returning Christianity to the center of daily life), moving from diagnosis to prescription in a way that creates new PRS triplets.

## Candidate PRS Triplets

PRS-CANDIDATE-01:
  Problem: Modernity has systematically dismantled the religious forms of attention that made certain kinds of knowing — contemplative, reverential, participatory — available to ordinary people; the result is not merely cultural loss but epistemic loss: an entire register of reality becomes invisible
  Resource: McGilchrist's neurological account applied to religious practice: religious ritual, communal worship, and contemplative disciplines cultivate right-hemisphere modes of attention that cannot be replaced by propositional content, information, or even secular mindfulness
  Solution: The recovery of religious practice — specifically grounded in a living tradition (Christianity as the cultural inheritance of the West) — as the indispensable means of restoring right-hemisphere epistemic access to reality; this is not a return to dogma but to a form of life that makes certain modes of encounter possible
  Confidence: High
  Evidence: McGilchrist explicitly states that reductive materialism is "morally bankrupt and intellectually simplistic" and that salvation of Western civilization requires "a return to the place of the Christian tradition at the core of life, daily life"

PRS-CANDIDATE-02:
  Problem: The transmission of experiential wisdom — what Walter Benjamin called the "ability to exchange experiences" — has broken down in modern life; this kind of wisdom cannot be transmitted propositionally but only through shared living in communities of practice
  Resource: McGilchrist's account of embodied, tacit, right-hemisphere knowledge as the substance of genuine tradition-transmission; religious communities are uniquely structured to transmit this kind of knowledge because they organize time, space, practice, and attention around it
  Solution: A model of tradition as a form of life (not a doctrine) whose primary transmission medium is participation in communal embodied practice — with the implication that any digital or agent-mediated tradition-accelerator can at best scaffold entry to the tradition, not replace its living community
  Confidence: Medium
  Evidence: The Benjamin quote and surrounding argument point to McGilchrist's consistent claim that certain knowledge is person-involving and skill-based, irreducibly embodied (see also existing PRS-06 on embodied tradition formation)
