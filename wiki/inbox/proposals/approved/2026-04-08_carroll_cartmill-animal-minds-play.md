## Summary
Mindscape #346 features Erica Cartmill (Indiana University / Santa Fe Institute), a cognitive scientist and anthropologist who studies play, teasing, humor, and comparative cognition across species. Cartmill discusses how great apes demonstrate sophisticated social intelligence — including surprise recognition, humor, and playful teasing that navigates the grey zone between aggression and play. Carroll frames the episode around the idea that intelligence is "a many splendored thing" that varies across species in unexpected ways: chimpanzees can excel at some numerical tasks but not others, suggesting that minds are not single-axis phenomena.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
Carroll's framing — intelligence as multi-dimensional, play as a cognitive window — is directly relevant to his emergence program: how does something as complex as humor arise from physical substrate? This episode also represents Carroll engaging with SFI-adjacent science (Cartmill is an SFI External Professor), extending his network's reach into the biological cognition cluster. For C2A2, it adds a new source of evidence for the "cognition all the way down" thesis (Levin) and challenges any simple linear account of mind.

## Candidate PRS Triplets

  Problem: Carroll's poetic naturalism needs a positive account of what "mind" tracks across different biological systems — not just that mind-language is a valid higher-level description, but what feature of complex systems it describes; animal comparative cognition provides the empirical test bed
  Resource: Comparative cognitive science (Cartmill): intelligence is multidimensional, non-linear across species — some numerical competencies are present in chimpanzees but not in ways that generalize; social intelligence (play, teasing, humor) is a distinct cognitive axis
  Solution: Carroll's emergence thesis gains empirical specificity: minds are not points on a single axis from simple to complex, but multi-dimensional systems where different capacities emerge independently — this supports the view that consciousness/agency are emergent real patterns, not just useful fictions
  Confidence: Medium
  Evidence: Carroll's own framing — "intelligence is a many splendored thing" — and Cartmill's research on play as a distinct cognitive axis separate from problem-solving intelligence

## Cross-Tradition Signals
Cartmill's work on animal play and social cognition connects to Levin's "cognition all the way down" program — both are asking what the minimal conditions for something like a mind are, and how far down the biological hierarchy proto-cognitive functions extend. Carroll's framing that intelligence varies "across species in unexpected ways" echoes Levin's claim that problem-solving is not uniquely neural. Also relevant to Friston: play behaviors may reflect free-energy minimization strategies rather than pure stimulus-response — active inference predicts that organisms will explore novel states (play) to build better predictive models. The SFI connection (Cartmill is External Faculty) reinforces Carroll's ongoing engagement with complexity science, a domain of overlap with Wolfram and Levin.
