## Summary

McGilchrist sits with Stanford lecturer Greg LaBlanc on the unSILOed podcast for an extended conversation that pivots the divided-brain thesis into the language of disciplinary range and decision-making. The frame is McGilchrist's claim that the two cerebral hemispheres embody distinct *modes of attention* — narrow, acquisitive, instrumental focus on the left versus broad, open, vigilant attention on the right — and that the mode of attention deployed *changes what is perceived*, not merely how it is interpreted.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition

LaBlanc is not a clinician or a humanities-side McGilchrist sympathizer; he runs an interdisciplinary podcast aimed at decision scientists, business academics, and broadly cross-trained scholars. McGilchrist articulating the hemispheric thesis in *that* register — and being received there as a source rather than as a controversialist — extends the institutional reach signaled by the 2026 ISSR Boyle Lecture (PRS-19) into a different professional audience. It also gives McGilchrist a fresh, comparatively concise statement of the *attention-changes-perception* claim that can be cited without dragging the full 1,500 pages of TMWT behind it.

## Candidate PRS Triplets

PRS-CANDIDATE-01:
  Problem: McGilchrist's hemispheric thesis is routinely described in the secondary literature as a claim about *interpretation* — as if the two hemispheres take the same world and frame it differently. That framing concedes the contested ground (a single antecedent world) and reduces the thesis to a perspectivism.
  Resource: McGilchrist's reformulation in conversation with LaBlanc — that the two modes of attention are not stylistic differences applied to a common percept but *constitutive* of what is perceived, such that broad/vigilant attention and narrow/acquisitive attention deliver different worlds rather than different views of the same world.
  Solution: A sharpened public statement of the *attention-constitutive* reading of the hemispheric thesis, suitable for citation in interdisciplinary settings (decision science, organizational behavior) where the perspectivist softening usually creeps in. Converts hemispheric-attention from a *frame* on perception into an *operator on* perception.
  Confidence: High
  Evidence: Episode framing — "how we attend changes what we perceive" — delivered to a non-clinical, business-academic audience as McGilchrist's first-person summary statement.

PRS-CANDIDATE-02:
  Problem: The "decision-making" literature in management and behavioral science has its own well-developed two-system frame (System 1/System 2, Kahneman). Without an active bridge, the McGilchrist hemispheric account either gets folded into System 1/2 and loses its specificity, or stays sealed in humanities discourse and never reaches that audience.
  Resource: McGilchrist's appearance on a podcast whose explicit franchise is *cross-disciplinary translation* (LaBlanc's editorial frame), where McGilchrist is invited to articulate the hemispheric distinction *next to* the System 1/2 frame rather than instead of it.
  Solution: Opens an explicit translation channel between hemispheric attention theory and dual-process decision science — a non-trivial extension of the C2A2-relevant claim that mature inhabitants of one tradition can speak to the cutting edge of another. Provides citation surface for any C2A2 work that wants to relate hemispheric attention to operating decision-science vocabularies without reducing one to the other.
  Confidence: Medium
  Evidence: unSILOed's editorial mission as an interdisciplinary podcast plus McGilchrist accepting that frame for the conversation rather than re-asserting humanities-only territory.

## Cross-Tradition Signals

- **McGilchrist × Hawkins**: Both posit attention/cortical-modeling as constitutive rather than interpretive. A right-hemisphere "broad vigilant attention" producing a different perceived world maps interestingly onto the Thousand Brains claim that each cortical column maintains its own model and that perception is a vote across models. Worth flagging for the pattern detector — possible operational bridge between hemispheric attention modes and column-population voting dynamics.
- **McGilchrist × Friston**: An attention-constitutive reading of perception fits naturally with the active-inference claim that perception is a generative model whose precision-weighting (i.e., attention) determines what is inferred. Possible formal bridge: hemispheric attention as a precision-weighting regime over the generative model, rather than as a separate cognitive faculty.
