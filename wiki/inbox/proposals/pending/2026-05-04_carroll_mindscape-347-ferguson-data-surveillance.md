## Summary
Mindscape episode 347 (March 16, 2026) — Carroll interviews law professor Andrew Guthrie Ferguson (George Washington University Law School) about his book *Your Data Will Be Used Against You: Policing in the Age of Self-Surveillance*. The episode covers the legal-epistemic terrain of how data emitted by smart devices becomes evidence available to authorities, the concept of "self-surveillance," and how this evidence type will reshape courts and police investigations. This is a Carroll-as-host episode rather than a solo, but the framing and emphasis place it inside Carroll's "physics of information / poetic naturalism" continuum applied to social-epistemic structures.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
Carroll's tradition has been heavily weighted toward physics, cosmology, and emergence in the wiki. This episode is an instance of Carroll deploying his methodological commitments — the conviction that the world is intelligible from a structural-informational standpoint and that ethical/legal questions are continuous with empirical structure — to a non-physics domain. It complements the recent Carroll AMA April 2026 (already in wiki) and Heilborn lecture series entries by demonstrating Carroll's interest in how poetic naturalism behaves when applied to socio-legal architectures of accountability. The Ferguson interview is also a useful seed for cross-program signals about agency and surveillance that connect to Friston / Hawkins agency phenotyping.

## Candidate PRS Triplets

PRS-CANDIDATE-01:
  Problem: As consumer devices generate dense streams of behavioral and locational data, the legal and epistemic frameworks that govern evidence have not caught up. When does data emitted by a person constitute testimony, observation, or admission — and against what warrant standards should it be admissible?
  Resource: Ferguson's "self-surveillance" framing — data is treated not as testimony given but as a continuous low-friction emission, requiring a fresh legal-epistemic category that is neither speech nor mere observation.
  Solution: A reframing of digital evidence as a hybrid epistemic object whose evidential weight depends on the inferential pipeline (sensor → log → query → retrieval) rather than on a discrete moment of communication. This pulls evidence theory toward a process / pipeline model that is structurally cousin to Carroll's emergence-as-pipeline thinking.
  Confidence: Medium
  Evidence: Mindscape 347 episode discussion of self-surveillance, courts, and evidence reform.

## Cross-Tradition Signals

- **Friston (agency phenotyping):** Self-surveillance-derived evidence is essentially behavioral phenotyping at scale. The Wilson et al. agency-phenotyping paper (PROP-2026-05-04-001) uses empowerment to read off agency from action–observation channels — the legal system is doing a coarser version of this on humans. Pipeline / channel framing is shared.
- **Hawkins (sensorimotor reference frames):** Ferguson's "data emitted by smart devices" is data about a sensorimotor agent's reference-frame trajectories. Hawkins' framework would predict that such data is highly inferential about cognitive state, supporting Ferguson's concerns about evidential power.
- **Wolfram (rulial-space search):** Self-surveillance evidence retrieval is a query path through a rulial-space of behavioral possibilities. Wolfram's "geometry of search path" framing of AI knowledge (PROP-2026-04-27-003 PRS-CANDIDATE-01) applies directly to evidence retrieval — both are second-order properties of pipelines.
- **Stump (narrative and accountability):** Self-surveillance evidence is *non-narrative* — it bypasses the agent's own account of their actions. This is a sharp contrast to Stump's narrative-knowledge thesis: a juridical regime built on self-surveillance is built on the structural opposite of Stumpian Franciscan knowledge.
