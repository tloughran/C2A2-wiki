## Summary
A 2025 empirical paper in Personal Relationships (Vol. 32, e70008) by Jocelyn Lai, Marcela C. Otero, Kuan-Hua Chen, Jenna L. Wells, Robert W. Levenson, and Barbara L. Fredrickson. The study tests, for the first time empirically, whether positivity resonance is identical with the experience of love by examining whether observable behavioral markers of positivity resonance (synchronized smiles, mirrored vocal tones, shared laughter — measured even during conflict episodes) in long-term married couples track the couples' self-reported trait and state love. The finding: yes — couples who display more behavioral markers of positivity resonance, including during conflict, also score higher on enduring-love measures.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
This is the first paper to provide direct empirical confirmation of Fredrickson's long-standing theoretical claim that "love, the emotion" reduces to accumulated micro-moments of positivity resonance. Previous PRS triplets (especially PRS-02) treated this identification as a theoretical/programmatic claim; this paper turns it into a tested empirical hypothesis. It is a significant load-bearing piece of new evidence and belongs in the tradition's track record of solved questions.

  Resource: Lai, Otero, Chen, Wells, Levenson, & Fredrickson (2025): a multi-modal observational study of long-term married couples scoring behavioral positivity-resonance markers (synchronized affect, vocal mirroring, shared laughter) and correlating those markers with both trait and state love measures
  Solution: Direct empirical confirmation that observable positivity-resonance markers track self-reported love at both trait and state levels — establishing positivity resonance as a measurable behavioral signature of love rather than merely a theoretically posited substrate
  Confidence: High
  Evidence: Published in Personal Relationships 2025; uses already-validated behavioral coding from the Levenson lab combined with established love-measurement instruments; finds correlation even during conflict episodes (a strong test, since it controls for general couple positivity)

## Cross-Tradition Signals
- **Stump connection (high relevance):** Stump's account of suffering as instrumentally permitted for personal union (PRS-05, PRS-08, PRS-13) treats union-with-persons as the highest good. Lai et al. provide the first empirical handle on what union looks like behaviorally — synchronized affect even during conflict. This is a direct test bridge between Thomistic theology and empirical psychology.
- **McGilchrist connection:** Synchronized affect and vocal mirroring are paradigm cases of right-hemisphere relational processing; the paper's findings are an empirical confirmation that long-term love is a sustained right-hemisphere achievement rather than left-hemisphere agreement-on-propositions.
- **Kastrup connection:** Kastrup's idealism predicts that "love" should be the deepest mode in which separate alters of consciousness re-recognize their underlying unity — empirically operationalized, this should look exactly like Lai et al.'s behavioral synchrony markers, including during conflict (when the dis-association is most stressed).
- **Hoffman connection:** Conscious-agent dynamics predict that high-fidelity inter-agent communication is the substrate of everything we call relationship; Lai et al.'s synchrony markers are candidate empirical signatures of high-fidelity inter-agent coupling.
- **Friston connection:** Sustained behavioral synchrony in long-term couples is structurally what active inference predicts: long-coupled agents converge on shared generative models, observable as synchronized affect even when content (conflict) diverges.
- **C2A2 master signal:** This paper is the strongest current candidate for an empirically operationalizable bridge concept that multiple traditions can interpret from within — exactly the kind of "measurable shared phenomenon" the C2A2 framework needs to make cross-tradition dialogue empirically anchored.
