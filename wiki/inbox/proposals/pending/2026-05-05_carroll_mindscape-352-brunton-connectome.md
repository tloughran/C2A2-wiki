## Summary
Mindscape #352 features neuroscientist Bing Wen Brunton (University of Washington) on connectomes and bodily motion. The discussion centers on how the recent fruit-fly connectome — a complete neuron-by-neuron wiring diagram — can be used to understand how neural circuits guide bodily behavior, and what the next step is in linking connectomes to embodied cognition. The conversation foregrounds the methodological gap between "wiring diagram" and "what the organism actually does" — a hard problem requiring data-science, applied math, and biology in concert.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
For Carroll's poetic naturalism, the Brunton conversation is empirical traction for the emergence thesis. Carroll's open question — what the higher-level descriptive vocabulary of "mind" or "behavior" actually tracks in the physical substrate — is exactly what connectome-to-behavior research operationalizes. It also bears on the Active Research Question "How should we understand emergence — is it purely epistemic or ontologically significant?" If the connectome plus dynamics determines behavior up to noise, that's a test case for emergence as ontologically significant pattern-rather-than-bookkeeping. Lastly, this is another Mindscape episode where Carroll brings a rival computational/biological paradigm into conversation with his physicalism — the podcast-as-tradition-dialogue function the C2A2 wiki has flagged as a structural model.

  Solution: A concrete test bed for Carroll's emergence-via-supervenience picture: if behavior supervenes on connectome plus dynamics, then "fly behavior" is real-but-emergent in Carroll's sense; if not, the residue is informative about what physicalism is missing
  Confidence: Medium
