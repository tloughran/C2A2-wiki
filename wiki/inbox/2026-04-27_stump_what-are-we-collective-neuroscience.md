## Summary
Stump's published version of her 2025 Maritain Lecture (Notre Dame) appears in the Cambridge journal *Religious Studies* (DOI prefix S0034412525). The paper takes recent neuroscience of the mirror-neuron system and other "collective neuroscience" findings as evidence that human persons are constitutively related to one another at the neural level, and argues that explaining what we are requires a metaphysics of *united somethings-or-others* — composite entities that include human persons as constituents without dissolving them. Stump deploys hylomorphism to ground philosophical and theological notions of "a people," communal guilt and shame, the church as the body of Christ, and human perfection as likeness to a triune God.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
This is Stump's most direct treatment to date of the Thomistic metaphysics of *collective entities* — exactly the conceptual pivot the C2A2 framework needs to ground its claims about traditions, communities, and second-first-language speakers as intelligible *kinds*. It moves Stump's program past individual hylomorphism (soul as form of body) into the metaphysics of supra-individual wholes that are nonetheless real, formed entities. Not yet captured in the wiki and arguably the most important new Stump piece for the C2A2 project.

## Candidate PRS Triplets

PRS-CANDIDATE-01:
  Problem: Contemporary metaphysics has no rigorous account of supra-individual wholes (peoples, communities, the church) that can include persons as constituents without either reducing the whole to an aggregate or dissolving the persons into a collective
  Resource: Thomistic hylomorphism extended via collective-neuroscience findings (mirror-neuron system, neural coupling, shared affective-cognitive substrate) to model "united somethings-or-others" as genuinely formed composite entities
  Solution: A hylomorphic metaphysics of the collective — peoples, communal guilt and shame, the church-as-body-of-Christ — in which the supra-individual whole has a real form that organizes its human constituents without dissolving their personhood
  Confidence: High
  Evidence: Stump explicitly argues that mirror-neuron-system findings make possible "a kind of direct and immediate knowledge" between persons that requires metaphysical accommodation in a theory of what we are

PRS-CANDIDATE-02:
  Problem: Theological claims about "the body of Christ" and "the people of God" are routinely treated as either metaphors or ecclesiological shorthand because no rigorous metaphysics of corporate persons is available in modern philosophy
  Resource: Stump's collective-neuroscience-informed hylomorphism, in which neural-level interpersonal coupling provides the empirical anchor for a metaphysics of corporate substance
  Solution: A non-metaphorical reading of "body of Christ" and analogous corporate-person claims — these become claims about real hylomorphic composites, empirically grounded but not reducible to neural mechanism
  Confidence: Medium
  Evidence: The lecture explicitly addresses "the church as the body of Christ" and "the understanding of human perfection as a likeness to a triune God" as test cases for the proposed metaphysics

PRS-CANDIDATE-03:
  Problem: MacIntyre's tradition-constituted rationality posits the tradition as a real bearer of inquiry, but lacks a metaphysical account of how a tradition is one thing — an entity whose constituents are persons but whose inquiry exceeds any individual's
  Resource: Hylomorphic collective-substance framework: the tradition as a united-something-or-other whose form organizes its human constituents' inquiry over time
  Solution: A Thomistic-MacIntyrean metaphysics of tradition: traditions are real composite entities, not aggregates of individual minds; their persistence across generations is form-persistence, not just memetic survival — directly serving the C2A2 architecture
  Confidence: Speculative
  Evidence: Implicit in the framework — the lecture's apparatus naturally extends from "people," "church," and "communal guilt" to MacIntyrean traditions, though Stump does not explicitly draw this extension here
