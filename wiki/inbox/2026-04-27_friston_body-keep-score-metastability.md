## Summary
Kotler, Mannino, Fox & Friston (Frontiers in Systems Neuroscience, April 2026) argue from an active-inference perspective that the popular "body keeps the score" framing of trauma is biologically inaccurate. The paper reframes trauma as a disturbance in the brain's **metastability** — the capacity to flexibly switch between coherent neural states — and proposes that recovery is the restoration of metastable dynamics, modeled formally within predictive coding and FEP. This is Friston's first major published intervention into the trauma-and-therapy literature, opening a formal bridge between active inference and clinical practice.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
This paper extends FEP into clinical territory in a publicly visible way (popular trauma framing) while maintaining mathematical rigor (metastability as formal dynamic). It introduces **metastability** as a cardinal FEP concept: the capacity for the inference engine to traverse multiple coherent states without collapsing to either rigidity or chaos. This is a major addition to the wiki's account of how FEP applies to mental health and adds a new clinical axis (trauma) alongside the existing computational psychiatry axis.
