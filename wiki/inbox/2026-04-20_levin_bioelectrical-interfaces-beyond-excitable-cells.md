## Summary
Perspective/review article published in Advanced Materials Interfaces (April 8, 2026) co-authored by Michael Levin with a materials-science team led by Cadinu. The paper argues that bioelectrical interface technology — historically developed for *excitable* cells like neurons and cardiomyocytes — should be extended to *non-excitable* cells (epithelial, stromal, tumor, senescent). The authors map current and emerging solid-state interface platforms (microelectrode arrays, organic electrochemical transistors, conducting polymer scaffolds) onto three translational targets: cancer (bioelectric reversion of malignant phenotype), aging (modulating senescent-cell bioelectric state), and gene expression modulation (voltage-driven transcriptional control). The key claim: the bioelectric layer is a universal control surface for cell behavior, and materials science is now in a position to deliver clinically deployable interfaces.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
This paper operationalizes Levin's theoretical program on the materials-science side. Previous Levin PRS triplets (PRS-07, PRS-11 — bioelectric reprogramming therapeutics, cancer as dissociative identity disorder of the body) articulate the *conceptual* case that bioelectricity is a therapeutic layer distinct from genetic hardware. This paper is the *engineering* complement: it argues that the hardware to read/write this layer is now buildable for non-excitable tissue, which is where most of the translational opportunity (cancer, aging, regenerative medicine) lives. It also extends the diverse-intelligence claim empirically — if voltage-driven transcriptional control works on non-excitable cells, the boundary between "excitable" and "non-excitable" becomes a gradient, not a categorical distinction, matching Levin's broader continuity thesis.

Directly strengthens the read/write interface claim from the UCSF bioelectric-interface talk (PROP-2026-04-20-001) by showing the materials infrastructure is catching up with the theoretical vision.
