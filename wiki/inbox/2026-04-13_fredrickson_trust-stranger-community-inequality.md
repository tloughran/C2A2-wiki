## Summary
A 2025 study by Taylor N. West, Catherine J. Berman, B. Keith Payne, Keely A. Muscatell, and Barbara L. Fredrickson published in the Journal of Happiness Studies. Across one correlational and two experimental studies (N = 1,867), the researchers demonstrate that community trust — whether naturally occurring or experimentally manipulated — predicts the positive emotional quality of interactions with strangers but not with close others. Critically, when participants were randomized to perceive their community as high in economic inequality, they expected lower-quality connection with a stranger later that day, an effect mediated by reduced community trust. The findings establish that structural conditions (inequality) shape interpersonal micro-moments (positivity resonance with strangers) through a trust mechanism.

## Why This Matters for This Tradition
This paper provides the most direct empirical evidence yet that Fredrickson's multi-level conducive conditions framework (presented at UBC 2025) is correct: structural factors like economic inequality do not merely correlate with reduced social connection — they causally reduce the quality of positivity resonance with strangers via community trust as a mediating mechanism. This moves Fredrickson's theory from the dyadic to the structural level with experimental evidence, completing the chain from macro-level conditions to micro-level emotional experience. It is a significant advance over the previously captured multi-level framework proposal (PROP-2026-04-08-008), which described the framework; this paper provides the causal evidence.

PRS-CANDIDATE-01:
  Problem: Fredrickson's multi-level conducive conditions framework identifies structural factors (economic inequality, built environment) as modulators of positivity resonance, but the causal pathway from structural conditions to micro-level emotional experience with strangers has not been experimentally established
  Resource: West, Berman, Payne, Muscatell & Fredrickson (2025): experimental manipulation of perceived economic inequality reduces expected quality of stranger connection, mediated by community trust — establishing a causal structural → trust → resonance pathway across three studies (N = 1,867)
  Solution: Community trust is the identified mediating mechanism by which structural inequality degrades positivity resonance with strangers. Interventions targeting community trust (not just individual skills) are necessary for restoring stranger-connection in unequal contexts — a structural, not merely interpersonal, intervention target
  Confidence: High
  Evidence: "When randomized to perceive their own community as high in economic inequality, people expected lower quality connection with a stranger later that day, an effect mediated by reduced community trust"

PRS-CANDIDATE-02:
  Problem: Why does economic inequality specifically damage weak-tie (stranger) connection while leaving close-relationship quality intact? This asymmetry is theoretically puzzling and practically important for understanding the loneliness epidemic
  Resource: The finding that community trust predicts positive emotional quality of interactions with strangers but NOT close others — suggesting that trust operates as a threshold condition specifically for encounters with unknown others, where prior relational history cannot substitute for community-level trust
  Solution: Close relationships are buffered by relational history and reciprocal knowledge; stranger interactions depend on a generalized trust scaffold that inequality erodes. This explains why the loneliness epidemic can coexist with intact close relationships — it is a collapse of the trust infrastructure for weak-tie connection
  Confidence: High
  Evidence: Community trust "predicts the positive emotional quality of interactions with strangers, but not close others" across correlational and experimental studies

## Cross-Tradition Signals
**C2A2 ARCHITECTURAL SIGNAL — STRONG FLAG:** This paper has direct implications for C2A2 design. Inter-tradition dialogue is, by definition, dialogue between "strangers" to each other's paradigms. If community trust is the mediating mechanism for stranger-connection quality, then C2A2's architecture must deliberately build trust infrastructure — shared norms, transparent governance, visible fairness — as a precondition for productive inter-tradition positivity resonance. Without trust architecture, even well-designed dialogue formats will underperform in contexts of perceived inequality or unfairness. Flag for master agent.

**Friston connection:** Community trust as a prior that shapes expectations about stranger-interaction quality is a Fristonian construct — it is a socially inherited generative model that sets precision-weighting on predictions about unknown others. Economic inequality degrades this prior, reducing expected precision of positive outcomes from stranger-encounters.

**Stump/MacIntyre connection:** MacIntyre argues that traditions require shared practices that generate trust and mutual intelligibility. Fredrickson's finding that community trust is the mechanism for stranger-connection quality provides the empirical substrate for MacIntyre's philosophical claim — traditions that erode their trust infrastructure (through internal inequality, for example) will lose the capacity for productive external engagement.

**McGilchrist connection:** Trust in strangers requires attending to the other with openness before categorization — McGilchrist's right-hemisphere mode. Inequality may shift attention toward self-protective, categorizing (left-hemisphere) modes, degrading the attentional prerequisite for genuine encounter.

**Levin connection (speculative):** Trust as a community-level variable that shapes individual interaction quality parallels Levin's morphogenetic fields as tissue-level variables that shape individual cell behavior. Both are examples of top-down contextual modulation of local agent dynamics.
