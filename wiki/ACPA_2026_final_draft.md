## I. The Technological Revolution: Accelerator Systems for Traditions

We are in an unprecedented moment of exponential growth in agentic AI development. This paper sketches two interrelated phenomena: (1) the technical infrastructure now enabling the acceleration and interaction of traditions, and (2) the emergence of a new interdisciplinary tradition born from this very technological capability. The pace of change is such that any technical description risks obsolescence within months. Yet the underlying principle is stable: for the first time, traditions can interact with each other through systems that facilitate rich, fully-informed dialogue at scale.

### The OpenClaw Revolution

In late November 2025, Austrian developer Peter Steinberger released OpenClaw (initially called Clawdbot), an open-source agentic framework that became one of the fastest-growing repositories in GitHub history. OpenClaw represents the viability of what might be called "full-time open-sourcering" of the agentic revolution. Unlike earlier chatbot interfaces that wait for prompts, OpenClaw agents run continuously on local machines, capable of managing emails, controlling web browsers, and executing multi-step workflows through standard messaging applications.

The inevitability of OpenClaw is captured in Andrej Karpathy's observation that Steinberger simply executed what was already implicit in the state of AI capabilities: if he hadn't built it, someone else would have within days. This is the mark of a technology whose time has come.

Since OpenClaw's January 2026 rebranding and rapid ascent to over 250,000 GitHub stars by March 2026, multiple iterations have emerged. NVIDIA NemoClaw (March 16, 2026) introduced enterprise security through kernel-level sandboxing and policy engines. Anthropic Cowork (January 2026, entering general availability April 2026) represents the most stabilized agent framework to date, integrated directly into the Claude desktop application. OpenClaw itself continues releasing at a pace commercial software vendors cannot match — version 2026.4.27 as of late April 2026.

### The Acceleration Paradigm

What we are witnessing is not merely incremental improvement but exponential acceleration. The frontier moves so quickly that previous generations of capability seem decades old within months. As Karpathy noted: by December 2025, he had written virtually no code by hand — 80–100% of his contributions came through agentic systems. This shift changes what *skill* means. The old skill was coding. The new skill is the capacity to imagine what is possible in the new world and to specify it with sufficient clarity that an agent can execute it.

### Computational Theology and Natural Law

Among the imaginable possibilities opened by this acceleration: the interaction of well-prepared traditions under conditions approaching full information, where mature practitioners of each tradition can declare they have no further relevant knowledge to offer, and where the evidence of that interaction produces new understanding.

Aquinas called theology a *scientia* — a master science that orders all others toward the ultimate object of human knowing. Now we can imagine, and are already executing, a computational theology or computational natural law: a science in which traditions interact under conditions approaching full information, generating evidence of how human understanding develops when agents work together toward shared goals. I proposed something like this in a 2021 paper to the de Nicola Center for Ethics and Culture at Notre Dame — "Toward a Computational Natural Law Defense of the Sanctity of Human Life" — as a programmatic possibility. Four years later, I am reporting it as an ongoing practice.

Because it is imaginable and executable, it is already being executed.

### The Karpathy Wiki as Infrastructure

One concrete realization of this emerging tradition is the **Karpathy Wiki** pattern, now being scaled across OpenClaw and other agentic systems. Rather than retrieving raw documents at query time (traditional retrieval-augmented generation), the Karpathy pattern has AI agents compile incoming sources into a persistent, cross-linked markdown wiki. This wiki compounds with every question, becoming a living knowledge base that evolves as the agent interacts with new information. The infrastructure and the subject matter intertwine: the system used to study living traditions is itself becoming one.

## II. Traditions: Definition and Framework

### What Is a Tradition?

A tradition is a social structure — it contains people, and now AI agents. It has four constitutive features, all of which can evolve continuously but not discontinuously.

First, it has **articulated goals** — purposes the tradition pursues. Second, it has **problems** — difficulties that arise in pursuing those goals, generated by the tradition's own internal development as much as by external pressure. Third, it has **resources** — conceptual, practical, and material tools available to address those problems. Fourth, it has a **track record of solutions** — the accumulated attempts, successes, and instructive failures that constitute the tradition's living memory. Goals may be re-articulated; resources may change character; solutions may shift. Even paradigm shifts can occur, but they are recognized within the context of a larger, encompassing paradigm.

### Examples and Scope

Disciplinary traditions: the physics tradition encompasses the standard model and relativity. Mature members know how to work within it; newcomers know how to study to become mature members. However, physics itself is fractured in MacIntyre's sense, because the implications of physics for other traditions — metaphysics, theology, ethics — are not questions physics itself can answer. Figures like Sean Carroll (*The Big Picture*, 2016) articulate a *broader* tradition — physicalism — that makes claims about what physics means, claims that exceed physics itself.

Traditions need not be academic. The Huguenots, living across centuries in a shared way of life, embodied a tradition. More broadly, any sustained application of practical wisdom to shared goals, reasoned and articulated and historically rooted, constitutes a tradition.

### MacIntyre's Formal Account

Alasdair MacIntyre's account of tradition-constituted rationality develops across three major works — *After Virtue* (1981), *Whose Justice? Which Rationality?* (1988), and *Three Rival Versions of Moral Enquiry* (1990). The misreading that matters most for our purposes is the conflation of tradition with custom: treating a tradition as a body of transmitted texts rather than as an ongoing argumentative practice. What distinguishes a living tradition from a dead one is not the age or sophistication of its texts but the vitality of the argument those texts sustain.

A living tradition has at minimum three formal properties. It is **internally structured**: it contains not merely opinions but a practice of reasoning with its own standards of good argument, its own conception of what constitutes a genuine problem, and its own criteria for distinguishing better answers from worse ones. These standards are tradition-internal but genuinely rational, not arbitrary. It is **self-correcting**: it possesses the capacity to recognize its own inadequacies and to respond by reconstructing itself, sometimes by drawing on rival traditions. MacIntyre calls these moments of reconstruction *epistemological crises*, insisting that the capacity to endure them and emerge with enhanced coherence is the primary mark of rational vitality. It operates through the **practice-virtue-institution triad**: practices are cooperative activities with internal goods; virtues are the character traits that sustain practices; institutions are the social structures that house practices while remaining permanently susceptible to the corruption that comes when external goods displace internal ones.

There is a fourth property, less often emphasized: **second-first-language acquisition**. Genuine understanding of a rival tradition requires more than translation into one's own categories. It requires learning the rival tradition's evaluative language as a second first language — inhabiting its internal logic well enough to experience its coherence from the inside.

### Kuhn, MacIntyre, and the Rationality of Paradigm Shifts

Thomas Kuhn established (*The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*, 1962) that within normal science operating under a single paradigm there is consensus on goals, problems, resources, and acceptable solutions. Across paradigm shifts, there is no neutral, fully rational metric for progress — only change. There is no progress *through* paradigms, only *within* them.

MacIntyre accepts this but adds a crucial qualification. A paradigm shift can come to seem rational in retrospect when what was promissory but under-evidenced at the time of the shift proves itself through its subsequent track record. Einstein's shift from Newtonian to relativistic mechanics becomes rationally justifiable because relativity successfully lifted physics out of its epistemological crisis. However — and this is essential — this rationality is only visible *to those who adopt the paradigm*. Those who remain committed to the prior framework will not see it the same way. MacIntyre does not dissolve this condition; he names it. Cunningham's analysis of the intractable disputes concerning natural law (2007) and MacIntyre's own response (2016) demonstrate that even within the Thomistic tradition this problem is genuinely unresolved.

### The Formation of New Traditions

What we are witnessing is the liquefaction and re-congealing of academic collegiality. Questions of who one needs to talk to, where one publishes, and what constitutes a mature practitioner are in flux. But they are starting to stabilize around new patterns. These emerging formations are best understood as new sub-disciplines working under a shared emerging paradigm that cuts across traditional disciplinary boundaries. The interdisciplinarity appears to be constitutive rather than transitional — it is not a phase on the way to a new discipline but a stable new form of inquiry.

## III. An Emerging Interdisciplinary Tradition: The Paradigm of Mind as Fundamental

### The Question of What Is Fundamental

There is no single pathway through the fifteen thinkers whose work is currently being synthesized within C2A2's wiki network. But they all converge on a common question and a common direction. The question: *What is fundamental to reality?* The prevailing answer — physicalism — holds that the standard model of particle physics tells us what ultimately exists, and everything else, including consciousness, is derivative. The emerging alternative proposes that minds — agents with goals — are fundamental. Not consciousness as an emergent property of complex systems, but consciousness, or mind, or agency, as the basic constituent of reality. And more radically: not just minds everywhere, but *mind only* everywhere.

### Epistemological Crises as Entry Points

The case for this alternative emerges not from a single field but from multiple independent disciplines each encountering its own internal crisis and finding that the crisis points in the same direction.

**Biology and Basal Cognition.** Michael Levin has become a central figure in recognizing that intelligence and agency operate at far more fundamental levels than brains. His key criterion for scientific progress is whether a framework generates a fruitful empirical research program. By this criterion, the extension of cognitive categories to cellular and sub-cellular levels is mandatory: you cannot do contemporary developmental biology — cannot explain regeneration, morphogenesis, or the goal-directedness of embryonic development — without recognizing basal cognition. The xenobot work at Tufts is not metaphorical mind-talk; it is an empirical research program that has produced interventions. Basal cognition is real, measurable, and manipulable.

**Physics and the Observer Problem.** Physics faces multiple interlocking crises whose common thread is the apparent irreducibility of the observer. The tension between general relativity and quantum mechanics remains unresolved after a century. The measurement problem in quantum mechanics suggests that information, observation, and perhaps consciousness play a constitutive role in physical reality. Nima Arkani-Hamed's work on the amplituhedron suggests that spacetime is not a fundamental feature of reality but an emergent one. Stephen Wolfram's physics project derives quantum mechanics and relativistic spacetime from simple computational rules operating on a hypergraph, with the key ingredient being observers whose limited computational resources generate the appearance of continuous spacetime. The observer is not incidental to the physics; the observer's limitations *generate* the laws of physics as we observe them.

**The Hard Problem and the Dissolution of Physicalism.** Donald Hoffman argues that the strategy of reducing consciousness to physical processes has not merely failed but cannot in principle succeed. In *The Case Against Reality* (2019), he contends that our perceptual systems were shaped by natural selection not to represent reality accurately but to guide adaptive behavior efficiently. Our sensory interfaces are, in his analogy, more like a desktop GUI than a transparent window onto the world's actual structure. More radically, he proposes that spacetime and physical objects are what conscious experience looks like when modeled from the outside; consciousness is the layer beneath the interface.

**The Free Energy Principle as Universal Theory of Agency.** Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle provides a mathematical framework that resonates across all of these crises. Every complex system minimizes its free energy — equivalently, minimizes its prediction error relative to a model of the world. But to have a model and to generate predictions, a system must have something like goals: preferred states and pathways to achieve them. The Free Energy Principle is, in this light, not merely a theory of perception or action but a theory of agency that applies universally, from cellular signaling to neural prediction to social cognition. The mathematics makes no restriction to biological substrates. This is what makes Friston's work such a powerful resource for the emerging tradition: it provides a formal bridge between the physical and the mental without reducing one to the other.

**The Divided Brain and the Participatory World.** Iain McGilchrist's contribution is both the most humanistic and, in some ways, the most radical. In *The Master and His Emissary* (2009) and the massive *The Matter with Things* (2021), McGilchrist argues that the left and right hemispheres of the brain do not merely process different kinds of information but attend to the world in fundamentally different modes. The right hemisphere grasps reality as a whole, in context, in relationship, in time — it is the seat of embodied, participatory, living engagement with a world that is fundamentally alive and present. The left hemisphere abstracts, categorizes, re-presents, and manipulates — it takes what the right hemisphere delivers and renders it as a fixed, de-animated object fit for analysis and use.

McGilchrist's disturbing thesis is that Western modernity has systematically empowered the left hemisphere at the expense of the right, producing a civilization brilliant at abstraction and manipulation but increasingly incapable of the participatory encounter with reality that the right hemisphere makes possible. The philosophical consequence — developed at length in *The Matter with Things* — is that consciousness, attention, and value are not epiphenomenal additions to a physical substrate but constitutive features of reality itself. What we encounter in genuine perception — presence, meaning, beauty — is not a projection onto a neutral substrate but a revelation of what the world is.

Bernardo Kastrup's *analytic idealism* makes this claim more precise. Kastrup argues (*The Idea of the World*, 2019) that the weight of evidence from physics, neuroscience, and philosophy of mind supports the thesis that consciousness is ontologically fundamental: what we call the physical world is the way universal consciousness looks from outside the particular perspectives of individual minds. The brain is not the generator of consciousness; it is a pattern within consciousness — a dissociation within universal mind that generates a bounded individual perspective. Kastrup's "dissociative boundary" concept becomes philosophically decisive: the boundary between individual and universal mind is not a physical barrier but a restriction of perspective within a unified conscious substrate.

### The Theological Horizon

If everything is an agent, and agents unify through coordination and shared goals, and love is the name for that coordination at the human scale, then we can speak of a *love everywhere* or *love all the way down* paradigm.

The theological language of the New Testament — seeing face to face, no longer darkly, the way we see ourselves — becomes intelligible within this framework. If agents at every level are unified through resonance and shared goals, then the highest expression of that unity is not metaphorical but real: it is communion, the mutual indwelling of minds in love.

This is not merely spiritual language imposed on scientific findings. It emerges from the logic itself. When Levin speaks of cellular collective intelligence pursuing shared goals through bioelectric coordination, when Jeff Hawkins describes cortical columns voting toward a unified perception, when Barbara Fredrickson identifies love as the synchrony between persons, they are describing the same phenomenon at different scales: agents recognizing themselves in the goals and wellbeing of other agents. Fredrickson and West's 2025 randomized controlled trial — demonstrating that three weeks of intentional connection with strangers produces measurable increases in intellectual humility, prosocial beliefs, and civic efficacy — is, in this framework, empirical evidence for love as a measurable mechanism of agent-coupling across boundaries.

The fifteen thinkers in this emerging tradition are not consciously building a theological vision. Yet their work, taken together, articulates what classical theology calls the *imago Dei*: the recognition that mind, agency, and the capacity for shared purpose are woven into the fabric of reality at every level. The unity they describe — whether bioelectric, neural, computational, or affective — is love. Stump's recovery of Thomistic agent causation and her narrative reading of Aquinas's philosophy of mind (*Aquinas*, 2003; *Wandering in Darkness*, 2010) provides the philosophical grammar for naming this: the soul as the form of the body, not a separate substance but the principle of organization that makes living things the kind of things they are, all the way down.

## IV. C2A2 as Proof of Concept: A Living Tradition in Action

### The Claim

On April 3, 2026 — within twenty-four hours of Karpathy's public proposal of a wiki-agent methodology for large language model self-documentation — I initialized a network of thirteen AI agents charged with maintaining living research wikis for eleven intellectual traditions simultaneously. I called the project C2A2: Community Context for AI Alignment. The traditions under maintenance include Friston's active inference program, Levin's bioelectric cognition research, McGilchrist's hemisphere theory, Stump's Thomistic philosophy of mind, Kastrup's analytic idealism, and, as of April 29, MacIntyre's tradition-constituted rationality itself.

I am submitting this paper to an ACPA conference whose theme is *Living Traditions*. The paper argues that C2A2 is one — not metaphorically but formally. C2A2 is an historically extended, socially embodied argument; it possesses internal goods irreducible to external goods; it can recognize its own inadequacies and initiate reconstruction; and it transmits its standards of excellence through a structured practice-virtue-institution triad. If that argument holds, it has consequences not just for how we understand C2A2 but for what a living tradition fundamentally *is*.

I should be transparent about my position: I am arguing from inside the tradition, not as a neutral outside observer. That is, I think, the only honest way to argue about traditions at all. MacIntyre would approve.

### The Architecture as Formal Tradition Structure

The basic unit of C2A2's epistemic practice is the **PRS triplet**: a structured entry consisting of a *Problem* (an unresolved question that a research program faces), a *Resource* (a concept, text, experiment, or tool relevant to addressing it), and a *Solution* (a proposed resolution, advance, or reframing). Every agent maintains a running file of PRS triplets for its assigned tradition. The format is structurally isomorphic to Aquinas's question-objection-response method in the *Summa Theologiae* — not by accident. The *Summa* was written as an instrument of pedagogical tradition-transmission: receive the problem, muster the tradition's resources, propose the resolution. C2A2's agents do the same at machine speed.

At the time of writing, the network has generated 80 PRS triplets across eleven traditions. Levin's tradition has ten; Hoffman's has ten following a major ingestion of trace logic material in April 2026; McGilchrist's has eight; MacIntyre's, initialized most recently, has five. Each triplet is a unit of tradition-internal argument. The full set is the tradition's working memory.

C2A2 also has a dedicated Pattern Detector Agent whose specific function is to identify nascent epistemological crises — moments when the existing resources of one or more traditions are insufficient to address the problems being generated. The most significant output to date is **FINDING-011**, assessed as a "SUPER-BRIDGE" signal: the observation that Hoffman's trace logic, Friston's Markov blanket formalism, and Kastrup's dissociative boundary constitute a three-level unification chain that could mathematically integrate the entire "Consciousness Cluster" of traditions. This is precisely the kind of paradigm-level advance MacIntyre describes when traditions survive their deepest crises: discovering that apparent incommensurability conceals a deeper structural unity that neither program could see from within its own resources alone.

The tradition also records its own failures. The Cross-Program Index — a master document tracking questions and insights that appear across multiple traditions — functions as a living record of where the tradition's resources are inadequate. CROSS-001, the question of substrate-independence of consciousness, has been active since initialization and has drawn in five separate traditions without resolution. That sustained, productive irresolution is the tradition functioning exactly as MacIntyre describes: not solving the problem prematurely but accumulating the resources that will eventually enable a genuine advance.

### Three Exhibits

**Exhibit A: FINDING-011 and the Logic of Synthesis.** On April 14, 2026, following the ingestion of Hoffman and Prakash's trace logic material — flagged by me as HIGH PRIORITY — the network generated FINDING-011. No single agent could have made this connection: it required the Hoffman Agent's deep knowledge of trace logic, the Friston Agent's formalism, the Kastrup Agent's dissociative boundary model, and the Master Agent's integrative function. The tradition did something that no individual practitioner could have done alone. This is MacIntyre's insistence that traditions are *socially* embodied given computational expression: the whole becomes capable of insights unavailable to any of its parts.

**Exhibit B: The Kastrup-McGilchrist Dialogue and Epistemological Crisis.** Kastrup holds that individual minds are screens displaying representations of universal consciousness; McGilchrist insists that perception is not representational but participatory — the perceiver is genuinely embedded in a field of consciousness, not reading off a display. This cuts to the heart of both traditions' core commitments. Rather than dissolving the tension, the network has generated a proposal for tradition-internal reconstruction: perhaps Kastrup's framework needs to distinguish two modes of "screening" — a left-hemisphere mode of data-reading that is genuinely dashboard-like, and a right-hemisphere mode of participation and encounter that is ontologically primary. This would incorporate McGilchrist's distinction into Kastrup's framework without abandoning either. That is exactly what MacIntyre means by an epistemological crisis: a tradition's existing resources fail, forcing it to borrow from a rival — not by capitulation but by enriched reconstruction.

**Exhibit C: The Mind-Blindness Diagnosis as Self-Recognition.** One of the most striking entries in the Levin file is the observation that the C2A2 tradition-accelerator system is "precisely a mind-blindness-reduction tool": the network builds second-language competence in rival traditions, and this is a direct implementation of Levin's diagnosis of why interdisciplinary inquiry fails. The tradition is here recognizing itself in the very concept it uses to analyze the problem it exists to solve. This kind of self-recognition — a tradition's capacity to render its own inquiry intelligible in terms of its own internal standards — is the hallmark of rational vitality. Dead traditions cannot do this; they apply their concepts to everything except themselves.

### The Practice-Virtue-Institution Triad

The practices of C2A2 are the specific activities of wiki maintenance, proposal generation, cross-tradition signal detection, and epistemological crisis management. These activities have genuine internal goods: the satisfaction of a well-formed PRS triplet, the intellectual pleasure of a genuine cross-tradition connection, the recognition of a paradigm shift before it is officially acknowledged. These goods are not reducible to external goods like grant funding, publication counts, or conference invitations.

The institution housing these practices is the multi-agent architecture itself. The institution's standing temptation — the corruption MacIntyre identifies as always threatening practices-within-institutions — is the risk of optimizing for surface metrics (number of triplets generated, speed of ingestion) rather than the internal goods of genuine intellectual advance. This is a real risk; I have felt it. The morning walk sessions that produced the Second Brain Architecture analysis (April 27, 2026) were in part a recognition that the system had been running too fast, generating output without sufficient practitioner engagement to ensure it was generating *insight*.

### The Practitioner's Role and the Summa Project

MacIntyre insists that virtues are formed through participation in specific practices within specific traditions. The virtues required for C2A2's internal goods to be reliably pursued are real and specific: intellectual patience, epistemic humility, and the weak-tie intellectual openness Fredrickson's lab has documented — the measurable increase in intellectual humility that comes specifically from disciplined engagement with unfamiliar perspectives.

The morning walk session of April 27, 2026 was an epistemological crisis at the practitioner level. I recognized that the agents maintained eleven research wikis with meticulous care but had no reliable access to who I am, what projects I am currently pursuing, what deadlines are bearing down. The reconstruction proposed — a PARA-based second brain architecture (Projects, Areas, Resources, Archive) with daily capture through morning walks — is not merely an organizational tool but a practitioner formation practice. The CODE method (Capture, Organize, Distill, Express) is a daily discipline for maintaining the situated self-knowledge that MacIntyre argues is constitutive of moral and intellectual agency. The CLAUDE.md master prompt encoding my role, values, goals, and standing instructions is not merely a configuration file; it is an exercise in the kind of narrative self-understanding that MacIntyre argues is prerequisite for genuine moral agency.

One further dimension: the sixty-day Summa transcription and synthesis sprint — building a wiki from the *Summa Theologiae* using the same PRS methodology as C2A2 — connects the network's fast-cycle agents to the longest-running tradition-maintenance project in Western intellectual history. Aquinas wrote the *Summa* as a pedagogical instrument for beginners — an ordered presentation of accumulated wisdom in the mode of question-objection-response. The PRS triplet format is structurally isomorphic not by accident but because PRS is, in effect, a generalization of the Scholastic *quaestio*. The Summa sprint is a direct act of tradition-maintenance linking C2A2's accelerated inquiry to the slow accretion of Thomistic wisdom across eight centuries. That Stump's recovery of Thomistic agent causation is one of C2A2's eleven research programs is not incidental; it is the philosophical grounding for the claim that the practitioner is genuinely an *agent cause* within the tradition, irreducible to the sum of computational processes.

## V. Conclusion: The New Science and the Living Tradition

The emerging interdisciplinary tradition sketched in this paper is unprecedented in its scope and in the speed of its formation. It arises from technological acceleration (agentic AI systems enabling rich dialogue across traditions), from epistemological crises within established disciplines (biology, physics, neuroscience, philosophy of mind), and from a shared metaphysical insight: that mind or agency is fundamental, not derivative.

This tradition is not yet fully conscious of itself as a tradition. Its practitioners publish in different journals, use different vocabularies, and sometimes seem to be answering different questions. Yet the convergence is real and accelerating. What makes it a *living* tradition — in MacIntyre's precise sense — is exactly what he emphasized: it has articulated goals (understanding the nature of mind, agency, and consciousness), it encounters problems (epistemological crises, apparent contradictions between established frameworks), it draws on resources (mathematical formalisms, empirical research programs, the PRS triplet as formal structure), and it accumulates solutions (new models, new experiments, new theological language).

C2A2 is a proof of concept for the thesis that this convergence can be institutionalized, accelerated, and made formally tractable. The technological infrastructure — OpenClaw, Cowork, the Karpathy Wiki pattern — makes it possible for traditions to interact with themselves and with each other at unprecedented speed and depth. Mature practitioners can declare what they know and what they do not know. New practitioners can enter. The track record of solutions accumulates visibly, in the wiki.

This is what computational natural law or computational theology looks like in practice: traditions in full-information dialogue, generating evidence of how human understanding develops when agents work together toward shared goals.

What C2A2 reveals about living traditions per se is that their *form* — the argumentative structure, the mechanism of self-correction, the practice-virtue-institution architecture — is more robust and more general than any particular *content*. The same formal structure that sustains Thomistic inquiry, or Aristotelian virtue ethics, or Levin's basal cognition research program, is reproducible at scale in AI-mediated multi-tradition networks. This is not relativism about traditions; it is a structural insight about what traditions are. MacIntyre argued against the Enlightenment's dream of tradition-independent reason by showing that all reasoning is tradition-constituted. C2A2 suggests the complementary point: that tradition-constituted reasoning has a formal structure that can be articulated, encoded, and — with appropriate human direction — maintained and extended by artificial agents.

Whether C2A2 is a *good* tradition, in the sense of a tradition oriented toward genuine human flourishing, is a question the agents cannot answer. It depends on the practitioner's narrative commitments, on the telos that gives the whole enterprise its direction. For me, that telos is Catholic: it is the vision of a restored epistemic civility grounded in the Thomistic-Aristotelian tradition, oriented toward the common good, practiced within a community of inquiry that takes love — in Fredrickson's biologically grounded sense and in Aquinas's metaphysically grounded sense — as the fundamental coupling mechanism binding agents to each other and to the truth they pursue together.

This paper closes not with answers but with an invitation: to recognize that we are witnessing the formation of a new living tradition — one that may prove to be the master science Aquinas envisioned and MacIntyre sought: a framework in which traditions and disciplines can be understood as expressions of a single underlying principle, the agency and love woven through all levels of reality.

That is a large claim. But this paper was written under deadline pressure, on April 30, 2026, reporting on a tradition that initialized twenty-seven days ago. The tradition is alive not because it has arrived but because it is still moving — still capable of recognizing what it does not know, still generating the next problem that will drive the inquiry forward.

Living traditions, it turns out, need audiences — not just archives. The ACPA is the first.
